# Sources — incremental-vs-leap-growth

> *Primary sources for the [incremental-vs-leap-growth](../SKILL.md) skill.*

1. **Clayton Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma*** (1997). https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=46 — Foundational framework for distinguishing sustaining (incremental) from disruptive (leap) innovation. The distinction between improving performance along existing dimensions vs. introducing a different value proposition maps directly to the incremental/leap mode distinction. Verbatim: "Sustaining technologies... improve the performance of established products, along the dimensions of performance that mainstream customers in major markets have historically valued... Disruptive technologies bring to market a very different value proposition than had been available previously."

2. **John Kotter, *Leading Change*** (1996). https://hbr.org/2007/01/leading-change-why-transformation-efforts-fail — Empirical analysis of why organizational transformation (leap) initiatives fail, with the critical finding that skipping phases produces illusory speed. The multi-phase requirement of transformation maps directly to the stage-transition model of leap growth. Verbatim: "By far the biggest mistake people make when trying to change organizations is to plunge ahead without establishing a high enough sense of urgency... Transformation efforts must go through phases that... take considerable time. Skipping steps only creates the illusion of speed."

3. **Lewis, T. *Divided Highways: Building the Interstate Highways, Transforming American Life*** (Cornell University Press, 2013) — Primary historical source on the transition from incremental to leap mode in US highway construction, providing the documented before/after comparison used in the Method in Action section.

4. **Federal Highway Administration, Interstate Highway History.** https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/interstate/history.cfm — Primary government source for the Interstate System statistics (41,000 miles target, $25B funding, 1956 Act provisions).

**What was NOT cited and why:** Gary Hamel and C.K. Prahalad's *Competing for the Future* (1994) contains related "strategic stretch" concepts but frames the distinction as ambition level rather than mode incompatibility — which is a less precise lens for the execution-level problems this skill addresses. Jim Collins' *Built to Last* flywheel concept is not cited because it conflates incremental and leap within a single "flywheel" metaphor that does not distinguish their differing tool requirements.
