# Method in Action: Nvidia's Manic-Depressive Quote (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [mr-market](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between 2023 and 2026, Nvidia became the clearest large-cap illustration of Ben Graham's Mr. Market since the 2008 crisis: a single stock whose quote swung by hundreds of billions of dollars in a day on sentiment and headlines, while the underlying business — designing the GPUs and the CUDA software stack that trained the world's AI models — compounded on a far smoother curve. This walks the case through the [mr-market](../SKILL.md) skill's six process steps. It is an illustration of the discipline, not investment advice.

## Step 1 — State quote and business reality

**The quote.** Nvidia's market capitalization crossed roughly **$1 trillion in mid-2023**, then approximately **$3 trillion in mid-2024** (briefly making it one of the most valuable companies in the world alongside Apple and Microsoft), and reached about **$4 trillion in mid-2025** — the first company reported to touch that milestone. But the path was violent. The most vivid single episode: on **January 27, 2025**, news that the Chinese lab **DeepSeek** had trained a competitive model (R1) at reportedly far lower cost triggered a one-day selloff that wiped out a record amount of market value — widely reported as roughly **$600 billion** in a single session, the largest one-day market-cap loss for any company on record at the time. The quote also lurched repeatedly on US **export-control** headlines restricting advanced-chip sales to China (successive tightening rounds in 2022, 2023, and 2025).

**The business reality.** Over the same window, Nvidia's actual results compounded steadily. Data-center revenue — the segment selling AI accelerators — grew from a few billion dollars per quarter in early fiscal 2023 to the dominant share of a company whose **annual revenue rose from roughly $27 billion (FY2023) to about $60.9 billion (FY2024) to about $130.5 billion (FY2025)**, with gross margins reported in the 70%+ range. The CUDA software moat — a nearly two-decade-old developer ecosystem that competitors' hardware could not easily displace — did not change on January 27, 2025.

**The divergence.** The quote moved on mood and headlines by the hour; the business moved on a quarterly curve that kept climbing. They diverged sharply — most obviously on the DeepSeek day, when the intrinsic-value story (does one efficient model make the world need *fewer* GPUs?) was genuinely debatable, but a ~$600B one-day repricing was a Mr. Market mood swing, not the arrival of that much settled information.

## Step 2 — Form an independent view of intrinsic value

The discipline requires your *own* estimate before you look at the quote. For a hypothetical long-term holder in early 2026, the value question is not "what did the stock do today" but: how durable is Nvidia's earning power?

- **Durability of demand:** AI training and inference capex from hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) was, on their own public guidance, still rising into 2025–2026. That supports high near-term earnings but says nothing about whether the pace is sustainable.
- **Durability of the moat:** CUDA plus the installed base of developers and frameworks is the real asset. The bear case (custom silicon from hyperscalers, AMD, cheaper-to-train models) attacks *margin* and *pricing power*, not near-term volume.
- **Sensitivity:** the estimate is highly sensitive to two unknowns — (1) whether AI capex is a durable secular build-out or a bubble that mean-reverts, and (2) whether efficiency gains (the DeepSeek thesis) reduce total GPU demand or, via the Jevons-paradox counter-argument, *increase* it by making AI cheaper to deploy everywhere.

The honest output of Step 2 is a *wide* value range with explicitly flagged sensitivities — not a single number. That width is the point: it tells you which quote moves are inside the noise and which would be genuine dislocations.

## Step 3 — Diagnose Mr. Market's state

- **Manic** through much of 2023–2024 and again into mid-2025: enthusiasm about AI drove the quote to successive trillion-dollar milestones faster than any prior company, with commentary openly debating an "AI bubble."
- **Depressed** in acute bursts: the January 2025 DeepSeek session, and repeated drawdowns on export-control news and broader tech selloffs. On those days the quote was set by fear, not by a revised earnings model.
- **Sober** in the quieter stretches when the price tracked earnings beats and guidance.

The tell that this is Mr. Market and not new information: the *same* company was "the most valuable in the world" and "the biggest one-day loser in history" within months, with no comparable swing in the quarterly fundamentals.

## Step 4 — Choose the response

Mr. Market discipline does not say "Nvidia is cheap" or "Nvidia is expensive" — it says *respond to dislocations relative to your own value range, and ignore the rest*.

- When the quote ran to fresh milestones on pure enthusiasm (manic) and pushed *above* the top of your value range, the disciplined response was to **trim or ignore — not chase** the momentum.
- When a headline like DeepSeek knocked the quote *below* the bottom of your range in a single fear-driven session (depressed), the disciplined response — *only if* your Step 2 view survived the news — was to treat the panic price as an **offer you may take**, not an instruction to sell.
- When the quote sat inside your (wide) value range, the disciplined response was to **ignore Mr. Market entirely** and watch the business.

The forced honesty: if the DeepSeek thesis genuinely changed your intrinsic-value view (fewer GPUs needed long-term), then that day was *information*, and the mr-market skill explicitly tells you **not** to activate — you should reprice, not "buy the dip." The discipline is distinguishing which it was.

## Step 5 — Check for emotional contagion

The contagion test: *would I make the same decision if I hadn't seen today's quote?* On January 27, 2025, the honest answer for most holders was no — the urge to sell was the crowd's fear transmitting through a red screen, not a revised DCF. Symmetrically, at the $4T milestone the urge to buy more was the crowd's euphoria and "everyone owns it" social proof (see [`social-proof`](../social-proof/SKILL.md)), not a fresh valuation. The quote itself was acting as an anchor (see [`anchoring`](../anchoring/SKILL.md)) in both directions.

## Step 6 — Document

A documented Mr. Market decision for this case would read: *Decision — hold core position; treat panic sessions as optional buy windows only if the intrinsic-value thesis survives the specific news; do not chase milestone euphoria. Rationale — quote volatility (a ~$600B one-day swing) vastly exceeded any single-day change in earning power; the CUDA moat and hyperscaler capex are the value drivers, not the daily tape. Re-evaluation trigger — next earnings report and hyperscaler capex guidance (a calendar date), not the next price alert.*

The calendar trigger is what defends against the Red Flag the skill warns about: checking the quote hourly and letting your view of value move in lockstep with a screen that swings by hundreds of billions on sentiment.

## The lesson

Nvidia 2023–2026 is Mr. Market at industrial scale. The instructive part is not that the stock rose — it is that a company could be crowned the world's most valuable and set an all-time one-day loss record within the span of a year, while its quarterly business marched along a comparatively smooth curve. The quote reflected Mr. Market's mood about the AI build-out; the value reflected the CUDA moat and the durability of AI demand. Graham's rule from 1949 applied without modification: transact when Mr. Market's pricing serves your independent view, and ignore him the rest of the time.
