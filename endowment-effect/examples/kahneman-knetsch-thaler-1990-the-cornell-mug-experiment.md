# Method in Action: Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler 1990 — The Cornell Mug Experiment

> *Example for the [endowment-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Richard Thaler** (1945–; Nobel Laureate in Economics 2017) had identified the endowment effect as a theoretical construct in his 1980 paper mapping departures from standard consumer theory. The empirical proof came from a collaboration with **Daniel Kahneman** and **Jack Knetsch** in a series of experiments run at Cornell University in the late 1980s.

The experimental design was deliberately simple to rule out confounds. Students were randomly given one of two assignments at the beginning of a class session:

- **Sellers:** received a Cornell University coffee mug (retailing for approximately $6 at the campus store) and were told they could either keep it or sell it in the market that was about to open.
- **Buyers:** received no mug but were told they could purchase a mug in the market if they wished.

A double-auction market was then run. According to the Coase theorem — a cornerstone of standard economic theory — rational agents should trade until all gains are exhausted. Because mugs were randomly assigned, approximately half of the mugs should end up traded to buyers who value them more than the random sellers do. The standard prediction: trading volume of approximately 50% of available mugs, with prices converging around the true market value of ~$6.

The actual results:

| Group | Median valuation | Prediction | Actual |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sellers (owners) | Market value ~$6 | ~$6 | $5.25 |
| Buyers (non-owners) | Market value ~$6 | ~$6 | $2.25 |
| Predicted trade volume | ~50% | ~50% | ~15% |

The median seller demanded $5.25; the median buyer offered $2.25. The ratio of approximately 2.3:1 is consistent with the loss aversion coefficient estimated independently from prospect theory experiments (~2.0–2.5). Trade volume was approximately 15% — far below the 50% predicted by standard theory.

> "Contrary to theoretical expectations, the Coase theorem fails to hold in this setting. The mere fact of ownership significantly raises the value that people place on goods."
>
> — Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1990), p. 1344.

This result has been replicated extensively across cultures, age groups, and object types. The magnitude (roughly 2:1 asking-to-offer ratio) is remarkably stable.

**Business applications across domains:**

**Real estate.** Homeowners consistently list above market comparables. The psychological mechanism is pure endowment: the home the seller is "giving up" is coded as a loss, inflating their minimum acceptable price beyond what the market will offer. Studies of listing vs. sale prices show a systematic premium that narrows only under financial pressure.

**M&A — founder endowment.** Founders selling companies routinely attach a higher valuation than acquirers are willing to pay. The gap is partly explained by information asymmetry (the founder knows the pipeline better), partly by genuine disagreement on growth trajectory, and partly by endowment. Earnout structures — where a portion of consideration is contingent on post-acquisition performance — are a structural answer to the endowment gap: the seller gets closer to their subjective value if the company performs; the buyer pays market value if it does not.

**Used car sales.** Sellers of used vehicles consistently price above market comparables and above what they would have paid for the same vehicle when they were buyers. Dealerships exploit this: when taking a trade-in, they redirect attention from the new vehicle price, reducing the salience of the endowment premium the seller would otherwise anchor on.

**Free trials and SaaS churn.** SaaS companies have empirically found that the longer and more personalized a free trial, the lower the churn upon conversion to paid. The mechanism is endowment: users who have configured settings, imported data, and built workflows around the product feel they would be "losing" these configurations if they cancel — even when the same configurations could be rebuilt in a competing product.

**Employee equity attachment.** Employees holding unvested or vested company equity consistently value it above market price. This creates retention effects beyond pure financial incentive: the employee is not just calculating expected value; they are experiencing the equity as "theirs" in a way that inflates both its subjective value and the psychological cost of forfeiting it (as in resignation before vesting).

Three operational lessons:

**First, quantify the endowment gap explicitly.** In any negotiation where one party is an owner and the other a buyer, estimate what portion of the valuation gap is market-based vs. endowment-based. This prevents wasting deal time on a gap that is structural, not factual.

**Second, introduce neutral reference prices early.** Comparable transactions, market benchmarks, and third-party appraisals reduce the weight of the owner's self-anchor. The earlier these references are introduced, the less the endowment premium anchors the conversation.

**Third, use structural bridges, not just price negotiation.** Earnouts, deferred consideration, and performance-linked payments address endowment gaps more efficiently than raw price negotiation because they convert the seller's "I'm giving this up" into "I'm receiving what it's worth if it performs."
