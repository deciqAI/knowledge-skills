# Method in Action: Amazon's Everyday Low Price Commitment (1994–present)

> *Example for the [strategic-commitment](../SKILL.md) skill.*

When Jeff Bezos launched Amazon in 1994, the company made a strategic commitment that seemed impossible to enforce: that Amazon would always offer the lowest available price. The competitive logic was clear — if customers believe Amazon is always cheapest, they do not comparison-shop, which dramatically lowers Amazon's customer acquisition cost. But the commitment requires customers and suppliers to believe it, and belief requires credibility.

Run the Commitment Design on Amazon's price commitment:

**Step 1 (threat stated precisely):** "On any product in our catalog, Amazon's price is at or below any competitor's listed price. If a competitor lists a lower price, Amazon will match it — automatically and in real time."

**Step 2 (Credibility Audit):**
- *Capability:* Amazon had the cash and logistics to absorb price matching costs. ✓
- *Action taken:* Amazon deployed automated pricing algorithms (its proprietary pricing engine) that scan competitors' prices continuously and adjust Amazon's prices — estimated at approximately 2.5 million price changes per day as of 2013 (Profitero price tracking data). The algorithm is the irreversible action: it executes automatically without human decision at each instance. ✓
- *Visibility:* Price comparisons are publicly visible; customers and sellers can verify Amazon's prices in real time. ✓
- *Enforcement:* Amazon's A9 search algorithm penalizes third-party sellers who list at higher prices, creating a market-level enforcement mechanism. ✓

**Step 3 (current cost of retreat):** Before the algorithm, a human decision to not match a competitor's lower price was cheap — it required no action. After the algorithm, matching is automatic; *not* matching requires a deliberate override, which would be visible to sellers and customers and would immediately damage Amazon's core brand claim.

**Step 4 (commitment device):** Primarily organizational lock-in (automated algorithm) combined with reputation. The algorithm is the commitment device — it converts a policy statement into an automatic consequence, removing the temptation to defect case-by-case.

**Step 5 (post-commitment game tree):** Competitors who lower price know Amazon will automatically respond. The expected gain from undercutting Amazon is zero or negative (Amazon matches, competitor margins fall, neither gains share). This suppresses price competition among rivals without Amazon needing to fight each case.

**Step 6 (rigidity risk):** The commitment created rigidity during inflationary periods (2022–2023) and when suppliers demanded higher wholesale prices. Amazon managed this through its third-party marketplace model — many "Amazon" prices are set by third-party sellers, not Amazon directly — maintaining the appearance of lowest-price commitment while shifting some rigidity risk to sellers.

Primary sources: Dixit, A. & Nalebuff, B. (1991), *Thinking Strategically*; Schelling, T.C. (1960), *The Strategy of Conflict*; Profitero (2013), *The State of Online Retail Pricing*.
