# Method in Action: Suzy Welch, 1990s; Jeff Bezos, 1994

> *Example for the [10-10-10](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Suzy Welch describes the origin of 10-10-10 in her 2009 book. It was not invented in a corporate boardroom or a research lab. It came from a specific moment in 1990s Hawaii, when Welch — a working mother juggling four children, a high-profile editorial career, and constant travel — had a decision-making meltdown in front of her 6-year-old daughter, Sophia.

Welch had been invited to give a keynote address in a distant city. Sophia had an important school event the same week. Welch was paralyzed: missing the speech would damage her professional reputation; missing Sophia's event would damage her relationship with her daughter. After hours of going in circles, Welch tried to think clearly about *what would matter, when*. She forced herself to ask: how would she feel about each option in 10 minutes, in 10 months, in 10 years?

The 10-minute answer favored the keynote (avoiding the immediate embarrassment of canceling). The 10-month answer was ambiguous — both options had recoverable trajectories. The 10-year answer was clear: in 10 years, Sophia would remember which her mother chose. The keynote would be utterly forgotten.

Welch cancelled the keynote and attended Sophia's event. The pattern repeated in subsequent decisions, and Welch began to apply the question deliberately. By the time she wrote *10-10-10*, she had used the framework on hundreds of decisions across personal, professional, and creative domains, and had interviewed others applying it. The empirical pattern she documents:

> "10-10-10 forces you to do something most of us don't do naturally — to take into account multiple time horizons when deciding. When we're caught up in an emotional moment — when a colleague has just insulted us, or when we've just spotted a sample sale, or when a child is throwing a tantrum — we tend to optimize for the 10-minute view. The 10-month and 10-year views are not subtle improvements; they are entirely different decisions. The discipline is to consult them deliberately."
>
> — Welch, S. (2009). *10-10-10: A Life-Transforming Idea.* Scribner. ISBN 978-1416591818. pp. 24-25.

Welch's framework is operationally identical, on the 10-year horizon, to **Jeff Bezos's "regret minimization framework"**, which he developed independently in 1994. Bezos was 30, working as Senior Vice President at D.E. Shaw, a quantitative hedge fund. He had identified the rapid growth of internet commerce as a once-in-a-generation opportunity and had begun secretly drafting a business plan for an online bookstore. The decision: leave a lucrative, fast-track Wall Street career, or stay.

Bezos described his reasoning in a 1998 Academy of Achievement interview (reproduced in many places, including his 2020 book *Invent and Wander*):

> "It was a hard decision. I was already in the middle of the year and I knew that the year-end bonus was coming. And I had this idea. I wanted to start an internet bookstore. I told my boss about it. He was very smart. He took me on a long walk in Central Park, listened to me carefully, and then said, 'You know what, Jeff, this is a really good idea. But it would be an even better idea for someone who didn't already have a good job.' That logic made some sense to me, and he convinced me to think about it for 48 hours before making a final decision.
>
> So I went away and was trying to find the right framework for making that decision. The framework I found, which made the decision incredibly easy, was what I called — only a nerd would call it this — a 'regret minimization framework.' So I wanted to project myself forward to age 80 and say, 'OK, now I'm looking back on my life. I want to have minimized the number of regrets I have.' I knew that when I was 80, I was not going to regret having tried this. I was not going to regret trying to participate in this thing called the Internet that I thought was going to be a really big deal. I knew that if I failed, I wouldn't regret that. But I knew the one thing I might regret is not ever having tried. I knew that that would haunt me every day. So that made the decision incredibly easy."
>
> — Bezos, J. (1998). Academy of Achievement interview. Republished in *Invent and Wander: The Collected Writings of Jeff Bezos* (HBR Press, 2020), ISBN 978-1647820718, pp. 41-42.

The 10-year horizon, applied at age 30, said *go start the bookstore*. The 10-minute horizon — the year-end bonus, the safe career, the confused friends — said *stay*. Bezos's framework forced the 10-year horizon to dominate. Amazon launched on July 5, 1995.

The structural identity between Welch's 10-year question and Bezos's regret-minimization question is not coincidence. Both are operational implementations of an older insight: that **immediate cognition systematically discounts distant consequences**, and that the corrective is to **deliberately consult the distant viewpoint**. Marcus Aurelius wrote in *Meditations* IX.2:

> "Look back over the past, with its changing empires that rose and fell, and you can foresee the future too. Its pattern will be the same. ... A man of forty endowed with a moderate degree of intelligence has in a sense seen all that is past and all that is to come."

The Stoic and Buddhist practices of *contemplating death*, *contemplating impermanence*, and *prospective hindsight* are all variations on the same temporal-distance device. Welch and Bezos modernized it into a usable, repeatable decision instrument.

**Daniel Kahneman**'s research provides the cognitive science. In *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), Kahneman documents the systematic over-weighting of immediate emotional states (System 1) over deliberate long-horizon analysis (System 2). The 10-10-10 framework is a System-2 forcing device — a deliberate intervention that requires the slow-thinking apparatus to engage on a decision where fast-thinking would otherwise dominate.

Three operational lessons:

**First, the framework works because the answers usually differ.** A decision where all three horizons converge on the same answer is a low-stakes decision; you can stop deliberating. A decision where they diverge is one where you were systematically underweighting one horizon — almost always the longest.

**Second, the medium horizon (10 months) is the most useful.** The 10-minute horizon is dominated by emotion; the 10-year horizon by comfortable abstraction. The 10-month horizon is concrete enough to plan and far enough that emotional override has faded. Many decisions are decided in the 10-month answer.

**Third, the framework also resolves over-deliberation.** When 10-10-10 returns "fine, fine, fine," you know the decision is low-stakes and you can move on. Some of the framework's value is in giving permission *not* to deliberate further when deliberation is unwarranted.
