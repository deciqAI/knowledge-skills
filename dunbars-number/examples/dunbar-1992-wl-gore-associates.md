# Method in Action: Dunbar 1992 + W.L. Gore & Associates

> *Example for the [dunbars-number](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Robin Dunbar** (b. 1947) is a British anthropologist and evolutionary psychologist at the University of Oxford. His 1992 paper originated in a specific observation: across primates, species with larger neocortex ratios live in larger stable social groups. The neocortex, the "thinking" part of the brain responsible for social cognition, appears to be the binding constraint on social group size — because maintaining stable relationships requires tracking each person's identity, reliability, relationship history, and current status relative to every other group member. The cognitive load scales with group size, and at some point the neocortex hits capacity.

Dunbar analyzed 38 primate genera, measuring neocortex ratio and mean social group size. The regression line, extrapolated to human neocortex size, predicted a stable group size of 147.8 — consistently rounded to 150. Dunbar then cross-validated:

> "Predicted group size for humans on the basis of our neocortex volume is 147.8. ... The distributions of group sizes for hunter-gatherer societies, Neolithic farming villages, and modern communities all peak in the range 150–200 individuals."
>
> — Dunbar, R. I. M. (1992). "Neocortex size as a constraint on group size in primates." *Journal of Human Evolution*, 22(6), 469–493, p. 488.

The cross-validation hit multiple independent data streams: 21 hunter-gatherer societies (median group size 148.4); Neolithic village sizes from archaeological remains; Roman military units (the *centuria*, nominally 80 men, sits in the 50-layer; the *cohors* of 480 sits between 500 and 150 layers, requiring formal centurion hierarchy); Hutterite agricultural communities (mandated split at 150 for centuries before Dunbar's paper).

**W.L. Gore & Associates** is the most-cited modern organizational case. Bill Gore founded the company in 1958 after leaving DuPont, where he had observed that small, informal groups produced dramatically better innovation than large, hierarchically managed ones. He built Gore on a "lattice structure" — no formal hierarchy, no titles, no bosses, only "sponsors" and "associates." The principle was that coordination through relationships was more efficient than coordination through hierarchy.

Gore discovered empirically — before Dunbar published — that the lattice structure broke down above approximately 150 people. The solution: every plant was capped at 150. When a plant grew to capacity, a new plant was opened, often literally next door. By the late 1990s, Gore had 11,000+ employees across dozens of plants, each under 150 — and still maintained its lattice structure with minimal hierarchy.

Gladwell's documentation of the Gore case (2000, *The Tipping Point*, Chapter 5) introduced Dunbar's number to a mass business audience. The Gore case demonstrates that the 150-person cap is not a theory — it is a structural engineering choice with measurable operational consequences. Plants above 150 showed formal management emergence, political behavior, and productivity loss. Plants below 150 maintained voluntary coordination, peer accountability, and Gore's characteristic innovation culture.

The mechanism: in a group of 150, every person can maintain a mental model of every other person's trustworthiness, competence, and relationships. Free-rider problems are visible and self-correcting. Reputation mechanisms work because everyone knows everyone. Above 150, this breaks down: you cannot know and track 200 people's relationships — so you must rely on formal rules, titles, and documented processes to substitute for the relationship knowledge you no longer have.

This has direct implications for acquisition integration, org design at Series B/C, and community building:

- **Series A → B transition** (30–60 → 80–150 people): the founding team's personal knowledge of every employee becomes unsustainable. First VP hires should happen before, not after, crossing 80.
- **Series B → C transition** (80–150 → 150–300 people): the Dunbar ceiling is hit. Cross-functional processes must be formalized. Culture cannot be maintained by founder presence alone.
- **Acquisition integration** (combining two 150-person units): combining creates a 300-person entity requiring formal hierarchy — even if both units functioned informally before. The integration failure common in M&A is often Dunbar failure: informal cultures that worked below 150 shatter above it.
