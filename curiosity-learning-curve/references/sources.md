# Sources — curiosity-learning-curve

> *Primary sources for the [curiosity-learning-curve](../SKILL.md) skill.*

## Sources

1. Loewenstein, G. (1994). "The Psychology of Curiosity: A Review and Reinterpretation." *Psychological Bulletin*, 116(1), 75-98. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.116.1.75
   > "Curiosity is defined as a form of cognitively induced deprivation that arises from the perception of a gap in knowledge or understanding."

2. Faraday, M. (1839-1855). *Experimental Researches in Electricity*, 3 volumes. Richard and John Edward Taylor, London. Project Gutenberg edition: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/14986
   > "Nothing is too wonderful to be true, if it be consistent with the laws of nature; and in such things as these, experiment is the best test of such consistency." (*Diary*, 19 March 1849.)

3. James, W. (1890). *The Principles of Psychology*, Vol. 2, Chapter XXV ("The Emotions"). Henry Holt. https://archive.org/details/theprinciplesofp02jameuoft
   > "The physiological condition of curiosity is, then, the arousal of an uncertain situation, with its corresponding uncertain motor response."

**What is NOT cited and why:** The "three-curve" visual model (biological age / curiosity / learning capacity plotted against time) is a synthesis framework, not a figure from Loewenstein's paper or any single primary source. Loewenstein's paper establishes the cognitive mechanism; the three-curve synthesis is this framework's contribution. Einstein's widely-attributed statement "I am only passionately curious" (attributed to a 1955 letter to Carl Seelig) is often cited in this context but its archival provenance is disputed; it is not cited here. The Einstein Archives at Hebrew University of Jerusalem holds the primary archive (https://einsteinarchives.huji.ac.il/) but the specific letter text requires primary-source verification.
