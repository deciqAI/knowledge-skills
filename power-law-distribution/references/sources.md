# Sources — power-law-distribution

> *Primary sources for the [power-law-distribution](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Pareto, V. (1896). *Cours d'économie politique.* Lausanne: F. Rouge. Original empirical documentation of power-law wealth distribution.
- Mandelbrot, B. (1963). "The Variation of Certain Speculative Prices." *Journal of Business*, 36(4), 394–419. The foundational paper on power-law financial returns.
- Newman, M. E. J. (2005). "Power laws, Pareto distributions and Zipf's law." *Contemporary Physics*, 46(5), 323–351. Comprehensive empirical survey across domains.
- Taleb, N. N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable.* Random House. ISBN 978-1400063512. Operational treatment of power-law tail risk.
- Clauset, A., Shalizi, C. R., & Newman, M. E. J. (2009). "Power-law distributions in empirical data." *SIAM Review*, 51(4), 661–703. Statistical methodology for identifying power laws.
- O'Boyle, E. H., & Aguinis, H. (2012). "The best and the rest: Revisiting the norm of normality of individual performance." *Personnel Psychology*, 65(1), 79–119. Power-law output distributions in organizational settings.
- Correlation Ventures. (2014). "Returns to Venture Capital." Internal dataset analysis cited in Andreessen Horowitz blog and subsequent academic literature on VC return distributions.
- Nvidia Corporation. Investor Relations quarterly results and press releases, fiscal 2024–2025 (AI-driven data-center revenue growth; approximately $3 trillion market capitalization reached June 2024). https://investor.nvidia.com/ — contemporary illustration of extreme top-1 concentration in the AI-compute value chain.
- U.S. Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), advanced-computing and semiconductor manufacturing export-control rules (initial rule Oct 7, 2022; subsequent updates 2023–2024). https://www.bis.gov/ — policy driver of AI-compute concentration and a fat-tail risk factor; the Jan 27, 2025 DeepSeek-driven AI-stock sell-off is a widely reported illustration of the associated tail risk.
