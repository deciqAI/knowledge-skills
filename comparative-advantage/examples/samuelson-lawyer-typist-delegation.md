# Method in Action: Samuelson's Lawyer-and-Typist Delegation Case (1948 → present)

> *Example for the [comparative-advantage](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Ricardo stated the theorem at the scale of nations. Paul Samuelson's *Economics* (first edition 1948) — the dominant economics textbook of the twentieth century — carried it down to a single office, with the illustration that became the standard classroom test of whether a student really understands comparative advantage: the best lawyer in town is also the best typist in town. Should the lawyer type her own letters?

The intuitive answer — "yes, she's faster" — is the absolute-advantage trap. The textbook answer is no, and the case walks cleanly through the skill's Process:

**Step 1 — Producers and tasks.** Producer 1 = the lawyer. Producer 2 = the secretary. Task A = legal work. Task B = typing. Horizon = the working week.

**Step 2 — Productivity.** The lawyer is superior at *both* tasks: she bills legal work at a high hourly rate, and (illustratively, in the spirit of the textbook treatment) types faster than the secretary. The secretary can type but cannot produce billable legal work at all.

**Step 3 — Opportunity-cost ratios.** Each hour the lawyer spends typing forgoes an hour of high-value legal work — her opportunity cost of typing is enormous. The secretary's opportunity cost of an hour of typing is close to zero legal output forgone. The ratios diverge by orders of magnitude, so the comparative advantages are unambiguous: lawyer in law, secretary in typing.

**Step 4 — Specialization.** The lawyer practices law full-time; the secretary types. Each individual page may now be produced more slowly, yet the office's total output — legal value plus finished documents — is strictly higher than under self-sufficiency. Absolute superiority at typing was never a reason to type.

**Step 5 — Transaction costs.** Delegation is not free: dictation, review cycles, error correction, confidentiality risk. In this case they are small relative to the gap in opportunity costs, so specialization wins. The same net calculus governs the modern descendants of the example — founder time allocation, executive assistants, and outsourcing decisions — and where coordination or confidentiality costs dominate the value gap, doing it yourself remains correct.

**Step 6 — Implement and monitor.** The arrangement holds only while the productivity ratios hold. Word processing and then email collapsed the typing-speed gap and the cost of self-production, dissolving the dedicated-typist role in most offices — the delegation frontier moved rather than disappearing. Re-evaluation when ratios shift is part of the method, not an afterthought.

The case is why Samuelson could later call comparative advantage the only proposition in economics that is both true and non-obvious: the non-obvious part is visible even inside one office, with no borders, currencies, or tariffs anywhere in sight. Any professional who has said "it's faster if I just do it myself" is standing exactly where the lawyer stands.

The mapped steps:
1. Producers and tasks: lawyer vs. secretary; legal work vs. typing, over the working week
2. Productivity: lawyer absolutely better at both tasks; secretary capable only of typing
3. Opportunity-cost ratios: lawyer's OC of typing is high-value legal work forgone; secretary's OC is near zero — ratios diverge sharply
4. Specialization: lawyer in law, secretary in typing; total office output rises despite slower typing
5. Transaction costs: dictation, review, and confidentiality costs computed and found small relative to the OC gap
6. Implement and monitor: technology shifts (word processors) later changed the ratios and redrew the delegation boundary

Primary source: Samuelson, P. A. (1948). *Economics: An Introductory Analysis.* McGraw-Hill. Comparative-advantage discussion, including the lawyer-and-typist illustration; retained through subsequent Samuelson & Nordhaus editions.
