# Method in Action: The AI-Chip Supply Chain — ASML, TSMC, Nvidia (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [comparative-advantage](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By the mid-2020s the most valuable products on earth — the accelerators that train frontier AI — were built by a supply chain that no single company controls. ASML in the Netherlands makes the extreme-ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines; TSMC in Taiwan operates the leading-edge fabs that turn designs into silicon; Nvidia in California designs the GPUs and owns the CUDA software stack. Each is dominant in its own layer and buys, rather than builds, the others. Recurring political pressure to "re-shore the whole stack" is, in Ricardo's terms, a proposal to abandon specialization — and the framework explains why that is so costly.

The case walks cleanly through the skill's Process, treating each firm as a "producer" and each layer of the stack as a "task."

**Step 1 — Producers and tasks.** Producers: ASML (lithography-tool maker), TSMC (foundry), Nvidia (chip designer). Tasks: build EUV machines · run leading-edge fabrication · design GPUs and the software ecosystem. Horizon: the multi-year capital cycle of a chip generation.

**Step 2 — Productivity.** Each producer's output-per-unit-effort in its own layer is extraordinary and hard to reproduce. ASML is the sole commercial supplier of EUV lithography systems, having consolidated the technology after decades of R&D and a global supplier base. TSMC is the dominant leading-edge foundry, running advanced-node capacity at a scale and yield competitors have struggled to match. Nvidia, whose data-center GPUs and CUDA platform anchored the AI-training boom, saw its market capitalization climb from a reported ~$3 trillion in 2024 to a reported ~$4 trillion by mid-2025. Crucially, none of the three is vertically integrated across the others' layers: ASML does not fabricate chips, TSMC does not design GPUs, Nvidia owns no fabs (it is "fabless").

**Step 3 — Opportunity-cost ratios.** This is where the absolute-advantage instinct fails. Suppose Nvidia *could* eventually learn to run its own fabs — the question is not "could it be good at fabrication?" but "what does an engineering-hour spent building fab capability forgo?" Every dollar and top engineer Nvidia diverts into fabrication is one not spent on architecture and CUDA, where its opportunity cost of *not* investing is highest. The same logic binds each firm: ASML's marginal genius is best spent on the next lithography node, not on chip design; TSMC's on yield and process, not on EUV-machine manufacturing. The opportunity-cost ratios diverge sharply, which is exactly the condition under which specialization dominates self-sufficiency.

**Step 4 — Specialization.** Each firm concentrates on its comparative-advantage layer, and the *joint* output — frontier accelerators produced at volume — is vastly higher than any single vertically integrated national champion could achieve. This is Ricardo at the scale of an industry: total output rises even though every firm "could" in principle do more of the chain itself.

**Step 5 — Transaction costs.** Specialization is not free, and this chain shows the qualification vividly. The transaction costs here are unusually large and partly *geopolitical*: the entire chain runs through a few chokepoints (ASML in the Netherlands, TSMC concentrated in Taiwan), creating single-point-of-failure risk. From 2022 onward the United States tightened export controls on advanced AI chips and chip-making equipment to China across several rounds, and Nvidia designed lower-capability export variants in response — a direct tax on the specialized chain. TSMC began building fabs in Arizona (with US CHIPS Act support) to reduce geographic concentration. And in January 2025 the release of the DeepSeek models triggered a sharp market reaction, briefly wiping out a large share of Nvidia's market value in a single session on fears that AI compute demand had been overestimated — a reminder that the demand side of the specialization bet is itself uncertain.

The lesson is *not* "abandon specialization." It is that when transaction costs (geographic risk, export controls, single points of failure) rise, the right response is to price them into the calculus — diversify geography, dual-source at the margin, hold strategic buffers — *not* to attempt full vertical re-shoring, whose cost is the enormous specialization surplus you would forfeit. Rebuilding the entire stack domestically means re-learning three separate multi-decade capabilities at once; the opportunity cost is measured in lost years and lost frontier output, which is why even aggressive industrial policy targets *resilience at the margin* rather than autarky.

**Step 6 — Implement and monitor.** The arrangement holds only while the productivity ratios and transaction costs hold. Re-evaluation triggers are already firing: each new export-control round, each new fab location, each shift in AI-compute demand (DeepSeek being the clearest 2025 shock) moves the boundary. The method's discipline is to keep re-computing — the delegation frontier moves; it does not disappear.

The mapped steps:
1. Producers and tasks: ASML vs. TSMC vs. Nvidia; lithography vs. fabrication vs. GPU/software design, over a chip generation's capital cycle
2. Productivity: each dominant and hard to replicate in its own layer; none vertically integrated across the others
3. Opportunity-cost ratios: an engineering-hour spent re-shoring another layer forgoes the layer where each firm's OC of not investing is highest — ratios diverge sharply
4. Specialization: each firm owns its layer; joint output of frontier chips far exceeds any integrated national champion
5. Transaction costs: geographic concentration, export controls, and demand uncertainty are large and rising — price them in, don't abandon specialization
6. Implement and monitor: export-control rounds, TSMC Arizona, and the Jan-2025 DeepSeek shock are live re-evaluation triggers moving the delegation frontier

The AI-chip stack is Samuelson's lawyer-and-typist case scaled to three continents: each player is superb at its own task, none is best served by doing the others' work, and the surplus that specialization creates is precisely what a "build it all ourselves" mandate would destroy.
