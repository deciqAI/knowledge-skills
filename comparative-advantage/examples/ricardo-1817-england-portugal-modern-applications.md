# Method in Action: Ricardo 1817 England-Portugal + Modern Applications

> *Example for the [comparative-advantage](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Ricardo's 1817 *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* was the first systematic statement of the theorem. His example used England and Portugal, cloth and wine. Suppose:

- Portugal can produce 1 unit of cloth with 90 labor-hours
- Portugal can produce 1 unit of wine with 80 labor-hours
- England can produce 1 unit of cloth with 100 labor-hours
- England can produce 1 unit of wine with 120 labor-hours

Portugal is absolutely more productive at both. Naive analysis: Portugal should produce both, England should produce neither.

Ricardo's analysis: look at the *ratios*. Portugal's opportunity cost of cloth = 90/80 = 1.125 wine per cloth. England's opportunity cost of cloth = 100/120 = 0.833 wine per cloth. England gives up less wine per cloth than Portugal does. England therefore has *comparative advantage* in cloth, despite Portugal's absolute advantage in cloth.

The mutually beneficial allocation: England specializes in cloth, Portugal specializes in wine, they trade. Total production rises versus each producing both goods themselves.

Ricardo's full argument:

> "Under a system of perfectly free commerce, each country naturally devotes its capital and labour to such employments as are most beneficial to each. This pursuit of individual advantage is admirably connected with the universal good of the whole. By stimulating industry, by rewarding ingenuity, and by using most efficaciously the peculiar powers bestowed by nature, it distributes labour most effectively and most economically: while, by increasing the general mass of productions, it diffuses general benefit, and binds together by one common tie of interest and intercourse, the universal society of nations throughout the civilized world."
>
> — Ricardo (1817), ch. 7.

The principle has been formalized many times since 1817 and remains the central theoretical foundation for the case for international trade and specialization. It has also been substantially extended by economists like Eli Heckscher, Bertil Ohlin, Paul Samuelson, Wassily Leontief, and Paul Krugman, who have integrated the framework with factor endowments, increasing returns, and other complications.

For founders and operators, the framework's most operationally important applications:

**Founder time allocation.** Marc Andreessen articulated this clearly in his 2007 essay "The Pmarca Guide to Personal Productivity":

> "Most founders are smart, capable, and energetic enough to do many things on the team better than the people they hire to do them. The temptation to do those things yourself is enormous. The discipline is to refuse. Your opportunity cost is higher than anyone else's on the team; an hour you spend on a task you could delegate is an hour you didn't spend on strategy, key relationships, or the next product bet. Even if you'd do the task 30% better than the delegate, the strategic activities you should have done are worth 300% more. Comparative advantage routes you to the activities only you can do. Everything else, delegate."
>
> — Andreessen, M. (2007). "The Pmarca Guide to Personal Productivity."

**Outsourcing / specialization in supply chains.** Apple's manufacturing strategy is comparative advantage in action. Apple has world-class design, marketing, retail, and software engineering — and chooses to outsource component manufacturing to specialists (Foxconn, TSMC, etc.) who have comparative advantage in their respective domains. Apple could, in principle, build its own factories, but the opportunity cost (foregone product innovation, design, software) would dwarf the manufacturing savings.

**Team specialization.** Within engineering teams, comparative advantage operates constantly. The senior engineer who is best at architecture should not be reviewing every junior's pull request; the senior is better off architecting and mentoring at the senior level, while junior engineers handle their work and learn. Even if the senior would do a 30% better job on the junior's task, the senior's opportunity cost (foregone architectural work) is much larger.

**International labor markets.** The rise of global remote work (engineers in Argentina, designers in Vietnam, etc.) is comparative advantage operating at the level of individual contributors. Each location specializes in what they have comparative advantage in; the productivity ratios drive specialization patterns.

**M&A logic.** Apple acquired NeXT in 1997 for the operating system technology (NeXT's comparative advantage in OS); the acquisition gave Apple the OS foundation that became macOS. Apple didn't try to build a new OS from scratch — the opportunity cost would have been too high.

Three operational lessons from Ricardo:

**First, "I'm best at X" is the wrong question.** The right question is "am I better at X *relative to* my next-best alternative use of my time?" Founders who allocate time based on absolute advantage spread themselves across many activities; founders who allocate based on comparative advantage focus on a small set of activities where the leverage is highest.

**Second, even absolute superiority doesn't justify doing everything.** The richest, most capable person in a room can still benefit from specialization. The opportunity cost calculus is what matters.

**Third, transaction costs are the practical brake.** In theory, comparative advantage suggests aggressive outsourcing. In practice, transaction costs (coordination, communication, quality monitoring) often make in-house production correct. The operational discipline is to compute both — pure comparative advantage and transaction cost — and make the net decision.
