# Method in Action: The New Jersey–Pennsylvania Auto Insurance Default Experiment (1988–1993)

> *Example for the [status-quo-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

In the late 1980s, two neighboring U.S. states reformed auto insurance with the same menu and opposite defaults — producing the cleanest field demonstration of status quo bias in consumer law, in a domain far from laboratory experiments or health policy.

**The setup.** Both states offered drivers the same two-option choice: a cheaper policy with a *limited* right to sue for pain and suffering, or a more expensive policy retaining the *full* right to sue. In 1988, New Jersey made the cheaper limited-tort policy the default; drivers could pay extra to acquire the full right. In 1990, Pennsylvania passed a reform offering the identical menu — but made the full-tort policy the default, letting drivers actively opt down to the cheaper limited option.

**Step 1 — Identify default.** In each state the default was set by the legislature, shaped by lobbying and legislative compromise — not by any analysis of what an informed driver would choose. Millions of drivers inherited a default they never examined.

**Step 2 — Identify alternatives.** The alternative was always one form away, with a clearly stated price difference. Switching cost was minimal: check a different box on the insurance form. Any substantive theory of preference predicts similar choices across the two demographically similar states.

**Step 3 — Fresh-choice test.** The outcomes diverged dramatically. Roughly 20% of New Jersey drivers acquired the full right to sue, while roughly 75% of Pennsylvania drivers retained it. Same menu, same prices in structure, opposite defaults — opposite population-level "preferences." Johnson, Hershey, Meszaros, and Kunreuther then replicated the effect in a controlled experiment: when full coverage was framed as the default, a majority of subjects kept it; when limited coverage was the default, most kept that instead. Stripped of a default, no stable majority preference existed — the default itself manufactured the choice.

**Step 4 — Cost of inaction.** The defaults carried large aggregate price tags. Contemporary estimates cited by the authors put the additional premiums Pennsylvania drivers paid for the full-tort default on the order of $200 million per year — money spent not because drivers valued the right to sue at that price, but because changing the default required an active decision almost no one made.

**Step 5 — Direction (Design).** The case is a design failure: neither legislature stated a justification for its default in terms of driver welfare. The audit question — "what would an informed driver choose if forced to decide actively?" — was never asked. The experimental evidence showed the honest answer was "it depends on the default," which is precisely why the default direction was a policy decision worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

**Step 6 — Implement.** The study became a canonical exhibit for deliberate default design in regulation: either require an active choice at purchase, or choose the default explicitly for the average consumer's benefit and publish the justification. It is cited throughout the later defaults literature, including the organ-donation and 401(k) work, as the founding field evidence that defaults are policy.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify default: legislatively set tort defaults in NJ (limited) and PA (full), never re-evaluated by drivers
2. Identify alternatives: identical two-option menu in both states; switching cost near zero
3. Fresh-choice test: ~20% vs. ~75% full-tort retention; lab replication showed the default, not preference, drove choice
4. Cost of inaction: on the order of $200 million per year in extra Pennsylvania premiums
5. Direction: Design — defaults reflected legislative bargaining, not driver interest; no stated justification
6. Implement: active-choice or intentionally justified defaults became the standard prescription for consumer regulation

Primary source: Johnson, E. J., Hershey, J., Meszaros, J., & Kunreuther, H. (1993). "Framing, Probability Distortions, and Insurance Decisions." *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, 7(1), 35–51.
