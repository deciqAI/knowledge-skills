# Method in Action: Enterprise AI Adoption and the Incumbent-Vendor Default (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [status-quo-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

From late 2022 onward, generative AI made a class of new, AI-native tools broadly available to enterprises — coding assistants, retrieval-and-chat layers over internal documents, AI-first customer-support and analytics products. Yet across 2023 and into 2026 many organizations kept routing work through incumbent SaaS suites and existing manual workflows, waiting for their established vendors to ship AI features rather than evaluating AI-native challengers on the merits. The recurring justification — "let's wait and see, switching now is risky" — is a textbook instance of inertia dressed up as prudence. This example walks that pattern through the skill's six steps.

**Step 1 — Identify default.** The default is "keep the incumbent stack and the current workflow; add AI only when our existing vendor bundles it in." The default is controlled jointly by procurement, IT/security, and the line-of-business owner, and was established by prior multi-year contracts and accumulated familiarity — not by any fresh comparison against AI-native alternatives. In many organizations this arrangement had not been re-evaluated since before generative AI was capable enough to matter (roughly pre-2023), so the tool landscape shifted underneath a default that nobody reopened.

**Step 2 — Identify alternatives.** Alternatives were unusually visible and cheap to trial in this period: AI-native point tools frequently offered self-serve or low-commitment pilots, and the major cloud and model providers made capable models available via API. The stated reasons for not adopting were real in part — data governance, security review, integration effort, model reliability — but were frequently *cited wholesale* to avoid an evaluation rather than *modeled* against the specific tool and use case. The skill's core test applies: is the incumbent retained because it was compared and won, or because comparison requires effort?

**Step 3 — Fresh-choice test.** The decisive question: *if we were choosing our tooling for this workflow from scratch today, with no existing contract and no installed habit, would we pick the incumbent suite or the AI-native option?* For many narrow, high-volume workflows (drafting, code completion, first-line support triage, document retrieval), the honest answer by 2025 increasingly favored an AI-assisted approach — while the switching cost (integration, security review, retraining staff) was real but bounded and one-time. Where the fresh-choice answer diverges sharply from the status quo and the switching cost is finite, inertia — not analysis — is holding the default in place.

**Step 4 — Cost of inaction.** Unlike a one-off purchase, the cost of inaction here compounds. Each quarter of deferral is a quarter of foregone productivity on the affected workflow, plus a widening capability gap versus competitors who did adopt. Critically, the status quo is *deteriorating* rather than static: as rivals compress their cost and cycle-time on the same tasks, standing still loses ground in relative terms even if absolute performance is unchanged. The audit therefore must price not just "the incumbent costs the same as last year" but "the gap to the better alternative grows each period we defer."

**Step 5 — Direction (Audit).** This is an Audit case, not a Design case: the organization is defaulting rather than deliberately choosing. The correct output is not blanket "adopt everything" — that would substitute hype-driven change for evidence. It is to force the fresh-choice question workflow-by-workflow, run bounded pilots on the highest-volume candidates, and let each incumbent-vs-AI-native decision be made on measured results. Note the boundary the skill draws: if a specific workflow was genuinely evaluated and the incumbent won on security, accuracy, or total cost, that is *correct analysis, not bias* — and it should be documented as such, with a review date.

**Step 6 — Implement.** The implementation is a review cadence rather than a single switch: identify the two or three workflows where the fresh-choice gap is largest, assign an owner, run time-boxed pilots with predefined success metrics, and set an explicit re-evaluation date so that "wait and see" cannot quietly become the next unexamined multi-year default. The point is not to abandon incumbents reflexively — it is to stop letting the contract and the habit make the decision.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify default: keep incumbent stack + current workflow, add AI only when the existing vendor bundles it; controlled by procurement/IT/LOB; not re-evaluated since before AI was capable
2. Identify alternatives: AI-native point tools and model APIs, cheap to pilot; governance/security concerns often cited wholesale rather than modeled per use case
3. Fresh-choice test: from scratch, many narrow high-volume workflows now favor AI-assisted tools; switching cost real but bounded and one-time
4. Cost of inaction: compounds quarter over quarter; status quo deteriorates in relative terms as rivals adopt
5. Direction: Audit — force the fresh-choice question per workflow; document any workflow where the incumbent genuinely won (analysis, not bias)
6. Implement: bounded pilots on highest-gap workflows, named owners, predefined metrics, explicit review date

*Sources: Samuelson, W., & Zeckhauser, R. (1988), "Status Quo Bias in Decision Making," Journal of Risk and Uncertainty 1(1), 7–59 (the framework applied here). McKinsey, "The state of AI" survey series (2023–2025), reporting that generative-AI adoption rose sharply while measured enterprise-level bottom-line impact and scaled deployment lagged the pace of experimentation. MIT Sloan Management Review / Boston Consulting Group, annual "AI and Business Strategy" research (2023–2024), on the gap between AI investment and realized value. General, widely reported facts about the post-2022 generative-AI tool landscape (availability of AI coding assistants, model APIs, and AI-native SaaS challengers) are used qualitatively; no specific proprietary figures are asserted.*
