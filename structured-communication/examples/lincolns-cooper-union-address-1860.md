# Method in Action: Lincoln's Cooper Union Address (1860)

> *Example for the [structured-communication](../SKILL.md) skill.*

On February 27, 1860, Abraham Lincoln addressed approximately 1,500 people at the Cooper Union in New York. He was largely unknown to the Eastern establishment audience. His goal was to persuade Republican opinion-makers that he was a credible presidential candidate and that the Republican position on slavery's restriction in new territories was constitutionally grounded.

Lincoln's address is a documented use of what we now recognize as ORER combined with RIDE elements, demonstrating that structured communication precedes the formal codification of its models.

**ORER Structure:**

- **Opinion (stated first):** Lincoln opened by addressing Senator Douglas's claim directly and stating his own thesis: that the founding fathers who drafted the Constitution intended to restrict slavery in federal territories. This was the conclusion, stated at the outset.

- **Reason:** Lincoln provided the logical basis: the Constitution's framers held and acted on this view in their own legislation. This is not opinion — it is documented legislative behavior.

- **Example (34 specific examples):** Lincoln had spent months in the Springfield library researching the voting records of the 39 framers of the Constitution. He documented, signer by signer, how 21 of the 39 had voted on legislation that restricted slavery in federal territories. This is the "Example" slot filled to a standard of evidentiary rigor that made the argument empirically unfalsifiable in the room.

- **Result (conclusion restated):** Lincoln closed by restating the thesis: "Let us have faith that right makes might, and in that faith, let us, to the end, dare to do our duty as we understand it." The argument had been made; the conclusion was now emotionally grounded.

**RIDE elements integrated:**

- **Risk:** Lincoln named the risk of capitulating to slavery's expansion — the destruction of the constitutional compact.
- **Interest:** He addressed the self-interest of Northern Republican moderates — constitutional legitimacy for their position.
- **Difference:** He distinguished the Republican position from abolitionism, defusing the opposition's framing.
- **Effect:** He named the effect of standing firm: moral and political legitimacy.

**Outcome:** The Cooper Union address transformed Lincoln's national standing. Within three months he was the Republican presidential nominee. The New York Tribune called it "one of the most convincing political arguments ever made in this city."
