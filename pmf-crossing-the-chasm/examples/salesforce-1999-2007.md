# Method in Action: Salesforce, 1999 → 2007

> *Example for the [pmf-crossing-the-chasm](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example. Not founder hagiography — primary-source documented in Marc Benioff's autobiography and SEC filings.

**Salesforce.com** was founded by **Marc Benioff** and three co-founders in March 1999. The product was a CRM application delivered as a *web service* — what would later be called SaaS. The dominant CRM at the time was **Siebel Systems**, sold as enterprise on-premises software with multi-year implementations costing millions.

**The PMF phase (1999–2001).** Salesforce's earliest customers were small-to-medium sales teams who wanted CRM but could not afford Siebel implementations. The Ellis Test wasn't used by name (Ellis published the methodology in 2009), but the equivalent signal was unmissable: monthly subscriptions grew from ~3,000 paying users in early 2001 to >100,000 by mid-2003 (Benioff, *Behind the Cloud*, 2009, ch. 4–5), with retention strong among the early small-team buyers. By any reasonable PMF criterion — the product was loved by its beachhead segment, retention was high, organic word-of-mouth was real — Salesforce had PMF in the small-team segment by ~2001.

**The chasm.** That PMF did not translate to **enterprise CRM sales**, which Siebel dominated. Enterprise buyers required things Salesforce's early-adopter SMB customers had not: SSO integrations with existing identity systems, audit trails for SOX compliance, customizable workflows for industry verticals (financial services, healthcare, pharmaceuticals), 24×7 enterprise support contracts, three-tier reference customers in their own vertical, and a procurement process matched to corporate purchasing. **The product was not the gap; the whole product was.**

**Salesforce's chasm strategy (2001–2007).** Pick beachhead-by-beachhead in the early majority:

- **2002–2003 financial services** — built compliance, audit, security to a level that won several mid-market financial-services firms; used those as references to win larger ones.
- **2005 AppExchange launch** — third-party application marketplace; solved the "whole product" gap by enabling partners to build the vertical-specific extensions Salesforce itself couldn't build.
- **2006 Service Cloud / customer-service expansion** — extended the platform beyond pure CRM, addressing the "expand into adjacent use cases" pin in Moore's bowling-pin model.
- **By 2007**, Salesforce was a credible enterprise vendor. Revenue: $497M FY2007 (SEC 10-K), up from $176M FY2005. The chasm was crossed.

Walk the audit on Salesforce circa 2001:

- **PMF (Part A):** Strong in SMB sales-team segment. Retention solid. Word-of-mouth high. ✓
- **Rogers classification (B1):** Mostly Early Adopters in SMB; some Innovators.
- **Chasm geography (B2):** Selling today = SMB sales teams; next segment = mid-market financial services, then enterprise.
- **Whole-product gap (B3):** Compliance, audit trails, integrations, vertical references, enterprise support — all missing.
- **Beachhead choice (B4):** Financial services first (not "the enterprise" generically).
- **Whole-product plan (B5):** Compliance certs, SSO integrations, financial-services case studies, dedicated enterprise sales team.
- **Positioning shift (B6):** From "no software, transformative SaaS rebel" (the early-adopter anti-Siebel pitch) to "proven, compliant, supported enterprise SaaS" (the mainstream pitch).

Salesforce did all six. The framework's payoff in this case: **the chasm did not feel like a product problem; it felt like a sales-execution problem.** Diagnosing it as a whole-product + segment-positioning problem is what unlocked the path through. Treating it as a sales problem alone would have produced a bigger sales team selling to the same early-adopter segment, with diminishing returns.
