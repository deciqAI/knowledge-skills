# Method in Action: Shell Group Planning and the 1973 Oil Crisis (1971–1973)

> *Example for the [scenario-planning](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A documented, primary-source-citable case with no pop-figure mythology.

**Focal question (Step 1):** How should Shell configure its global oil procurement, refinery operations, and product pricing over the 1972–1985 period?

**Key drivers (Step 2):** Wack's team mapped: global oil demand growth rate, OPEC member pricing cohesion, Seven Sisters' pricing power, Middle East political stability, alternative energy development pace. They identified demand growth and political stability as the two most uncertain and impactful variables.

**Critical uncertainties (Step 3):** Axis 1 — *OPEC pricing control* (integrated/collaborative vs. fragmented). Axis 2 — *Supply-demand balance* (surplus capacity available vs. tight with no buffer). These were genuinely bi-directional; mainstream industry opinion assumed Axis 1 remained fragmented and Axis 2 remained surplus.

**Scenario matrix (Step 4):** Shell built two primary scenarios: "Scenario A" (mainstream: stable Seven Sisters-led market, gradual price increase) and "Scenario B" (non-consensus: OPEC unification, supply restriction, sharp price shock). Wack insisted Scenario B be written as a full narrative, not just a data point — including specific political triggers (the Yom Kippur War as a plausible ignition event, the Libyan nationalization precedent as evidence of willingness).

**Strategic implications (Step 5):** Under Scenario B, the most valuable capabilities were: pre-positioned crude inventory, refinery flexibility to process a wider crude slate, alternative supply routes from non-Middle East producers (North Sea acceleration became a priority), and operating cost structures that could survive at high input prices.

**Robust moves and contingent bets (Step 6):** Shell moved on the robust moves — it built crude inventory buffer, accelerated North Sea development planning, and modeled refinery conversion costs. It also defined the leading indicator that would trigger full Scenario B activation: OPEC joint production coordination signals.

**Outcome:** October 1973, the Yom Kippur War triggered the Arab oil embargo. Oil prices rose approximately 400% within months (from ~$3/barrel to ~$12/barrel). Shell's pre-positioned inventory and operational flexibility allowed it to maintain supply continuity and take market share from competitors who had no Scenario B preparations. Wack later wrote that the more important work was not constructing the matrix but persuading Shell's managing directors to take Scenario B seriously enough to act on it — the cognitive challenge of internalizing a non-consensus future.
