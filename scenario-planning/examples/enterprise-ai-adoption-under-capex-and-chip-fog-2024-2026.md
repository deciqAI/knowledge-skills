# Method in Action: An Enterprise Buyer's AI-Adoption Bet Under the Capex + Chip Fog (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [scenario-planning](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A companion to the [infrastructure-provider case](ai-capex-and-chip-decoupling-2024-2026.md), viewed from the *other side of the invoice*. Where that example is a compute-heavy vendor sizing its buildout, this one is a large enterprise (say, a bank, retailer, or manufacturer) deciding how deeply to commit to a single AI vendor and platform over a multi-year horizon. Same two macro uncertainties; a different decision-maker seat, and therefore different robust moves. As of early 2026 the outcome was genuinely open, which is the condition under which the method earns its keep — the goal is a procurement and adoption posture that survives all four futures, not a prediction of which arrives.

**Focal question (Step 1):** Over 2025–2029, how deeply should this enterprise standardize on one AI provider and model family — signing multi-year platform, seat, and committed-spend agreements and rebuilding core workflows around it — given that both the durability of the industry's AI capital spending and the trajectory of US–China chip policy are unresolved and could reshape pricing, availability, and vendor viability?

**Key drivers (Step 2):** Map the forces, then sort them.
- *Predetermined elements* (background constants over the horizon): AI capability keeps improving on current trajectories; frontier models remain compute-intensive to train; the US export-control machinery for advanced computing exists and is bipartisan; governance expectations are hardening (the NIST AI Risk Management Framework 1.0 was published in January 2023 and is a durable reference point for enterprise risk teams). These are broadly agreed and are not the source of the strategic risk.
- *Critical uncertainties* (highly impactful AND genuinely bi-directional): (a) whether the sector's AI capital-expenditure boom *sustains* (adoption and revenue catch up to spend, so vendors stay well-funded and pricing competition persists) or *corrects* (a spending pullback that thins the vendor field, raises prices, or strands some providers); (b) whether US–China semiconductor *decoupling deepens* (tighter controls, bifurcated supply, tighter GPU allocation and higher inference costs) or *eases* (carve-outs and stabilization, more predictable compute supply and pricing).

**Critical uncertainties (Step 3):** Axis 1 — *AI capex regime*: **sustains** vs. **corrects**. Axis 2 — *Chip-decoupling trajectory*: **deepens** vs. **eases**. Each pole is plausible rather than "good vs. bad," and the axes are largely independent: a capex correction can be driven by ROI skepticism regardless of trade policy, while decoupling is driven by national-security politics largely independent of whether AI spending is booming. Both pass the stop-rule — highly impactful on the focal question and genuinely bi-directional as of early 2026.

**Scenario matrix (Step 4):** Four quadrants, each a full internally consistent narrative, read from the *buyer's* seat.
- **"Buyer's Market"** (capex sustains × decoupling eases): the vendor field stays crowded and well-capitalized; compute is abundant enough that inference prices keep falling; multiple providers remain credible. The enterprise's risk is *over-committing* to one vendor and missing the price and capability curve elsewhere. Optionality is cheap and valuable.
- **"Locked Shelves"** (capex sustains × decoupling deepens): demand and vendor funding hold, but constrained advanced-chip supply tightens GPU allocation and props up inference costs; providers ration capacity to their largest committed customers. Here the enterprise that signed a committed-capacity deal *early* is advantaged, and the latecomer pays a scarcity premium or gets queued. Secured allocation, not price, is the prize.
- **"Vendor Shakeout"** (capex corrects × decoupling eases): a spending pullback thins the field — some AI providers cut roadmaps, raise prices to reach profitability, get acquired, or fold — even as trade tension relaxes. An enterprise that rebuilt core workflows around a single now-wobbling vendor faces migration under duress. **This is the uncomfortable scenario** — a buyer racing to standardize on the current leader is least inclined to price in that leader thinning out or repricing capacity. Portability and a tested exit are what save you.
- **"Squeeze"** (capex corrects × decoupling deepens): a pullback *and* a supply shock at once — fewer viable vendors, scarcer compute, rising per-token costs, and a hardening governance/compliance load. The worst quadrant for an enterprise with deep single-vendor lock-in and no fallback provider or on-prem option.

Quality check: at least one quadrant ("Vendor Shakeout," and worse "Squeeze") directly contradicts the "just pick the leader and go all-in" mental model that a fast-moving adoption team defaults to — the discomfort test passes.

**Strategic implications (Step 5):** Read each quadrant for what an aggressive single-vendor lock-in strategy does.
- *Current strategy performance (deep lock-in):* fine in Locked Shelves (early commitment secures allocation), risky in Buyer's Market (forgoes cheaper/better options), dangerous in Vendor Shakeout, potentially crippling in Squeeze.
- *Biggest opportunities:* Buyer's Market rewards preserved optionality and competitive re-bidding; Locked Shelves rewards early committed-capacity agreements with priority allocation.
- *Biggest threats:* stranded integration work and forced migration if the chosen vendor reprices or fails (Vendor Shakeout / Squeeze); scarcity pricing and rationing for the uncommitted (Locked Shelves / Squeeze); compliance debt if governance is bolted on late under any quadrant.
- *Most valuable capabilities across quadrants:* a portability layer that abstracts the application from any one model provider; an evaluated second-source vendor kept warm; contracts with renegotiation and exit terms; a data and prompt/eval asset the enterprise owns rather than the vendor; a governance program mapped to a durable public framework (e.g., NIST AI RMF) so compliance is not rebuilt each time the vendor changes.

**Robust moves and contingent bets (Step 6):**
- *Robust moves (act now — they pay off or protect in 3+ quadrants):* build against a provider-abstraction layer so switching model vendors is a configuration change, not a rewrite; keep one alternate vendor evaluated and integration-tested (a live BATNA); negotiate committed spend with volume discounts *and* exit/renegotiation clauses rather than maximal lock-in; own your evaluation datasets, prompts, and fine-tuning data so they survive a vendor change; stand up governance against a durable public framework now so it is portable across vendors.
- *Contingent bets (pre-committed, triggered only when a scenario's leading indicators fire):* large multi-year committed-capacity / reserved-throughput deals with one provider for priority allocation — hold until Locked Shelves indicators (tightening allocation, rising inference prices, lengthening queue times) confirm; deep single-vendor standardization to capture integration depth and price — hold until Buyer's Market conditions look durable and the vendor's funding is unambiguous.
- *Leading indicators, owners, cadence:* Axis 1 (capex) — sector capex guidance revisions, AI-product revenue-vs-spend commentary from major providers, and any vendor funding-round or layoff/roadmap-cut news; owner: procurement + strategy; cadence: monthly, with a quarterly matrix review. Axis 2 (decoupling) — new BIS export-control rule changes, retaliation/countermeasure announcements, and observed GPU lead-time and per-token inference pricing trends; owner: IT supply/vendor-management lead; cadence: monitored continuously, reviewed quarterly. Re-run the full matrix annually or when any indicator moves materially.

**Why this is a live case, not a settled one:** unlike the 1973 Shell case, the outcome here was undetermined at the time of writing (early 2026). That is the correct use of the method — the payoff is the pre-thought response so that when signals appear the enterprise reacts faster and less reactively, *not* a claim about which quadrant wins. Note how the same two axes produce *different* robust moves for the buyer (portability, second-source, exit clauses) than for the vendor in the companion case (hardware-portable stack, diversified fabrication, cash buffer) — scenario planning is seat-specific.
