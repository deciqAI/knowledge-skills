# Method in Action: The AI-Era MVP — When Building Got Cheap and Validation Didn't (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [mvp](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between 2023 and 2026, AI coding assistants and "vibe-coding" tools collapsed the cost of *building* software. A working prototype that took a seed-funded team a quarter in 2019 became a weekend project for a solo founder prompting an AI agent. This looks like it should have made the MVP discipline obsolete — if you can build the whole product in a week, why build a *minimum* one? Run the case through the Process and the opposite is true: **cheap building made MVP discipline more valuable, because the bottleneck moved from engineering to evidence.**

This is a *pattern* worked example about a structural shift, not a claim about any single company's private metrics. Where figures would be needed, treat them as illustrative and run your own numbers.

**Step 1 — Name the single assumption being tested.** The 2023–2026 failure mode: founders skipped this step *because building was so easy*. When the prototype costs a weekend, the temptation is to build first and look for the assumption afterward. The graveyard of "GPT-wrapper" startups from this period is substantially a graveyard of products that were never a test of any named assumption — they were demos in search of a market. The discipline is unchanged: write "*<segment> will <behavior> at <rate> for <value>*" **before** prompting the coding agent. What changed is that nothing external now forces you to pause — the forcing function must be self-imposed.

**Step 2 — Pick the MVP type that fits the assumption.** AI shifted the menu's economics without changing its logic:
- **Video demo and smoke test got less trustworthy.** In 2007, a Dropbox-style demo video was costly enough to signal a real team; by 2025, anyone could produce a polished AI-generated demo, so audiences discounted them — and sign-up-page interest became a weaker signal amid AI-fatigued buyers who had seen a dozen lookalike tools that month.
- **Concierge and Wizard-of-Oz got dramatically cheaper.** An LLM can *be* the human-behind-the-curtain's assistant: a founder can manually serve 20 customers with AI-drafted outputs, testing perceived value with no product at all. The classic WoZ pattern — automated front, human back-end — now often inverts into "human front, AI back-end," and it is frequently the correct first test for an AI product: it tests whether the *outcome* is valued before you invest in reliability engineering.
- **Paid pilot rose in importance for B2B AI.** Because enterprise buyers in 2024–2026 were flooded with AI pilots, free pilots stopped producing evidence — pilots that stall are the era's signature false positive. Three to five design partners paying real money became the only trustworthy enterprise signal.

**Step 3 — Specify the actionable metric in advance.** The AI era added a specific trap: **demo-metrics masquerading as actionable metrics.** Sign-ups, waitlist joins, tweet impressions, and "wow" reactions all inflated during the 2023–2025 AI hype cycle and predicted nothing about retention. The metrics that survived contact: week-N *task completion* (did the AI actually finish the user's job), retention after the novelty week, and — for anything with per-use inference cost — *contribution margin per completed task*. An AI MVP can validate demand and still be invalidated by unit economics; the metric card needs both.

**Step 4 — Time-box aggressively.** Unchanged in principle, tighter in practice: when the build takes days, a 1–4 week box now covers build *plus* a real measurement window. The failure mode inverted — teams no longer blow the time-box building; they blow it *polishing*, because AI makes adding features nearly free. Adding is cheap; each addition blurs which feature drove the signal.

**Step 5 — Strip every feature not load-bearing.** Harder now, for the same reason: when auth, billing, dashboards, and dark mode each cost one prompt, "why not include it" wins by default. The discipline: everything the *test* doesn't require is noise that muddies attribution. A 2026 MVP with 30 AI-generated features is not a bigger test — it is no test at all.

**Step 6 — Build, measure, throw away.** The one step AI made *easier to honor*. Disposability used to be psychologically brutal — months of hand-written code. When the artifact took a weekend of prompting, throwing it away and rebuilding for production from validated learning is cheap, and rebuilding matters *more*: AI-generated MVP code is optimized for speed-to-signal, not for security, cost, or maintainability, and the model landscape it was built on shifts every few months.

**Step 7 — Document validated learning.** Unchanged and now the scarcest asset. In a period when any competitor can replicate your *product* in weeks, the only compounding artifact is what you *learned* about the segment, the behavior, and the price — knowledge the next model release does not commoditize.

**Stop-rule applied.** The era's verdict, visible in the 2023–2026 cohort: teams that treated cheap building as a license to skip validation shipped fast into markets that didn't exist; teams that kept the discipline used the same tools to run *more tests per quarter*. The MVP was never about minimizing engineering — it was about maximizing validated learning per unit of time. AI changed the denominator's price, not the objective function.

**Founder takeaway (startup):** When everyone can build, building is not the test. Your edge is cycle count: run more named-assumption tests per quarter than the competitor who is still polishing. Write the MVP Design Card before you prompt the coding agent — the card now costs more discipline than the code.

**Founder takeaway (SMB):** You no longer need a developer to run a real MVP. A landing page, an AI-assisted concierge service for your first 10 customers, and a paid deposit will tell you whether the neighborhood wants the offering — before you sign a lease or buy inventory. The tools got cheap; the rule is the same: get a real customer behavior, not a compliment.
