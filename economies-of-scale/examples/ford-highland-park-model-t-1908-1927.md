# Method in Action: Ford's Highland Park Assembly Line and the Model T Price Decline (1908–1927)

> *Example for the [economies-of-scale](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The Model T is the most thoroughly documented single-product scale curve in industrial history — and its ending is an equally well-documented diseconomy lesson. Hounshell's *From the American System to Mass Production* (1984) reconstructs the whole arc from Ford's internal records.

**Step 1 — Map cost structure.** In 1908 automobiles were craft products: skilled fitters filed non-interchangeable parts to fit, so cost was dominated by variable skilled labor and average cost barely moved with volume. Ford's bet was to invert the structure — pour capital into single-purpose machine tools, gauging systems, and the purpose-built Highland Park plant (opened 1910), converting variable craft labor into fixed capital that volume could amortize.

**Step 2 — Identify scale sources.** Three compounded: (1) fixed-cost spreading across the plant and dedicated tooling; (2) technical efficiency and specialization — truly interchangeable parts eliminated fitting, and the moving assembly line (introduced 1913–1914) cut chassis assembly labor from roughly 12.5 man-hours to about 1.5; (3) a learning curve — continuous process refinement, documented year over year in Hounshell's account, kept pushing unit cost down as cumulative volume grew.

**Step 3 — Estimate the scale curve.** The curve is visible directly in the price series Ford himself publicized: the touring car listed around $850 at introduction in 1908 and fell to under $300 by the mid-1920s, even as the $5 day (announced January 1914) roughly doubled wages. Output rose from tens of thousands of cars a year around 1910 to roughly two million a year by the early 1920s. Ford's stated strategy in *My Life and Work* (1922) was to cut price first and force costs down to meet it — deliberately riding the curve ahead of demand.

**Step 4 — Map competitive positions.** No competitor sat anywhere near Ford's point on the curve; by the early 1920s the Model T accounted for around half of American automobile output. Rivals selling at craft-era costs could not match the price. Their only viable closure path was Ford-scale capital investment — or exiting the cost game entirely, which is precisely what General Motors under Alfred Sloan did, competing on variety, annual model changes, and installment credit rather than on cost.

**Step 5 — Diseconomy threshold.** Ford's scale advantage was welded to a single unchanging product. The dedicated single-purpose tooling that made the Model T cheap made changing it ruinously expensive — scale rigidity as a diseconomy. As buyers shifted to GM's differentiated offerings, the Model T's volume advantage decayed. When Ford finally discontinued the car in May 1927, the changeover to the Model A forced a months-long shutdown and a wholesale re-equipping of the plant.

**Step 6 — Strategic decision.** The episode demonstrates both halves of the stop rule. Competitors for whom Ford's MES was unreachable rationally pivoted to differentiation — and won. Ford, beyond MES on an obsolete curve, paid the full cost of having optimized scale over adaptability.

The case also shows Marshall's internal/external distinction in one city: Highland Park's advantages were internal economies (Ford-owned tooling, process learning), while Detroit itself became an external economy — a cluster of parts suppliers, machine-tool builders, and experienced production labor that lowered costs for every automaker located there, including the GM that eventually beat Ford.

Two durable lessons for applying the skill. First, the scale curve is not found — it is built: Ford chose a cost structure that could bend, then spent capital to bend it. Second, position on a curve is worthless if the curve itself moves; always ask what the scale-optimized asset base cannot do, because that is where the diseconomy hides.

The mapped steps:
1. Cost structure: craft-era variable skilled labor converted into fixed capital (plant + single-purpose tooling)
2. Scale sources: fixed-cost spreading, interchangeable parts + moving assembly line, learning curve
3. Scale curve: touring car ~$850 (1908) → under $300 (mid-1920s); output tens of thousands → ~2 million/year
4. Competitive positions: Ford ~half of US output; rivals' closure path = matching capital or pivoting
5. Diseconomy threshold: single-product tooling rigidity; 1927 shutdown to retool for the Model A
6. Strategic decision: GM pivoted to differentiation per the stop rule and overtook Ford

Primary source: Hounshell, D. A. (1984). *From the American System to Mass Production, 1800–1932: The Development of Manufacturing Technology in the United States.* Johns Hopkins University Press. Corroborated by Ford, H., with Crowther, S. (1922). *My Life and Work.* Doubleday, Page & Company.
