# Method in Action: Leading-Edge Fab Economics and TSMC's Scale Moat (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [economies-of-scale](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The AI compute boom of 2023–2026 pushed one of the most extreme scale-economy structures in the modern economy — the leading-edge semiconductor foundry — into the center of both markets and geopolitics. It is a clean case for this skill because almost the entire cost of a cutting-edge chip is fixed, sunk before a single wafer ships, and only a handful of firms can reach minimum efficient scale at all.

**Step 1 — Map cost structure.** A leading-edge logic fab is nearly pure fixed cost. Building and equipping a single advanced fab has been reported in the range of roughly $20 billion and up, and the process R&D to develop each new node runs to billions more before production begins. The single most expensive tool class — ASML's EUV lithography machines — is sold by ASML, the only supplier of EUV, at prices publicly reported in the range of ~$150 million per EUV scanner and higher for the newer high-NA systems. Once the fab and the node exist, the marginal cost of an additional wafer (materials, labor, power) is comparatively small. The dominant category is therefore fixed capital and non-recurring engineering, not per-unit variable cost.

**Step 2 — Identify scale sources.** Several compound at once: (1) **fixed-cost spreading** — the fab and the multi-billion-dollar node R&D are amortized over wafers, so cost per good chip falls as cumulative volume rises; (2) **learning curve / yield** — early production of a new node yields relatively few good dies per wafer, and yield improves as engineers accumulate process experience, which is the classic learning effect and the hardest advantage for a follower to copy; (3) **purchasing and roadmap leverage** — the largest buyer of leading-edge tools can secure scarce EUV capacity and co-develop the next node; (4) **R&D amortization across a broad customer base** — a foundry serving Apple, Nvidia, AMD and others spreads node development across enormous aggregate volume that no single captive chipmaker can match.

**Step 3 — Estimate the scale curve.** The curve here is dominated by yield and utilization. At low cumulative volume on a fresh node, cost per working chip is very high because yields are low and the enormous fixed base is spread thin. As volume and process learning grow, yield climbs and the fixed base amortizes, so cost per good die falls steeply — then flattens once the fab is near full utilization. The critical implication: a fab that runs below full utilization never reaches the bottom of its curve, because the fixed base is spread over too few wafers. This is why the minimum efficient scale of a leading-edge node is effectively a global-demand-scale volume — the Pack table in this skill lists the semiconductor-fab MES as "$20B+ fab," with the diseconomy threshold "not reached," meaning the industry has not observed a size at which average cost turns back up.

**Step 4 — Map competitive positions.** By the mid-2020s only three firms were manufacturing at the leading edge: TSMC, Samsung, and Intel — and by most public reporting TSMC held the dominant share of leading-edge foundry output and the strongest yield position, which is itself a scale-and-learning advantage. TSMC is also reported to be among the lead customers co-developing next-generation nodes with ASML. A would-be entrant faces a closure path that is close to impossible: match ~$20B+ per fab, secure EUV allocation from a single sole-source supplier, and then climb a multi-year yield learning curve against an incumbent already at the bottom of it. That combination — capital intensity plus a sole-source tool chokepoint plus a learning curve — is why this market has consolidated toward a handful of firms rather than fragmenting.

**Step 5 — Diseconomy threshold.** The classic internal diseconomy (coordination overhead turning average cost back up) has not been the binding constraint here; the industry keeps building bigger. The real limit is **external and geopolitical**, not managerial. Concentrating minimum-efficient-scale production in one company on one island created a strategic single point of failure, prompting government intervention — the U.S. CHIPS and Science Act (signed August 2022) subsidizing domestic fabs, and successive U.S. export-control rounds (notably the October 2022 controls and later tightenings) restricting advanced-chip and tool sales to China. This is the mirror image of the skill's diseconomy lesson: when re-shoring drives firms to build fabs *below* the scale and cluster density that made the original location cheap, unit costs rise. TSMC's Arizona project has been widely reported to face higher costs than its Taiwan fabs, driven by construction, labor, and the absence of Taiwan's dense supplier cluster — a live demonstration of Marshall's *external economies*: much of the cost advantage lives in the surrounding industrial ecosystem, not only inside the firm.

**Step 6 — Strategic decision.** For a buyer or policymaker, the stop rule bites hard. For a private entrant, leading-edge MES is structurally unreachable against TSMC — so the rational move is to *not* compete on the leading-edge cost curve and instead pivot to differentiation: specialty/mature nodes, packaging, chip design (fabless), or design tools. For a government, the calculus is different: it is knowingly paying a scale penalty (higher-cost, sub-scale domestic fabs) to buy supply-chain resilience — a deliberate trade of economic efficiency for strategic insurance, which is exactly the kind of decision the skill exists to make explicit rather than leave implicit.

The mapped steps:
1. Cost structure: near-pure fixed — ~$20B+ per fab plus billions in per-node R&D; low variable cost per wafer; EUV tools reportedly ~$150M+ each from sole supplier ASML
2. Scale sources: fixed-cost spreading, yield/learning curve, tool-allocation leverage, R&D amortized across a broad customer base
3. Scale curve: cost per good die falls steeply with yield + utilization, then flattens; sub-scale utilization never reaches the bottom
4. Competitive positions: three leading-edge makers (TSMC, Samsung, Intel); TSMC dominant on share and yield; entrant closure path near-impossible
5. Diseconomy threshold: not internal/managerial but external + geopolitical — re-shoring below scale (TSMC Arizona) raises cost; Marshall's external economies live in the cluster
6. Strategic decision: private entrants pivot to differentiation per the stop rule; governments knowingly pay a scale penalty (CHIPS Act) for resilience

The enduring lesson: when minimum efficient scale is a global-demand-scale volume and a sole-source tool sits in the middle of the supply chain, "just get bigger" is available to almost no one — and the most important costs of *not* being at scale show up not on the income statement but in geopolitics.
