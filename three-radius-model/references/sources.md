# Sources — three-radius-model

> *Primary sources for the [three-radius-model](../SKILL.md) skill.*

1. **Buffett, Warren** (1996). Berkshire Hathaway Annual Report, Letter to Shareholders. https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1996.html. Verbatim: "What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word 'selected': You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." This passage establishes the foundational principle of the cognition radius: the boundary is as important as the content, and operating within the boundary — not expanding it without validation — is the discipline.

2. **Tesla, Nikola** (1919). *My Inventions: The Autobiography of Nikola Tesla*. Originally published in *Electrical Experimenter* magazine, February–June and October 1919. Full text at Project Gutenberg: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/13576. Verbatim: "I do not rush into actual work. When I get an idea, I start at once building it up in my imagination. I change the construction, make improvements and operate the device in my mind. It is absolutely immaterial to me whether I run my turbine in thought or test it in my shop." This passage documents Tesla's cognition radius — his ability to simulate complex systems mentally — and the autobiography as a whole documents the divergence between his technical cognition/capability and his business cognition/capability, providing the primary-source evidence for the Method in Action case.

3. **Kant, Immanuel** (1781/1787). *Critique of Pure Reason*, Preface to Second Edition, B xiv-xv. Project Gutenberg translation: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/4280. Verbatim: "Our reason has this peculiar fate that, with reference to one class of its knowledge, it is always troubled with questions which cannot be ignored, as they are brought forward by the very nature of reason itself, and which cannot be answered, as they transcend every faculty of the mind." This passage identifies the outer boundary of the cognition radius — not just what is unknown, but what reason itself cannot resolve from within its own resources — establishing the philosophical grounding for why the cognition radius has a meaningful and identifiable boundary.

**What is NOT cited and why:** The Dunning-Kruger effect (Kruger & Dunning, 1999, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) is referenced in Applying It Well but not formally cited because it is invoked as a general phenomenon rather than as a specific empirical claim. Readers who want the primary study should access: Kruger, J., & Dunning, D. (1999). Unskilled and unaware of it: How difficulties in recognizing one's own incompetence lead to inflated self-assessments. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 77(6), 1121–1134. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.77.6.1121.
