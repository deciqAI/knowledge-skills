# Method in Action: The "AI" Halo — Builder.ai and the AI-Washing Wave (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [halo-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between 2023 and 2026, the two letters "AI" functioned as the strongest halo in business evaluation: a single glowing attribute ("they're an AI company") that silently inflated investors' ratings of every other attribute — revenue quality, technology depth, management integrity, even audit reliability. The cleanest documented case is **Builder.ai**, a Microsoft-backed "AI-powered app development" unicorn once valued around $1.5 billion, which filed for insolvency in May 2025 after its revenue and its "AI" both turned out to be substantially less than the halo suggested. Regulators saw the same pattern early: in March 2024 the U.S. SEC brought its first "AI-washing" enforcement actions against two investment advisers (Delphia and Global Predictions) for claiming AI capabilities they did not have. Run the case through the Process.

**Step 1 — Identify target and attributes.** Target: Builder.ai as an investment / vendor. Attributes actually being rated: (a) technology (is the "Natasha" AI assistant really assembling apps?), (b) revenue quality (are reported figures real and durable?), (c) unit economics, (d) management credibility. Decision: invest / lend / buy the platform. Global impression circa 2023–2024: *"a leading AI company in a once-in-a-generation AI boom"* — reinforced by marquee backers (Microsoft, Qatar Investment Authority) and a ~$450M funding history.

**Step 2 — Test for halo.** All three tests fire. *All attributes rated similarly?* Yes — the "AI leader" impression lifted technology, revenue, and management ratings together. *Rating disproportionate to attribute-specific evidence?* Yes — subsequent reporting showed much of the platform's output was produced not by autonomous AI but by hundreds of human engineers at outsourced "capacity partners" in India, with "Natasha" mostly generating front-end scaffolding; the AI attribute was rated far above its evidence. *Did the global impression precede the ratings?* Yes — "AI unicorn backed by Microsoft" arrived before any attribute-level diligence in most public discussion, and the boom-era prior ("AI companies grow fast") made a $220M revenue projection feel plausible.

**Step 3 — Decouple attributes.**
- *Technology:* Specific evidence — what fraction of delivered code is model-generated vs. human-written? A blind test (evaluate the product's output with the "AI company" label removed) would have read as: a development agency with a nice scoping UI. Contrarian view existed in industry reporting well before the collapse.
- *Revenue quality:* Audits later found 2024 revenue of roughly $55M against about $220M projected to investors and lenders; Bloomberg's post-mortem (July 2025) detailed alleged round-trip invoicing with the Indian firm VerSe Innovation from 2021–2024 — near-identical invoices exchanged for services allegedly never rendered — reported to have inflated revenue figures dramatically. Attribute-specific evidence was checkable (receivables, cash conversion, customer references) and, per Moody's early-warning post-mortem, warning signs were visible in the financials before insolvency.
- *Management credibility:* the founder's charisma and the investor roster substituted for verification — a textbook halo transfer ("Microsoft invested, so the diligence must have been done").

**Steps 4–6 — Structure, compare, adjust.** A structured evaluation would have scored each attribute against a rubric with independent evaluators and blinding where possible: technology scored by engineers on output alone (label removed); revenue scored by auditors on cash, not projections; the "AI" claim scored against a falsifiable definition (what does the model do end-to-end, on what inputs, with what human fallback?). Compared against base rates — most "AI-powered" vendors of the era were thin wrappers or human-in-the-loop services — the halo-adjusted rating lands near the sector median, not the top decile a $1.5B valuation implied. The adjusted decision: fund/buy only what the attribute evidence supports; treat the "AI" label as a claim to verify, not an attribute rating. The SEC's March 2024 orders made the same move in regulatory form: it fined the two advisers a combined $400,000 for stating AI capabilities they could not evidence — forcing the attribute back down to its evidence.

**The general 2023–2026 pattern.** Builder.ai is the sharpest case, not an isolated one. The era's evaluation failures ran through the same halo channel: "AI" in a pitch deck lifted valuations before diligence; an association with a marquee lab or a charismatic technical founder lifted credibility ratings of unrelated attributes; and inside companies, "the AI said so" began to halo machine outputs themselves — fluent model answers rated as accurate *because* they were fluent. The defense is unchanged since Thorndike (1920): decouple the attributes, demand attribute-specific evidence, and blind the label whenever the label is doing the rating's work.

**Founder takeaway (startup):** The AI halo is borrowed valuation — it re-prices to attribute-level evidence at the worst possible moment (diligence, audit, renewal). Build the attribute evidence early: a falsifiable description of what your model actually does, unit economics that survive the label being removed, and revenue an auditor would recognize. And when *you* evaluate — a hire from a famous lab, a vendor with "AI" in the name — blind the halo attribute first.

**Founder takeaway (SMB):** When an "AI-powered" vendor pitches you, run the blind test: describe what the service does without the letters "AI" and price that. Ask what is model-generated vs. human-done, what happens when the model fails, and ask for two customer references you choose from their list — attribute evidence, not label. If the pitch collapses without the halo, so will the service.
