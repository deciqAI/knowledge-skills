# Method in Action: Thorndike 1920 + Nisbett-Wilson 1977 + Rosenzweig 2007 Business Application

> *Example for the [halo-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Edward L. Thorndike** (1874–1949) was an American educational psychologist whose 1920 paper documented the halo effect in military officer ratings. Thorndike was an early figure in the psychology of learning (his "law of effect" was foundational) and also a pioneer of psychological measurement. His 1920 paper was a methodological critique of trait-rating: he showed that supposedly independent traits were rated so similarly that the raters could not be assessing them independently.

Thorndike's data: when commanding officers rated their subordinates on traits like intelligence, physique, leadership, character, and dependability, the cross-correlations were so high (often >0.6) that the same global impression was driving all the ratings. Independent trait-rating was, empirically, impossible — the global impression overwhelmed the specific judgments.

> "It seemed probable that the estimates of the same man in a number of different traits, such as intelligence, industry, technical skill, reliability, etc. were very highly correlated and very evenly correlated. ... The correlation between intelligence and physique was as high as the correlation of physique with itself measured at different times."
>
> — Thorndike (1920), pp. 25, 28.

**Nisbett & Wilson (1977) demonstration.** Richard Nisbett (1941–) and Timothy Wilson (1951–) ran an elegant experiment. They showed students a video of a lecturer (the same person) in two versions: one in which he behaved warmly and likably, one in which he behaved coldly and condescendingly. Students rated the lecturer's physical appearance, mannerisms, and accent.

Result: the warm-version students rated his physical appearance, mannerisms, and accent as more attractive — even though those attributes were physically identical in both videos. The warmth/coldness halo had bled into ratings of unrelated visible attributes.

Critically: when asked whether their global impression of the lecturer had influenced their specific ratings, students denied any influence. They believed they were rating physical appearance, mannerisms, and accent on the merits — and they were wrong. The halo effect operates unconsciously.

**Rosenzweig (2007) business application.** Phil Rosenzweig, IMD professor and former HBS faculty, applied the halo-effect framework to celebrated business books. His critique:

> Rosenzweig, P. (2007). *The Halo Effect.* Free Press. ISBN 978-1416541387.

Rosenzweig analyzed *In Search of Excellence* (Peters & Waterman 1982), *Built to Last* (Collins & Porras 1994), and *Good to Great* (Collins 2001) and showed that:

1. The "great companies" identified were selected because of recent strong performance.
2. The traits described as causing the greatness (strong culture, customer focus, disciplined execution, etc.) were derived from analyzing those companies after their success.
3. Many of the "great companies" subsequently underperformed dramatically. Of *In Search of Excellence's* 43 celebrated companies, many declined or failed within years (Atari, Wang Labs, Data General, etc.). Of *Good to Great's* 11 companies, several (Circuit City, Fannie Mae) had subsequent disasters.
4. The same observers describing the companies during success used positive language; after decline, the same companies were described in negative terms — by the same observers, about the same underlying organizations.

Rosenzweig's conclusion:

> "Most of what is published as serious business analysis is heavily contaminated by the halo effect. The 'descriptions' of company culture, strategy, leadership, and execution are descriptions of the company's performance, not of underlying attributes that caused performance. The lessons drawn from these descriptions are essentially attributions, not analyses."
>
> — Rosenzweig (2007), p. 64.

**Operational case: stock-price-driven CEO narrative.** Watch financial coverage of any major CEO over a decade. When stock price is high, the CEO is described as visionary, decisive, customer-focused. When stock price is low, the same CEO is described as out-of-touch, indecisive, distracted. The CEO did not actually change; the halo did. (See the trajectories of Jeff Immelt at GE, Steve Ballmer at Microsoft, Marissa Mayer at Yahoo, etc.)

**Operational case: unstructured interviews.** Decades of meta-analyses (Schmidt & Hunter 1998; Highhouse 2008) show that unstructured interviews have weak predictive validity for job performance (r ≈ 0.20) compared to structured interviews with calibrated rubrics (r ≈ 0.50). The mechanism: unstructured interviews are halo-dominated. An attractive, articulate candidate gets high marks on competence; a quiet competent candidate gets low marks. Structured rubrics partially break the halo.

**Operational case: Tesla and Apple brand halo.** Blind taste tests, blind product comparisons, and blind feature evaluations consistently show that brand-loyal customers rate their preferred brand higher than blind comparisons predict. The halo is real, measurable, and economically significant.

**Operational case: Theranos.** Elizabeth Holmes was profiled in *Forbes*, *Fortune*, and *The New Yorker* in glowing terms when Theranos was at $9B valuation. The same outlets later described her as a fraud. The actual evidence available to journalists in both periods was overlapping; the halo flipped between periods.

Three operational lessons:

**First, decouple attributes via structure.** Calibrated rubrics, blinded evaluation, independent evaluators, and time-separated ratings all reduce halo. Unstructured intuitive judgment is halo-saturated.

**Second, business books and analyst reports overclaim.** When you read that successful companies have certain traits, recognize that the description is halo-inflated. The traits may not generalize, and the same companies may be described oppositely after underperformance.

**Third, halo flows from outcomes to descriptions, not descriptions to outcomes.** Causal claims based on observing successful companies are often backwards: the success caused the favorable description, not the reverse. Generalizing from such descriptions to predict future performance is unreliable.
