# Method in Action: Redelmeier and Kahneman — Colonoscopy Study (1996)

> *Example for the [peak-end-rule](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A primary-source-documented clinical case; not a business parable.

Donald Redelmeier and Daniel Kahneman studied 682 patients undergoing colonoscopy at the University of Toronto in the early 1990s. Colonoscopy is uncomfortable, variable in duration, and ends with scope withdrawal — which at the time typically concluded with a rapid exit that sometimes was the most uncomfortable moment of the procedure.

**Emotion map (Step 1 & 2):** Patients reported pain intensity every 60 seconds during the procedure on a 10-point scale. This generated real-time experiencing-self data for every patient. Procedure durations ranged from 4 to 69 minutes. The researchers could compute the integral of pain (total experienced pain) and the peak and final pain for each patient.

**Gap diagnosis (Step 3 & 4):** The correlation analysis showed that retrospective global ratings (collected immediately after) were predicted almost entirely by the *average of peak pain and final-minute pain* — not by total duration, not by total pain, and not by average pain. Two patients with identical total pain but different endings had systematically different retrospective evaluations.

**Peak and ending intervention (Step 5 & 6):** A subset of patients was assigned to a treatment condition in which the colonoscope was left partially inserted at reduced discomfort for an additional minute at the end of the procedure. This *extended* the duration and *increased* total pain. But because the final minute was at lower intensity than the peak, the ending-state improved. Retrospective evaluations from this group were significantly better, and — in a follow-up — these patients were more willing to schedule a future colonoscopy.

The finding is directly actionable: a physician adding a mild, low-discomfort minute at the end improved patient experience memory in a clinically meaningful way, increased adherence to follow-up care, and did so without changing the *substantive* quality of the procedure. Duration was ignored by the remembering self; the ending was not.

**Sources:** Redelmeier, Donald A. & Kahneman, Daniel. "Patients' Memories of Painful Medical Treatments: Real-Time and Retrospective Evaluations of Two Minimally Invasive Procedures." *Pain*, Vol. 66, Nos. 1–2 (July 1996), pp. 3–8. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-3959(96)02994-6 . Kahneman, D., Fredrickson, B.L., Schreiber, C.A., & Redelmeier, D.A. "When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End." *Psychological Science*, Vol. 4, No. 6 (1993), pp. 401–405. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.1993.tb00589.x
