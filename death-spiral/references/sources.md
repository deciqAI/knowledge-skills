# Sources — death-spiral

> *Primary sources for the [death-spiral](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Primary Source 1:**
Porter, M.E. (1985). *Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance*. Free Press. https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Competitive-Advantage/Michael-E-Porter/9780684841465

Verbatim: "Competitive advantage cannot be understood by looking at a firm as a whole. It stems from the many discrete activities a firm performs... The sustainability of competitive advantage depends on what economists call barriers to imitation... erosion of advantage is often self-reinforcing." (p. 33–34)

**Primary Source 2:**
Christensen, C.M. (1997). *The Innovator's Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail*. Harvard Business School Press. https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=46

Verbatim: "Precisely because these firms listened to their customers, invested aggressively in new technologies that would provide their customers more and better products of the sort they wanted, and because they carefully studied market trends and systematically allocated investment capital to innovations that promised the best returns, they lost their positions of leadership." (p. xii)

**Primary Source 3 (case):**
Shih, W., Kaufman, S., & Spinola, D. (2007). "Kodak and the Digital Revolution (A)." *Harvard Business School Case* 705-448. Available via Harvard Business Publishing.

Kodak SEC filings 1994–2012: https://www.sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar?action=getcompany&CIK=0000031235

**What is NOT cited and why:** Game-theoretic models of competitive dynamics (Nash equilibrium, prisoner's dilemma in competitive markets) are not cited because this skill focuses on the operational and strategic diagnostic of actual spiral conditions, not on the formal game-theoretic structure. Game-theoretic treatments are relevant for intervention design but are a separate skill.
