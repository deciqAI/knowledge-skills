# Method in Action: Chegg's AI Death Spiral (2022–2026)

> *Example for the [death-spiral](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Kodak's spiral took eighteen years. Chegg's took three. The education-technology company — a ~$12B business at its 2021 peak, built on a subscription library of textbook answers and expert Q&A — met a technology shift that breached every moat simultaneously: ChatGPT launched November 30, 2022, and gave students a free, instant, general-purpose answer engine. Chegg is the cleanest modern demonstration of the framework because each Process step is visible in SEC filings and earnings calls, quarter by quarter, at 6× Kodak's speed.

**Step 1 — Audit moat dimensions (as of late 2022).** (a) *Content library* (tens of millions of expert answers): strong, but its value assumed answers were scarce — **rapidly eroding** once generation became free. (b) *Brand* ("Chegg it" was campus vernacular): moderate, eroding — brands tied to a job-to-be-done die with the job. (c) *Switching costs*: weak — monthly consumer subscription, cancellable in a click. (d) *Network/data* (new questions feeding the library): moderate, eroding — the flywheel only compounds if students keep asking Chegg first. No moat rated strong-and-stable against a generative substitute.

**Step 2 — Identify breach conditions.** For the content library: "a model generates comparable answers on demand, free, across all subjects." That single condition breached three moats at once — library value, the ask-Chegg-first habit, and the data flywheel. It went from hypothetical to live in one product launch. The audit lesson: Chegg's moats were all downstream of one assumption (answer scarcity), so one breach cascaded everywhere — a concentration the Step-1 audit is designed to surface *before* the breach.

**Step 3 — Check early-warning signals.** Textbook sequence, compressed: new-account growth slowed within months of ChatGPT's launch; on May 2, 2023 — barely five months in — then-CEO Dan Rosensweig told investors ChatGPT was hurting customer growth, and the stock fell about 48% in a single day. Churn and stalled acquisition confirmed; the spiral had started while absolute revenue still looked survivable.

**Step 4 — Calculate the cascade.** The filings trace it: subscriber losses accelerated (by Q1 2025, subscribers were down ~31% year-over-year to ~3.2 million; revenue down ~30% to ~$121M; subscription revenue for H1 2025 down ~34% versus H1 2024). Revenue decline forced cost cuts — including a May 2025 reduction of about 22% of the workforce (~248 employees) — which constrained the product investment needed to answer the threat, while Google's AI Overviews squeezed the top of the funnel and AI labs courted students with free access. Peak-to-trough, public reporting put the market-value destruction on the order of $14B. Share loss → revenue decline → cost cuts → product lag → further share loss: the canonical loop, running at consumer-subscription speed.

**Step 5 — Identify the intervention window.** Working backwards from irreversibility: once engineering capacity fell below what a competitive AI product required *and* the ask-Chegg-first habit was gone, no internal response could close the gap. That put the last viable intervention around 2023 — the year of the warning, not the year of the layoffs. Chegg did attempt Step-6-type responses inside the window (Cheggmate, an AI assistant announced with GPT-4 in April 2023, later its own models); they failed not for lack of speed but because the substitute was free, general, and distributed by default — the breach condition eliminated the *category's* pricing power, which no feature response restores.

**Step 6 — Design intervention.** The framework's verdict, applied honestly in 2023, points away from competitive response (Step 1–2 moves) toward strategic repositioning or platform shift while resources remained: reposition around what a general model can't generate (accredited outcomes, institutional distribution, proctored assessment — the direction of its Busuu language asset), partner-or-sell the library as training data from a position of strength, or return capital. The general lesson the case adds to the skill: **when the breach eliminates the scarcity your whole category monetized, "add AI features" is a Step-1 response to a Step-5 problem.**

**Stop-rule check.** Every moat had a named breach condition (Step 2 complete); decline was confirmed structural, not cyclical — student demand didn't shrink, it rerouted to a free substitute. Verdict: spiral confirmed at Step 3 by May 2023, cascade fully realized by 2025.

**Founder takeaway (startup):** Run Step 1 on your own company and ask Chegg's question: do all my moats secretly rest on one scarcity assumption? If a free general-purpose model breaks that assumption, your intervention window is the first quarter of *slowing growth* — not the first quarter of decline. At consumer speed the spiral runs in quarters, not decades.

**Founder takeaway (SMB):** Chegg's spiral began the day its customers found a free way to get the same job done — the equivalent for a local business is the moment your bookkeeping clients, tutoring students, or design customers realize an AI tool does 80% of it. Don't compete with free on the 80%; reprice around the 20% that needs a human who's accountable (accuracy, liability, relationships), and watch your own early-warning line: new-customer inquiries slowing is the signal, not revenue falling.
