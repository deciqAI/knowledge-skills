# Method in Action: Kodak's Death Spiral (1994–2012)

> *Example for the [death-spiral](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Kodak's moat in the 1980s was among the most formidable in consumer markets: proprietary chemistry for film and paper (technology moat), a massive global distribution network through pharmacies, photo labs, and retailers (distribution moat), and brand trust so deep that "Kodak moment" had become a cultural idiom (brand moat).

The spiral trigger occurred from 1994 to 1999 when digital cameras — including an early prototype Kodak's own engineers had developed in 1975 but shelved as a threat to the film business — began establishing a durable alternative. The full spiral:

- **Breach (1994–2000):** Digital cameras achieved sufficient quality to serve mainstream consumers. Kodak's distribution moat had no value in digital (cameras sold through electronics retailers, not photo labs). The technology moat was irrelevant (chemistry knowledge did not transfer to CCD sensors).
- **Share loss (1996–2003):** Digital cameras took an accelerating share of new-camera purchases. Film volume began declining.
- **Revenue decline (2000–2005):** Film revenue, which had been Kodak's high-margin core, began structural decline. Total revenue fell from $13.8 billion (1996) to $9.4 billion (2003).
- **R&D constraint (2001–2006):** Kodak cut R&D as revenue fell. Research headcount was reduced. Investment in digital was insufficient to establish a competitive position against Canon, Nikon, and Sony — who had invested in digital for years.
- **Product lag (2003–2008):** Kodak's digital products were consistently one to two generations behind category leaders on image quality, user experience, and ecosystem integration.
- **Further breach (2006–2012):** The accelerating decline of film revenue removed the last source of cash that could have funded a recovery. Chapter 11 bankruptcy was filed January 19, 2012.

The spiral was structurally visible and analytically predictable by 2000. Management's response — positioning Kodak as a "digital company" in communications while continuing to invest primarily in film — was a misidentification of the intervention required. The last viable intervention window was 1997–2001: establish genuine digital category leadership using the brand moat that still existed before the brand became associated with decline.
