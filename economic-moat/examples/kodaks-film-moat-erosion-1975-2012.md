# Method in Action: Kodak's Film Moat Erosion (1975-2012)

> *Example for the [economic-moat](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Eastman Kodak is the canonical moat-erosion case: a genuinely wide moat — not a fake one — destroyed not by a rival crossing it, but by a technology shift that made the castle behind it worthless. It is the strongest historical argument for Step 4 (trajectory) and Step 5 (smart-attacker) over static moat width.

**Step 1 — Frame.** Business: consumer film, cameras, and photo processing. Industry: silver-halide photography, which Kodak dominated in the United States for most of the twentieth century. Time horizon: multi-decade. Claimed advantage: brand, manufacturing scale, and a razor-and-blades model — cameras sold cheaply, high-margin film and processing sold forever. Financial signal: decades of dominant market share and above-competitive returns in film.

**Step 2 — Test the five sources.** Circa 1980, Kodak passed almost all of them:

1. **Intangible assets — present.** The Kodak brand was among the most recognized in the world; "Kodak moment" entered the language. A deep patent estate covered film chemistry.
2. **Switching costs — partial.** Consumers were habituated to Kodak film and the retail processing network; professionals were locked into Kodak color workflows.
3. **Network effects — absent.** No user-to-user mechanism.
4. **Cost advantages — present.** Film manufacturing at Kodak's scale involved formidable chemical-engineering process knowledge; matching its unit economics required enormous capital and decades of accumulated expertise.
5. **Efficient scale — partial.** Globally, essentially only Fujifilm and Agfa competed at scale; the structure tolerated few entrants.

**Step 3 — Greenwald structural test.** The mechanisms were articulable and real: replicating Kodak's brand, patent estate, and process-scale economics would have cost a well-funded rival billions of dollars and decades. By the skill's own criteria, this was a wide moat — the analysis so far is not where Kodak's story goes wrong.

**Step 4 — Trajectory.** This is where the case turns. A Kodak engineer, Steven Sasson, built the first digital camera prototype inside Kodak in 1975. From that point, the relevant question was never "can a rival make better film?" but "will photographs stop being made of film at all?" Through the 1990s the static moat still looked wide — share, brand, and margins held — while the trajectory was narrowing catastrophically: digital sensor quality improved on a compounding cost curve, and every convert to digital exited the film-and-processing profit pool permanently. Kodak's management saw the shift and still could not respond adequately, because — as Christensen's disruption framework explains — the disruptive product was initially worse, lower-margin, and cannibalized the profit engine the whole organization was built to defend.

**Step 5 — Smart-attacker test.** The decisive attack was never a frontal assault on the moat. No attacker tried to out-brand Kodak or out-scale its film plants; that attack would indeed have cost billions with low probability of success. The smart attackers — consumer-electronics firms, then camera-equipped mobile phones — bypassed the moat by making silver-halide chemistry irrelevant. Kodak's brand, patents, and manufacturing scale all still existed in 2010; they simply protected a shrinking market. Fujifilm, facing the identical erosion, survived by redeploying its chemistry expertise into adjacent markets — evidence that the failure was strategic response to a narrowing trajectory, not the absence of a moat.

**Step 6 — Synthesize.** Moat width circa 1980: wide (brand + cost/scale, structurally verifiable). Trajectory from the mid-1990s: narrowing, terminally. Duration of above-competitive returns: long, then abruptly over. Operational implication realized in full: Kodak filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection in January 2012 — an outcome *The Economist* framed as the ending of one of the century's great franchises by technological substitution.

The operational lesson: a wide static moat plus a narrowing trajectory destroys more value than a narrow moat that is widening, because the width finances denial. Moat analysis that stops at Step 3 would have rated Kodak a fortress in 1995. Steps 4 and 5 exist precisely for this case.

The mapped steps:

1. Frame: dominant film franchise, razor-and-blades economics, multi-decade horizon
2. Five sources: brand and cost/scale strongly present; switching costs partial; verdict wide
3. Greenwald test: passed — mechanisms specific, replication cost measured in billions and decades
4. Trajectory: narrowing from 1975 onward; digital cost curve emptied the film profit pool
5. Smart attacker: bypassed the moat entirely — made the protected asset irrelevant rather than contesting it
6. Synthesis: wide-but-narrowing; Chapter 11 in January 2012; trajectory outranks static width
