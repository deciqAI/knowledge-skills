# Method in Action: The AI Thesis War (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [confirmation-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

From the public release of ChatGPT in late 2022 through 2026, the debate over generative AI split into two camps that read the *same* evidence in opposite directions. Bulls treated every new model release, every capability demo, and every capex announcement as confirmation that "AI changes everything." Bears treated every hallucination, every stalled deployment, and every disappointing benchmark as confirmation that the technology was overhyped. Both were doing the same thing: seeking and weighting evidence that fit a belief already held. This example applies the skill's process to a single, common version of the error — an operating team converging on an AI product thesis by accumulating confirming signals and quietly discounting disconfirming evaluation results.

The setting is deliberately generic because the pattern was near-universal in this period: a company decides "our workflow will be transformed by an LLM agent," greenlights the build, and the internal narrative hardens faster than the evidence justifies.

**Step 1 — State the claim.** Claim: "An LLM agent will reliably automate our core workflow, so we should commit the roadmap to it." Evidence cited: impressive vendor demos, a few striking internal successes, rising industry adoption surveys, and very large announced infrastructure spend by major AI labs and cloud providers. Confidence: high — often expressed as "the direction is obvious."

**Step 2 — Construct falsification.** The disciplined question: what observation would show the thesis is wrong? A concrete falsifier is available and cheap to specify — a held-out evaluation set of real tasks on which the agent must hit a defined success threshold (say, task completion without human correction) before the roadmap commits. The failure mode of this period was that teams rarely stated the thesis in falsifiable form. "AI changes everything" and "AI is transformative for us" are not hypotheses; they name a direction, not a testable outcome. If you cannot say what result would make you abandon the plan, you have a description, not a hypothesis.

**Step 3 — Audit evidence-seeking.** The characteristic bull audit reveals one-directional search: the team collected winning demos, favorable analyst reports, and cherry-picked internal wins, while disconfirming signals — evaluations where the agent failed on messy real inputs, pilots that stalled at integration, tasks where a human still had to redo the output — were reframed as "prompting problems," "edge cases," or "it'll be fixed in the next model." A recurring public form of this: reports through 2024–2025 that a large majority of enterprise generative-AI pilots did not reach production or show clear measured return, findings that bulls tended to discount and bears tended to over-generalize. Neither camp sought the strongest version of the *other* side's case; each read the ambiguous middle as support.

**Step 4 — Re-evaluate ambiguous evidence.** Most of the cited evidence was ambiguous and read as supporting. Massive AI capex is compatible with "transformative technology arriving" *and* with "classic bubble over-investment ahead of returns" — the same datapoint fits both theses, so it discriminates between neither. A dazzling demo is compatible with genuine capability *and* with a scenario chosen to hide failure modes. High reported adoption is compatible with real value *and* with experimentation that never converts to retained, paid usage. When the opposite reading fits the fact equally well, the fact is interpretation, not evidence — and the only items that actually move the thesis are the unambiguous ones, chiefly the held-out eval results the team was least eager to run.

**Step 5 — Install structural countermeasure.** Personal vigilance ("we're being rigorous") does not survive contact with a motivating narrative, so the fix is structural. Concretely: a rotated, mandatory red team assigned to build the kill-the-thesis case; a pre-mortem (Klein 2007) imagining the agent shipped and quietly failed, working backward to why; blind evaluation where reviewers score agent output against human output without knowing which is which; and a falsification-first rule — require the agent to pass three hard, adversarial task cases before one polished demo is allowed to count. The eval harness must be owned by someone who does not gain from the thesis being true.

**Step 6 — Establish update conditions.** State in advance what would change the decision: "If the agent scores below the threshold on the held-out set, or if a pilot cannot reach production within a defined window, we pause or narrow scope." Fix a re-examination date rather than a vibe, and name who is empowered to halt the roadmap. This is the step that the two-camp AI debate structurally lacks — commentators rarely pre-commit to the evidence that would flip them, which is exactly why positions polarized rather than converged as data accumulated.

The mapped steps:
1. State claim: "An LLM agent will reliably automate our core workflow" — evidence: demos, select internal wins, adoption surveys, industry capex; confidence: high
2. Construct falsification: held-out eval threshold and a pilot-to-production window — usually never specified, leaving a direction rather than a hypothesis
3. Audit evidence-seeking: one-directional collection of confirming demos and reports; failed evals reframed as prompting or edge-case problems
4. Re-evaluate ambiguous evidence: capex, demos, and adoption figures fit both the bull and bear reading equally, so they discriminate nothing; only unambiguous eval results move the thesis
5. Structural countermeasure: rotated red team, pre-mortem, blind eval, falsification-first (three refuting cases before one confirming demo), eval owned by a disinterested party
6. Update conditions: pre-committed eval threshold and production window, a fixed re-examination date, and a named person empowered to stop

The broader 2023–2026 debate is the same failure at population scale: two intelligent camps, the same public datapoints, opposite conclusions — because each sought confirmation rather than testing. The skill's claim holds in both directions. The bear who reads every hallucination as proof AI is a dead end is committing the identical error as the bull who reads every demo as proof it is inevitable. The corrective is not choosing a side more carefully; it is building a test that could embarrass whichever side you are on.
