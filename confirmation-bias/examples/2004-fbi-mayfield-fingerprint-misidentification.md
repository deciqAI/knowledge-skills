# Method in Action: The FBI's Mayfield Fingerprint Misidentification (2004)

> *Example for the [confirmation-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

On March 11, 2004, coordinated bombs on Madrid commuter trains killed 191 people. The Spanish National Police (SNP) recovered a plastic bag of detonators and shared digital images of latent fingerprints from it through Interpol. The FBI ran one print — Latent Fingerprint 17 — through its IAFIS database and identified Brandon Mayfield, a Portland, Oregon attorney with no criminal record who had never been to Spain. What followed is the best-documented real-world case of confirmation bias in forensic science, dissected in the U.S. Department of Justice Office of the Inspector General's 2006 review.

**Step 1 — the claim.** An FBI examiner declared the latent print a "100% identification" of Mayfield. Confidence was absolute, and the language of fingerprint examination at the time permitted no probabilistic hedging: a match was a match.

**Step 2 — no falsification constructed.** No one asked what observation would falsify the identification, even though a falsifying observation existed and was found later: the print belonged to someone else. Mayfield's candidate print came from a computer search of millions of records, which mathematically guarantees close near-misses — a base-rate hazard the examiners never treated as a live alternative hypothesis.

**Step 3 — evidence-seeking ran one direction.** The OIG found the examiners engaged in what it called circular reasoning: having formed the identification, they worked backward from Mayfield's known ten-print card to "find" corresponding features in the blurry latent, and features that did not match were explained away as artifacts, overlays, or distortions rather than counted as disconfirming. Verification made it worse, not better: the second examiner and a supervisor confirmed the match *knowing* the first examiner's conclusion — non-blind verification that inherited the original bias. Even a court-appointed independent examiner, reviewing with knowledge of the FBI's conclusion, concurred.

**Step 4 — ambiguous evidence read as supporting.** After the match, investigators learned Mayfield was a Muslim convert married to an Egyptian national who had once represented a convicted terrorism defendant in a child-custody matter. None of this was evidence about the fingerprint. All of it was absorbed as corroboration, hardening confidence in an identification it logically could not support.

**Step 6 failure — the update trigger fired and was ignored.** In April 2004 the SNP formally informed the FBI that its own examination of the print was negative for Mayfield. This was exactly the event that should have forced re-examination. Instead, the FBI sent examiners to Madrid to persuade the Spanish that the FBI was right. Mayfield was arrested as a material witness on May 6, 2004, and detained for about two weeks.

**Step 5 — the structural countermeasure arrived from outside.** The SNP kept working the print as an open question and on May 19, 2004 matched it to Ouhnane Daoud, an Algerian national. Mayfield was released the next day; the FBI issued a formal apology and later paid a $2 million settlement. The OIG review recommended the structural fixes the process had lacked: blind verification (verifiers must not know the first examiner's conclusion), mandatory documentation of *differences* between prints — not just similarities — and explicit caution about candidates produced by large database searches.

The case demonstrates the skill's core operational claim: three trained FBI examiners plus an independent expert — all personally vigilant, all professionally certain — converged on the same wrong answer, while an outside team that never adopted the hypothesis found the truth. Personal expertise did not defeat the bias; an adversarial second process did.

The aftermath reshaped forensic practice beyond the FBI. Mayfield became the standard citation whenever fingerprint identification's claim to infallibility was challenged, and it pushed the discipline toward contextual-information management: shielding examiners from case facts (the suspect's religion, the crime's severity) that cannot inform the comparison but reliably bias it.

The mapped steps:
1. State claim: "Latent Fingerprint 17 is Mayfield's" — cited evidence: examiner comparison; confidence: "100% identification"
2. Construct falsification: never done — the database-search base rate of near-miss candidates was not treated as an alternative hypothesis
3. Audit evidence-seeking: circular reasoning backward from Mayfield's known prints; non-blind verification inherited the first conclusion; differences explained away
4. Re-evaluate ambiguous evidence: Mayfield's religion and legal clients — irrelevant to the print — read as corroboration
5. Structural countermeasure: supplied externally by the SNP's independent examination; institutionalized afterward as blind verification and mandatory documentation of differences
6. Update conditions: the SNP's negative finding was the built-in trigger — the FBI overrode it instead of re-examining, extending a wrongful detention

Primary source: U.S. Department of Justice, Office of the Inspector General (2006). *A Review of the FBI's Handling of the Brandon Mayfield Case.* Washington, DC: DOJ OIG, March 2006.
