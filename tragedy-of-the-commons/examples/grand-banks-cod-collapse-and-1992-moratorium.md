# Method in Action: The Grand Banks Cod Collapse and the 1992 Moratorium (1950s–1992)

> *Example for the [tragedy-of-the-commons](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The northern cod fishery off Newfoundland ran for roughly five centuries — European fleets worked the Grand Banks from the late 1400s onward — and then collapsed within a few decades of industrialization. It is the canonical natural-resource commons tragedy, and it also demonstrates the skill's sharpest lesson: central regulation without Ostrom's institutional structure fails just like an unmanaged commons.

**Step 1 — Identify.** Shared resource: the northern cod stock (Gadus morhua) off Newfoundland and Labrador. User community: Newfoundland inshore fishers (small boats, local knowledge), Canadian offshore trawler companies, and foreign distant-water factory fleets. Failure mode: stock collapse. Current rules (pre-collapse): open international access beyond territorial waters; after Canada's 200-mile exclusive economic zone declaration in 1977, centralized quotas set by the federal Department of Fisheries and Oceans (DFO).

**Step 2 — Verify commons structure.** Shared access: yes — no single actor could exclude others. Rivalrous: yes — every tonne caught is unavailable to everyone else and to the spawning stock. Diffuse responsibility: yes — no fleet bore the cost of its own overfishing; the cost was spread across all users and the future. Hardin pattern: yes — each captain's rational move was to catch more before competitors did. Post-WWII factory trawlers from Europe and the USSR drove reported catches to a historic peak around 800,000 tonnes in 1968, several times the long-run sustainable harvest. All four conditions present: a textbook commons.

**Step 3 — Ostrom audit.** This is the instructive part — Canada *did* regulate after 1977, and the stock still collapsed, because nearly every design principle was weak or absent:

- **1 Boundaries — weak.** The 200-mile zone came only in 1977; foreign fleets kept fishing the "Nose" and "Tail" of the Banks that lay outside it.
- **2 Congruence — absent.** DFO quotas rested on offshore survey models that overestimated the stock; harvest rules did not match the resource's actual condition.
- **3 Collective-choice — absent.** Inshore fishers reported shrinking fish and falling catches through the 1980s; their local knowledge had no channel into quota-setting, and the warnings were dismissed as anecdote.
- **4 Monitoring — weak.** Discarding, misreporting, and high-grading at sea were widespread and largely unobserved.
- **5 Graduated sanctions — weak.** Quota violations carried little consequence relative to the profit from cheating.
- **6 Conflict resolution — absent.** The inshore–offshore dispute over who was destroying the stock festered for a decade with no legitimate forum.
- **7 Right to organize — absent.** Fishing communities had no recognized authority to set or enforce local rules.
- **8 Nested enterprises — absent.** A single centralized regulator managed a resource spanning local, national, and international scales.

**Step 4 — Intervene (what actually happened).** The intervention came only after the resource was gone. On July 2, 1992, Canada's fisheries minister declared a moratorium on the northern cod fishery. The spawning stock had fallen to a small fraction of its historical level; roughly 30,000 fishers and plant workers lost their livelihoods — often described as the largest single industrial layoff in Canadian history. The moratorium was announced as a two-year pause; the stock did not meaningfully recover for decades.

**Step 5 — Monitor.** The post-mortem literature (Hutchings & Myers 1994) concluded the collapse was driven by overfishing, not environment alone — the monitoring system had measured the wrong things and ignored the users closest to the resource.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify: northern cod stock · inshore/offshore/foreign fleets · stock collapse · open access, then centralized quotas
2. Verify commons structure: shared ✓ rivalrous ✓ diffuse responsibility ✓ Hardin pattern ✓
3. Ostrom audit: principles 2, 3, 6, 7, 8 absent; 1, 4, 5 weak — regulation without institutional design
4. Intervene: 1992 moratorium — imposed after collapse instead of designed before it
5. Monitor: scientific post-mortem confirmed overfishing under a monitoring regime that excluded local knowledge

The operational lesson inverts the "just regulate it" reflex: Canada had a regulator, quotas, and science, yet reproduced the unmanaged-commons outcome because congruence, collective-choice, and nested governance were missing. Contrast with Ostrom's enduring fisheries commons, where users who monitor each other and set their own rules sustained harvests for centuries.
