# Method in Action: Metacognition While Working With AI Copilots (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [metacognition](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example on a case almost everyone now faces. By 2024–2026, AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude, ChatGPT) and general-purpose chatbots became a daily tool for a large share of knowledge workers and developers. GitHub reported that Copilot had more than a million paying developers and, in a widely cited 2022 study, that developers completed a benchmark task substantially faster with Copilot than without. Stack Overflow's annual Developer Survey found, by 2023–2024, that a large majority of developers were already using or planning to use AI tools in their workflow.

The metacognitive problem is specific and well-documented: LLM output is **fluent**. It reads confident and polished whether or not it is correct. That fluency attacks exactly the monitoring loop this skill is about — it manufactures the **illusion of understanding** and invites **automation complacency** (trusting an automated aid past the point where you should verify). Two failure modes are already public record:

- **Fabricated citations.** In 2023 a U.S. federal case (*Mata v. Avianca*, S.D.N.Y.) became widely reported after lawyers submitted a brief containing case citations that ChatGPT had invented; the court sanctioned the attorneys. The lawyers' own explanation was, in effect, that the tool's answers looked authoritative and they did not verify them.
- **Automation bias / complacency** is a decades-old human-factors finding (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997; Mosier & Skitka, 1990s aviation studies): humans under-monitor an automated aid they perceive as reliable, and *miss* errors they would have caught working unaided. LLM fluency is a potent new trigger for the same effect.

The correct move is not "distrust AI" or "trust AI" — it is to run the monitoring loop *on your own confidence* and decide, per output, whether to accept the fluent answer or slow down and verify. Walk the protocol.

1. **Understand (with monitoring).** Restate what you actually asked the AI and what you need back: a fact, a citation, working code, a plan, a judgment call? Identify the unknown: *is this answer verifiable by me, and at what cost?* Honest check: *do I understand the AI's answer, or do I just recognize that it reads fluently?* Fluency is not comprehension. If you could not re-derive or spot-check the claim yourself, you do not yet understand it — you are recognizing a confident-sounding topic.

2. **Devise a plan (with monitoring).** Choose a verification strategy *matched to the stakes*, and articulate why. Cheap-to-check + low-stakes (a syntax reminder, a brainstorm) → accept and move on; verification would be over-monitoring. Expensive-if-wrong (a legal citation, a financial figure, a security-relevant code path, a medical claim, anything you will publish or ship) → **verify at the source before use**. State the rule out loud: *"Citations, numbers, and code touching money or safety get independently checked; low-stakes scaffolding does not."* Set a time budget so verification is scheduled, not vibes-based.

3. **Carry out the plan (with monitoring).** Execute the check. For a citation: does the source exist and say this? For a figure: trace it to a primary source. For code: run it, read it, test the edge cases the model glossed. The recurring monitoring question: *"Am I actually verifying, or am I just re-reading the fluent answer and feeling reassured?"* Re-reading the model's own confident prose is not verification — it is the complacency loop tightening. Set a reset trigger: the moment you notice yourself pasting output you have not checked into something that ships, stop.

4. **Look back (with monitoring).** Did the answer hold up? Where the AI was wrong, *what signal would have tipped you off earlier* — over-specific detail, a citation you couldn't find, code that was suspiciously clean? Record the meta-lesson, not just the fix: e.g., "fabrications cluster in exactly the specifics that look most authoritative — named cases, precise page numbers, exact statistics." That lesson transfers to the next tool and the next model version.

5. **Recognize the stuck-loop.** A distinct AI-era stuck-loop: you and the model iterate 30+ minutes, each reply confidently wrong in a new way, and you keep re-prompting instead of stepping outside the tool. That is a signal to reframe — solve a piece by hand, consult a primary source, or ask a human. Staying in the chat because each answer *sounds* like progress is motion, not advancement.

6. **Calibrate confidence explicitly.** Separate two numbers you tend to fuse: *the model's apparent confidence* (always high — it is fluent by construction) and *your justified confidence in the claim* (a function of what you actually verified). State the second as a number: *"I've checked the citation exists and says this; 90%. I have not run the code; 40% until I do."* "The AI sounded sure" is not calibration and never was.

7. **Pre-commit re-monitoring schedule.** For ongoing AI-assisted work, complacency creeps back silently as the tool earns trust. Pre-commit: periodically **spot-audit** a sample of accepted AI outputs (e.g., every N pull requests, a fixed fraction of AI-drafted claims) even when nothing looks wrong — precisely because "nothing looks wrong" is what automation complacency feels like from the inside.

**The meta-lesson.** The scarce skill in the AI era is not prompting — it is calibrated **knowing when to trust the fluent answer and when to slow down and verify**. That is metacognition applied to a second reasoner in the loop. Schoenfeld's experts spent 30–40% of their time monitoring their *own* reasoning; working with a copilot, you now monitor two reasoners, and the fluent one is engineered to disarm exactly the loop that keeps you accurate.
