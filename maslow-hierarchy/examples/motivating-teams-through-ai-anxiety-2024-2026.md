# Method in Action: Motivating a Team Through the AI-Anxiety Era (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [maslow-hierarchy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between 2023 and 2026, generative AI moved from novelty to a standing feature of knowledge work. Large employers publicly tied restructuring to AI, tech-sector layoffs continued at scale, and survey after survey found that a large share of workers worried AI could reduce or eliminate their jobs. In that climate, a familiar management move stops working: leaders roll out an ambitious "become an AI-native team" mission — new tools, frontier projects, upskilling budgets — and find the team disengaged, anxious, or quietly resistant rather than energized. This example walks that case through the skill's six-step Process.

## Step 1 — State the situation

- **Who is being motivated:** a knowledge-work team (say, 20 people in engineering, product, and ops) at a mid-size company that has just announced an "AI-first" strategy.
- **Desired behavior:** enthusiastic adoption of AI tooling, volunteering for frontier projects, self-directed upskilling, and creative use of the new capability.
- **Current incentive:** the mission itself ("do meaningful frontier work"), a training budget, access to premium AI tools, and public praise for early adopters.
- **Observed response:** low uptake, hedged questions in all-hands ("does this mean fewer of us next year?"), private slumps in morale, some quiet job-searching. The incentive that "should" excite ambitious people is landing flat.

## Step 2 — Map need-state

For each level, mark substantially met / partially met / unmet — from the *team's* perceived reality, not the leader's assumption.

| Level | State | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Physiological | Substantially met | Salaries paid; no one is going hungry. |
| **Safety** | **Unmet / threatened** | The AI-first announcement, read against public news of AI-linked layoffs, is heard as "the company is automating my job." Job security, income predictability, and professional-identity security are all in question. |
| Belonging | Partially met, eroding | Layoff rumors and internal competition to "prove you're AI-proof" fray team cohesion. |
| Esteem | Threatened | If the skills a person built a career on are being automated, their sense of competence and status is under attack. |
| Self-actualization | The *target* of the incentive | "Master AI, do frontier work" is a self-actualization appeal. |

The key observation: the incentive is aimed at the top of the hierarchy while the operative unmet need sits near the bottom.

## Step 3 — Identify incentive level

Mapping the current incentives to the levels they address:

- Mission / "meaningful frontier work" → **self-actualization**
- Training budget, mastery of a new tool → **self-actualization + esteem**
- Premium tool access, public praise for early adopters → **esteem**

None of the current incentives addresses **safety**. There is no credible signal about job security, and "praise for early adopters" can even *raise* safety anxiety ("the ones who don't adapt fast will be cut").

## Step 4 — Diagnose mismatch

Clear mismatch, in the **incentive-too-high** direction: the incentives target self-actualization and esteem, but the operative unmet need is safety. This is the deficiency-dominance case the skill is built for — a severely threatened lower need crowds out motivational attention to higher needs. A frontier-work appeal cannot compete with the amygdala-level question "will I still have a job?" The mismatch is not that the mission is wrong; it is that the mission is being offered before the floor beneath it is stable.

This matches the skill's Diagnostic Pack row *"Team in crisis after layoffs → Safety dominates → Leadership pushes new initiatives instead of stability."* The AI-anxiety era generalizes that row from "after a layoff" to "under a credible, ambient threat of one."

## Step 5 — Choose intervention

Address the unmet lower-level need first, then match higher-level incentives to the now-operative need.

1. **Safety first (make it concrete, not slogans).** Say plainly what the AI strategy means for headcount and for each role, to the degree honestly known. Where roles are genuinely changing, name a transition path ("we are retraining, not replacing, this function") and back it with a commitment — reskilling time, redeployment intent, notice/severance clarity. Vague reassurance ("don't worry") does not meet a safety need; predictability does. If layoffs *are* coming, honesty about scope beats a false all-clear, which destroys trust and deepens the safety deficit.
2. **Belonging next.** Frame AI adoption as a team learning journey, not an individual survival contest. Paired learning, shared experiments, and blameless "what broke" sessions rebuild cohesion and stop the internal every-person-for-themselves dynamic.
3. **Esteem, reframed.** Position AI as *augmenting* proven expertise, not erasing it — the person's domain judgment is what makes the tool valuable. Recognize contributions in ways that reinforce competence rather than pit adopters against laggards.
4. **Self-actualization last, and only now.** Once the floor is stable, the frontier-work mission becomes genuinely motivating — this is where mastery, autonomy, and meaningful problems do their work. Note the ordering: the *same* mission that fell flat in Step 1 can succeed here, unchanged, because the need beneath it is no longer screaming.

Supplement, per the skill's guidance: for high-stakes design, layer in Self-Determination Theory. Safety is the Maslow layer; SDT then explains the higher-level engine — autonomy (let people choose where to apply AI), competence (visible mastery gains), relatedness (learning together). Maslow diagnoses *why the mission failed*; SDT designs *what makes it succeed*.

## Step 6 — Verify by direct conversation

The skill insists that outside-observer needs assessment is unreliable — so test the diagnosis against the people, not the model.

- Ask directly (survey and 1:1s): "When you hear 'AI-first,' what's your honest first reaction?" If the dominant answer is about job security, the safety diagnosis holds. If people are already excited and the blocker is, say, tooling friction, the diagnosis was wrong and the intervention should change.
- Compare answers to the framework's prediction; update if contradicted. Some individuals will genuinely be in self-actualization mode about AI (the framework predicts variance across people — founders and some senior ICs often invert the hierarchy). Do not force one intervention on everyone.
- Schedule a re-check (e.g., 60–90 days after the safety commitments land) to confirm engagement with the mission actually rose.

## Output: Needs-Hierarchy Diagnosis

`Needs Diagnosis: AI-first knowledge team | Situation: want enthusiastic AI adoption + frontier work / incentive = mission + training budget + tool access + praise / response = disengagement, layoff fear, quiet job-search | Need-state: Physiological met · Safety UNMET (job-security fear) · Belonging partial-eroding · Esteem threatened · Self-actualization = incentive target | Incentive target level: self-actualization/esteem | Mismatch: YES, incentive-too-high (targets top while safety unmet) | Intervention: concrete job-security/transition clarity (safety) → team learning journey (belonging) → augment-not-replace reframe (esteem) → re-offer frontier mission (self-actualization), supplement with SDT | Owner: exec sponsor + managers | Verify: honest-reaction survey + 1:1s, re-check 60–90 days`

---

The timeless point, made current: a bigger bonus, a better tool, or a more inspiring mission cannot motivate a person who believes the same initiative may cost them their livelihood. In the 2024–2026 AI transition, the leaders who got engagement first stabilized the safety floor — honestly — and only then asked their teams to reach for the frontier.
