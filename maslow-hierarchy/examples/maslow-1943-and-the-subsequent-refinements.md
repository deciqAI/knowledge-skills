# Method in Action: Maslow 1943 and the Subsequent Refinements

> *Example for the [maslow-hierarchy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Maslow's 1943 paper was the founding statement of humanistic psychology — a third movement positioning itself between behaviorism (which Maslow viewed as too reductive about human motivation) and psychoanalysis (which Maslow viewed as too pathology-focused). Maslow's project was to develop a psychology of healthy, thriving people — not a psychology of mental illness.

The 1943 paper presented the five-level hierarchy in its now-canonical form. Maslow's framing of the prepotency principle:

> "If all the needs are unsatisfied, and the organism is then dominated by the physiological needs, all other needs may become simply nonexistent or be pushed into the background. ... When there is plenty of bread and when his belly is chronically filled, at once other (and higher) needs emerge and these, rather than physiological hungers, dominate the organism."
>
> — Maslow (1943), p. 374.

And on self-actualization:

> "This need we may call self-actualization. ... It refers to the desire for self-fulfillment, namely, to the tendency for him to become actualized in what he is potentially. This tendency might be phrased as the desire to become more and more what one is, to become everything that one is capable of becoming."
>
> — Maslow (1943), p. 382.

The framework was developed in Maslow's 1954 book *Motivation and Personality*. The book added detail to each level and articulated the methodology Maslow used to identify "self-actualizing" individuals — biographical study of historical figures Maslow judged as having reached high levels of personal development.

In subsequent editions and his later book *Toward a Psychology of Being* (1962, 1968), Maslow softened the strict hierarchy claim and emphasized that real motivation is usually multi-determined.

The empirical literature on the framework:

**Wahba, M. A., & Bridwell, L. G. (1976).** "Maslow reconsidered: A review of research on the need hierarchy theory." *Organizational Behavior and Human Performance*, 15(2), 212-240. The canonical meta-analysis. Reviewed 10+ years of empirical studies attempting to test the prepotency claim. Found:

> "The empirical evidence we reviewed does not support Maslow's strong proposition that needs are arranged in a strict hierarchy of prepotency, with lower-level needs requiring satisfaction before higher-level needs become operative. We did find empirical support for the weaker proposition that severely deprived needs receive disproportionate motivational attention; this is consistent with deprivation-domination logic but does not require the full Maslow hierarchy. We recommend that researchers and practitioners treat the hierarchy as a useful organizing heuristic rather than as a tested theory."
>
> — Wahba & Bridwell (1976), pp. 230-231.

**Tay, L., & Diener, E. (2011).** "Needs and subjective well-being around the world." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 101(2), 354-365. The most-comprehensive cross-cultural test, surveying 60,865 people across 123 countries. Found that the *content* of Maslow's needs was empirically supported (people do report seeking physiological, safety, belonging, esteem, and self-actualization needs), but the *hierarchy* was not (people pursue higher-level needs even when lower-level needs are unmet, and the hierarchy varies across cultures).

**Kenrick, D. T., Griskevicius, V., Neuberg, S. L., & Schaller, M. (2010).** "Renovating the pyramid of needs: Contemporary extensions built upon ancient foundations." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 5(3), 292-314. A modern reformulation grounded in evolutionary psychology. Replaces self-actualization with parenting and mate-related goals; retains lower levels. Important academic alternative.

**Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2000).** "The 'what' and 'why' of goal pursuits: Human needs and the self-determination of behavior." *Psychological Inquiry*, 11(4), 227-268. Self-Determination Theory (SDT), the modern empirically-grounded alternative. Identifies three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness. SDT has far stronger empirical support than Maslow's hierarchy and is the framework serious motivation researchers use today.

**Pink, D. H. (2009).** *Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us.* Riverhead Books. Popular synthesis of SDT and related research. Argues, with empirical support, that for cognitive work in the modern economy, autonomy, mastery, and purpose are more important than extrinsic rewards — a finding Maslow's framework anticipated but did not prove.

The framework has had massive operational influence despite its empirical limitations:

**Management and organizational behavior.** Maslow's hierarchy appears in essentially every introductory management textbook. Even as serious motivation research has moved to SDT and JD-R, Maslow remains the most-recognized framework among practitioners. The simplification — "people need basics before higher-level motivation works" — captures most of what practitioners need without the empirical detail.

**Product marketing.** Marketers routinely position products against Maslow levels: insurance and security products at safety; social products at belonging; luxury and status products at esteem; education and self-improvement at self-actualization. The framework gives a quick taxonomy of "what need does this product address?" — useful even when imprecise.

**Healthcare.** Patient-care frameworks (especially in palliative and chronic-illness care) often use Maslow-style needs assessment. The framework underlies the recognition that addressing pain (physiological) is prerequisite to addressing existential meaning (self-actualization).

**Education.** Educational psychology has used the hierarchy to argue for ensuring students' basic needs (safety, belonging) before high-stakes academic engagement. Trauma-informed teaching practices are essentially Maslow-applied.

**Crisis response and disaster recovery.** Humanitarian response frameworks (Sphere Standards, WHO Mental Health and Psychosocial Support) explicitly use Maslow-style sequencing: meet physiological and safety needs first, then address community / belonging needs, then individual psychological needs.

**Startup and team management.** The framework is widely used in early-stage company management: ensure the team's basic needs (compensation, basic safety, predictability) before expecting high engagement with the mission. The startup-specific point: founders often *invert* the hierarchy, pursuing self-actualization while accepting low pay and instability, but this is unusual and not generalizable.

Three operational lessons from Maslow and the subsequent research:

**First, lower-level deficiency dominance is real and operationally important.** A team member whose family is in financial distress will not be motivated by a stretch goal. A customer who is food-insecure will not buy luxury products. A community in physical danger will not engage with civic projects. The strict hierarchy is wrong; the *deficiency-dominance* principle is right.

**Second, higher-level needs are real and persistent.** Money and security alone do not produce engagement, meaning, or excellence. Maslow's contribution was to legitimize the study of higher-level needs at a time when behaviorist psychology dismissed them as epiphenomena. Even with empirical limitations, this contribution is foundational.

**Third, use Maslow as a diagnostic heuristic, not a strict theory.** For high-stakes motivation design, supplement with Self-Determination Theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness) and direct conversation with the people being motivated. The framework's value is in asking "what level of need is operative?" — not in dictating that the answer must come from a strict five-level taxonomy.
