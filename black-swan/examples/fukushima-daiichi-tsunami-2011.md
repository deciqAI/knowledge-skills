# Method in Action: Fukushima Daiichi and the Tsunami Design Basis (2011)

> *Example for the [black-swan](../SKILL.md) skill.*

On March 11, 2011, the magnitude-9.0 Tōhoku earthquake sent a tsunami roughly 13–15 meters high into the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear plant. The plant's design-basis tsunami was about 5.7 meters. Seawater flooded the basements housing the emergency diesel generators, causing a total station blackout, loss of reactor cooling, and meltdowns in three units.

TEPCO and regulators initially described the wave as *soteigai* — "beyond expectation." The skill's five steps show why that label was wrong.

**Step 1 — Classify the domain.** Tsunami run-up heights on the Japanese Pacific coast are Extremistan: the largest event in the record dominates the damage total of all others combined. The historical record itself carried the evidence — geological studies of the 869 Jōgan tsunami (Minoura et al., 2001) showed that waves far exceeding the design basis had inundated the same coastline and recurred on a roughly millennial cycle. A domain whose record contains a dominating outlier cannot be modeled from its recent average.

**Step 2 — Identify hidden Mediocristan assumptions.** The 5.7-meter design basis was anchored to the largest tsunami of the recent instrumental record (the 1960 Chile event as observed locally), treating the plant's operational history as if it sampled the full distribution. "No tsunami of that size has struck here since the plant opened" functioned exactly like the turkey's 1,000 days of feeding.

In 2008, TEPCO's own engineers ran a trial calculation indicating waves exceeding 15 meters were possible at the site. The result was set aside as unrealistic rather than acted upon — a documented case of dismissing tail evidence because it fell outside the assumed distribution.

**Step 3 — Design for tail survival (the step that failed).** Tail survival does not require predicting the wave's date or height; it requires that no single event breaks the system. Fukushima Daiichi violated every option on the menu:

- **Bounded downside:** absent — flooding caused meltdown, not just outage.
- **Redundancy:** nominal only — multiple backup generators shared one common-cause vulnerability, sitting in floodable basements behind the same inadequate seawall.
- **Skin in the game:** absent — those who shelved the 2008 calculation did not bear the tail consequences.

By contrast, the nearby Onagawa plant, sited on higher ground with a larger design margin, took the same tsunami and shut down safely. Same black swan, different design — the loss was not in the event but in the exposure.

**Step 4 — Defend against the narrative fallacy — in reverse.** The post-hoc story here was not "it was obvious all along" but its mirror image: "no one could have foreseen it." The actual epistemic state the day before contradicts this — the Jōgan evidence was published, the 2008 internal estimate existed, and regulators had discussed tsunami-margin upgrades. The National Diet of Japan's independent investigation commission (2012), chaired by Kiyoshi Kurokawa, concluded the accident was a profoundly manmade disaster that could and should have been foreseen and prevented.

**Step 5 — Distinguish black swans from grey swans.** This is the case's central lesson. A tsunami exceeding the seawall at a plant on a subduction-zone coast — with published geological precedent and an in-house calculation predicting it — is a **grey swan**: rare but foreseeable. Calling it a black swan after the fact was a category error that shifted a planning failure into the "unpredictable" column. Grey swan failure = planning failure; the label determines who is accountable and what gets redesigned.

The mapped steps:
1. Classify the domain: coastal tsunami magnitudes are Extremistan — the 869 Jōgan event dominated the historical record.
2. Hidden Mediocristan assumptions: design basis anchored to the recent instrumental maximum; operational history treated as the full distribution.
3. Tail-survival design: absent — unbounded downside, backups with a common-cause flooding vulnerability, no skin in the game for those dismissing the 2008 estimate; Onagawa's larger margin survived the same wave.
4. Narrative-fallacy check: "beyond expectation" claims versus an actual epistemic state that included published paleotsunami evidence and TEPCO's own >15 m calculation.
5. Grey vs. black swan: foreseeable and preventable per the Diet commission — a grey swan mislabeled black to excuse a planning failure.
