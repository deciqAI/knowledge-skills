# Method in Action: Concentration Risk in the AI Trade (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [black-swan](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By late 2024 and into 2025, a handful of AI-linked names — Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta and a few others — had come to dominate the S&P 500 and the returns of most large-cap equity funds. Nvidia in particular rode the data-center GPU cycle to become, in 2024, one of the most valuable companies in the world, first crossing the $1 trillion market-cap threshold in mid-2023 and reaching multi-trillion territory in 2024. The prevailing narrative was simple and one-directional: **"AI demand can only go up."** Hyperscalers were guiding capital expenditure ever higher to build out AI compute, and each capex increase was read by markets as bullish.

The black-swan skill does not predict a crash. It audits whether the position survives if the one-directional narrative fails.

**Step 1 — Classify the domain (Extremistan vs Mediocristan).** Equity index returns and technology-adoption cycles are Extremistan. A single name (Nvidia) and a single theme (AI capex) accounted for a large share of index gains in 2023–2025 — the largest observation dominating the sum. Technology adoption itself is fat-tailed: it does not glide along a bell curve but moves in S-curves, regime shifts, and abrupt sentiment reversals. A portfolio whose returns are dominated by one correlated cluster is, by the skill's own test, squarely in Extremistan.

**Step 2 — Identify hidden Mediocristan assumptions.** The bullish case embedded several assumptions that quietly treat a fat-tailed system as a normal one:

- *"AI demand can only go up"* extrapolates a young trend as if the next several years were an average draw from a stable distribution — the turkey's 1,000 days of feeding.
- *"Capex spend is guaranteed future revenue"* assumes the revenue thesis that justifies hundreds of billions in AI infrastructure will materialize on schedule. The spend is contractual and near-certain; the return on it is not.
- *"These are diversified holdings"* treats the mega-cap cluster as if the positions were independent, when Nvidia, the hyperscalers that are its largest customers, and the index they dominate are all exposed to the *same* underlying bet on AI-capex payoff.

The DeepSeek episode of January 2025 was a live demonstration. When the Chinese lab DeepSeek released a model reported to be trained far more cheaply than assumed, markets briefly repriced the "compute demand only rises" premise, and AI-linked equities — Nvidia most sharply — sold off hard in a single session before partially recovering. One news event moved a supposedly-diversified index because the underlying risks were the same risk.

**Step 3 — Design for tail survival (not tail prediction).** The goal is not to forecast when or whether the AI thesis disappoints. It is to ensure no single repricing breaks the portfolio:

- **Bounded downside:** cap exposure to the single correlated AI-capex bet so that a severe drawdown in it is survivable, not ruinous. Passive index ownership is not neutral here — it is a large, concentrated, active bet on the same names.
- **True redundancy:** diversify across *uncorrelated* risks, not merely across many tickers that all move on AI sentiment. Holding Nvidia plus its own biggest customers is one bet wearing several names.
- **Optionality / barbell:** a stable base plus small, convex positions — including hedges that pay off precisely in the scenario the consensus rates near-zero.
- **Skin in the game:** be wary of allocators whose incentives reward riding the momentum and who do not personally bear the tail.

**Step 4 — Defend against the narrative fallacy.** Both directions of the story are traps. Today's forward-looking trap is "AI demand can only go up." The future backward-looking trap — if the thesis does disappoint — will be "of course the AI bubble was obvious." Neither is a claim about predictability. The honest question is the skill's own: *what is the epistemic state of well-informed people right now?* As of early 2026 the outcome is genuinely uncertain — the capex could be repaid handsomely, or the revenue could lag the spend for years. That two-sided uncertainty is exactly the condition the skill says to design for, rather than resolve by narrative.

**Step 5 — Distinguish black swans from grey swans.** A sharp de-rating of a historically concentrated, single-theme equity market is not an unforeseeable black swan — concentration risk and fat-tailed reversals are well documented (the dot-com concentration of 2000, the Nifty Fifty of the early 1970s). This is a **grey swan**: rare, but structurally foreseeable given the concentration. Treating a future AI-trade drawdown as an unpredictable black swan would be the same category error made after Fukushima — using the "unforeseeable" label to excuse a foreseeable planning failure. The design discipline is to bound the downside of the concentrated bet *now*, while the narrative is still one-directional.

The mapped steps:
1. Classify the domain: index and tech-adoption returns are Extremistan — one name and one theme dominated 2023–2025 gains.
2. Hidden Mediocristan assumptions: "demand only goes up," "capex is guaranteed revenue," and "these holdings are diversified" all treat a correlated fat-tailed bet as a normal, independent one.
3. Tail-survival design: bound exposure to the single AI-capex bet, diversify across genuinely uncorrelated risks, hold convex hedges, watch incentives — survive a repricing rather than predict it.
4. Narrative-fallacy check: "AI demand can only go up" now, "the bubble was obvious" later — both are hindsight-shaped stories, not statements of predictability; the real state is two-sided uncertainty.
5. Grey vs. black swan: concentration-driven drawdowns are foreseeable (2000 dot-com, Nifty Fifty) — a grey swan; mislabeling it black afterward would excuse a planning failure.
