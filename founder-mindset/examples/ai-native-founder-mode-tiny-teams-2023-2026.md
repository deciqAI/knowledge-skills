# Method in Action: AI-Native "Founder Mode" and the Tiny High-Agency Team (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [founder-mindset](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example applied to the 2023–2026 AI-startup wave, when a handful of very small, high-agency teams reached revenue scales that historically required hundreds of employees. This case tests whether the timeless Founder-Mode Audit still holds when capital is abundant and headcount is cheap to add — precisely the conditions under which comfort-seeking and premature scaling are hardest to resist.

The backdrop: after ChatGPT's launch in **November 2022**, venture capital poured into AI startups. A recurring pattern emerged — companies reaching large annualized revenue with remarkably few people. **Midjourney**, the image-generation tool, was widely reported to be generating substantial revenue with a team of roughly a few dozen and no outside venture funding. **Anysphere**, maker of the AI code editor **Cursor**, was widely reported to have crossed roughly $100M in annualized revenue within about two years of launch with a small team, and raised large financing rounds in 2024–2025 at rapidly escalating valuations. The phrase "**founder mode**" itself was popularized by Paul Graham's essay of that title in **September 2024**, drawing on remarks by Airbnb's Brian Chesky about running a company by staying deep in the details rather than delegating to a layer of professional managers.

The tension: cheap capital and the promise of AI leverage make it *easy* to scale headcount and burn — which is exactly when Default-Alive discipline and "do things that don't scale" get abandoned. Walk the Founder-Mode Audit on a representative AI-native seed/Series-A founder in this environment (illustrative, applying the pattern the essays and reporting describe):

1. **Default Alive vs Default Dead (Step 1).** The founder raised a large round at a high valuation during the AI funding surge and *feels* Default Alive because the bank balance is big. Do the math instead of trusting the balance: cash $12M, net burn $600K/month and rising as the team doubles, so ~20 months of runway *at current burn* but far less as burn grows. Revenue is real but small. **The correct classification is "Default Dead unless one of {revenue ramps, next round closes} happens on time"** — and the January 2025 market reaction to **DeepSeek**'s low-cost model (which triggered a sharp one-day selloff in AI-exposed stocks including Nvidia) is a live reminder that the funding window and cost assumptions can shift abruptly. Founders raising in a hot market systematically misclassify a fragile Default-Dead position as Default Alive.

2. **Name the hard thing avoided (Step 2).** The painful action deferred for weeks: cutting a costly foundation-model provider contract and re-architecting onto a cheaper model — unglamorous, and it risks a short-term quality dip. It has been on the list because it is uncomfortable, not because it is unimportant. *Schedule it within 7 days.*

3. **What is being scaled prematurely (Step 3).** The founder is hiring aggressively — the market says "AI teams should be tiny," yet the plan is to triple headcount this year "to keep up." **The AI-native tiny-team pattern (Midjourney, Cursor) is evidence the opposite is often correct**: prove the product and the unit economics with a small team before adding people. Revert: freeze non-essential hiring until per-seat gross margin and retention are proven.

4. **What is not being scaled — the unscalable thing (Step 4).** The founder has handed user feedback to a support queue. In an AI product where output quality is the entire value, the founder should be personally reading failed generations and hard user sessions. *Restart founder-led review of real usage* — a fixed block each week watching actual users and reading the worst outputs, the way high-agency AI founders describe dogfooding their own tool relentlessly.

5. **Stress / pattern check (Step 5).** Pattern present: comfort-seeking on optimization — the founder is spending time tuning internal tooling and prompt-eval dashboards (feels productive, carries no customer risk) instead of the model-cost renegotiation and the retention problem. Name it: over-optimizing internal process is manager-mode creep.

6. **ONE action for the next 7 days (Step 6).** Not three. Renegotiate/re-architect the model-provider cost (Step 2) — because in an AI-native business, cost-of-goods on inference is the single lever that most changes the Default-Alive answer, and it is the thing being avoided.

7. **Re-audit weekly (Step 7).** Every Monday. In a fast-moving funding and model-price environment, quarterly is far too slow; the Default-Alive answer can flip in a single news cycle.

The through-line: the 2023–2026 AI gold rush did not repeal founder mindset — it raised the stakes on it. Abundant capital makes premature scaling *feel* safe; leverage from AI tools makes tiny high-agency teams genuinely possible; and volatile model economics make the Default-Alive question something to re-run constantly rather than annually. The tiny teams that reached large revenue are not evidence that discipline stopped mattering — they are the clearest demonstration of what "do things that don't scale" and "default alive or default dead" look like when executed under modern conditions.
