# Method in Action: Paul Graham's Essays and the YC Portfolio (2005–2024)

> *Example for the [founder-mindset](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example. Not Silicon Valley folklore — Graham's essays are publicly published primary sources, and YC portfolio data is publicly disclosed.

In **March 2005**, **Paul Graham** and three co-founders launched **Y Combinator** with $200,000 of personal capital, an explicit thesis that the bottleneck in early-stage company formation was *founder cognitive frameworks*, not capital or technology. Over the following decade, Graham wrote a continuing series of essays — each addressing a *specific* operational/cognitive failure mode he and his partners observed in YC founders. The essays became the most widely-read primary literature on early-stage company operations and were later collected on paulgraham.com. The essays most central to founder mindset (with dates):

- **"How to Start a Startup"** (March 2005) — the founding manifesto. http://paulgraham.com/start.html
- **"The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups"** (October 2006) — a Pareto of failure modes. http://paulgraham.com/startupmistakes.html
- **"Schlep Blindness"** (January 2012) — the cognitive failure of avoiding hard problems. http://paulgraham.com/schlep.html
- **"Do Things That Don't Scale"** (July 2013) — the most counter-intuitive operational discipline. http://paulgraham.com/ds.html
- **"Default Alive or Default Dead?"** (October 2015) — the load-bearing financial check. http://paulgraham.com/aord.html

The empirical case: by **2024**, YC had funded over **4,000 companies**, with combined enterprise value reportedly exceeding **$600 billion** (publicly disclosed in YC's portfolio listings and the press releases of major exits like Airbnb, Stripe, DoorDash, Reddit). The portfolio includes 5+ companies that reached $10B+ valuations, dozens at $1B+, and a much larger long tail. **The cognitive frameworks Graham articulated are the most consistent thing across the founders who succeeded**, per the YC partners' published reflections.

Walk the Founder-Mode Audit on a hypothetical Series A SaaS founder (illustrative, drawing on the patterns described in Graham's essays):

- **Default Alive check (Step 1):** Cash $4M. Net burn $250K/month (so 16 months runway). Revenue growth 8% MoM. *Calculate*: at 8% MoM compounding, monthly revenue in 16 months = current × 1.08^16 ≈ 3.4× current. If current MRR is $100K, in 16 months MRR is ~$340K, annualized $4.1M. If gross margins are 75%, cash contribution from rev ≈ $3.1M annual. With $250K/mo expenses growing at maybe 15% MoM (from hiring), expense run-rate in 16 months ≈ $2.3M/mo. **Default Dead**. The founder *felt* fine because revenue is growing, but the *expense growth rate exceeds revenue growth rate*. This is Graham's exact diagnostic case.
- **The hard thing avoided (Step 2):** Founder has been avoiding firing the head of engineering who is technically strong but slowing down decisions and demoralizing the team. On the list 7 weeks. *Schedule the action within 7 days.*
- **What is being scaled prematurely (Step 3):** Just hired a VP of Marketing to "scale acquisition" — but the founder has not yet found a paid-acquisition channel that works at a positive LTV/CAC ratio. **Reverse:** pause the VP-Marketing hire's program scaling, have them spend the first 60 days finding *one* channel that works manually before scaling any of them.
- **What is not being scaled (Step 4):** Founder has been delegating customer conversations to AEs for 6 months. *Restart founder-led customer interviews* — 5 calls per week, 30 minutes each.
- **Stress-pattern check (Step 5):** Pattern present: over-meeting (founder calendar is 80% internal meetings; only 5% customer-facing). *Cut internal meetings by 50%; redirect time to customers.*
- **ONE action for next 7 days (Step 6):** Have the difficult conversation with head of engineering. (Default Dead is a 6-month problem; firing is a 1-week problem; doing the harder one first is the founder move.)
- **Re-audit:** Monday next week.

The pattern Graham documents repeatedly: **founders who do well in YC are not smarter or better-funded than the median; they are more willing to do the specific things their cognitive comfort wants them to defer**. The Default Alive math, the hard conversation, the customer call, the unscalable manual process — each of these is uncomfortable. Each is the founder move. The framework's payoff is *making the uncomfortable thing the explicit ritual* rather than something the founder gradually drifts away from.
