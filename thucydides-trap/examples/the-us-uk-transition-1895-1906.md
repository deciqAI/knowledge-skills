# Method in Action: The US-UK Transition (1895–1906)

> *Example for the [thucydides-trap](../SKILL.md) skill.*

One of Allison's four peaceful cases — the only peaceful transfer of hegemonic leadership between great powers in the modern era. Primary sources: Allison, *Destined for War* (2017), ch. 4; and the documentary record of Anglo-American diplomacy in the period.

**Step 1 — Structural roles:** By the 1890s, the United States had surpassed Britain in industrial output and was asserting hemispheric authority under the Monroe Doctrine. Britain was the established global naval and commercial hegemon. The displacement threat covered naval, economic, and hemispheric-authority domains.

**Step 2 — Fear response in Britain:** The British fear response was activated but contained. Britain was simultaneously managing the German naval buildup (a much more immediate threat to its home waters), the Boer War, and resource constraints. The domestic political economy of maintaining global naval supremacy while facing multiple challengers was unsustainable.

**Step 3 — Structural accelerants:** The 1895 Venezuela Crisis (US Secretary of State Olney's assertion of US arbitration rights under the Monroe Doctrine) came close to triggering armed confrontation. The crisis was the tripwire moment.

**Step 4 — Escape paths:** Britain chose *accommodation*: in 1901, the Hay-Pauncefote Treaty gave the US unilateral right to build and control the Panama Canal, ceding a core strategic asset. In 1906, Britain withdrew its Caribbean naval squadron entirely — conceding hemispheric dominance to the US. These were costly, credible concessions. In parallel, *reframing* operated: British and American diplomats actively cultivated the "Anglo-Saxon" and "Special Relationship" narrative, framing US rise as an extension of British-originated civilization rather than its replacement.

**Step 5 — Outcome:** The US and UK avoided war. Britain retained its European and Asian naval position, gained a de facto ally against Germany, and was compensated for hemispheric concessions by the transatlantic alliance that proved decisive in both World Wars.

**Decision-relevant implication:** The peaceful outcome required Britain to make concessions that were domestically costly (withdrawal from the Western Hemisphere was not popular) and strategically counterintuitive. The accommodation came from the ruling power, not the rising power. No individual leader "solved" the Trap — it was resolved by structural adjustment over a decade.

**Source:** Allison, Graham. *Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides's Trap?* Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2017. Ch. 4, "From the Industrial Revolution to World War I," which documents the US-UK case in detail.
