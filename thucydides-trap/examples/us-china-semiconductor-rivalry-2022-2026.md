# Method in Action: US-China Semiconductor Rivalry (2022–2026)

> *Example for the [thucydides-trap](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A live, contemporary application of the Trap to the domain that most concentrates great-power competition today: advanced semiconductors and AI compute. This is not a settled historical case — it is an ongoing dynamic, which makes it useful for stress-testing the framework in real time rather than in hindsight.

**Step 1 — Structural roles:** The **ruling power** in advanced compute is the United States and its allied ecosystem — US firms designing the leading AI accelerators (Nvidia), the US-controlled EDA and chip-design toolchain, the Netherlands' ASML for EUV lithography, and Taiwan's TSMC for leading-edge fabrication. The **rising power** is China, pursuing semiconductor self-sufficiency across design, fabrication, and equipment. The displacement threat is primarily **technological and economic**, with a clear **military** overhang because advanced AI compute is dual-use. Domain: leading-edge logic chips, high-bandwidth memory, and the AI-training compute that depends on them.

**Step 2 — Fear response in the ruling power:** The US fear response is intense and has hardened over the case window. In **October 2022**, the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) imposed sweeping export controls restricting sales of advanced AI chips and chipmaking equipment to China, and restricting US-person support for certain Chinese fabs. These were tightened in **October 2023** and again in **December 2024**, expanding controls to more chipmaking tools and high-bandwidth memory and adding many Chinese entities to the Entity List. Observable signals: bipartisan legislation (the **2022 CHIPS and Science Act**, providing tens of billions in domestic-fab subsidies), coordinated pressure on the Netherlands and Japan to align their own export rules, and repeated public framing of Chinese AI progress as a national-security threat. Fear intensity: **High.**

**Step 3 — Structural accelerants:** (a) **Taiwan as the physical tripwire** — TSMC fabricates the large majority of the world's leading-edge chips on an island both powers treat as a core interest, so any Taiwan Strait incident is simultaneously a military and a supply-chain catastrophe. (b) **Domestic constraints that make de-escalation costly** — in the US, appearing "soft on China" is politically hazardous across both parties; in China, self-sufficiency is a stated leadership priority, so retreat is equally hard. (c) **The measurement shock of January 2025** — Chinese startup DeepSeek released a competitive open-weight reasoning model reportedly trained far more cheaply than assumed, and the market reaction wiped a large amount of value off Nvidia and other AI names in a single session (Nvidia's ~17% single-day drop on Jan 27, 2025 was widely reported as among the largest one-day market-cap losses for a single US company). This intensified the US fear that controls were failing to preserve a durable lead.

**Step 4 — Map the four escape paths:**
- **(a) Accommodation** — *availability: Low.* Costly, credible US concessions (rolling back controls, permitting leading-edge tool sales) run directly against the stated security rationale and would be domestically near-impossible. This is the escape path Allison's peaceful cases relied on, and it is the least available here.
- **(b) Deterrence + communication** — *availability: Partial.* Channels exist (leader summits, restored military-to-military contacts, working groups) and the two sides have at times paired restriction with dialogue. But controls-plus-talks is deterrence without accommodation, which historically is the more dangerous configuration.
- **(c) Institution-building** — *availability: Partial but eroding.* WTO mechanisms and export-control coordination (with the Netherlands and Japan) embed competition in rules, but these are increasingly used to *tighten* the contest rather than bound it.
- **(d) Reframing** — *availability: Low-to-partial.* A positive-sum framing ("both benefit from cheaper global compute") is technically coherent but politically unavailable while the dominant frame on both sides is zero-sum security.

**Step 5 — Current trajectory and decision-relevant implications:** Structural escalation probability in the technology domain is **High**; probability of *kinetic* great-power war remains lower and is gated almost entirely by the **Taiwan tripwire**. Key trigger points: a Taiwan Strait crisis; a further Chinese capability surprise (a DeepSeek-style event in chips or models) prompting a sharper US clampdown; or Chinese retaliation via critical-minerals export limits (China restricted gallium and germanium exports in 2023 and tightened rare-earth/critical-mineral controls in 2024–2025). For a business making an FDI or supply-chain decision, the framework says: do not model this as a temporary trade spat that reverts to the mean. Model it as a **structural, multi-year decoupling in the compute stack** with a low-probability but catastrophic Taiwan tail. Concretely: assume continued tightening rather than relaxation of controls, price in a bifurcated (US-aligned vs China-domestic) compute supply chain, and stress-test any single-fab or single-jurisdiction dependency against a Taiwan disruption.

**Decision-relevant implication:** The most-available escape path here is not accommodation (as it was for Britain in 1901–1906) but a disciplined **deterrence + communication** posture — and that is precisely the configuration Allison's history flags as unstable when unaccompanied by any real concession. The Trap's core warning applies: both governments may prefer to avoid war, yet domestic constraints and the Taiwan tripwire can make de-escalation rational-but-impossible.
