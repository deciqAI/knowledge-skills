# Method in Action: IDEO Shopping Cart Redesign (1999)

> *Example for the [lateral-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A primary-source-documented application. Not a parable — documented by ABC News *Nightline* (broadcast July 13, 1999) and subsequently analyzed in Tom Kelley's *The Art of Innovation* (Doubleday, 2001).

ABC News commissioned IDEO to redesign the supermarket shopping cart in five days, filming the process. The conventional improvement path (vertical thinking) was well-worn: lighter materials, larger capacity, smoother wheels, better folding. Decades of iteration had nearly exhausted that space.

IDEO's process applied lateral techniques across the five days:

**Step 2 — Assumption challenge:**
- "A cart must be a fixed-size container" → challenged → modular, task-specific form factors
- "Shopping is a solo activity" → challenged → parent-child dynamics as primary design driver
- "The cart holds products until checkout" → challenged → scanning-and-sorting as an integrated act

**Step 4 — Random stimulation (field observation as cross-domain input):**
Observing a children's hospital: nurses use modular push-carts with station organization systems. Forced connection: shopping could incorporate immediate categorization, not just accumulation.

**Step 5 — Concept extraction:**
From ATM banking: concept = "user retrieves exactly what they need at point of need." Applied to shopping: build-your-own basket configuration rather than one-size cart.

**Step 6 — PO:**
PO: "The shopping cart should not exist." Springboard: the cart's function is containment and transport — both could be separated. Executable direction: individual self-scanning baskets + fast-exit lane, removing the monolithic cart entirely.

The resulting prototype featured: detachable inner baskets (for easy bagging and theft prevention), child seat redesigned to face the parent (challenging the "child faces forward" convention), hook system for product organization, and integrated magnifier on the handle for elderly users reading small-print labels.

The five-day process became a design-education case study taught globally for 25+ years. IDEO's methodology — including the assumption-challenge and cross-domain observation techniques — directly influenced the Stanford d.school design thinking curriculum. Primary source: Kelley, Tom. *The Art of Innovation*. Doubleday, 2001, ch. 1.
