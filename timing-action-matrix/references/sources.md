# Sources — timing-action-matrix

> *Primary sources for the [timing-action-matrix](../SKILL.md) skill.*

1. **Niccolò Machiavelli, *The Prince*** (1532), Chapter 25. https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1232 — The foundational political science text on the dual requirement of virtù (action quality, skill, will) and fortuna (timing, circumstance, fortune). Machiavelli's diagnosis that most leaders fail either from excessive caution or from failing to read fortune (timing) maps directly to the matrix's right/wrong timing dimension. Verbatim: "I believe that it is better to be impetuous than cautious, for fortune is a woman and it is necessary, if you wish to master her, to conquer her by force... Fortune seems to govern the actions of those who cannot control them with prudence, directing her force where no resistance is prepared."

2. **George Soros, *The Alchemy of Finance*** (1987). https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Alchemy+of+Finance-p-9780471042211 — Primary source from one of the most successful investors of the 20th century explicitly separating the question of what to do from when to do it as two-step decision architecture. Verbatim: "I operate using a two-step decision-making process: in the first step I try to understand what is happening; in the second step I try to figure out how to act on that understanding... The key question is not what to do but WHEN to do it."

3. **Stagg, J.M. *Forecast for Overlord*** (Norton, 1971). https://archive.org/details/forecastforoverl0000stag — Primary source meteorologist's account of the D-Day timing decision; provides the technical record of the weather assessment that drove Eisenhower's 24-hour delay. Used for the Method in Action section.

4. **D'Este, C. *Decision in Normandy*** (HarperCollins, 1983) — Military history primary source on the Normandy invasion decision-making process; documents the timing deliberation and its outcome.

**What was NOT cited and why:** Sun Tzu's *Art of War* has abundant timing-related passages ("He who knows when he can fight and when he cannot will be victorious") but is not cited here because Machiavelli's explicit virtù/fortuna distinction maps more precisely to the 2×2 matrix structure. Malcolm Gladwell's popularization of "tipping points" is not cited because it does not provide the systematic two-variable independence that this skill requires.
