# Method in Action: D-Day Timing Decision (June 1944)

> *Example for the [timing-action-matrix](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The Allied invasion of Normandy on June 6, 1944 is a primary-source documented example of the timing-action matrix used under maximum stakes conditions.

**The action (assessed independently):** A cross-Channel amphibious assault on German-occupied Normandy had been the agreed Allied strategy since the Tehran Conference (November 1943). The action quality assessment was complete: over 150,000 troops trained and staged, 5,000 vessels assembled, 11,000 aircraft committed, German defenses mapped. By May 1944, the action was assessed as Right Action — the capability, the plan, and the strategic logic were validated.

**The timing decision:** The original planned date was June 5, 1944. By June 3, Chief Meteorologist James Stagg's team had identified that June 5 would produce Force 5 winds, heavy cloud cover, and 10–15 foot waves — conditions that would ground air support, make naval bombardment inaccurate, and swamp landing craft. The correct action in those conditions falls into the 抵制 quadrant: right action, wrong timing. The consequences would not be strategic resistance — they would be catastrophic casualties without air cover.

**The timing trigger:** Stagg's forecast identified a narrow weather window: June 6–7 would see a brief improvement — Force 3 winds, 3-foot seas, partial cloud cover. Imperfect timing conditions, but within the threshold for air support and naval fire. This shifted the quadrant assessment from 抵制 to 成功 — not perfect conditions, but within the range where the right action could succeed.

**Eisenhower's decision:** On June 5 at 04:15, Eisenhower said: "OK, we'll go." He postponed 24 hours from the original date. Same action, same troops, same plan — one timing variable changed. The matrix was live: all four quadrants were possible. Executing on June 5 would have placed the correct action in the 抵制 cell with catastrophic consequences.

**Post-event verification:** German High Command had assessed that the weather made an Allied landing impossible — their commanders were away from posts. This was a timing miscalculation on the German side: they assessed Allied timing based on their own timing preference criteria rather than Allied operational tolerance.

Sources: D'Este, C. *Decision in Normandy* (HarperCollins, 1983); Stagg, J.M. *Forecast for Overlord* (Norton, 1971). https://archive.org/details/forecastforoverl0000stag
