# Method in Action: Margin of Safety Against AI-Era Valuations (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [margin-of-safety](../SKILL.md) skill.*

From 2023 through the period covered here, a handful of AI-linked names — Nvidia most prominently, alongside the hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta) building out data-center capacity — drove an outsized share of U.S. equity-market gains. Nvidia's market capitalization crossed roughly $1 trillion in 2023, roughly $2 trillion in early 2024, and reached the multi-trillion-dollar range across 2024–2025, at times ranking among the most valuable companies in the world. Prices increasingly embedded an assumption that AI data-center capital expenditure would keep compounding at extraordinary rates.

Graham's discipline does not tell you whether to be bullish or bearish on AI. It tells you to separate **price** from a **conservatively estimated intrinsic value**, and to commit only where the gap gives you a buffer against being wrong. Here is the skill's process applied to a hypothetical position in a leading AI-infrastructure name in this period.

**1. Compute the point estimate honestly.**
Intrinsic value here rests on future free cash flow, which for an AI-infrastructure supplier depends almost entirely on one driver: how much AI data-center capex customers will sustain, and for how long, before it plateaus. A DCF is only as honest as this assumption. The disciplined move is to write down the *explicit* growth-and-margin path the current price requires — not "it's a great company," but "at this price the market is pricing sustained hyperscaler capex growth and durable gross margins for many years." Name the number the price implies before you decide whether you believe it.

**2. Quantify realistic uncertainty.**
The dominant uncertainty is capex durability, and it is not symmetric. Consider concrete adverse scenarios:
- **~20% adverse:** capex growth decelerates rather than compounds; buyers digest existing GPU inventory; pricing power softens as competition (custom silicon from the hyperscalers themselves, AMD, others) erodes margins.
- **~50% adverse:** a genuine capex air-pocket — a few of the largest customers, who account for a large share of demand, cut orders at once because AI monetization lags the buildout. Because demand is concentrated in a handful of buyers, the tail is fatter than a diversified-customer model would suggest.

The base rate is unkind to extrapolation: history's capital-buildout booms (railroads, telecom/fiber circa 1999–2001) show that even *correct* theses about a transformative technology routinely coexist with severe overbuild and multi-year drawdowns in the supplier stocks. Being right about AI and wrong about the timing/price are fully compatible.

**3. Choose the margin explicitly.**
Graham's equity discount is 30–50% below conservatively estimated intrinsic value. The higher the uncertainty and the more the price already extrapolates optimism, the closer to the 50% end you push. For a position whose entire value rests on a single, hard-to-forecast, concentrated demand driver, a large margin is warranted: buy only if the price sits well below a value estimate built on *conservative* (not consensus, not peak) capex assumptions. Adjusted commitment = conservative intrinsic value × (1 − margin%). In this regime that often means the disciplined answer is: no adequate margin exists at the current price, so do not commit.

**4. Refuse to commit beyond the margin.**
This is where the AI period is hardest, because the pressure is social. When a name has appreciated for consecutive quarters and every index and peer is riding it, FOMO — not analysis — drives buying. The discipline check is binary: will you walk away if the price does not offer the buffer? Buying because "it keeps going up" is momentum, the opposite of margin of safety. Graham's *Mr. Market* framing applies directly: an ebullient Mr. Market quoting ever-higher prices is offering you a chance to *not* transact, not an instruction to chase.

**5. Stress test.**
If capex growth disappoints by 30%, does the position survive without permanent impairment? If a 50% capex air-pocket hits and the multiple compresses at the same time (earnings *and* the price/earnings ratio fall together — the classic double-hit on richly valued growth names), does it survive? If either answer is "no," the margin is insufficient: increase it or don't commit. A margin large enough to survive the concentrated-demand tail is the whole point; a margin calibrated only to the mild scenario is not a margin at all.

---

**The generalizable lesson.** The margin-of-safety skill does not require a view on whether AI is transformative — it very plausibly is. It requires separating that (likely-true) thesis from the *price*, and refusing to pay a price that leaves no buffer for being wrong about *timing, competition, and margin durability*. Graham's own warning generalizes cleanly: the greatest losses come not from buying bad businesses but from buying good businesses at prices that already assume everything goes right. In an environment where valuations lean on optimistic capex extrapolation, the conservative estimate — and the discipline to demand a discount to it — is the entire edge.
