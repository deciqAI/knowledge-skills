# Sources — user-insight-engine

> *Primary sources for the [user-insight-engine](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Primary Source 1:**
Frederick Winslow Taylor, *The Principles of Scientific Management* (1911, Harper & Brothers). Full text available at: https://archive.org/details/principlesofscie00taylrich
> "The worker who comes to his task fresh, and who is willing to work hard, is surrounded by other workers who have carefully planned the maximum pace at which the work shall be done."
> — p. 19

Taylor's systematic documentation of the three-layer problem (Surface Layer self-report vs. Behavioral Layer capacity vs. Deep Layer social enforcement) is the earliest rigorous case of what the Engine formalizes. His identification of the Social Proof driver — group norm enforcement as the primary behavioral constraint — predates modern behavioral economics by 60 years and was confirmed through behavioral observation (time-and-motion studies) rather than interview or survey.

**Primary Source 2:**
Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk," *Econometrica*, Vol. 47, No. 2 (March 1979), pp. 263–292. https://www.jstor.org/stable/1914185
> "The value function is steeper for losses than for gains. A loss of X dollars is experienced as more aversive than a gain of X dollars is experienced as attractive."
> — p. 279

Kahneman and Tversky's empirical demonstration that loss aversion is a measurable, predictable feature of human decision-making — not a cognitive error but a structural aspect of how people evaluate options — provides the primary-source grounding for the Loss Aversion driver in the Engine. The original experimental data (choices between gambles of equivalent expected value) demonstrates that the asymmetry is not explained by risk aversion alone.

**What is NOT cited and why:** The three-layer framework (Surface/Behavioral/Deep) and the four-driver taxonomy (Loss Aversion, Social Proof, Cognitive Load, Trust Cost) are synthesized constructs developed for this skill. Loss Aversion derives from Kahneman-Tversky (cited above); Social Proof derives from Cialdini's *Influence* (1984); Cognitive Load derives from Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988); Trust Cost is a synthesis construct integrating trust theory (McKnight & Chervany 2002) with first-action abandonment research. None of the individual driver literatures maps to a single skill-level citation, and the four-driver combination is original to this skill.
