# Method in Action: Cost-Benefit Analysis of the Clean Air Act (1970)

> *Example for the [utilitarianism](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A primary-source-documented utilitarian welfare analysis. The US Environmental Protection Agency has conducted and published detailed cost-benefit analyses of the Clean Air Act since its passage, providing one of the most thoroughly documented public-policy utilitarian analyses available.

**Step 1 — Affected parties:**
- Primary beneficiaries: US population exposed to particulate matter, ozone, and NOx pollution; concentrated among urban residents, children, elderly, people with respiratory conditions.
- Primary cost-bearers: industrial and utility sector operators subject to emissions regulation; consumers of energy and manufactured goods (via price passthrough).
- Indirect affected: future generations (climate interaction), rural populations (acid rain), wildlife.

**Step 2 — Welfare impacts:**
The EPA's 2011 retrospective study (*The Benefits and Costs of the Clean Air Act from 1990 to 2020*) estimated: air quality improvements attributable to the Clean Air Act in 2010 prevented approximately 160,000 premature deaths, 130,000 heart attacks, and 13 million lost work days. Compliance costs to industry in 2010: approximately $53 billion annually. Health benefits (using EPA QALY-based valuation): approximately $30 trillion annually. Ratio: ~$30 in benefits per $1 in costs.

**Step 3 — Aggregation problem:**
The analysis requires converting lives and reduced suffering into dollar values (using EPA's Value of a Statistical Life = ~$7.4M in 2010 dollars). This conversion rate carries significant ethical weight — it implies that the life of an average American is worth $7.4M and that this value is comparable to compliance cost savings. The rate is derived from revealed-preference wage-risk studies (workers accepting wage premiums for higher-risk jobs), not from any intrinsic philosophical argument.

**Step 4 — Net welfare:**
At the stated assumptions, the Clean Air Act produced net positive welfare by an order of magnitude. Even if the VSL assumption is off by 50%, the benefits-to-costs ratio remains >15:1. The welfare-maximizing option (implement the Act) is robust to wide variation in the contested assumption.

**Step 5 — Most contested assumption:**
The VSL figure. Critics argue it undervalues elderly and minority populations (revealed-preference studies are drawn from working-age populations). If the relevant population's VSL is lower, the benefits estimate falls. However, the sensitivity analysis shows that even at 20% of the baseline VSL estimate, benefits exceed costs — the conclusion is robust.

**Source:** US Environmental Protection Agency. *The Benefits and Costs of the Clean Air Act from 1990 to 2020: Summary Report.* EPA-410/R-11-005, April 2011. Available at https://www.epa.gov/clean-air-act-overview/benefits-and-costs-clean-air-act
