# Method in Action: The U.S. Constitution's Design, 1787–Present

> *Example for the [change-and-constants](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The framers of the U.S. Constitution faced an explicit design challenge: how to create a governing document that would endure over time while remaining capable of adaptation. Their solution is arguably the most consequential application of the 变/不变 framework in recorded history.

**Identifying constants (不变):** The framers deliberately made certain principles extremely resistant to change. The Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments) was designed to be procedurally hard to alter — requiring a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers of Congress plus ratification by three-fourths of state legislatures. This difficulty was intentional: these were bedrock principles whose violation would cause irreversible damage to the system's legitimacy. The separation of powers, the federal structure, and individual rights protections were classified as constants.

**Designing the adaptation mechanism (变):** Article V of the Constitution provides the amendment process — the explicit mechanism for changing what must change. This is not an afterthought; it is a first-order design element. The framers recognized that specific laws, specific institutional arrangements, and specific rights interpretations would need to evolve with circumstances.

**Results documented in primary sources:** 27 amendments in 235 years. The Constitution remains the world's oldest active written national constitution. Countries that lacked this duality — treating everything as changeable (revolutionary constitutions that collapse) or nothing as changeable (rigid systems that shatter under pressure) — show dramatically worse outcomes.

**Primary source:** Madison, J., Hamilton, A., Jay, J. *The Federalist Papers* (1788), No. 49: "The reason assigned in the Federalist for the provision... is that it is a great desideratum in government to give it all the efficacy requisite to the attainment of the ends of its institution." https://avalon.law.yale.edu/18th_century/fed49.asp. Madison's argument explicitly addresses why certain things must be made hard to change (to prevent destabilization by temporary majorities) while others must remain adaptable.

The Constitution's design directly operationalizes Test A (bedrock principles protected) and Test B (adaptation mechanism explicitly built in) — not by accident, but by deliberate structural reasoning.
