# Method in Action: Cialdini, Vincent, Lewis, Catalan, Wheeler & Darby, 1975

> *Example for the [door-in-the-face](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The technique's primary experimental documentation is in **"Reciprocal Concessions Procedure for Inducing Compliance: The Door-in-the-Face Technique"** by Cialdini, Vincent, Lewis, Catalan, Wheeler & Darby, published in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* in February 1975 (Vol. 31, No. 2, pp. 206-215). The paper is the canonical empirical demonstration.

The experimental design was direct. The researchers approached college students on a university campus and asked them to do volunteer work. The asks were varied across three conditions:

**Condition 1 — Direct request:** *"Would you spend two hours chaperoning juvenile delinquents on a trip to the zoo?"*

**Condition 2 — DITF (rejection-then-moderation):** First ask: *"Would you spend two hours per week for two years as a counselor for juvenile delinquents?"* (Refused by ~100% of subjects.) Second ask: *"Would you spend two hours chaperoning juvenile delinquents on a trip to the zoo?"* (Same as Condition 1's only ask.)

**Condition 3 — Exposure control:** Subjects heard both requests but were asked to choose either, removing the rejection-then-retreat dynamic.

The results:

> "In the direct-request condition, 17% of subjects agreed to chaperone the delinquents on the zoo trip. In the rejection-moderation condition, 50% agreed — nearly three times the compliance rate."

— Cialdini, R. B., Vincent, J. E., Lewis, S. K., Catalan, J., Wheeler, D., & Darby, B. L. (1975). "Reciprocal Concessions Procedure for Inducing Compliance: The Door-in-the-Face Technique." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 31(2), p. 209.

The authors offered the reciprocal-concession explanation:

> "Subjects in the rejection-moderation condition apparently saw the experimenter's reduction in the size of the request as a concession on his part. Their compliance with the smaller request can be seen as a return concession, intended to reciprocate the experimenter's apparent move."

— Cialdini et al. (1975), p. 211.

The DITF finding has been replicated extensively across populations, requests, and cultures. Meta-analyses (Dillard et al. 1984; O'Keefe & Hale 1998) find consistent effect sizes — the technique produces roughly 2-3x compliance rates over direct requests, with the effect strongest when (a) the larger request is plausibly serious, not absurd; (b) the retreat is immediate and clearly framed as a concession; (c) the same person makes both requests (not different people in sequence).

Several operational lessons:

**First, the technique works on educated subjects who would intellectually reject the idea that two-stage requests should influence them differently.** Cialdini's 1975 subjects were university students — a population that, asked beforehand, would have predicted they could not be manipulated this way. The compliance rates tripled anyway.

**Second, the larger ask must be plausibly genuine.** When pilot studies used clearly-absurd opening requests, the DITF effect collapsed — subjects read the opening as bad faith and refused even the smaller request out of suspicion. The art of DITF is opening at the **upper bound of plausibility**, not beyond it.

**Third, the technique is reciprocity-based, not pity-based.** The retreat reads as a concession; compliance reads as a return concession. This is structurally why it composes with [`reciprocity`](../../reciprocity/SKILL.md) — DITF is one specific implementation of the reciprocity rule, focused on negotiation moves rather than gifts.

**Fourth, the most operationally useful defense is recognition.** Once the target recognizes the structure ("they opened with the large ask expecting refusal, then retreated to the actual ask"), the reciprocal-concession pull weakens substantially. Recognition is not perfect protection — even informed subjects show residual compliance lift — but it dramatically narrows the effect.

**Fifth, DITF is ethically defensible in some uses and not others.** A salesperson presenting a premium tier and then a standard tier is using DITF, but the customer can refuse both at any time and the transaction is voluntary. A negotiator opening with an indefensible position and retreating to a still-aggressive one to extract concessions is using DITF manipulatively. The ethics depends on whether the larger ask was genuine (or could have been accepted) and whether the target has full information about the alternatives.
