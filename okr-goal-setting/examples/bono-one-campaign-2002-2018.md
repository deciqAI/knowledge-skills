# Method in Action: Bono's ONE Campaign (2002–2018)

> *Example for the [okr-goal-setting](../SKILL.md) skill.*

OKRs are usually told as a corporate story. The ONE Campaign shows the framework working in the opposite domain: a nonprofit advocacy organization with no revenue, no product, and a mission — end extreme poverty and preventable disease — that could absorb infinite effort without ever being "done." In 2002, Bono co-founded DATA (Debt, AIDS, Trade, Africa) with fellow activists; it later folded into the ONE Campaign, an advocacy organization that lobbies governments rather than running programs. John Doerr, a board member, brought OKRs with him. Bono's account of what happened is documented in Doerr's *Measure What Matters* (2018).

The core problem was the activist's version of goal sprawl: passion generates causes faster than any organization can pursue them. Debt relief, AIDS treatment, trade rules, malaria, education — all worthy, all urgent. Without a forcing structure, an advocacy group campaigns on everything and moves nothing.

**Writing the Objective (Step 1).** ONE's objectives were qualitative and genuinely aspirational — for example, securing debt cancellation for the world's poorest countries, or getting AIDS treatment to Africans who could not afford it. Each passed the test "if achieved, would this objectively matter?" — the answer was measured in lives.

**Writing outcome KRs (Step 2).** The discipline that mattered was refusing activity metrics. For an advocacy group, the seductive KRs are activities: hold the concert, run the campaign, get the press coverage. ONE's key results were instead outcomes in the world it was trying to change: specific legislative and funding commitments from specific governments by specific dates. The 2005 Gleneagles G8 summit — where leaders committed to cancel the multilateral debt of the poorest countries and substantially increase aid to Africa — is the kind of external, verifiable result the campaigns were scored against. Concerts and coverage were tactics; the KR was whether the commitment materialized.

**Calibrating and cascading (Steps 3–4).** Objectives like "full debt cancellation" were deliberate stretch goals — low-probability asks aimed at heads of state. The small organization cascaded them directly: board-level objectives set the one or two fights for the period, and every campaign team's work had to trace to them. What OKRs forced ONE to do, in Bono's telling, was say no — to drop good causes that did not serve the chosen objectives.

**Scoring honestly (Steps 5–6).** The most consequential result came from the retrospective loop, not from a hit target. Reviewing what it was actually achieving, ONE confronted an uncomfortable finding: it was an organization campaigning *for* Africa that was not sufficiently *of* Africa — strategy set in Washington and London about a continent that wasn't in the room. Honest scoring turned that into a strategic pivot: ONE shifted from advocating for Africans to working with them, bringing African leaders into its board and staff and building its agenda with African partners. That is the framework functioning as designed — measurement changing what the organization chooses to do, not just recording it.

The broader results ONE and its predecessor counted among their era's wins — multilateral debt cancellation, the scale-up of AIDS funding through PEPFAR and the Global Fund, and the expansion of antiretroviral treatment from a wealthy-country privilege to millions of Africans — were collective achievements of many actors. But Doerr's and Bono's documented claim is narrower and defensible: OKRs gave a passion-driven organization focus, made its campaigns accountable to external outcomes rather than internal activity, and surfaced the strategic error that reshaped it.

The mapped steps:
1. Write the Objective: qualitative, aspirational, life-scale — debt cancellation, AIDS treatment access
2. Write 3-5 Key Results: external commitments secured (legislation, funding, debt cancelled), never activities (concerts, coverage)
3. Calibrate ambition: deliberate stretch asks to heads of state, low individual probability
4. Cascade and align: board objectives → campaign teams; OKRs used to say no to good-but-off-objective causes
5. Run the cycle: campaigns scored against whether commitments (e.g., Gleneagles 2005) materialized
6. Score honestly: retrospective exposed the "for Africa, not with Africa" gap → strategic pivot to African leadership and partnership

Primary source: Doerr, J. (2018). *Measure What Matters: How Google, Bono, and the Gates Foundation Rock the World with OKRs.* Portfolio. ISBN 978-0525536222. (Bono's ONE Campaign account.)
