# Method in Action: Holding AI Positions Through Drawdowns — The Disposition Effect in the 2023–2026 Market (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [loss-aversion-prospect-theory](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The AI-driven equity run of 2023–2026 produced a natural stress test for prospect theory. Nvidia became the market's clearest single-name proxy for the AI trade, and its price experienced several sharp, well-documented drawdowns even as its longer-run trajectory rose. On January 27, 2025, news that the Chinese lab DeepSeek had trained a competitive model (R1) at reportedly far lower cost triggered a broad sell-off in AI-exposed stocks; Nvidia fell sharply in a single session in what was widely reported as one of the largest one-day market-value declines for a single U.S. company on record. Retail and professional investors then faced a textbook loss-aversion decision: hold, add, or realize.

**The behavioral pattern:** The *disposition effect* — the tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long — is loss aversion applied to a portfolio. An investor's purchase price becomes a reference point. A position above it is coded as a gain (triggering risk-averse "lock it in" selling); a position below it is coded as a loss (triggering risk-seeking "hold until it comes back" behavior, because realizing the loss hurts roughly twice as much as the paper loss already does). Through 2023–2026, the same investors who trimmed AI winners early to "book the gain" often refused to trim or exit positions that had fallen below their entry, waiting to get back to breakeven rather than re-evaluating on forward merits.

The mapped steps of The Process:

1. **Specify decision:** An investor holds an AI position (say Nvidia) bought at reference price $P. After a shock like the January 2025 DeepSeek session, the position sits below $P. Options: (a) hold, (b) add, (c) sell. The implicit reference point is the personal purchase price $P — economically arbitrary, since the market does not know or care where you bought.

2. **Compute EV:** The correct EV input is the *forward* distribution of outcomes from today's price given today's information (demand for AI compute, competitive threats to margins, valuation), not the distance from $P. Two investors holding the identical share — one who bought at $P and one who bought far lower — face the identical forward EV, yet loss aversion makes them behave differently. That divergence is the tell.

3. **Identify distortions:**
   - *Reference dependence:* "getting back to what I paid" replaces "is this a good forward bet?"
   - *Loss aversion:* the ~2:1 pain of realizing a loss makes holding a deteriorated thesis feel safer than it is.
   - *Probability weighting:* a small-probability catastrophe (e.g., cheaper training collapsing GPU demand, or an AI-capex correction) can be over- or under-weighted depending on framing; the DeepSeek reaction looked like acute overweighting of a tail on the day, with much of the drop later retraced.
   - *Diminishing sensitivity:* once deep in the loss frame, investors become risk-seeking — "it's already down, might as well hold / double down" — the mirror of cautious profit-taking on winners.

4. **Reframe and re-test:** Ask the disposition-effect diagnostic: *"If I did not already own this share at this cost, would I buy it today at this price?"* This deletes the purchase-price reference point. If the answer is no yet you are holding only to avoid realizing the loss, prospect-theory distortion — not analysis — is driving the decision. The DeepSeek episode is a clean reframe test: the news changed the forward thesis for everyone identically, but individual reactions varied with each holder's entry price.

5. **Choose decision rule:** A liquid, repeatable, reversible position with non-catastrophic per-position stakes → maximize forward EV and use pre-committed rules (target weights, rebalancing bands, stop or trim thresholds) rather than the breakeven reference point. Reserve respect for loss aversion for genuinely catastrophic, irreversible stakes — which a single sized equity position, properly sized, is not.

6. **Document:** Decide on forward EV and position-sizing discipline; note explicitly that the purchase price was set aside and that any decision to hold reflects a forward thesis, not an aversion to recognizing a loss. The loss, if the thesis has broken, is already real — holding only chooses whether to recognize or compound it.

**Why this case matters for the skill:** It is the investing archetype in the Prospect Theory Patterns pack ("Disposition effect: sell winners early, hold losers"), made vivid by the most-watched stock of the era and a dated, widely-reported shock. The counter is exactly the pack's counter — *pre-committed exit rules* — because, as the PGA and cabdriver evidence show, the bias survives expertise and awareness. When a client says "I'll wait until it gets back to what I paid," that sentence is the red flag: the reference point is the purchase price, and the fix is to re-derive the decision from today's forward EV.
