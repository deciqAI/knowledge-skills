# Method in Action: Funding and Building AI Outside Your Circle (2024-2026)

> *Example for the [circle-of-competence](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The generative-AI boom that followed ChatGPT's late-2022 release created, by 2024-2026, the most widespread circle-of-competence stress test of the decade. Capital and attention flooded toward anything labeled "AI," and a large share of the people writing checks or building products could not honestly evaluate the technical claims they were betting on. The discipline here is not "avoid AI" — it is knowing the honest edge of your AI knowledge and either staying inside it or widening it deliberately, rather than letting hype substitute for competence.

This example walks a composite but representative decision — a generalist investor or non-technical founder weighing a bet on an "AI-powered" opportunity in 2025 — through the skill's own process.

**Step 1 — State the decision.** Domain(s): investing in (or building) a generative-AI product. Gut call: an enthusiastic yes — "AI is the biggest platform shift since mobile; I can't afford to miss it." Confidence: high. The high confidence paired with a novel domain is itself the first thing the skill flags.

**Step 2 — Circle test.** Framed honestly, the domain is not "software" or "technology in general" — it is *the specific technical and economic mechanics of large language models*: what a model can and cannot reliably do, the difference between a genuine moat and a thin wrapper over someone else's API, the real cost of inference and how gross margins behave at scale, and how quickly a foundation-model provider could ship the same feature and erase a startup's edge. Run against that honest domain, the test questions come back negative for a generalist:

- *Past decisions in this domain with measurable outcomes?* Reading about transformers or using ChatGPT daily is *exposure*, not a track record of correctly evaluating AI ventures through to outcome. Most 2024-2026 decision-makers had no such calibration data because the category was too young to have produced it.
- *Can you articulate the specific drivers experts would agree with?* The honest edge for a non-technical evaluator usually stops at the demo. Whether a product has defensibility — proprietary data, workflow lock-in, distribution — versus being a prompt wrapped in a UI that a model update or a well-funded incumbent can replicate is exactly the driver a generalist cannot reliably judge, and it is the driver that determines the outcome.
- *Would domain experts consider your evaluation credible?* Through this period, seasoned observers repeatedly warned that valuations and expectations had run ahead of demonstrated economics, and that many "AI" labels were marketing rather than substance. Regulators including the U.S. FTC publicly cautioned companies against overstating AI capabilities ("AI washing"), and the SEC brought enforcement actions in 2024 against firms for misleading AI-related claims. The expert signal that competence was scarce was public and available.

This is the exposure-versus-competence trap at population scale: familiarity with the *output* of AI (chatbots everyone can try) was mistaken for competence in the *investment and engineering judgment* the decision actually required. The very ease of using the product is what inflates the illusion of understanding it.

**Step 3 — If outside, pick a response.** For the generalist, all three legitimate responses were available and mostly ignored during the hype peak:

- **Defer:** route capital through, or hire, someone whose circle genuinely includes model economics and defensibility; retain only a mission/ethics and capital-allocation veto. Concretely: a technical partner or a specialist fund rather than a solo generalist conviction bet.
- **Wait:** name in advance what would move you inside — evidence of retention and pricing power over 12-36 months, gross margins that survive real inference costs, a moat that a foundation-model release did not evaporate — then build the feedback loop and watch for it before committing at scale.
- **Small-bet:** acknowledge outside-circle status explicitly and size to the uncertainty (quarter- or half-Kelly), rather than concentrating because "this is the one platform shift I can't miss." FOMO is precisely the condition under which the skill says circle discipline is most valuable, and booms produce concentrated losses for those who acted outside their circle.

The failure mode of the period was the opposite of all three: maximum-size, high-confidence bets justified by "everyone is doing this" and "I'll figure it out as I go" — the empirical signatures of acting outside the circle without admitting it.

**Step 4 — If inside, act decisively.** The mirror image is the honest specialist — an ML engineer or an investor with a real, calibrated track record in the domain. For them the correct move is *not* to hesitate: inside a genuine circle, speed and decisiveness are the empirical advantage, and circle discipline must not become an excuse to over-think a bet they are actually qualified to make. The discipline cuts both ways — it licenses boldness exactly where it forbids it elsewhere.

**Step 5 — Document the boundary.** The durable boundary marker from this era: *daily use of an AI product is not competence to evaluate an AI business or to build a defensible one.* The honest edge of most people's AI knowledge in 2024-2026 stopped at "I can see it's impressive" and well short of "I can tell whether this specific bet will pay off." Widening that circle is possible — but on the skill's timescale it takes years of calibrated feedback, not a season of enthusiasm. Re-evaluation trigger: revisit when you have outcome data on prior AI calls, not merely more conviction.

The mapped steps:
1. State the decision: bet on / build an "AI" opportunity; domain overstated as "tech"; gut yes at high confidence
2. Circle test: no outcome track record in LLM economics, cannot articulate defensibility drivers, credible experts and regulators flagging over-claiming — outside for the generalist
3. Outside-circle response: defer to a specialist / wait for margin-and-moat evidence / small-bet sized to uncertainty — mostly ignored at the hype peak
4. Act decisively inside: the calibrated specialist should not over-hesitate on a bet genuinely inside their circle
5. Document the boundary: using AI ≠ competence to fund or build it; expansion takes years of calibrated feedback
