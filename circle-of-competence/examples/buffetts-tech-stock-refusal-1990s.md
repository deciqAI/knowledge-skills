# Method in Action: Buffett's Tech-Stock Refusal, 1990s

> *Example for the [circle-of-competence](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The empirical demonstration of the circle-of-competence discipline is Warren Buffett's behavior throughout the 1990s technology boom. Buffett, who had been by then one of the most successful investors of the 20th century, refused to invest in technology companies on the basis that he could not evaluate them — and accepted the resulting opportunity cost as the price of the discipline.

The cost was real and visible. From 1990 to 1999, Microsoft's stock returned over 8,000%. Cisco returned over 75,000%. Dell returned over 30,000%. A Berkshire Hathaway portfolio with even modest allocations to these names would have produced returns substantially above Berkshire's actual performance over the same decade. Buffett's circle-of-competence discipline cost him — and Berkshire shareholders — billions of dollars in foregone gains.

Buffett was explicit about this in his 1999 *Fortune* essay (written near the peak of the tech bubble, when his "missed it" position was most visibly painful):

> "I have stayed out of certain industries because I lacked the ability to predict the long-term economics of any single business in those industries. I've also stayed out of industries that change too rapidly. ... The fact that I haven't found a tech company within my circle of competence doesn't mean others won't find tech companies within theirs. But I haven't.
>
> A great investment must be capable of being identified as great by the investor. The question 'Is Microsoft a great investment?' is, for me, the wrong question; the right question is 'Can I evaluate Microsoft well enough to know whether it's a great investment?' For me, the answer to the second question is no. For others, it may be yes. They have a circle of competence that includes technology; I do not.
>
> I will earn less than they earn during this period. I refuse to abandon the discipline that has produced my long-term results in order to capture short-term gains in an area where my judgment is not calibrated."
>
> — Buffett (1999), *Fortune*, November 22, 1999.

When the dot-com crash arrived in 2000-2002, the discipline vindicated itself in stark form. The Nasdaq dropped 78% from peak to trough. Many of the tech companies that had produced spectacular 1990s returns went to zero or near-zero. The investors who had abandoned circle-of-competence discipline during the boom paid the full cost during the bust. Buffett's foregone gains during the boom were preserved against the losses during the bust.

The longer-term lesson: the discipline of staying inside the circle costs visible upside in some periods and prevents catastrophic downside in others. The trade is not "circle discipline costs you" — it is "circle discipline trades upside variance for downside protection." For investors with long time horizons (Buffett's actual frame: forever), the protection-against-permanent-loss is structurally more valuable than the foregone gains.

The discipline's extension into Apple is instructive. Berkshire began significantly investing in Apple in 2016-2018, and Apple became Berkshire's largest holding by 2020. Buffett's explanation was that he had come to see Apple not as a "technology company" but as a *consumer products company* — and Buffett's circle had long included consumer products (See's Candies, Coca-Cola, Gillette). The circle did not expand to include all of technology; it included Apple specifically because Apple fit a pattern Buffett's circle could evaluate. This is the legitimate form of circle expansion: identify a specific case that, on careful examination, fits a pattern you can already evaluate.

The framework's intellectual lineage extends through:

**Charlie Munger's 1994 USC lecture** "A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom As It Relates To Investment Management and Business," reprinted in *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005). Munger systematized the framework and extended it from investing to general business judgment:

> "I've often felt there might be more to be gained by really understanding the lessons of past behavior — and I mean the lessons of past errors — than there is by trying to think clearly about novel situations. ... You should learn intensely in those domains where you have a real chance to learn something useful. And then act in those domains where you understand what you're doing. As for the rest — the things that are 'too hard' — they go into a permanent 'too hard' pile, and you act somewhere else."
>
> — Munger (1994), USC lecture.

Munger's "too hard pile" is the operational form of the circle discipline. Most decisions in life present themselves as choices that you must make. The discipline is to recognize that some decisions can be classified as "too hard for me to evaluate competently" and declined — leaving the decision to someone whose circle includes it, or simply not engaging with the decision at all.

**Howard Marks' application to investment risk management.** In *The Most Important Thing* (2011), Marks describes the circle as one of the three or four foundational disciplines of long-term investing:

> "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant. The investor who genuinely understands the boundary of his competence — and refuses to act on judgments outside that boundary, no matter how confident those judgments feel — has a structural advantage over the investor who acts on every judgment with equal confidence. The disciplined investor's portfolio will have lower variance and longer-lasting outperformance. ... I have seen many smart investors destroy themselves by acting outside their circle. I have rarely seen an investor destroyed by staying inside it."
>
> — Marks, H. (2011). *The Most Important Thing.* Columbia Business School Publishing, ch. 14.

**Application to operational decisions in startups.** The circle framework applies to founder decisions across all functions, not just investing. A technical founder asked to make a critical sales hire is outside the circle on sales evaluation; a sales founder asked to make a critical engineering architecture decision is outside the circle on engineering. The legitimate responses are the same: defer to expert judgment, wait until you've expanded the circle through structured exposure, or act with small-bet sizing.

The framework has shaped operational practice in:

**Investment management.** Major funds (Berkshire, Marks' Oaktree, Munger's Wesco, Pabrai Funds, Sequoia Capital) explicitly use circle-of-competence discipline in their investment processes. Investment committees often have explicit "outside our circle" categories that decline opportunities even if they appear attractive.

**Corporate governance.** Effective boards distinguish between matters where individual directors have specific competence (where their judgment carries weight) and matters outside (where the board defers to management or external experts). Bad governance often results from directors acting decisively in areas outside their circle.

**Executive decision-making.** Successful CEOs of late-stage companies typically narrow their decision focus rather than broaden it as the company grows. The CEO of a public company cannot retain decision competence across all functions; the discipline is to identify which 3-5 decisions are inside the CEO's circle and to delegate the rest to functional experts.

**Career and entrepreneurship.** The circle-of-competence framing applies to "what business should I start?" Founder-market fit is, in essence, an articulation of "what businesses are inside my circle?" Founders who pick businesses outside their circle face the same expected-value penalty as investors who do.

Three operational lessons from Buffett and Munger:

**First, the size of the circle is not the goal; the boundary is.** A founder with a small but well-bounded circle outperforms a founder with a large but poorly-bounded circle. The discipline is not "expand your competence as fast as possible"; it is "know exactly where your competence ends."

**Second, opportunity cost of refusing to act outside the circle is real and must be paid honestly.** Buffett missed the 1990s tech boom; he made his peace with the cost. Founders who apply the circle discipline will refuse opportunities that turn out to have been great. The discipline only makes sense if you accept the cost as the price of avoiding the much-larger expected cost of acting outside the circle.

**Third, the circle can be expanded — slowly, with deliberate work, and with humility about how long it takes.** Years, not weeks. Most "circle expansion" claims are actually circle illusion — exposure mistaken for competence. The honest sign of expansion is the building of calibrated feedback data, not the increase of confidence.
