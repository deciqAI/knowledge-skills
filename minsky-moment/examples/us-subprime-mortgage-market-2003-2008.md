# Method in Action: US Subprime Mortgage Market (2003–2008)

> *Example for the [minsky-moment](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Primary-source-documented case. McCulley coined the phrase in 1998; the subprime application became the canonical Minsky Moment diagnosis. Primary sources: Minsky (1986, 1992); McCulley, Paul. "Teton Reflections," *PIMCO Global Central Bank Focus*, September 2007; and the Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission Report (2011).

**Step 1 — Debt structure classification (2005–2007):**
- Hedge financing: traditional 30-year fixed mortgages with 20% down, full income documentation. Service capacity: high.
- Speculative financing: adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs) where borrowers required near-zero introductory rates and expected to refinance before reset. Rollover-dependent.
- Ponzi financing: "Option ARM" and "neg-am" mortgages where monthly minimums were below interest accrual — principal was *growing*, not serviced. Entirely appreciation-dependent.

By 2006, Ponzi and speculative financing had grown to represent a majority of new mortgage originations in stressed markets (California, Nevada, Florida). The shift had happened gradually during a five-year period of rising prices and falling defaults.

**Step 2 — Stability runway (2001–2006):**
National home prices had not declined year-over-year since the Great Depression. Mortgage default rates were at historic lows. Lending standards loosened continuously: stated-income loans, 100% LTV, no-doc loans, teaser rates. Each year of stability reduced apparent risk and encouraged more aggressive lending.

**Step 3 — Trigger stress identification:**
- Speculative units: Fed funds rate increases (2004–2006, from 1% to 5.25%) raised ARM reset rates, converting serviceable to unserviceable.
- Ponzi units: Any cessation of home price appreciation — since prices plateaued in late 2006, Option ARM borrowers could no longer refinance before reset.
- Most plausible trigger (identified in advance by analysts including Minsky scholar Jan Hatzius): national home price decline + rising ARM resets simultaneous.

**Step 4 — Collapse cascade:**
- First-order: borrowers unable to refinance → defaults → mortgage-backed security losses.
- Second-order: MBS losses → bank capital impairment → credit tightening → further price compression.
- Cascade amplifier: mortgage securities had been repacked into CDOs, which were used as collateral for repo funding of hedge funds and bank SIVs — a second-order leverage structure that converted a housing correction into a banking system liquidity crisis.
- Cascade floor: Federal Reserve liquidity facilities (Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility, etc.) and TARP capital injections were the structural backstop.

**Source:** Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission. *The Financial Crisis Inquiry Report.* U.S. Government Printing Office, 2011. Available at https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GPO-FCIC/pdf/GPO-FCIC.pdf — the primary government investigation documenting the debt structure evolution and cascade mechanism.
