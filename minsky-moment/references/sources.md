# Sources — minsky-moment

> *Primary sources for the [minsky-moment](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- **Minsky, Hyman P.** *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy.* Yale University Press, 1986 (reissued McGraw-Hill, 2008). **Primary source for the Financial Instability Hypothesis.** Verbatim quote from p. 230 in Overview.
- **Minsky, Hyman P.** "The Financial Instability Hypothesis." *Working Paper No. 74*, Jerome Levy Economics Institute of Bard College, May 1992. **Primary source for the three-stage debt classification and the "stability is destabilizing" formulation.** Verbatim quote from p. 8. https://www.levyinstitute.org/pubs/wp74.pdf
- **McCulley, Paul.** "Teton Reflections." *PIMCO Global Central Bank Focus*, September 2007. **Primary source for the application of the "Minsky Moment" term to the subprime crisis.** McCulley coined the term in 1998 for the Russian crisis; this document applied it to the 2007 subprime events.
- **Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission.** *The Financial Crisis Inquiry Report.* U.S. Government Printing Office, 2011. https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GPO-FCIC/pdf/GPO-FCIC.pdf — **Primary government documentation of the 2008 crisis mechanics**, including the debt structure evolution consistent with the Minsky framework.
- **Kindleberger, Charles P.** *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises,* 1st ed. Basic Books, 1978 (later editions through 2011). Historical documentation of credit cycle dynamics consistent with Minsky's mechanism across multiple centuries and markets.

**Not cited and why:** Popular media uses "Minsky Moment" loosely to mean any sharp market decline — this skill uses it in Minsky's precise technical sense: a collapse caused specifically by the debt-stage shift dynamic during a stability period. General claims that "markets are always Minsky-fragile" appear in commentary but are not analytically specific enough to count as Minsky analysis.
