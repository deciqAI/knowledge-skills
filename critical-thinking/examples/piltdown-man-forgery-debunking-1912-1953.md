# Method in Action: The Debunking of the Piltdown Man Forgery (1912 → 1953)

> *Example for the [critical-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example in paleoanthropology, not intelligence — the same disconfirmation discipline overturning a claim that had gone unexamined for four decades.

In **1912**, Charles Dawson and Arthur Smith Woodward announced fossil fragments from a gravel pit at Piltdown, Sussex: a skull with a human-like braincase and an ape-like jaw, named *Eoanthropus dawsoni* and celebrated as the "missing link" between apes and humans. It fit the era's expectation that a large brain evolved before modern anatomy elsewhere, and it flattered the ambition for an English specimen. For roughly forty years the community accumulated **confirming** interpretations rather than testing the claim against alternatives.

The 1953 re-examination by **Joseph Weiner, Kenneth Oakley, and Wilfrid Le Gros Clark** treated the specimen the way ACH demands: enumerate the hypotheses first, then let diagnostic evidence — not the prestige of the original find — decide.

- **Hypotheses (Step 1):**
  - H1: Genuine transitional hominid, as claimed since 1912.
  - H2: An accidental, natural mixture of an old human skull and an unrelated ape jaw washed into the same deposit.
  - H3: A deliberate forgery — a human cranium paired with an ape jaw, artificially altered and aged.

- **Evidence (Step 2), including expected-but-absent evidence:**
  - E1: Fluorine testing (Oakley's method) showed the bones had absorbed too little fluorine to be as ancient as claimed — and the jaw and cranium did not match each other.
  - E2: Under magnification the molar surfaces showed flat, artificial abrasion — file marks inconsistent with natural wear.
  - E3: The bones had been stained to imitate great age; freshly exposed surfaces betrayed the coloring.
  - E4: The jaw was essentially that of an ape while the cranium was essentially human — the two pieces did not form an anatomically integrated whole.
  - E5 (absence): in forty years no comparable specimen appeared anywhere else, the pattern a genuine lineage should have produced.

- **Matrix (Step 3), scored C / I / N/A:**

| Evidence | H1 (genuine) | H2 (accidental mix) | H3 (forgery) |
|---|---|---|---|
| E1 (fluorine mismatch) | I | C | C |
| E2 (filed teeth) | I | I | C |
| E3 (artificial staining) | I | I | C |
| E4 (discordant anatomy) | I | C | C |
| E5 (no other specimens) | I | C | C |
| **Inconsistencies** | **5** | **2** | **0** |

- **Surviving hypothesis (Step 5):** H3 (deliberate forgery), zero inconsistencies. The forty-year consensus (H1) carried the most.
- **Diagnostic evidence (Step 4):** E2 and E3 discriminate hardest — filing and staining are consistent only with deliberate fabrication, ruling out the innocent-mixture reading (H2).
- **Linchpin assumption (Step 6):** the fluorine chemistry and microscopy are sound. If E1–E3 were artifacts of testing, H2 would revive.

The lesson the framework names: the claim survived for forty years not for lack of evidence but because it was tested against confirming readings instead of an array of competing hypotheses. Once each hypothesis was scored against every piece of evidence, the forgery hypothesis emerged with zero inconsistencies.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify hypotheses: genuine / accidental mix / deliberate forgery (≥3)
2. List evidence including the absent expected evidence (E5: no other specimens)
3. Score the matrix C / I / N/A against every hypothesis
4. Isolate diagnostic evidence (filed teeth, artificial staining) that discriminates
5. Count Inconsistencies: forgery survives with zero
6. Name the linchpin assumption: the fluorine and microscopy results are valid

Primary source: Weiner, J.S., Oakley, K.P., & Le Gros Clark, W.E. (1953). "The Solution of the Piltdown Problem." *Bulletin of the British Museum (Natural History), Geology*, 2(3), 139–146. See also Weiner, J.S. (1955). *The Piltdown Forgery*. Oxford University Press.
