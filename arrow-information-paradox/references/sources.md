# Sources — arrow-information-paradox

> *Primary sources for the [arrow-information-paradox](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Arrow, K. J. (1962). "Economic Welfare and the Allocation of Resources for Invention." In *The Rate and Direction of Inventive Activity: Economic and Social Factors*, NBER, Princeton University Press, pp. 609–626. The founding statement of the information paradox and of information's three problematic properties — indivisibility, inappropriability (non-excludable / non-rivalrous), and uncertainty. Verbatim: *"there is a fundamental paradox in the determination of demand for information; its value for the purchaser is not known until he knows the information, but then he has in effect acquired it without cost."* https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2144/c2144.pdf
- Akerlof, G. A. (1970). "The Market for Lemons: Quality Uncertainty and the Market Mechanism." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 84(3), pp. 488–500. The adverse-selection cousin: absent a mechanism to convey quality under asymmetric information, quality-uncertain markets can unravel toward the lowest quality ("lemons"). Verbatim: *"the presence of people who wish to pawn off bad wares as good wares tends to drive out the legitimate business."* https://doi.org/10.2307/1879431
- Spence, A. M. (1973). "Job Market Signaling." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 87(3), pp. 355–374. The companion mechanism from the other side of the asymmetry: how a party can *credibly reveal* quality through a costly, hard-to-fake signal — the tool this skill hands off to when the problem narrows to "prove quality without full disclosure." https://doi.org/10.2307/1882010
- The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (2001). "Markets with Asymmetric Information." Nobel Memorial Prize background note — the official summary of the 2001 Prize to Akerlof, Spence, and Stiglitz, situating Arrow's paradox within the broader economics of asymmetric information. https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2001/popular-information/
- The Coca-Cola Company. "The Chronicle Of Coca-Cola" / company history and public statements on the secret formula — used only for the company's *public positioning* on secrecy vs. patenting; the formula's contents are not public and are not reproduced or claimed. https://www.coca-colacompany.com/about-us/history

*On the "Reverse Information Paradox":* This AI-era framing — that a *buyer* may have to disclose proprietary knowledge to use what they bought, inverting Arrow's paradox — was named publicly by Satya Nadella (post on X, 12 July 2026: "That is what I think of as the Reverse Information Paradox"). It is a contemporary thesis, not established empirical canon, and is kept out of the skill's executable core; it is not attributed to Arrow. No patent-office statistics, dollar figures for named private deals, or contents of trade secrets are cited, because they are non-public or not verifiable from the source material; deal patterns in the examples are composite illustrations, not claims about specific filings or transactions.
