# Method in Action: The Coca-Cola Formula — Trade Secret vs. Patent

> *Example for the [arrow-information-paradox](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The Coca-Cola formula is the most famous illustration of the patent-vs-secrecy horn of Arrow's paradox. The recipe for Coca-Cola ("Merchandise 7X") has been kept as a trade secret since the drink was created in 1886, and the company has repeatedly and publicly declined to patent it. Walking the choice through The Process shows *why* secrecy dominates a patent here — and, more usefully, when it would not.

**Step 1 — Audit appropriability.** The information is the precise formula and process for the syrup. The concrete leak: anyone who learns the formula can produce an identical or near-identical beverage and never pay Coca-Cola a cent — the recipe is non-rivalrous (their making it doesn't stop the company making it) and, once known, non-excludable. This is a real, nameable leak, so the paradox binds. Gate passed.

**Step 2 — Identify the minimum a "buyer" needs.** For a formula, the relevant "buyer" is the market and any prospective licensee/bottler. The market does not need the formula to value the product — it values the *taste and brand outcome*, which it experiences by drinking the product. Bottlers are licensed the finished syrup or concentrate, not the recipe. So the gap between "what convinces the buyer" (the drink itself) and "what would let them reproduce it" (the formula) is enormous — which is exactly the room in which a mechanism can live.

**Step 3 — Choose the mechanism.** The two candidates are a patent and a trade secret.
- A **patent** would grant a time-limited, enforceable monopoly, but only in exchange for *publishing the formula* — after which the protection expires (historically a finite term) and the recipe becomes free for all. For a product whose advantage is meant to last generations, publishing on a clock is a poor trade.
- A **trade secret** keeps the formula hidden with no expiry, as long as access is controlled and it is hard to reverse-engineer. A beverage's exact formulation is genuinely hard to reproduce precisely from the finished product, and the company can control access to the written recipe.

Because the advantage is long-lived and reverse-engineering is hard, secrecy closes the leak better than a patent — which is precisely the company's documented choice.

**Step 4 — Build the disclosure ladder.** Under secrecy, the ladder is severe: the reproducible core (the formula) sits on the top rung and is disclosed to essentially no external party. Bottlers receive concentrate, not recipe. Employees with access are bound and compartmentalized. There is effectively no low rung on which the core appears — the product itself is the only thing disclosed to the market.

**Step 5 — Pre-commit the walk-away.** The standing commitment is that the formula is not licensed, sold, or published under any ordinary commercial pressure — the walk-away is total. A one-time patent filing would have been irreversible; secrecy preserves the option to keep protecting the advantage indefinitely.

**Disclosure Plan:**
```
# Disclosure Plan: Coca-Cola formula → the market / bottlers
## The information: the precise syrup formula and process (Merchandise 7X)
## Appropriability leak: anyone who learns it can produce the drink and never pay
## Minimum-to-convince: the finished product's taste — no formula needed to value it
## Reproducible core (never disclosed): the written formula
## Mechanism: trade secret — closes the leak with no expiry; advantage is long-lived and hard to reverse-engineer
## Disclosure ladder:
   Rung 1 (public):     the finished beverage (outcome, not mechanism)
   Rung 2 (bottlers):   licensed concentrate, not the recipe
   Rung 3 (never):      the formula
## Walk-away: do not patent, license, or publish the formula
```

**What it explains.** The paradox says you cannot let a buyer *value* information without risking giving it away. Coca-Cola resolves it by ensuring buyers never need the information to value the product — they value the *outcome* (taste, brand) and receive only that. A patent would have solved a *different* problem (safe disclosure of an easy-to-copy invention) at a cost — publication and a finite term — that made no sense for a hard-to-copy, long-lived advantage.

The mapped steps:
1. Audit: formula is non-rivalrous/non-excludable once known → real leak
2. Minimum: the market values the drink, not the recipe → huge convince-vs-reproduce gap
3. Mechanism: secrecy beats patent because advantage is long-lived and hard to reverse-engineer
4. Ladder: product public, concentrate to bottlers, formula never
5. Walk-away: never patent/publish — preserve indefinite protection

**Founder takeaway:** Patent vs. secrecy is not a legal-department formality — it is Arrow's paradox as a strategy decision. Ask two questions: *Is my advantage easy to copy once someone sees it?* and *Will it outlive a patent's term?* If it is easy to copy and short-lived, a patent's safe-disclosure trade can be worth it. If it is hard to reverse-engineer and durable — like a formula — secrecy dominates, because patenting would hand rivals the exact thing you are trying to protect.
