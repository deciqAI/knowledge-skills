# Method in Action: AI Agents vs. Seat-Based SaaS (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [disruptive-innovation](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A live, unfinished case — useful precisely because the arc has *not* fully run, so the framework is doing diagnostic rather than post-hoc work. After ChatGPT's late-2022 launch, a wave of AI-native "agent" tools began attacking established SaaS categories (customer support, coding, sales, marketing copy, analytics). The interesting question is not "is AI big" — it obviously is — but whether the attack is genuine *disruption* (structural, with rational incumbent neglect) or *sustaining innovation* the incumbents will simply absorb. Run the six steps and the answer is: it depends on the pricing model, and that is exactly where the structural asymmetry lives.

**Step 1 — Map performance-price landscape.** Incumbent B2B SaaS is overwhelmingly sold **per seat** — a fixed recurring price per human user (Salesforce, ServiceNow, Zendesk, Adobe, Microsoft 365, and most of the category). That model assumes a human sits in front of the software and does the work. Incumbents have spent a decade adding features to justify seat-price increases, so many buyers pay for capability well beyond what an average seat actually uses — the classic overshoot signature. AI-native entrants price differently: **per outcome, per action, or per token of usage** (e.g., support tools that charge per resolved ticket rather than per agent seat). On the mainstream enterprise metrics — breadth of features, compliance certifications, integrations, reliability at scale — the AI-native newcomers of 2023 were plainly *inferior* to entrenched suites. That inferiority-on-mainstream-metrics is condition (a) of the stop-rule.

**Step 2 — Find ignored segments.** Two ignored pools, matching the two disruption paths:
- *Low-end (overserved):* teams paying for many seats where much of the work is routine and automatable — tier-1 support queues, boilerplate code, first-draft marketing copy, routine data pulls. They buy seats they don't fully use.
- *Non-consumption (blocked):* small teams and individuals who could never justify enterprise per-seat contracts at all, and who now get "a colleague's worth of work" from an agent for a usage fee. This is the larger prize — consumption created among people previously priced out.

**Step 3 — Define "good enough" for entry.** The AI-native MVP deliberately strips the incumbent's breadth: no sprawling admin console, limited integrations, narrow scope (resolve *this* class of ticket; write *this* kind of code). What it must nail is the single automatable job, done cheaply enough that outcome-pricing undercuts the loaded cost of a human seat plus the incumbent license. The removed breadth is not a defect — it is what funds the price. GitHub Copilot (general availability June 2022) and later coding agents are the cleanest example: narrow job (autocomplete, then whole-task coding), priced far below a developer-hour.

**Step 4 — Analyze rational neglect.** This is the moat, and it is genuinely structural for the incumbents' *pricing*, not their technology. A per-seat incumbent that fully embraces "the AI does the work" is arguing that its customers need *fewer seats* — i.e., voluntarily shrinking its own revenue base. Worse, frontier-model inference is a real, usage-scaling cost of goods sold; bolting metered AI onto an ~80%-gross-margin software P&L dilutes the margin the public markets reward. So incumbents face the Christensen bind: the rational, ROI-defensible move is to ship AI as a *seat add-on* (a premium upsell that protects seat count and margin) rather than to reprice around outcomes. That is condition (c). **But the neglect is time-stamped and partial, not absolute** — and this is the crucial discipline. Microsoft priced Copilot as a per-user add-on (announced 2023) rather than cannibalizing seats; Salesforce launched Agentforce (announced 2024) with consumption-style pricing, showing incumbents *can* move when threatened. Per the AI/software-tools stop-rule, if an incumbent can replicate a thin agent via API in two sprints, the entrant has a feature, not a disruption. The durable disruptors are the ones whose advantage is a *pricing-model and cost-structure* asymmetry the incumbent cannot copy without repricing its whole book.

**Step 5 — Map upward migration path.** Stage 1: automate one narrow, low-stakes job at usage pricing (tier-1 tickets, autocomplete). Stage 2: expand scope and reliability, move to higher-stakes work and mid-market accounts, accumulate proprietary workflow data. Stage 3: sell the "agentic platform" into the enterprise core the incumbents defend — the tier where seats and margins are richest. The migration is visibly in progress (coding assistants moving from autocomplete to multi-file agents; support tools moving from deflection to full resolution), but Stage 3 is **not yet proven**, which is why the verdict below is *partial, not confirmed*.

**Step 6 — Stop-rule applied.** (a) *Inferior on mainstream metrics?* Yes for the 2023 entrants — narrower, less proven than incumbent suites. (b) *Low-end or non-consumer target?* Yes — over-seated routine work, plus previously-priced-out small teams. (c) *Rational incumbent neglect?* **Partial.** The margin-and-seat-cannibalization bind is real, but incumbents have shown they can respond with add-on and consumption pricing. Because condition (c) is contested, the honest label is **genuine disruption *of the seat-based pricing model*, partial disruption of the incumbents themselves** — the firms that reprice fast enough survive; those anchored to protecting seat count are the ones the pattern predicts will be hollowed out from below.

**Why this case earns its place.** It is the discipline's hardest test: a technology everyone agrees is huge, where the naive move is to slap "disruptive" on every AI wrapper. The framework refuses that. It locates the actual structural fault line — **per-seat vs. per-outcome pricing and the AI-inference cost of goods** — and shows that the disruption is real *at the pricing layer* while remaining unproven at the incumbent-destruction layer. Two transferable disciplines:
- Disruption can target a *business model*, not just a product tier. The AI here is often available to everyone (via the same model APIs); the durable asymmetry is who can afford to price by outcome.
- Time-stamp the neglect. "Incumbents haven't responded" was true in 2023 and materially less true by 2025. A neglect analysis with no expiry date is not analysis.

The mapped steps:
1. Performance landscape: per-seat incumbents overshooting; AI-native entrants price per outcome/usage, inferior on breadth
2. Ignored segment: over-seated routine work (low-end) + priced-out small teams (non-consumption)
3. "Good enough": one automatable job, narrow scope, usage-priced below loaded seat cost
4. Rational neglect: repricing around outcomes shrinks seat revenue and dilutes software margin with inference COGS — but only partial, incumbents shipping AI add-ons and consumption tiers
5. Upward migration: narrow job → higher-stakes/mid-market → enterprise core (Stage 3 unproven)
6. Verdict: **partial** — genuine disruption of the seat-based *pricing model*; incumbent-level outcome undecided, hinges on repricing speed
