# Method in Action: Netflix vs. Blockbuster (1997–2010)

> *Example for the [disruptive-innovation](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A documented case where the disruption pattern ran its full arc — from entry to constitutional repeal of the incumbent.

**Step 1 — Performance landscape.** In 1997, Blockbuster operated roughly 9,000 US stores with revenues of approximately $6 billion. The incumbent's model was optimized for impulse renters who wanted same-day availability of new releases. Late fees — approximately $800 million annually, ~16% of revenue — were structural to the model and deeply resented by a subset of customers.

**Step 2 — Ignored segment.** Netflix's initial DVD-by-mail service was *inferior* to Blockbuster on the mainstream metric: same-day availability. But it served a segment Blockbuster ignored — heavy movie watchers who planned viewing in advance, hated late fees, and had no urgent need for the newest release. These customers were not non-consumers; they were *overserved* by the physical-store model's premium pricing and *underserved* by late-fee risk.

**Step 3 — "Good enough" definition.** DVD by mail, 1–2 day lag, no late fees, $19.99/month unlimited. No store presence, no impulse rentals, no new-release priority — all eliminated. The cost structure (no retail leases, centralized distribution) funded the flat-rate model.

**Step 4 — Incumbent rational neglect.** Blockbuster's late-fee revenue was not peripheral — it was ~$800M/year and structurally embedded in store economics. Eliminating late fees to compete with Netflix would have immediately impaired earnings. In 2000, Blockbuster had the opportunity to acquire Netflix for $50M; executives declined, reportedly viewing the market as too small. The rational neglect held until it was too late.

**Step 5 — Upward migration.** 2007: Netflix launched streaming, eliminating the mail-lag disadvantage entirely. 2010: Netflix passed 20 million paid subscribers. 2010: Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy. 2024: Netflix revenue ~$39B; Blockbuster's remaining single store in Bend, Oregon operates as a tourist attraction.

**Step 6 — Stop-rule applied.** All three conditions met: (a) DVD mail was inferior on the mainstream metric; (b) it targeted overserved heavy-users; (c) Blockbuster had a documented structural barrier (late-fee dependency) preventing response. Pattern confirmed as genuine low-end disruption.
