# Method in Action: Steel Minimills vs. Integrated Mills (1965–2001)

> *Example for the [disruptive-innovation](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Christensen's own canonical case — the one he used to show that disruption is a structural pattern, not a technology story. The disruptor's product was worse, the incumbents' retreat was profitable at every step, and the pattern still ran to completion.

**Step 1 — Performance landscape.** Through the 1960s, US steel was made in integrated mills: blast furnaces converting iron ore to steel at enormous capital cost, serving every quality tier from reinforcing bar (rebar) up to sheet steel for automobiles and appliances. Minimills — electric arc furnaces melting scrap steel — emerged with a cost advantage Christensen estimates at roughly 20 percent, but their scrap-based output had inconsistent metallurgy. On the mainstream metric, quality, minimills were plainly inferior.

**Step 2 — Ignored segment.** The lowest tier of the market was rebar: undemanding specifications, commodity pricing, the thinnest margins in the product line. Rebar buyers were overserved by integrated-mill quality — they paid for metallurgical consistency that concrete reinforcement did not need. This was the segment integrated mills were happiest to lose.

**Step 3 — "Good enough" definition.** For rebar, minimill steel was good enough: it met spec, and nothing more was required. The minimills stripped everything else — no blast furnace, no ore supply chain, no full product line — and the cost savings funded prices integrated mills could not match on their capital base.

**Step 4 — Incumbent rational neglect.** When minimills took rebar, the integrated mills' *reported margins improved* — they had shed their least profitable business and reallocated capacity to higher-margin tiers. Exiting was the rational, ROI-maximizing move, and it was applauded. This is the sharpest documented instance of the disruptor's moat: the incumbents were rewarded by their own accounting for retreating. The same logic repeated at each tier — every retreat looked like margin improvement, until there was nowhere left to retreat to.

**Step 5 — Upward migration.** With rebar conquered, minimill competition collapsed rebar prices, forcing the winners to move up to survive: bars and rods, then angle iron, then structural beams. In 1989 Nucor opened its Crawfordsville, Indiana plant using thin-slab continuous casting, cracking the sheet steel tier — the integrated mills' last and most profitable refuge. Nucor grew into the largest US steelmaker; Bethlehem Steel, once the emblem of American industrial might, filed for bankruptcy in 2001.

**Step 6 — Stop-rule applied.** All three conditions met: (a) scrap-based steel was inferior on the mainstream quality metric; (b) it targeted overserved low-end buyers (rebar); (c) the incumbents had a structural, documented reason not to respond — their cost structure and margin math made every retreat rational. Pattern confirmed as genuine low-end disruption, run to full arc.

**Why this case anchors the skill.** No manager at an integrated mill made an identifiably bad decision. Each exit from a low-margin tier passed every financial test the firm applied — that is Christensen's point, and it is what separates disruption analysis from ordinary competitive analysis. Two disciplines transfer directly:

- Test the incumbent's *incentive to retreat*, not just its capability to respond. Integrated mills could have built electric arc furnaces; their margin structure told them not to. Capability without incentive is not a response.
- Price the tier collapse into the migration plan. Every tier the minimills won turned into a commodity knife-fight among minimills, forcing the next move upward. A disruptor's roadmap that assumes the entry tier stays profitable has misread the pattern.

The mapped steps:
1. Performance landscape: integrated mills overshooting the low tier; minimills ~20% cheaper but lower quality
2. Ignored segment: rebar buyers — overserved on quality, paying for consistency they didn't need
3. "Good enough": meets rebar spec at minimill cost structure; everything else removed
4. Rational neglect: each tier abandoned *improved* incumbent reported margins — retreat was the ROI-optimal move
5. Upward migration: rebar → bars/rods → angle iron → structural beams → sheet steel (thin-slab casting, 1989)
6. Verdict: genuine low-end disruption — incumbents bankrupted by a sequence of individually rational decisions
