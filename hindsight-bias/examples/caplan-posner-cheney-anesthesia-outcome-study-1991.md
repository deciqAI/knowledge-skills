# Method in Action: Outcome Bias in Anesthesia Malpractice Review — the Caplan-Posner-Cheney Study (1991)

> *Example for the [hindsight-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Fischhoff's lab experiments showed that outcome knowledge distorts probability reconstruction in undergraduates. The Caplan-Posner-Cheney study showed the same distortion operating in the highest-stakes professional judgment there is: board-certified physicians deciding whether a colleague's care was negligent.

**Step 1 — State the outcome and the claim.** In malpractice review, the claim is always the same: "the care was inappropriate — a competent anesthesiologist would have managed this differently." The reviewer renders that judgment *after* learning the patient's outcome. The question the researchers isolated: does the outcome itself — rather than the care — drive the verdict?

**Step 2 — Reconstruct the pre-outcome information set.** Working from the American Society of Anesthesiologists' Closed Claims Project, Caplan, Posner, and Cheney selected 21 real case summaries describing the clinical facts available to the anesthesiologist during the case. Each case was prepared in two versions that were *identical in every clinical fact* — same patient, same monitoring data, same decisions — and differed only in the final sentence: one version ended in a temporary injury, the other in a permanent or fatal one. This is the cleanest possible reconstruction of the pre-outcome information set: hold it constant by construction, vary only the outcome.

**Step 3 — Decompose the components.** 112 practicing anesthesiologists were randomly assigned one version of each case and rated the care as appropriate or less than appropriate. Because the ex-ante facts were identical across versions, any difference in ratings could come only from outcome knowledge. The result: the same care was judged significantly less appropriate when the reviewer believed the outcome was permanent rather than temporary. In a substantial fraction of the matched cases, changing nothing but the outcome sentence flipped the majority verdict on the quality of care. This is the **foreseeability** and **inevitability** components in the wild — expert reviewers, evaluating their own specialty, could not quarantine outcome knowledge from their judgment of the decision process.

**Step 4 — Evaluate the decision process, not the outcome.** The study is the empirical demolition of "outcome is what matters" in professional review. Identical process, different luck, opposite verdicts. Reviewers were not careless or malicious; they were applying the standard implicit test — "would a good anesthesiologist have ended up here?" — which smuggles the outcome into the process evaluation. Baron and Hershey (1988) had shown the same conflation in lay subjects; Caplan et al. showed that domain expertise does not immunize.

**Step 5 — Install pre-commitment documentation.** The operational lesson for medicine: appropriateness of care must be assessed against the information state at each decision point, documented contemporaneously (anesthesia records, monitoring logs, notes), not against the end state of the patient. The Closed Claims Project itself institutionalized this by having reviewers work from structured case abstracts of intraoperative data.

**Step 6 — Run a blind post-mortem.** The study's matched-versions design *is* the blind post-mortem protocol, run as an experiment: review the process with the outcome masked or varied, and see whether the verdict survives. Modern morbidity-and-mortality conference protocols and two-phase accident investigations (facts first, analysis second) descend directly from this finding — the bias cannot be waived away by warning reviewers, so the review structure must withhold or neutralize outcome data.

The mapped steps:
1. Outcome and claim: "the care was inappropriate" — rendered by reviewers who know how the patient fared
2. Pre-outcome information set: held constant by construction — identical clinical facts, only the outcome sentence varied
3. Three-component decomposition: foreseeability and inevitability isolated experimentally; expert reviewers judged identical care worse when the outcome was severe
4. Process vs. outcome: same process, different luck, opposite verdicts — outcome alone confounds skill and luck
5. Pre-commitment documentation: contemporaneous anesthesia records and structured case abstracts as the basis of review
6. Blind post-mortem: outcome-masked review protocols; the matched-versions design as the template

Primary source: Caplan, R. A., Posner, K. L., & Cheney, F. W. (1991). "Effect of outcome on physician judgments of appropriateness of care." *JAMA*, 265(15), 1957-1960.
