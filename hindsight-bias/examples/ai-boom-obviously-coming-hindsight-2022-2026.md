# Method in Action: "The AI Boom Was Obviously Coming" — Retrospective Inevitability in the ChatGPT/Nvidia Era (2022–2026)

> *Example for the [hindsight-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Fischhoff's lab work and the Caplan-Posner-Cheney medical study both isolated hindsight bias in controlled settings. The 2022–2026 AI boom is the same distortion playing out at civilizational scale and in real time: after ChatGPT's launch and Nvidia's run, a dominant narrative emerged that the generative-AI wave and the compute buildout were "obviously coming" — a claim that, if believed, corrupts how investors, operators, and policymakers calibrate the *next* set of bets.

**Step 1 — State the outcome and the claim.** The outcome: OpenAI released ChatGPT on November 30, 2022; it reached a consumer adoption curve widely reported at the time as among the fastest on record (roughly 1M users in its first days and, by contemporaneous estimates, ~100M monthly users within about two months). Over 2023–2025, Nvidia's data-center GPU business drove its market capitalization from well under \$1 trillion into the multi-trillion-dollar range, briefly the most valuable company in the world, and hyperscalers announced enormous AI-infrastructure capital expenditure. The foreseeability claim, heard constantly by 2024–2025: *"This was obvious — transformers had been around since 2017, scaling laws were published, of course LLMs and the GPU buildout were inevitable; anyone paying attention should have seen it coming."* Who is credited/blamed: those who bought Nvidia early or funded AI look like visionaries; those who didn't are cast as having missed the obvious. Time elapsed: roughly 1–3 years — short enough that contemporaneous records are abundant, which is exactly what makes this case testable rather than merely arguable.

**Step 2 — Reconstruct the pre-outcome information set.** What was genuinely knowable in mid-2022: the transformer architecture existed (Vaswani et al., 2017); OpenAI's scaling-law papers and GPT-3 (2020) were public; Nvidia GPUs dominated ML training. What was genuinely uncertain: whether a chat interface would trigger mass *consumer* adoption; whether enterprises would pay at scale; whether capability would keep improving with scale or plateau; whether Nvidia's margins and lead would hold against custom silicon and competitors. The contemporaneous consensus is the tell. Before the launch, generative AI was a niche within ML; the prior consumer-AI cycles (chatbots, IBM Watson, voice assistants) had underdelivered relative to hype, priming informed observers toward skepticism. OpenAI itself reportedly launched ChatGPT as a low-key "research preview," not expecting the response — a strong signal that the people closest to the technology did not treat the outcome as inevitable. *Nixon test:* if you had polled informed contemporaries in October 2022 on "a chatbot will reach ~100M users within two months and a chip vendor will become the world's most valuable company on AI demand," the assigned probability would have been far below 30%. That gap is the measure of the hindsight distortion.

**Step 3 — Decompose the three components.**
- **Memory distortion** ("I said it would happen"): people now recall having been more bullish than their contemporaneous record shows. The countermeasure is records — 2021–2022 fund letters, analyst notes, forum posts, and price action. The market's own pricing is the cleanest record: Nvidia traded at a small fraction of its later valuation, and most AI-exposed equities were not priced for this outcome, which is direct evidence that the aggregate informed view did *not* foresee it.
- **Inevitability** ("it had to happen"): the boom is retold as the only possible result of transformers + scaling + GPUs. Countermeasure — list 3–5 plausible alternatives that the *same* starting structure could have produced: (a) capability plateaus after GPT-3.5-class models, deflating the wave; (b) a competitor or open-source stack commoditizes inference and compresses Nvidia's margins; (c) the consumer interface flops and generative AI stays a B2B API niche; (d) an early high-profile safety, legal, or hallucination failure triggers regulatory freezes; (e) custom accelerators erode the GPU moat faster. Each was a live branch in 2022.
- **Foreseeability overclaim** ("anyone should have seen it"): applying "obvious" to ex-ante-uncertain facts. The existence of the ingredients (transformers, scaling laws) did not make the *outcome* — timing, magnitude, and specifically the consumer-adoption and valuation shocks — predictable. Ingredients being visible is not the outcome being foreseeable.

**Step 4 — Evaluate the decision process, not the outcome.** *Bridgewater matrix:* an investor who bought Nvidia in 2022 for a *wrong* reason (e.g., a gaming-GPU thesis) and profited had a good outcome from a mediocre process — good luck, don't over-learn from it. An analyst who assigned the AI boom a reasoned 15% probability and sized a bet accordingly ran a *good process* even though the base case didn't happen — don't punish that as a miss. Conversely, a skeptic who reasoned carefully from the weak track record of prior consumer-AI cycles made a defensible decision that a low-probability branch overturned: bad outcome, not necessarily bad process. The danger of the "obviously coming" narrative is that it rewards process by outcome, teaching everyone to chase the last trade and to treat the winners as oracles — precisely the miscalibration that inflates the *next* bubble.

**Step 5 — Install pre-commitment documentation.** The structural fix is a decision journal, not vigilance. For each AI-era bet, log contemporaneously: the thesis, the probability assigned to the key uncertain event (adoption, capability continuation, margin durability), the disconfirming signals that would change your mind, and the position size. This converts "I always knew AI would be huge" into a checkable record. Applied forward — to 2025–2026 questions like AI capex sustainability, data-center ROI, and whether frontier valuations are justified — a pre-registered thesis is the only thing that will let you distinguish, later, between having been right and merely remembering yourself as right.

**Step 6 — Run a blind post-mortem.** When reviewing AI bets in 2026 and beyond, read the decision journal *before* looking at returns. Evaluate the reasoning against the information available at decision time; only then admit the outcome data. This is what separates "our AI thesis was well-reasoned and got lucky/unlucky" from "our thesis was sound." Without it, every post-mortem collapses into the inevitability narrative, and the lesson learned will itself be hindsight-biased — miscalibrating the next capital-allocation and risk decision, whether that is doubling down on AI infrastructure or fleeing it.

The mapped steps:
1. Outcome and claim: ChatGPT's launch and Nvidia's rise; "obvious — the ingredients existed, anyone should have seen it coming"
2. Pre-outcome information set: ingredients knowable (transformers, scaling, GPUs); outcome uncertain (consumer adoption, valuation magnitude, margin durability); pre-launch consensus skeptical; ex-ante probability well below 30%
3. Three-component decomposition: memory distortion (records/prices contradict recalled bullishness); inevitability (5 live alternative branches in 2022); foreseeability overclaim (visible ingredients ≠ foreseeable outcome)
4. Process vs. outcome: Bridgewater matrix separates lucky winners from good process; the narrative wrongly rewards process by outcome
5. Pre-commitment documentation: decision journal with logged thesis, probability, disconfirmers, and size — applied forward to AI capex/valuation bets
6. Blind post-mortem: read the journal before returns; keep the lesson from becoming hindsight-biased and miscalibrating the next bet
