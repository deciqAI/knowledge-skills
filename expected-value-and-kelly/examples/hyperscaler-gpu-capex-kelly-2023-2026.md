# Method in Action: Sizing Hyperscaler GPU/Compute Capex in the AI Boom (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [expected-value-and-kelly](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example on the *capex* side of the 2023–2026 AI gold rush, told as a single decision-maker's repeated allocation problem. Where the companion example ([Bet-Sizing the 2023–2026 AI Gold Rush](ai-gold-rush-vc-bet-sizing-2023-2026.md)) treats a venture fund's whole portfolio, this one narrows to one recurring board-level choice: **how much of annual free cash flow should a large cloud provider commit to AI compute (GPUs, data centers, power) each planning cycle?** It is a positive-expected-value bet with a fat upside, a wide confidence interval, and a genuine ruin tail (stranded, hard-to-repurpose capital). That combination is exactly what fractional Kelly exists for.

The setting is durable and well documented. From roughly 2023 onward — after ChatGPT's late-2022 public release — the large hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta) each raised AI-related capital expenditure sharply, guiding through 2024–2025 to tens of billions of dollars per year, much of it tied to AI compute; Nvidia's data-center GPU demand drove its revenue and market capitalization to among the largest of any public company. The *structure* of the capex decision — not the specific dollar figures, which move fast and are reported inconsistently — is what the EV-Kelly lens clarifies.

Walk the **EV-Kelly Sizing** through one hyperscaler's annual capex-allocation policy:

- **Confirm the decision is repeated (Step 1):** This is not a one-shot. Capex is re-set every planning cycle — quarterly guidance, annual budgets, multi-year build-outs — and the *sizing policy* recurs across cycles even as any single data-center build is one-off. You are sizing the recurring allocation rule, not a single facility. Kelly applies. ✓
- **Map the bet (Step 2):** Stake = the fraction of free cash flow (the "bankroll") committed to AI compute this cycle. On a **win** (AI demand monetizes: cloud consumption, ads, subscriptions, enterprise seats), payoff *W* is large and compounding — you own the scarce capacity when demand arrives. On a **loss** (demand disappoints or a cheaper compute regime arrives), loss *L* is the stranded portion of the capex: GPUs and buildings that can't be fully repurposed, depreciating on a short useful-life schedule. This is a **skewed, power-law-flavored** payoff, not a symmetric coin flip — see [power-law-distribution](../power-law-distribution/SKILL.md).
- **Compute EV (Step 3):** EV = p·W − q·L per dollar committed. Because *W* (owning scarce AI capacity in a demand surge) is large and the marginal loss on a miss is a fraction of the stake (some capacity is reusable for general cloud), portfolio EV can be clearly positive even if the probability of the *full* upside is well under one-half. The math tolerates a modest hit rate because the win is big and the downside is bounded, not total.
- **Edge gate (Step 4):** Positive EV is necessary but not sufficient. The edge is real only if the firm has a genuine advantage — distribution, existing cloud demand, model or data assets — over merely paying boom-era prices for capacity everyone else is also buying. If the "edge" is just fear of missing out, EV is illusory and no sizing rescues it.
- **Estimate input uncertainty (Step 5):** Wide and largely irreducible. *p* (that demand monetizes before the capex is written off) and *W* are forward guesses about an unprecedented technology mid-hype-cycle — not measured frequencies (a deck) or decades-fitted (Benter's races). The 80% CI on the edge is broad and could approach zero if a capability plateau or a compute-cost collapse arrives. This alone forces a small Kelly fraction.
- **Compute Kelly fraction (Step 6):** In the continuous framing, f\* ≈ μ/σ². The dispersion σ of AI-capex outcomes is enormous — the range from "generational franchise" to "expensive stranded silicon" is wide — so the Kelly-optimal fraction of the free-cash-flow bankroll on *any single* cycle's bet is modest even for a genuinely positive edge. High variance shrinks Kelly.
- **Apply fractional Kelly (Step 7):** Given both the estimation uncertainty (Step 5) and the fat-tailed variance (Step 6), the discipline is **well under full Kelly** — quarter-Kelly territory. In capex language: size the AI build so that a *wrong* bet is survivable, not fatal — retain balance-sheet slack, stage commitments so they can be paused, and prefer capacity that can fall back to general-purpose cloud. Bounded downside, retained exposure to the upside. That *is* fractional Kelly, implemented as staged, survivable capex.
- **Set stop trigger (Step 8):** Pre-commit to re-estimating the edge when AI revenue attach-rates diverge from the model, when utilization of deployed capacity runs persistently low, when GPU price/performance shifts enough to strand the current generation, or when a capability plateau or funding-market freeze breaks the demand thesis.

**Where correlation bites.** Kelly assumes independent bets; hyperscaler AI capex is anything but. The capex bet, the firm's own frontier-model bet, its cloud customers' AI startups, and the public GPU complex all rest on the same load-bearing assumptions — that capability keeps improving, that compute stays available, and that demand monetizes before write-off. When those co-move, a "diversified" AI position behaves like one concentrated bet. The skill's rule is blunt: **correlation eats Kelly fast** — same narrative, same cycle, same underlying assumption means a *lower* portfolio-level Kelly, not the naive sum of per-project Kellys.

The lesson this case adds to Thorp and Benter: their edges were *measured* or *fitted* on stationary processes. Hyperscaler AI capex is neither — probabilities are guesses, payoffs are skewed, ruin (stranded capital) is possible, and the bets are secretly one bet. Under those conditions full Kelly is not merely aggressive; it is a category error. **Fractional Kelly, expressed as staged and survivable capex, is the correct response to a genuinely positive but genuinely uncertain edge.** The question is not "how big can we bet on AI?" — it is "how do we stay solvent and flexible long enough to still own the capacity if and when the demand actually shows up?"

*Sources: Kelly, J. L., Jr., "A New Interpretation of Information Rate," Bell System Technical Journal 35(4), 1956, pp. 917–926 (the Kelly formula). Thorp, Edward O., "The Kelly Criterion in Blackjack, Sports Betting, and the Stock Market," 1997 (the fractional-Kelly trade-off under estimated edges). For the 2023–2026 hyperscaler AI-capex environment — annual capital-expenditure guidance tied to AI compute and Nvidia's data-center-driven revenue and market capitalization — see the companies' own public filings and earnings materials (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Nvidia SEC filings and quarterly reports, 2023–2025) as durable primary sources; specific capex and valuation figures cited in contemporaneous business press (e.g., The Wall Street Journal, Financial Times, The Information, 2023–2026) should be checked against those filings, as boom-era figures move quickly and are reported inconsistently.*
