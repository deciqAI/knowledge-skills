# Sources — knowing-and-doing-as-one

> *Primary sources for the [knowing-and-doing-as-one](../SKILL.md) skill.*

## Sources

- Wang Yangming (王阳明), *Chuanxi lu* (《传习录》, "Instructions for Practical Living"), compiled by Xu Ai (徐爱) and other students c. 1518, with later additions through 1527. The canonical primary source for the unity-of-knowing-acting doctrine. Modern English translation: Wang Yang-ming (1963). *Instructions for Practical Living and Other Neo-Confucian Writings*, translated with notes by Wing-tsit Chan. Columbia University Press. ISBN 978-0231083386. Chinese standard edition: 《王阳明全集》, Shanghai Guji Chubanshe, 1992.
- Ivanhoe, P. J. (2009). *Readings from the Lu-Wang School of Neo-Confucianism*. Hackett Publishing. The most accessible scholarly introduction to Wang's framework for non-specialists. ISBN 978-0872209602.
- Tu Wei-ming (杜维明) (1976). *Neo-Confucian Thought in Action: Wang Yang-Ming's Youth (1472–1509)*. University of California Press. The standard scholarly biography covering the Longchang exile and the philosophical development of the unity-of-knowing-acting doctrine. ISBN 978-0520029767.
- Pfeffer, J., & Sutton, R. I. (2000). *The Knowing-Doing Gap: How Smart Companies Turn Knowledge into Action*. Harvard Business School Press. The modern management-science treatment of the structurally similar problem; arrived at independently without explicit reference to Wang Yangming but reaching parallel operational conclusions. ISBN 978-1578511242.
- Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). "Implementation Intentions: Strong Effects of Simple Plans." *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 493–503. The modern psychological-research literature on the same phenomenon: that the specification of when, where, and how an action will be performed dramatically increases the rate of follow-through compared to mere "knowing one should." https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493
- Wood, W., & Neal, D. T. (2007). "A New Look at Habits and the Habit-Goal Interface." *Psychological Review*, 114(4), 843–863. The modern psychological treatment of habit formation and the structural reasons knowing alone does not produce doing. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.843
- Ries, E. (2011). *The Lean Startup*. Crown Business. The build-measure-learn loop is a contemporary engineering-and-product reformulation of "knowing and doing as one" — the recognition that what you know about customers is constituted by what you have shipped and observed. ISBN 978-0307887894.
- Graham, P. (2013). "Do Things That Don't Scale." *Essay*, July 2013. The startup-context articulation of Wang's framework: the knowing about what customers need is constituted by personally delivering for them, not by reading or planning. http://paulgraham.com/ds.html
