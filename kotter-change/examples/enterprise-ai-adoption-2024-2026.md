# Method in Action: Driving Enterprise AI Adoption (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [kotter-change](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By 2024–2026, most large enterprises had bought AI tools — copilots, assistants, internal chatbots, and workflow automation — but adoption frequently stalled at the pilot stage. A widely reported pattern emerged: seat licenses were paid for but lightly used, pilots proliferated without scaling into production, and returns on heavy AI capital spending were hard to demonstrate. A recurring, under-named cause was quiet resistance from middle managers, whose roles, headcount justifications, and control over process were most directly threatened by AI-native ways of working. This example walks a generic large-enterprise AI transformation through Kotter's eight steps.

**Pre-diagnostic (highest-risk failure modes for AI transformations):**
- *Complacency / no urgency* — often HIGH: "we already bought licenses, we're doing AI."
- *Obstacles not removed* — often HIGH (this is the Digital/AI pack's flagged step): legacy metrics, security review, procurement, and data-access rules block new ways of working.
- *No short-term wins* — often HIGH: pilots are measured on activity, not business outcomes.

**Step 1 (urgency):** Announcing "we're an AI company now" at a town hall does not create urgency. Urgency here comes from sustained external evidence: AI-native competitors shipping faster with smaller teams, and the concrete cost of inaction. The gate — 75%+ of leadership genuinely believing the status quo is dangerous — is failed when leaders treat AI as an IT procurement line rather than a competitive threat. Monthly reviews of competitor velocity and internal usage data sustain urgency far better than a single kickoff.

**Step 2 (guiding coalition):** The most common failure is staffing the coalition entirely with the CIO's office and a few executives. That coalition cannot move the middle of the organization — precisely where resistance lives. The fix per the model: recruit 2–3 informal influencers who are *not* VPs — a respected senior engineer, a trusted operations lead, a long-tenured frontline manager who has genuinely adopted the tools. Their credibility reaches the resistant middle-management layer that a top-down coalition cannot.

**Step 3 (vision):** "Become an AI-first enterprise" fails the 30-second front-line test — it is a slogan, not something a frontline worker can act on. A workable vision is concrete and imaginable: e.g., "every customer request gets a first draft answer in minutes, and our people spend their time on judgment, not retrieval." Crucially, an adoption vision must address the middle-manager fear directly — reframing AI as augmenting the manager's team output rather than as a headcount-reduction program, or resistance hardens.

**Step 4 (communicate):** Leaders typically under-communicate an AI shift by roughly an order of magnitude, and silence is read as a threat — especially by managers wondering whether the initiative targets their jobs. The vision must be repeated in every forum, review, and one-on-one. The gate: random sampling confirms employees — including skeptical managers — can accurately restate what the change is *and is not*.

**Step 5 (remove obstacles):** For AI this is the most underinvested step and the one where middle-manager resistance becomes structural rather than merely attitudinal. Blockers to identify and remove: data-access and security review processes that make AI tools unusable on real work; procurement cycles that stall integration; and — most importantly — performance metrics and incentives that reward the old way. If a manager is still measured on headcount managed or hours logged rather than outcomes delivered, they are structurally incentivized to resist. The gate requires three specific blockers removed, not a general "we support AI" statement.

**Step 6 (short-term wins):** Pilots measured on "number of users onboarded" are activity, not wins. Define one unambiguous business outcome, dated at launch — e.g., a specific team cutting cycle time on a real workflow by a target percentage within two quarters, with the measurement system built in beforehand. The win should ideally be owned by a *previously skeptical* team, so its credibility travels to the resistant middle.

**Step 7 (consolidate):** The classic AI-adoption error is declaring victory when a pilot succeeds. A successful pilot is a Step 6 win, not completion — disband the change team and the organization reverts to old workflows. Instead, use the win's credibility to attack the larger obstacles from Step 5 (the incentive and process changes), and hold the stop-rule: wins must trace causally to the new ways of working, not to a motivated pilot team's extra effort.

**Step 8 (anchor in culture):** AI adoption becomes permanent only when it is embedded in the four mechanisms — hiring (AI-fluency in job criteria), promotion (rewarding managers who redesign work around AI, not those who protect legacy processes), onboarding (new joiners learn the AI-native workflow as the default), and KPIs (outcome metrics replace activity metrics). The test: a person who joins two years later experiences the AI-augmented way as simply "how we work here." Until managers are promoted for it rather than despite it, the transformation remains a wish.

**Why middle managers resist, in Kotter terms:** the resistance is not irrational. It concentrates at the exact points the model flags — Step 2 (they are the un-recruited middle), Step 5 (their incentives still reward the old way), and Step 8 (promotion criteria have not changed). Treating it as an attitude problem to be overcome with more town halls (Step 4 alone) is the predictable error; the model locates it as a coalition, obstacle, and anchoring problem.
