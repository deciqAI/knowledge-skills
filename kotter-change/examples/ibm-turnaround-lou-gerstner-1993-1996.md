# Method in Action: IBM's Turnaround under Lou Gerstner (1993–1996)

> *Example for the [kotter-change](../SKILL.md) skill.*

In 1993, IBM reported a net loss of $8 billion — the largest in US corporate history at the time. The company had missed the shift from mainframes to distributed computing. Multiple analysts recommended breaking IBM into separate units. Lou Gerstner was appointed CEO from outside the technology industry — the first outsider in IBM's history.

Kotter's eight steps applied to Gerstner's turnaround:

**Step 1 (urgency):** Gerstner refused to publish a five-year strategic plan for the first six months — famously saying "the last thing IBM needs right now is a vision." Instead he forced the organization to absorb the real financial numbers: 35,000 layoffs in 1993, public acknowledgment of the full loss, and direct customer communication. The urgency was built from *evidence*, not rhetoric.

**Step 2 (coalition):** Gerstner brought in an outsider CFO and CMO, deliberately breaking IBM's "only-from-within" promotion tradition. The new coalition had market credibility that internal promotees lacked; its presence forced the organization to accept that the old rules had changed.

**Steps 3 and 4 (vision and communication):** By late 1993, Gerstner established "IBM will not be broken up; IBM will pivot to services" as the vision. He communicated it through extensive external appearances (analysts, customers, media) and internal road shows personally covering over 100,000 employees.

**Step 5 (remove obstacles):** The main structural obstacles were IBM's internal silos — business units acting as independent competitors. Gerstner imposed cross-unit integration and eliminated unit-level profit measures that incentivized internal competition. This was organizationally painful and required several senior departures.

**Step 6 (short-term wins):** IBM returned to profit in 1994 — one year after Gerstner's arrival. IBM Global Services (then a new unit) was cited as a specific win: from near-zero to $30B in revenue by 1999, demonstrating that the services pivot was real.

**Step 7 (consolidate):** Rather than celebrating the 1994 return to profit as completion, Gerstner used it to accelerate the services buildout, acquire Lotus (1995) and Tivoli (1996), and push deeper organizational integration.

**Step 8 (culture):** Gerstner's memoirs (*Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?*, 2002) document the most difficult step: changing IBM's "we are never wrong and never in trouble" cultural assumption. This required five years of sustained demonstration that the new behaviors produced better results, plus systematic changes to promotion criteria (results over tenure) and hiring (market-facing skills prized).

**Results:** IBM's market capitalization recovered from approximately $30B in 1993 to approximately $160B at Gerstner's departure in 2002. IBM Global Services became the world's largest IT services firm.

Primary source: Gerstner, L.V. (2002). *Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?: Inside IBM's Historic Turnaround.* HarperBusiness.
