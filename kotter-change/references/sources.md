# Sources — kotter-change

> *Primary sources for the [kotter-change](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Kotter, J.P. (1995). "Leading Change: Why Transformation Efforts Fail." *Harvard Business Review*, 73(2), 59-67. One of HBR's most widely reprinted articles; the foundational diagnosis of the eight failure modes. https://hbr.org/1995/05/leading-change-why-transformation-efforts-fail-2
- Kotter, J.P. (1996). *Leading Change.* Harvard Business School Press. The book-length treatment; contains the urgency quote and the full eight-step framework with detailed case studies. ISBN 978-0875847474.
- Kotter, J.P. (2014). *Accelerate: Building Strategic Agility for a Faster-Moving World.* Harvard Business Review Press. The Dual Operating System update; makes the eight steps compatible with agile organizational structures. ISBN 978-1625271747.
- Gerstner, L.V. (2002). *Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?: Inside IBM's Historic Turnaround.* HarperBusiness. The primary-source account of the IBM transformation — a documented case of Kotter's eight steps in sequential practice. ISBN 978-0060523800.
- Kotter, J.P. & Cohen, D.S. (2002). *The Heart of Change.* Harvard Business School Press. Contains primary case material from eight-step implementations across industries; the companion to *Leading Change* with practitioner detail. ISBN 978-1578512546.

**Contemporary context (2024–2026 enterprise AI adoption):**
- McKinsey & Company. *The State of AI* (annual global survey; 2024 and 2025 editions). Widely cited industry source documenting that generative-AI adoption rose sharply while measurable enterprise-wide bottom-line impact lagged, with organizational barriers cited as a leading obstacle to scaling. https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai
- MIT Sloan Management Review / Boston Consulting Group, ongoing "AI and the workforce" research (2024–2025). Reporting on the persistent gap between AI tool deployment and realized value, emphasizing change-management, workflow-redesign, and middle-management adoption as the binding constraints rather than model capability. https://sloanreview.mit.edu/

**What is not cited and why:** Claims that "70% of change initiatives fail" are frequently attributed to Kotter but appear in popularized summaries, not in Kotter's primary research documents. The claim is plausible given the patterns Kotter documented, but the specific percentage is not sourced in his primary works. This skill does not cite that statistic. The IBM transformation numbers are sourced to Gerstner's memoir (primary source), not to secondary accounts.
