# Method in Action: Post-9/11 Flight Avoidance and Road Fatalities (2001–2002)

> *Example for the [availability-heuristic](../SKILL.md) skill.*

After September 11, 2001, millions of Americans made the same private risk calculation: flying now feels dangerous, so drive instead. The attacks were the most vivid, most heavily covered, most imaginable catastrophe in living memory — all five availability triggers firing at once. U.S. domestic air travel dropped sharply in the months that followed, while vehicle-miles driven on interstate highways rose. Psychologist Gerd Gigerenzer later ran the population-scale version of the skill's Process on this episode, and the result is the canonical field demonstration of what availability-driven risk substitution costs in lives.

**Step 1 — Specify the estimate.** The implicit claim made by each traveler: "the probability of dying on a commercial flight is now unacceptably high; driving is the safer choice." Basis: memory of the attacks and saturation media coverage — not data. Decision at stake: mode of travel for trips of a few hundred miles.

**Step 2 — Identify the reference class.** Not "flights on September 11" but *all U.S. commercial flight segments* versus *all equivalent long-distance car trips*, measured over years, per mile or per trip. Getting this wrong was the core public failure: people reasoned from a reference class of one unforgettable day.

**Step 3 — Get the base rate.** The actuarial record was unambiguous. Commercial aviation was so much safer per mile than driving that, as Sivak and Flannagan showed in their 2003 analysis, driving the distance of a typical nonstop flight segment carried a fatality risk many times greater than the flight itself — even after including the September 11 deaths in aviation's toll.

**Step 4 — Diagnose availability distortions.** Recency: the attack had just happened, and a one-day event was treated as the new steady-state flight risk. Vividness and imaginability: a hijacked airliner is dramatic and easy to picture; a fatal highway crash is routine and statistical. Media coverage: months of continuous replay. Gigerenzer framed the reaction as "dread risk" — the special fear of rare events that kill many people at once — which the availability of the attack images amplified far beyond any base-rate change.

**Step 5 — Compare.** Intuitive estimate versus base rate diverged by orders of magnitude, and behavior followed the intuition. Analyzing federal fatal-crash statistics, Gigerenzer (2004) found that fatal traffic accidents in the final months of 2001 ran significantly above the baseline of the preceding years, consistent with travelers substituting road miles for air miles. His follow-up analysis (Gigerenzer, 2006) estimated the excess toll over the twelve months after the attacks at roughly 1,500 additional road deaths — a number exceeding the total passenger deaths aboard the four hijacked aircraft. The availability-driven "safe" choice killed more Americans than the dreaded event it was meant to avoid.

**Step 6 — Document.** The episode is now a standard case in risk-communication curricula: present absolute per-mile fatality frequencies for competing travel modes, name the dread-risk distortion explicitly, and log the prediction ("flying will remain far safer than driving") against outcomes — which it did; U.S. aviation fatality rates stayed near historic lows in the following years.

Note the boundary the skill draws. This was not a case where precautionary weight on a rare catastrophic risk was warranted: the substitute behavior (driving) carried a *higher* expected fatality rate than the feared one (flying), so the precaution itself was the hazard. When the "safe" alternative is measurably deadlier than the vivid risk it replaces, the availability correction is not optional — it is the entire decision.

The case also shows why the structural fix must be institutional, not personal. No individual driver could observe the aggregate death toll of the collective mode shift; only reference-class analysis of national crash statistics made the cost visible, and only years later. Base rates have to be supplied *before* the decision, by whoever holds the data.

The mapped steps:

1. Specify the estimate: "flying is now too dangerous; driving is safer" — based on memory of one vivid event, not data
2. Reference class: all U.S. flight segments vs. equivalent car trips, per mile, over multi-year horizons — not the single day of the attacks
3. Base rate: commercial flight far safer per mile than driving, even counting 9/11 (Sivak & Flannagan, 2003)
4. Distortions: recency, vividness, saturation media coverage, imaginability — compounded by dread risk
5. Compare: intuition and base rate diverged by orders of magnitude; behavior followed intuition, producing an estimated ~1,500 excess road deaths in twelve months
6. Document: absolute-frequency risk communication and the dread-risk label became standard countermeasures

Primary source: Gigerenzer, G. (2004). "Dread Risk, September 11, and Fatal Traffic Accidents." *Psychological Science*, 15(4), 286-287.
