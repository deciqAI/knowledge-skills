# Method in Action: HP's Acquisition of Autonomy (2011)

> *Example for the [winners-curse](../SKILL.md) skill.*

In 2011, Hewlett-Packard agreed to acquire the British enterprise-software company Autonomy for roughly $11.1 billion, a large premium over Autonomy's pre-bid market value. Just over a year later, in November 2012, HP took an **$8.8 billion write-down** on the deal, alleging serious accounting improprieties at Autonomy. Years of litigation followed; courts later found that fraud explained only *part* of the loss and that HP had also simply overpaid — a UK High Court ruling indicated HP would have paid meaningfully less absent the misstatements, but that a large aggressive premium was independently in play. That confound is exactly why this case is instructive for the winner's curse: strip out the fraud, and you still have a contested acquisition where the buyer paid a premium for an asset of highly uncertain value and later concluded it had overpaid.

*Caveat up front:* fraud and the winner's curse are different failures. Autonomy's alleged accounting improprieties are a due-diligence / information-integrity problem, not adverse selection. This example uses the *residual* overpayment — the part courts attributed to an aggressive premium rather than fraud — as the winner's-curse illustration.

**Step 1 — Classify the value.** An acquisition target is largely *common-value*: the shared uncertain quantity is *Autonomy's true standalone cash flows plus the synergies HP could realize*. HP, and any rival suitor, were estimating the same underlying business. Gate passed.

**Step 2 — Count bidders and size dispersion.** Autonomy was an attractive, in-play target; HP was bidding into a competitive landscape for a hot enterprise-software asset, and software valuations carry wide dispersion (growth and synergy assumptions swing valuations by billions). Even a "competition of one that could have been many" imposes the discipline: to win a premium bid is to have the most optimistic view. Wide dispersion demands a large shade.

**Step 3 — Condition on winning.** The winner's-curse question HP should have pressed: *if we are willing to pay this premium when others were not, is that because we see unique synergy — or because we are simply the most optimistic reader of Autonomy's growth and the most trusting of its reported numbers?* Conditioning on "we're the high bidder" should have pulled the working valuation *down* and raised scrutiny of the very financials that justified the premium.

**Step 4 — Value-conditioned ceiling and pre-commit.** A curse-corrected process sets, before the offer, a hard price tied to a *haircut* standalone-plus-synergy valuation — with synergies discounted for the adverse selection of winning and for integration risk. Reports later alleged internal awareness that the price was rich; a pre-committed, value-conditioned ceiling is precisely the instrument that converts "this feels expensive" into "we do not exceed $X."

**Step 5 — Guard against escalation.** HP was under strategic pressure to pivot toward higher-margin software; that "we must transform, and this is the trophy asset" narrative is a classic escalation trap that suppresses walk-away discipline. The circuit-breaker is a ceiling that a strategic-transformation thesis cannot silently override, plus verification of the numbers underpinning the premium *before* committing.

**Bid Discipline Card (reconstructed):**
```
Item: Acquisition of Autonomy Corporation
Value type: common — shared unknown: Autonomy standalone cash flows + realizable synergies
Bidders (N): competitive/in-play target   Dispersion: wide (software growth + synergy assumptions)
Unconditional value estimate: HP's synergy-laden valuation
Condition-on-winning discount: haircut synergies + integration-risk + adverse-selection
Conditioned value: standalone value + discounted synergies
--------------------------------------------------
BID CEILING (pre-committed): conditioned value + defensible strategic premium
Escalation trap: "we must transform into software — this is the trophy"
Circuit-breaker: verify the financials behind the premium; do not exceed the cap
```

**What it explains.** Even setting fraud aside, the pattern is the winner's curse in M&A: a buyer willing to pay a premium that rivals would not, over an uncertain synergy case, ends up owning the deals it most over-valued — and writing them off. The $8.8bn write-down became the emblem of the risk. The discipline is not "value the target more precisely"; it is "recognize that a premium bid *means* you were the most optimistic, and shade the synergy case accordingly — while separately verifying the numbers you were handed."

The mapped steps:
1. Classify: common-value — shared unknown is standalone cash flows + synergies
2. Count/disperse: in-play target, wide software-valuation dispersion → shade
3. Condition on winning: a premium others won't pay signals you're the most optimistic — discount
4. Ceiling: cap at haircut valuation + defensible premium, pre-committed, financials verified
5. Escalation guard: don't let "must-transform" narrative override the cap
