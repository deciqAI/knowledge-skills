# Sources — winners-curse

> *Primary sources for the [winners-curse](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Capen, E. C., Clapp, R. V., & Campbell, W. M. (1971). "Competitive Bidding in High-Risk Situations." *Journal of Petroleum Technology*, 23(6), 641–653. The paper that coined "winner's curse," by three ARCO petroleum engineers analyzing offshore oil-lease auctions where winners earned poor returns. Verbatim: *"If it is true, as common sense tells us, that a lease winner tends to be the bidder who most overestimates reserves potential, it follows that the 'successful' bidders may not have been so successful after all."* https://onepetro.org/JPT/article/23/06/641/163651/Competitive-Bidding-in-High-Risk-Situations
- Thaler, R. H. (1988). "Anomalies: The Winner's Curse." *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 2(1), 191–202. The accessible survey that established the winner's curse as a robust behavioral anomaly across lab and field, and its bid-shading remedy. https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.2.1.191
- Harrison, G. W., & List, J. A. (2008). "Naturally Occurring Markets and Exogenous Laboratory Experiments: A Case Study of the Winner's Curse." *The Economic Journal*, 118(528), 822–843. Field-experiment evidence that experienced agents avoid the curse in *familiar* roles but fall prey to it in unfamiliar ones — experience inoculates only within the exact context it was earned. https://ideas.repec.org/a/ecj/econjl/v118y2008i528p822-843.html
- Kagel, J. H., & Levin, D. (2002). *Common Value Auctions and the Winner's Curse.* Princeton University Press. The definitive experimental synthesis: the curse persists even among experienced bidders, and grows worse as the number of bidders rises. https://press.princeton.edu/books/ebook/9781400830138/common-value-auctions-and-the-winners-curse
- Milgrom, P., & Weber, R. J. (1982). "A Theory of Auctions and Competitive Bidding." *Econometrica*, 50(5), 1089–1122. The formal common-value auction theory underpinning the "condition on winning" and "more bidders ⇒ shade more" results. https://doi.org/10.2307/1911865
- Klemperer, P. (2002). "How (Not) to Run Auctions: The European 3G Telecom Auctions." *European Economic Review*, 46(4–5), 829–845. Analysis of the 2000–2001 3G spectrum auctions, a real-world common-value bidding episode. Context for the 3G example.
- Binmore, K., & Klemperer, P. (2002). "The Biggest Auction Ever: The Sale of the British 3G Telecom Licences." *The Economic Journal*, 112(478), C74–C96. Documents the UK 3G auction (~£22.5bn). https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1468-0297.00020
- American Association of Petroleum Geologists, "Bid Winners Can Face 'Curse'." Industry account of the Capen/Clapp/Campbell origin and ARCO's bidding discipline. Context for the oil-lease example. https://www.aapg.org/news-and-media/explorer/bid-winners-can-face-curse/

*Not cited for quotes:* Thaler (1988) is cited for its thesis and existence but not quoted verbatim, because the primary PDF was access-restricted at authoring time and no verified verbatim passage was in hand. The "more bidders ⇒ shade more" claim is attributed to the qualitative reasoning in Capen et al. (1971) and the formal Milgrom–Weber (1982) framework rather than to a fabricated equation or page number. The HP–Autonomy example draws on contemporary press and court reporting (linked in that example file), not on a single academic primary source, and explicitly separates the fraud confound from the winner's-curse mechanism.
