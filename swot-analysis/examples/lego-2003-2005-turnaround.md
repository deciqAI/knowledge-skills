# Method in Action: LEGO's Near-Bankruptcy Turnaround (2003–2005)

> *Example for the [swot-analysis](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By 2003 the LEGO Group was close to collapse: sales had fallen sharply, the company posted the largest loss in its history, and a decade of diversification — theme parks, television, video games, clothing, electronic toys — had buried the core business. The turnaround led by Jørgen Vig Knudstorp (CEO from October 2004) and CFO Jesper Ovesen, documented in detail by Robertson, maps cleanly onto a SWOT-then-TOWS reconstruction:

**Step 1 — Unit and purpose:** The LEGO Group as of 2003–2004; decision: how to restore profitability and decide which businesses to keep, fix, or exit. Competitors were identifiable: Mega Bloks on price, video-game makers on children's attention.

**Step 2 — Strengths (internal+):** (S1) The brick system itself — an interlocking, backward-compatible platform competitors could imitate but not replicate as an installed base; (S2) one of the world's most trusted toy brands among parents; (S3) a passionate adult fan community (AFOLs) producing designs and advocacy at no cost to the company; (S4) proven story-driven franchises (Bionicle, licensed Star Wars sets) demonstrating the brick could carry narrative play.

**Step 3 — Weaknesses (internal−):** (W1) Runaway component complexity — the palette of unique elements had roughly doubled in the diversification years, inflating molds, inventory, and cost of goods (remediable); (W2) management could not tell which sets or product lines actually made money — costing systems lagged the sprawl (remediable); (W3) capital tied up in businesses far from the core competence, notably the LEGOLAND parks (structural, so long as they stayed owned); (W4) innovation culture that rewarded novelty over profitability.

**Step 4 — Opportunities (external+):** (O1) Sustained retailer and parent demand for classic construction play — the core category had not shrunk even as LEGO's share of it had; (O2) licensed entertainment properties releasing on a predictable film cycle; (O3) internet-organized fan communities willing to co-develop products, as later exploited with the Mindstorms NXT user panel.

**Step 5 — Threats (external−):** (T1) Children "getting older younger," migrating to video games earlier (probability H, impact H); (T2) lower-priced clone competitors such as Mega Bloks after key patent protections had lapsed (H/M); (T3) consolidated mass retailers squeezing margins and punishing unreliable suppliers (M/H).

**Step 6 — TOWS:** The decisive SO strategy was S1×O1 — "back to the brick": concentrate investment on core lines (City, Duplo, Creator) where the platform advantage was strongest. S3×O3 drove structured fan co-creation rather than treating AFOLs as a curiosity. The ST strategy was S2×T2 — defend against clones on quality, precision, and brand trust rather than price. The WO strategy was W1×O2 — cut the element palette roughly in half and impose profitability discipline on designers, so licensed lines produced margin instead of mold proliferation. The WT strategy was W3×T1 — sell the controlling stake in the LEGOLAND parks (2005) to stop the cash bleed and refocus capital while the attention war with video games intensified.

**Step 7 — Prioritize:** Knudstorp's sequencing was explicit: survive first (manage for cash, sell the parks, cut complexity), then restore profitability in the core, and only then resume growth. Growth initiatives and most non-core businesses were deliberately deprioritized — a textbook case of an explicit "not prioritizing" list rather than pursuing every quadrant at once.

**Validation:** LEGO returned to profit in 2005 and grew through the following decade into the world's most profitable toy maker — evidence that the strategy came from the cross-pairs, not the list.

The mapped steps:
1. Unit and purpose: LEGO Group, 2003–2004 — which businesses to keep, fix, or exit
2. Strengths: brick platform, brand trust, fan community, story-capable franchises
3. Weaknesses: element sprawl, invisible unit economics, capital in non-core assets
4. Opportunities: intact core demand, licensed film cycle, fan co-creation
5. Threats: video games, post-patent clones, retailer consolidation — each rated
6. TOWS: back-to-the-brick (SO), quality defense (ST), complexity cut (WO), parks divestiture (WT)
7. Prioritize: survival → core profitability → growth, with explicit deprioritization

Primary source: Robertson, D., with Breen, B. (2013). *Brick by Brick: How LEGO Rewrote the Rules of Innovation and Conquered the Global Toy Industry.* Crown Business.
