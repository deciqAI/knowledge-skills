# Method in Action: An Incumbent Seat-Based SaaS Vendor Faces the AI Agent Shift (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [swot-analysis](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between 2024 and 2026 a familiar strategic tension came to a head across established software-as-a-service companies: AI became simultaneously the biggest *opportunity* (new features, new value) and the biggest *threat* (commoditization of the core product, and pressure on the per-seat pricing model that generated the recurring revenue in the first place). This worked example runs a representative incumbent — a mature, publicly traded "seat-based SaaS" vendor (think a horizontal productivity, CRM, support, or collaboration suite sold per named user) — through this skill's process. Figures are kept qualitative and directional on purpose; the point is the *structure* of the audit, not a specific company's internal numbers.

**Step 1 — Unit and purpose:** The incumbent's flagship seat-priced product line as of 2024–2026; decision: how to respond to AI-native entrants and to AI's downward pressure on seat-based pricing — which capabilities to build, buy, or defend, and whether to change the pricing model. The unit is boundable and competitors are identifiable: incumbent platform peers adding AI copilots, and a wave of well-funded AI-native startups attacking single workflows.

**Step 2 — Strengths (internal+):** (S1) A large installed base under multi-year enterprise contracts and deep workflow integration — the switching cost is real, not rhetorical; (S2) proprietary customer data and usage history that can ground and personalize AI features in ways a cold-start entrant cannot; (S3) enterprise trust assets — security certifications, compliance posture, procurement relationships, and an existing sales motion into IT buyers; (S4) distribution: the ability to ship an AI feature to millions of existing seats overnight rather than acquiring users one at a time. *Regress-test:* "AI is exciting" is **not** a Strength — every competitor has access to the same foundation models. The Strength is the data, the distribution, and the installed base that the models attach to.

**Step 3 — Weaknesses (internal−):** (W1) The revenue model itself: pricing per human seat is structurally exposed if AI agents do work that once required more humans, or if buyers consolidate seats — this is closer to *structural* than remediable, because it is the mechanism of the recurring revenue; (W2) dependency on third-party foundation-model providers for frontier capability, creating margin and roadmap risk (partially remediable via multi-model strategy); (W3) legacy architecture and a large feature surface that slows shipping relative to a focused startup (remediable over 12–24 months, at cost); (W4) an incentive to protect existing seat revenue that can delay the very cannibalization the market is about to force anyway (an organizational Weakness, surfaced by honest win/loss review, not by management self-assessment).

**Step 4 — Opportunities (external+):** (O1) Rapidly falling inference cost and rising model capability across roughly 2023–2025 (the cost of a GPT-3.5-equivalent query fell more than 280-fold between late 2022 and late 2024, per the Stanford HAI AI Index 2025) made AI features economically shippable at scale — a condition that exists for the whole market, independent of any one vendor; (O2) enterprise buyers moved from AI experimentation toward wanting AI *embedded in tools they already run and trust*, favoring vendors already inside the account; (O3) the growing acceptance of outcome- and usage-based pricing in enterprise SaaS opened a path to charge for AI work delivered, not just seats occupied; (O4) agent interoperability standards (e.g., the Model Context Protocol, open-sourced by Anthropic in late 2024) lowered the cost of connecting AI systems to existing tools and data.

**Step 5 — Threats (external−):** (T1) Commoditization — if the differentiating feature becomes "a chat box over your data," a foundation-model provider or a platform owner can offer it natively, eroding the incumbent's premium (probability H, impact H); (T2) AI-native startups attacking one high-value workflow with a product built agent-first, unburdened by legacy or by seat-revenue defensiveness (H/M–H); (T3) the pricing-model threat crossing over from Weakness to external pressure: customers and analysts openly questioning per-seat SaaS economics in an agent era, compressing renewal expansion (H/H); (T4) dependence on a small number of frontier-model providers who are also potential competitors and who control cost and access (M/H).

**Step 6 — TOWS cross-matrix:**
- **SO (S2×O2):** Ship AI features grounded in the customer's own data and workflow *inside the existing product*, converting the data-plus-trust advantage into features a cold-start entrant cannot match on day one. Distribution (S4) turns this from a bet into a fait accompli — release to the installed base at once.
- **ST (S1×T2):** Use the installed base and switching costs to buy time against AI-native attackers, while fast-following on the specific workflow they target — the incumbent does not need to win the demo, only to be "good enough plus already deployed."
- **WO (W1×O3):** Address the seat-pricing Weakness by *leading* the shift to usage/outcome pricing for AI work rather than being dragged into it — capture revenue from AI value delivered so growth is no longer hostage to seat count. **Window check:** the WO strategy is viable only if the pricing transition ships before renewal cycles reprice the account against a cheaper AI-native alternative; if the transition takes 24 months but competitive repricing hits in 12, the WO strategy fails and must be resequenced.
- **WT (W2×T4):** Reduce single-provider dependence with a multi-model architecture and an abstraction layer, so no one foundation-model vendor controls the incumbent's cost, roadmap, or competitive fate.

**Step 7 — Prioritize:** Top choices: (1) the **SO** embedded-AI play — highest value, fastest signal, leverages the strongest assets; (2) the **WO** pricing-model transition — the highest-stakes move, because it directly defuses the structural Weakness *and* the T3 pricing Threat, but must be sequenced against the renewal-cycle window; (3) the **WT** multi-model hedge — lower glamour, but it protects margin and optionality. *Not prioritizing:* pure defensive protection of legacy seat revenue (WT-style entrenchment) — the honest reading is that the seat model will be cannibalized by someone, so the strategic choice is to cannibalize it yourself on your terms rather than defend it into decline. Review cadence: re-rate T1 and T3 each renewal cycle and on any major foundation-model or platform-native launch, because a Threat rated H/H can shift timing quarter to quarter.

**Why this is the honest version:** The tempting but wrong SWOT lists "AI" as a Strength and stops at a feature roadmap. The disciplined version keeps AI where it belongs — an external **Opportunity available to everyone** and, in the same breath, an external **Threat** — and forces the uncomfortable admission that the company's own pricing model is a **Weakness**. The strategy then falls out of the cross-pairs (embed via the data moat; reprice before the window closes; hedge the model dependency), not out of the list.

The mapped steps:
1. Unit and purpose: incumbent's seat-priced product line, 2024–2026 — build/buy/defend and whether to reprice
2. Strengths: installed base and switching costs, proprietary data, enterprise trust, instant distribution
3. Weaknesses: seat-pricing model (structural), model-provider dependency, legacy velocity, revenue-defensiveness
4. Opportunities: cheap capable inference, buyer demand for embedded AI, growing usage-pricing acceptance, agent interop standards
5. Threats: commoditization, AI-native workflow attackers, per-seat pricing pressure, provider concentration — each rated
6. TOWS: embed-AI-on-data (SO), installed-base fast-follow (ST), lead the pricing shift (WO), multi-model hedge (WT)
7. Prioritize: embed → reprice-within-window → hedge, with explicit refusal to defend the seat model into decline
