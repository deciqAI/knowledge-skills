# Method in Action: Designing an AI Product That Finishes the Job, Not the Demo (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [design-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By 2024–2025, the generative-AI product landscape had a recognizable failure pattern: a wave of "chat with our AI" features shipped on top of large language models, and a wave of pilots that impressed in demos but stalled in real use. Industry surveys through 2024–2025 repeatedly reported that a large share of enterprise generative-AI pilots did not reach production, and consumer AI chat products showed high early sign-up followed by weak retention. The common shape: the technology worked, and users still did not adopt. That is a desirability failure, not a feasibility failure — exactly the condition design thinking is built for.

Consider a team building an AI assistant for a knowledge-work task (say, drafting and reconciling routine reports). The engineering-first instinct is to expose a bigger, more capable chat box. The design-thinking path is to observe first.

**Stage 1 (Empathize):** Sit beside real users and watch them use the current generic chatbot in context — not a survey, not a satisfaction score. The recurring behavioral observation across many 2024–2025 workflows: users open the chat, paste in a fragment, get a plausible-looking answer, and then quietly do the actual work themselves in another tab — copying, correcting, re-checking against the source system. The say/do gap is stark: users *say* the AI is "helpful" and *rate* it well, while their observed behavior is that they do not trust it to finish anything and treat its output as a first draft they must fully rework. The most surprising finding is that the blank chat box itself is a burden — users do not want to compose prompts; they want the job done.

**Stage 2 (Define):** The prior framing was "How might we make our chatbot answer more questions?" — a solution disguised as a problem. The reframe after Empathize: **"How might we let a user hand off a whole task and trust that it was actually completed correctly?"** Same underlying need, radically different solution space. This moves the target from *capability demonstration* (the model can talk about the task) to *job completion* (the task is done, verified, and safe to accept).

**Stage 3 (Ideate):** Diverge before converging. Generate many concepts against the reframed HMW — not just "better answers" but different shapes of hand-off: an agent that reads the source systems directly, a review-and-approve surface, a "show your work" trace so the human can verify without redoing, guardrails that refuse rather than guess, a way to correct the agent once and have the correction stick. Cluster and dot-vote; carry three to five forward. The discipline: no evaluation during generation, and no concept that is really "add more chat."

**Stage 4 (Prototype):** Build the minimum artifact that tests the core assumption — that users will hand off and accept an agent's completed work. The learning question is explicit: *when the agent completes the whole task and shows its trace, do users accept the output and move on, or do they still redo it by hand?* Fidelity is kept low: a scripted agentic workflow over one real task, with a verification/trace view, not a polished product. No effort is spent making the model marginally smarter until the hand-off behavior is validated.

**Stage 5 (Test):** Define hypotheses and observe rather than defend. H1: users hand the task off instead of doing it themselves. H2: the trace/verification surface is enough for them to trust and accept the result. H3: users prefer this to the generic chat box. Watch for the confusion and abandonment moments, not the compliments — "users loved the demo" is insufficient. The behavioral signal that would confirm the concept is the same class of signal as a child asking to re-enter the MRI: a user who stops opening the other tab to redo the work, and who returns to the agent for the next instance of the job. Iterate within a day; run two or three full cycles.

The 2026 lesson: in an era of enormous AI capex and AI-native competition, the scarce advantage is not model capability — that is increasingly commoditized and rented. The advantage is desirability: designing the hand-off, the trust surface, and the completed-job experience that observation — not the demo — proves people will actually adopt.
