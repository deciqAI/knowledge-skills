# Method in Action: GE Healthcare — Pediatric MRI Redesign (2007)

> *Example for the [design-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

In 2007, GE Healthcare conducted a study across major US pediatric hospitals and found that more than 40% of children aged 3–10 required sedation for MRI scans because they could not remain still. Sedation carried medical risk and added $7,000–$12,000 per scan in anesthesiologist costs.

The engineering-first response was to improve MRI hardware to shorten scan times. The design thinking response, led by GE designer Doug Dietz, was to spend time observing children in the actual MRI environment before designing anything.

**Stage 1 (Empathize):** Dietz stood in the hallway of a pediatric radiology ward and observed, for the first time from a child's perspective, what the MRI environment actually looked like: a massive white machine with loud mechanical sounds, chemical smells, and a clinical white corridor. He watched a child walk toward the scanner and stop, terrified. The observation that changed everything: the same children who were playing happily in the waiting room entered the MRI corridor and became a completely different kind of afraid. The environment was the trigger.

**Stage 2 (Define):** The prior problem framing: "How might we shorten scan time to reduce the need for sedation?" Dietz's reframe after Empathize: **"How might we make the MRI experience something children look forward to rather than fear?"** This is a design thinking Define-stage reframe: same underlying need, radically different solution space.

**Stage 3 (Ideate) and Stage 4 (Prototype):** Dietz assembled a cross-disciplinary team: a storyteller, interior designers, hospital nurses, child educators. The team generated multiple concepts — not just improving the machine but transforming the environment. Prototyped six themed experiences: pirate ship, space adventure, tropical jungle. The MRI machine was wrapped in thematic scenery; the procedure became a narrative ("you must stay very still so the pirates don't hear us").

**Stage 5 (Test):** The prototypes were tested with actual children in actual hospitals. The behavioral observation that confirmed the concept: children asked to go again after the scan was complete.

**Results:** Sedation rates fell from 40% to 3.3% — a 92% reduction. Patient satisfaction scores reached the 99th percentile. Per-scan cost savings of $6,000–$10,000 (versus the cost of anesthesia). Prototype cost per room: approximately $3,000. GE deployed the Adventure Series to 100+ hospitals globally.

The critical design thinking lesson: a 7-minute observation of a child walking into an MRI room produced an insight — the environment is the problem, not the machine — that decades of engineering-focused product development had never surfaced.

Primary source: Brown, T. (2008). "Design Thinking." *Harvard Business Review*, 86(6), 84–92; GE Healthcare Adventure Series documentation.
