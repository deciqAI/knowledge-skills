# Sources — design-thinking

> *Primary sources for the [design-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Brown, T. (2008). "Design Thinking." *Harvard Business Review*, 86(6), 84-92. The foundational commercial definition of design thinking and the three-lens framework (desirability, feasibility, viability). https://hbr.org/2008/06/design-thinking
- Cross, N. (1982). "Designerly Ways of Knowing." *Design Studies*, 3(4), 221-227. The epistemological basis for design thinking as a distinct knowledge-generating mode — abductive rather than analytic or creative. https://doi.org/10.1016/0142-694X(82)90040-0
- Simon, H.A. (1969). *The Sciences of the Artificial.* MIT Press. The conceptual foundation: design as the act of "changing existing situations into preferred ones"; frames design as a cognitive and problem-solving activity. ISBN 978-0262691914.
- IDEO (2015). *The Field Guide to Human-Centered Design.* IDEO.org. The practitioner manual for the five-stage process; contains methods documentation for each stage. Available at https://www.designkit.org/resources/1.html
- Liedtka, J. (2011). "Why Design Thinking Works." *Harvard Business Review*, 96(5), 72-79. Empirical analysis of design thinking's effectiveness in organizational contexts; documents why the method succeeds where analytical approaches fail. https://hbr.org/2018/09/why-design-thinking-works

**What is not cited and why:** The "Stanford d.school 5-stage model" is frequently cited as the primary source for design thinking's five stages, but the d.school's instructional materials are pedagogical documents, not primary research. The epistemological and methodological foundations are grounded here in Cross (1982) and Simon (1969), which are peer-reviewed primary sources. The GE Healthcare Adventure Series is documented through Brown's HBR article and GE Healthcare corporate communications — it is not independently verified in peer-reviewed research, but it is cited here with appropriate attribution.
