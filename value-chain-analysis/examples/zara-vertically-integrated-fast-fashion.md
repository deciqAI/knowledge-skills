# Method in Action: Zara's Vertically Integrated Fast-Fashion Value Chain (1990s–2003)

> *Example for the [value-chain-analysis](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By the early 2000s the apparel industry had converged on a standard value chain: design collections months in advance, outsource nearly all manufacturing to low-cost Asian contractors, ship by sea, and spend 3–4% of revenue on advertising to move the inventory. Zara (Inditex) reconfigured almost every activity in that chain — and the Harvard Business School case documenting it shows why activity-level analysis, not industry benchmarking, explains the result.

**Step 1 — Activity map:** Zara's primary activities diverged from the industry template at nearly every module. Inbound Logistics: fabric sourced largely undyed, allowing late-stage color decisions. Operations: roughly half of production made in-house or in nearby facilities in Spain, Portugal, and Morocco — against an industry norm of near-total outsourcing to Asia. Outbound Logistics: a centralized distribution hub in Arteixo, Galicia, with twice-weekly deliveries to every store worldwide, using air freight where speed demanded it. Marketing and Sales: advertising spend far below the industry's 3–4% of revenue; prime-location stores and constant assortment rotation served as the marketing. Service: store managers fed daily sales and customer-reaction data back to designers.

**Step 2 — Cost allocation:** On a per-garment basis Zara's Operations costs were *higher* than rivals sourcing from Asia — European labor is more expensive. A naive benchmark would flag this as value leakage. The activity-level view shows the offset: markdowns. Industry players who committed to collections six to nine months ahead routinely marked down a large share of stock; Zara, designing and delivering in roughly two to three weeks, sold far more at full price and marked down roughly half as much of its inventory as European competitors.

**Step 3 — Differentiation sources:** The genuine source was not any single activity but speed-to-market as a system property: design-to-shelf lead times measured in weeks rather than months. Customer evidence: shoppers visited Zara stores far more frequently than competitors' stores because assortments changed continuously, and scarcity (small batches, deliberate stockouts) converted visits into full-price purchases.

**Step 4 — Competitor comparison:** Against Hennes & Mauritz (H&M), Gap, and Benetton, Zara had structurally higher manufacturing cost per unit but structurally lower markdown losses, lower advertising cost, and lower inventory risk. Competitors' outsourced chains were cheaper per activity yet slower end-to-end — a comparison invisible without per-activity mapping.

**Step 5 — Key linkages:** The decisive linkages: (a) in-house Operations × Outbound Logistics — owning nearby factories made twice-weekly small-batch replenishment feasible; (b) Service × Technology Development — store-level demand signals flowed directly into design, so new styles responded to what sold last week; (c) Operations × Marketing — fresh assortments substituted for advertising spend. Optimizing any activity in isolation (e.g., outsourcing production to cut unit cost) would have destroyed the linkage that produced the margin.

**Step 6 — Strategic disposition:** Zara internalized the activities where speed created differentiation (design, dyeing/finishing, distribution) and accepted higher unit costs in Operations as an investment in the linkage — exactly the "high-cost but essential linkage to differentiated activity" disposition. Commodity basics with stable demand were still outsourced to Asia. The case reports Inditex earning operating margins above its major fast-fashion rivals while spending a fraction of their advertising budgets.

The mapped steps:
1. Map activities: every module specified against the industry template — in-house production, centralized Arteixo hub, near-zero advertising
2. Allocate cost: higher per-unit Operations cost, offset by markdown and inventory losses roughly half the industry rate
3. Differentiation source: weeks-not-months lead time, evidenced by visit frequency and full-price sell-through
4. Competitor comparison: H&M/Gap/Benetton cheaper per activity, slower end-to-end
5. Linkages: nearby factories × twice-weekly logistics × store-level demand feedback — the system, not any single activity
6. Disposition: internalize speed-critical activities, accept linked cost, outsource commodity basics

Primary source: Ghemawat, P., & Nueno, J.L. (2003). "ZARA: Fast Fashion." Harvard Business School Case 703-497. Boston: Harvard Business School Publishing.
