# Method in Action: Graham's Analysis of Northern Pipeline (1926)

> *Example for the [industry-learning-sprint](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Source:** Graham, B. & Dodd, D., *Security Analysis*, McGraw-Hill, 1934. https://www.amazon.com/Security-Analysis-Foreword-Buffett-Editions/dp/0071592539; also documented in Lowe, J., *Benjamin Graham on Value Investing*, Dearborn Financial Publishing, 1994.

In 1926, Benjamin Graham was a relatively unknown analyst when he undertook a financial structure study of Northern Pipeline Company, one of the Standard Oil successor companies. The company's stock was trading at approximately $65 per share. Graham's analysis followed exactly the 3-step sequence:

**Step 1 — Financial structure mapping:** Graham read Northern Pipeline's annual report filed with the Interstate Commerce Commission (the regulatory filing of the era). The financial structure revealed something that the company's narrative presentation had entirely obscured: Northern Pipeline held **$95 per share in railroad bonds** — high-grade investment securities completely unrelated to its pipeline operations. These assets were idle capital, earning modest returns, and were not required for the pipeline business.

**Step 2 — Expert dialogue:** Graham approached the company's management and board of directors directly, presenting his financial structure finding and arguing that the excess capital should be returned to shareholders as a special dividend. Management initially refused, arguing that the capital was necessary for business stability — the standard rationalization for capital hoarding. Graham then spoke with other major shareholders and documented the structural argument.

**Step 3 — Unique view formation:** Graham's non-consensus hypothesis was specific and contested: Northern Pipeline was worth $95 per share in liquidation value alone, before assigning any value to its operating business, and the market price of $65 was irrational. His view was explicitly contested by management. He calculated what would falsify the view: if management could demonstrate a credible business purpose for the railroad bonds.

The outcome: Graham accumulated shares, presented his analysis at the 1927 shareholder meeting, and eventually succeeded in pressuring Northern Pipeline to distribute $70 per share in a special dividend. The case is documented in *Security Analysis* as a foundational example of financial structure analysis producing a non-consensus view that management and market consensus had missed entirely.

The mechanism is the same as the sprint: financial reports first revealed the hidden structure; expert/management dialogue stress-tested the hypothesis; the unique view was specific, falsifiable, and contested.
