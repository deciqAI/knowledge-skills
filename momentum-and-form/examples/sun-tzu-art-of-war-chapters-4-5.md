# Method in Action: Sun Tzu's *Art of War*, Chapters 4–5

> *Example for the [momentum-and-form](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The form-momentum framework has a single founding text — *Sunzi Bingfa* (《孙子兵法》, *Sun Tzu's Art of War*) — and within that text, two specific chapters that contain its core formulation: Chapter 4, 《形篇》 (*Xíng* / "Tactical Dispositions" / "Form"), and Chapter 5, 《势篇》 (*Shì* / "Strategic Configuration of Power" / "Momentum"). The text was composed in the Warring States period of ancient China, traditionally attributed to Sun Wu (孙武) c. 5th century BCE. The standard modern scholarly text is based on the 1972 archaeological discovery at Yinqueshan (银雀山), Linyi, Shandong Province, of a Han-dynasty (2nd century BCE) bamboo-slip manuscript that predates the Song-dynasty standardized edition by more than a millennium.

The two-chapter structure is itself the framework. Sun Tzu treats *xíng* (Chapter 4) and *shì* (Chapter 5) as a pair — distinct enough to require separate chapters, but related closely enough that they must be reasoned about together. The text is not abstract philosophy; it is a strategic-operations manual, written for commanders making concrete decisions under conditions of uncertainty and asymmetric information. Its observations about how victory actually emerges from the interaction of structural and configurational advantage have been studied by military commanders from Cao Cao (3rd century CE, who wrote the most influential historical commentary) through Mao Zedong (whose military writings draw heavily on Sun Tzu) to modern American military doctrine (the Marine Corps' *Warfighting* manual cites Sun Tzu explicitly).

The canonical statements of the form-momentum framework appear in five passages, three from Chapter 4 (*xíng*) and two from Chapter 5 (*shì*).

From Chapter 4, on form:

> "昔之善战者,先为不可胜,以待敌之可胜。不可胜在己,可胜在敌。"

> *"The skilled warriors of old first made themselves invincible, and then waited for the enemy to become vulnerable. Invincibility lies in oneself; the enemy's vulnerability lies in the enemy."*

— *Sun Tzu's Art of War* (《孙子兵法》), Chapter 4 (《形篇》). English translation following Roger T. Ames (1993), *Sun-Tzu: The Art of Warfare*, Ballantine Books, based on the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo-slip manuscript. ISBN 978-0345379917.

This is the founding statement of form-priority. The skilled commander does not chase the enemy's mistakes; he first builds a structural position the enemy cannot defeat, and then waits for the configurational moment when the enemy's structural position becomes exploitable. **Invincibility is your responsibility; vulnerability is the enemy's.** In modern business terms: building the form (capital, product, distribution, brand) is something you control; the moment when a competitor becomes vulnerable is something you must wait for and recognize.

Sun Tzu elaborates:

> "故善战者,立于不败之地,而不失敌之败也。"

> *"Therefore the skilled warrior establishes himself on ground where he cannot lose, and does not miss the opportunity to defeat the enemy."*

— *Sun Tzu's Art of War*, Chapter 4.

**You first secure non-defeat; then you act on the chance for victory.** Modern translations: building cash reserves before pursuing growth; building distribution before pursuing brand; building product depth before pursuing scale. The sequence matters. Companies that pursue victory (growth, market share, acquisition) before building non-defeat (capital, product, organizational capability) lose at the first configurational shift.

From Chapter 5, on momentum:

> "激水之疾,至于漂石者,势也。"

> *"When rushing water displaces boulders, it is because of momentum (势)."*

— *Sun Tzu's Art of War*, Chapter 5 (《势篇》).

This is the image that has organized strategic thinking about configurational power for 2,500 years. Water, by itself, is weak; a boulder, by itself, is heavy. But water under the right configurational conditions — narrow channel, steep slope, sufficient volume, accumulated speed — displaces the boulder. *The water did not become heavier; the configuration of its release became sufficient.* The strategist's question is therefore not "how do I increase my mass?" but "how do I find or build the configuration that converts what I have into kinetic effect?"

The most operationally consequential passage is Sun Tzu's specific instruction about how the skilled commander uses 势:

> "故善战者,求之于势,不责于人,故能择人而任势。"

> *"Therefore the skilled commander seeks victory from configuration (势), not from individuals (人) — so he selects men and lets the configuration carry them."*

— *Sun Tzu's Art of War*, Chapter 5.

This is the diagnostic claim that makes the framework operationally sharp. **The skilled commander does not put the load on individual heroism. He builds or finds a configuration where the work mostly happens by structure, and then assigns ordinary capable people to roles where the structure does the work.** This inverts a great deal of modern Western leadership rhetoric, which often centers on finding "10x talent." Sun Tzu's framework treats the requirement-for-heroic-talent as a *diagnostic failure of the configuration* — a sign that the strategist has not yet found the right 势.

Sun Tzu's culminating image for momentum:

> "故善战人之势,如转圆石于千仞之山者,势也。"

> *"The momentum of one skilled in war is like that of rolling round stones from a mountain a thousand fathoms high — that is what we mean by 势."*

— *Sun Tzu's Art of War*, Chapter 5.

The image is exact: round stones rolling down a steep mountain do not require pushing. Once the configuration is set — round shape, steep slope, sufficient height — the work happens. **The strategist's contribution was in *establishing the configuration*, not in *exerting force at the moment of release*. The hardest work in strategy is configurational; the work at the moment of contact, in a well-configured engagement, is light.**

Five points worth internalizing.

**First, form and momentum are distinct and require separate analysis.** Most modern strategic discussion conflates them into "competitive advantage" or "the moat." Sun Tzu's two-chapter structure is itself the argument that they must be reasoned about separately. **A company with strong form but adverse momentum needs to reposition the form, not build more form. A company with strong momentum but weak form needs to build form rapidly under the momentum, not double-down on momentum.** The two diagnoses lead to opposite actions.

**Second, the form-priority sequence is non-negotiable.** "First make yourself invincible, then wait for vulnerability." In modern startup terms: **capital reserves before growth; product depth before scale; distribution before brand; organizational capability before strategic bets.** Companies that pursue wins before building non-defeat lose at the first configurational shift, and the shift always comes.

**Third, the *shì* test is the most operationally powerful sentence in the framework.** *"The skilled commander seeks victory from configuration, not from individuals."* This is the test you can apply to any proposed strategy: *if it requires heroic individual performance to succeed, the configuration is wrong.* The fix is not to find better individuals; it is to find or build the configuration where ordinary capable individuals can succeed.

**Fourth, momentum is buildable, not just discoverable.** Sun Tzu's strategist also *builds* configuration: positions troops to create slope; chooses terrain to channel water; times engagement to maximize configurational force. In modern business: building network effects creates configuration; building two-sided market liquidity creates configuration; creating the platform that competitors must build on creates configuration. **You can wait for 势, but you can also build 势.**

**Fifth, the framework is empirically tested at unusual depth.** Sun Tzu's text has been studied and applied by serious commanders for 2,500 years across radically different military, economic, and political contexts. Modern business strategists — Mintzberg, Porter, Christensen, the platform-economy theorists — have engaged with the framework and produced parallel formulations rather than refuting it. **The convergence is itself evidence of underlying validity.**

The *Art of War* is approximately 6,000 Chinese characters across thirteen chapters, of which Chapters 4 and 5 together are about 700 characters. From this compact text emerges one of the most enduring frameworks in the history of strategic thought.
