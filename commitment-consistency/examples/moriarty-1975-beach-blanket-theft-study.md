# Method in Action: Moriarty's Beach Blanket Theft Experiments (1975)

> *Example for the [commitment-consistency](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Thomas Moriarty's 1975 field experiments demonstrate the commitment-consistency dynamic in a domain far from sales funnels and onboarding flows: bystander intervention against crime. A single verbal "yes," given seconds earlier, transformed passive onlookers into people willing to physically confront a thief.

**The setup.** On a crowded New York beach, a confederate placed a blanket near a randomly selected sunbather, turned on a portable radio, and after a few minutes walked away. A second confederate then approached, grabbed the radio, and hurried off — a staged theft in plain view of the subject. The bystander literature predicted what happened in the control condition: only 4 of 20 subjects did anything to stop the thief. Diffuse good intentions did not produce action when action carried personal risk on behalf of a stranger's property.

**The commitment manipulation.** In the experimental condition, the confederate made one small addition before leaving: he asked the subject, "Excuse me, would you watch my things?" Virtually everyone said yes — a trivial, costless verbal agreement. When the staged theft then occurred, 19 of 20 subjects intervened, many running after the thief, grabbing him, or demanding an explanation. A replication in a Manhattan automat, with a suitcase in place of the radio, produced the same pattern.

**Why the effect is so large.** Run the commitment through the skill's Process. **Step 1 — Identify:** the current decision is whether to confront a fleeing stranger; the prior commitment is a five-word verbal agreement; the stakes are physical risk for someone else's radio. **Step 2 — the ladder:** the commitment has all four amplifying properties Cialdini later systematized — it was *active* (spoken aloud, not merely felt), *public* (made face-to-face to the requester), *effortful* enough to register (an explicit promise, not a nod), and *voluntary* (no pressure, no reward). Once "I am someone who agreed to watch this" enters the subject's self-perception, walking away from the theft becomes an act of self-inconsistency, and the drive to behave consistently overwhelms the default bystander passivity.

**Step 4 — the ethical line.** Moriarty's manipulation sits cleanly on the ethical side: the small commitment genuinely served the requester (his property was protected), the subject retained a real exit (they could have declined the request), and nothing about the ask was deceptive about what would be expected. This is the design template — a small, truthful, voluntary ask that aligns the committer's self-image with behavior that benefits both parties.

**Step 5/6 — the operational lesson.** For designers, the study shows that explicit verbal commitments are categorically stronger than assumed goodwill: if you need someone to act later, secure a small spoken "yes" now rather than relying on their general intentions. For defenders, it shows how fast a trivial agreement can obligate you to disproportionate follow-through — a five-word promise on a beach produced pursuit of a thief. The Cialdini fresh-start test applies: "Absent that small yes, would I choose this action on its own merits?" Sometimes the answer is yes anyway (stopping a theft is defensible fresh); the test's value is knowing *which* force is driving you.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify: decision = confront a thief; prior commitment = a verbal agreement to watch belongings; stakes = personal risk for a stranger's property
2. Commitment properties: active, public, effortful, voluntary — all four amplifiers present in a five-word exchange
3. Behavioral delta: intervention rose from 4 of 20 subjects to 19 of 20 once the verbal commitment was secured
4. Ethical line: truthful ask, real exit option, mutual benefit — clean design-side application
5. Design lesson: secure a small explicit "yes" instead of relying on diffuse goodwill; defense lesson: apply the fresh-start test to trivial promises before they compel disproportionate action
6. Robustness: replicated in a second field setting (automat, suitcase) with the same pattern

Primary source: Moriarty, T. (1975). "Crime, Commitment, and the Responsive Bystander: Two Field Experiments." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 31(2), 370-376.
