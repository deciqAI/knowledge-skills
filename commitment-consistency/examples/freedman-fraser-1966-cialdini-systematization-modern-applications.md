# Method in Action: Freedman-Fraser 1966 + Cialdini Systematization + Modern Applications

> *Example for the [commitment-consistency](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The **Freedman and Fraser 1966 experiment** is the canonical empirical demonstration. The experimental design was tight and the results dramatic: signing a trivial petition produced a 3× increase in compliance with a much larger unrelated request two weeks later.

The mechanism Freedman and Fraser proposed was self-perception change. The homeowner who signs the safety petition begins to think of themselves as "the kind of person who supports safe driving causes." When the billboard request comes, processing it goes through this updated self-perception: refusing would be inconsistent with the self-image they just constructed. The consistency drive produces compliance.

A 1976 follow-up study by Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett found that the foot-in-the-door effect was strongest when the initial commitment was attributed to free choice rather than to external pressure or reward. If the homeowner had been given $10 for signing the petition, the subsequent billboard compliance would have been lower — because the homeowner could attribute their petition signing to the $10 rather than to their values. Without external justification, the only available explanation is internal commitment, which then drives subsequent consistent behavior.

**Robert Cialdini's 1984 *Influence***, drawing on Freedman-Fraser and extensive subsequent work, systematized commitment-and-consistency as one of six "weapons of influence." Cialdini's framework identified four properties that strengthen the commitment-consistency dynamic:

1. **Active:** the commitment must be enacted, not just thought
2. **Public:** others must see it (or believe they see it)
3. **Effortful:** the commitment must require some real effort
4. **Voluntary:** the committer must believe they chose freely (not under pressure or for reward)

The more of these four properties a commitment has, the more it locks the committer into subsequent consistent behavior.

Cialdini's most important operational contribution was the **defensive question**:

> "I have found that the simplest defense against the consistency tactic is the question: 'Knowing what I know now, if I could go back in time, would I make the same initial commitment again?' If the answer is no, I should not let the commitment continue to drive my behavior. The first commitment was a mistake; continuing because of it is to deepen the mistake."
>
> — Cialdini (1984/2006), p. 109.

This is the operational version of the [`sunk-cost-fallacy`](../skills/sunk-cost-fallacy/SKILL.md) defense applied to social commitments.

**Modern applications** are extensive:

**Sales sequences.** Modern B2B sales operates on commitment ladders. The "discovery call" is a small commitment. Agreeing to a demo is larger. Agreeing to a trial is larger still. Each step makes the subsequent step more likely. The empirical literature is decisive that this works far better than asking for the full commitment up front. Done ethically (with genuine product fit and easy exits), it serves both parties.

**Behavior-change programs.** Weight Watchers, AA, Couch-to-5K, language-learning apps — all rely on small, public, voluntary commitments to build toward larger goals. The empirical evidence on these programs' efficacy depends substantially on the commitment-and-consistency dynamic.

**Political campaigns.** Voter contact strategies routinely use small commitments (yard sign, donation, attending an event) as foot-in-the-door techniques for the larger commitment of voting and persuading others.

**Online onboarding flows.** Apps that ask users for a small commitment first (set up profile, customize one setting, complete one task) substantially outperform apps that ask for the larger commitment immediately. LinkedIn's profile-completion gamification, Duolingo's daily streak commitments, GitHub's contribution graphs — all leverage the dynamic.

**Defensive applications.** Sophisticated negotiators, investors, and consumers learn to recognize when they're being escalated. The Cialdini fresh-start question, applied rigorously, prevents the consistency drive from being weaponized against the chooser.

Three operational lessons:

**First, small commitments are more powerful than they look.** The Freedman-Fraser 3× compliance increase from a trivial petition was startling at the time and has been replicated many times. Underestimating the power of small commitments — both when designing and when being targeted — is a common error.

**Second, the ethical line is on user benefit and exit options.** Commitment paths that serve the user (genuine product fit, easy exits, truthful representation) are ethical and operate at scale across modern commerce. Paths that don't (deceptive small step, locked-in commitment, no exit) are manipulation regardless of how effective they are.

**Third, the Cialdini fresh-start defense is operationally critical.** When you find yourself rationalizing why you must follow through on a prior commitment, the question "would I make this choice fresh?" is the structural defense. It separates principled consistency from consistency-as-trap.
