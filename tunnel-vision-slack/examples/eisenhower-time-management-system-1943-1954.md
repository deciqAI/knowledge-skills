# Method in Action: Eisenhower's Time Management System (1943–1954)

> *Example for the [tunnel-vision-slack](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Dwight Eisenhower faced a tunnel-generating environment of unmatched density: as Supreme Allied Commander (1943–1945) and then President (1953–1961), the volume of urgent demands was effectively infinite. His documented response was not to process more urgency faster — it was to architect protected slack for important-not-urgent work.

His principle, stated in a 1954 address to the Second Assembly of the World Council of Churches, has been widely paraphrased: "I have two kinds of problems, the urgent and the important. The urgent are not important, and the important are never urgent." This was not an observation — it was the governing principle of a structural system. Urgent matters were delegated aggressively to subordinates; Eisenhower reserved his own bandwidth for the non-urgent work that only he could do.

The clearest application was D-Day planning (Operation Overlord, 1943–1944). Coordinating five Allied armies, two navies, three air forces, logistics across the Atlantic, weather science, deception operations (Operation Bodyguard), and political negotiations with de Gaulle required months of sustained non-urgent, high-importance cognition. Eisenhower held that bandwidth by refusing to let urgent operational demands consume his strategic attention. Chief of Staff Walter Bedell Smith handled the urgent escalations; Eisenhower held the strategic watch.

The plan that resulted on June 6, 1944, was the most logistically complex military operation in recorded history — and it succeeded because one person maintained the cognitive space to see the whole system while everyone around him was inside their tunnels.
