# Method in Action: The Camp David Accords — Egypt, Israel, and the Sinai (1978)

> *Example for the [batna-zopa](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The thirteen-day Camp David summit (September 5–17, 1978) between Egypt's Anwar Sadat and Israel's Menachem Begin, mediated by US President Jimmy Carter, is the canonical diplomatic demonstration of the framework. Fisher and Ury themselves use the Sinai dispute as the flagship example of "focus on interests, not positions" in *Getting to Yes*, and the summit's mediation process as the leading real-world application of the one-text procedure.

**The BATNAs.** Israel had occupied the Sinai Peninsula since the 1967 war. For both sides, no-deal meant continued belligerency:

- Egypt's fallback: keep bearing the economic and political cost of a lost peninsula, plus the standing risk of another war — the 1973 war was only five years past.
- Israel's fallback: keep bearing the cost of occupation, permanent mobilization on the southern front, and mounting international isolation.

Both BATNAs were bad, specific, and mutually understood — which is precisely why both leaders accepted Carter's invitation. Weak BATNAs on both sides do not guarantee a deal, but they widen the potential ZOPA.

**Reservation prices — and an apparent empty ZOPA.** On the single issue as first framed — *where to draw the line in the Sinai* — the reservation prices did not overlap. Egypt's stated position: every inch of the Sinai returns to full Egyptian sovereignty. Israel's stated position: retain part of the Sinai — settlements, airfields, strategic depth — as a security buffer. No line on the map satisfied both reservations. On positions alone, the ZOPA was empty, and years of positional bargaining had confirmed it.

**The structural move.** The mediating team did not "negotiate harder." It unbundled positions into interests:

- Egypt's underlying interest was *sovereignty* — a matter of national dignity after centuries of foreign control. It did not need tanks on the border to have its flag on the territory.
- Israel's underlying interest was *security* — no Egyptian armor massed within striking distance. It did not need to own the land to keep the buffer.

Those interests were compatible even though the positions were not. The solution: the entire Sinai returns to Egyptian sovereignty, while large zones of it are demilitarized, with monitoring arrangements. Egypt got the flag; Israel got the buffer. Adding issues — phased withdrawal timelines, diplomatic normalization, evacuation of Israeli settlements and airfields, and US assistance to both parties — further expanded the deal space. This is Step 4's prescription executed at state scale: when no ZOPA exists, change the structure (reframe the issue, add variables), not the pressure.

**Distributing within the new ZOPA.** Carter's team used the one-text procedure: the mediator drafts a single negotiating text, each side criticizes it, and the mediator revises through repeated rounds — rather than letting the parties trade positional concessions face to face, where every concession signals room for more. The output was two framework agreements signed September 17, 1978 ("A Framework for Peace in the Middle East" and "A Framework for the Conclusion of a Peace Treaty between Egypt and Israel"), followed by the Egypt–Israel Peace Treaty of March 26, 1979. Israel completed its withdrawal from the Sinai in 1982. Sadat and Begin shared the 1978 Nobel Peace Prize — and the treaty has held for over four decades.

The lesson the case burns in: when reservation prices don't overlap, the answer is a structural change to the negotiation itself — unbundle positions into interests, add issues, change the process — not more pressure inside a zone that doesn't exist.

The mapped steps:
1. Compute your BATNA: both sides faced continued belligerency and war risk — specific, costly, and mutually understood fallbacks
2. Set reservation & aspiration: Egypt — full sovereignty over all of the Sinai; Israel — a guaranteed security buffer; on the map-line framing, mutually exclusive
3. Estimate their BATNA: the US mediation team assessed both sides' no-deal costs and used them to keep both delegations at the table for thirteen days
4. Find the ZOPA: on positions, no ZOPA existed → structural move: reframe from positions to interests (sovereignty vs. security) and add issues (timelines, normalization, aid)
5. Distribute within ZOPA: one-text procedure — a single mediator draft revised through repeated rounds until both sides could sign, avoiding concession-signaling
