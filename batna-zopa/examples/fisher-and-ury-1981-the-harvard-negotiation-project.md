# Method in Action: Fisher and Ury, 1981 — The Harvard Negotiation Project

> *Example for the [batna-zopa](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The framework's primary source is Fisher and Ury's *Getting to Yes*, which emerged from research at the Harvard Negotiation Project (founded 1979). Fisher had been a US negotiator and Harvard Law professor; Ury was a young anthropologist (later co-founder of the Program on Negotiation at Harvard). Their goal: distill a method of negotiation that was both ethical and effective — better than positional bargaining (each side haggling toward the middle) and better than soft accommodation (giving in to preserve the relationship).

The book introduced four principles of "principled negotiation": separate people from problem, focus on interests not positions, generate options for mutual gain, insist on objective criteria. **BATNA was the framework's bedrock concept** — without it, none of the other principles work, because you have no standard against which to evaluate proposed agreements.

Fisher and Ury's central passage on BATNA:

> "The better your BATNA, the greater your power. Whatever the merits of a particular proposal, you can always walk away from it; the question is whether walking away — that is, your BATNA — is a real and attractive option. People think of negotiating power as being determined by such resources as wealth, political connections, physical strength, friends, and military might. In fact, the relative negotiating power of two parties depends primarily upon how attractive to each is the option of not reaching agreement."

— Fisher & Ury (1981/1991), pp. 102-103.

The empirical foundation of the framework was Fisher's own diplomatic experience (he had been involved in US-Iran hostage negotiations, Camp David Accords mediation, and various international conflict-resolution efforts) plus the case-study collection assembled at the Negotiation Project. The framework was tested in subsequent decades across business, diplomacy, labor disputes, and litigation; the consistent finding has been that **negotiations where parties explicitly identified BATNAs produced better outcomes for both sides** than negotiations conducted from positional bargaining.

Subsequent refinements have come from Lax & Sebenius (1986, *The Manager as Negotiator*), Mnookin (2000, *Beyond Winning*), and Malhotra & Bazerman (2007, *Negotiation Genius*). The BATNA / ZOPA framework remains the standard starting vocabulary in MBA negotiation courses, executive education, and serious deal practice.

Several operational lessons from 40+ years of application:

**First, most negotiators dramatically over-rate their own BATNA and under-rate the counterparty's.** This produces overconfident asks that produce no-deal outcomes. The corrective is rigorous, specific BATNA computation (Step 1) — not vibes.

**Second, BATNA strengthening usually beats tactical sophistication.** A negotiator with a weak BATNA but great tactics typically loses to a negotiator with a strong BATNA and average tactics. **The single most powerful negotiation move is often to walk away from the table to strengthen your BATNA, then return.**

**Third, ZOPA non-existence is the most under-recognized situation.** Many negotiations fail not because the parties negotiated badly but because no deal was possible under the current structure. Recognizing this earlier saves enormous time and emotional energy.

**Fourth, multi-issue negotiations almost always create more value than single-issue.** When parties value variables differently, trading creates surplus. Single-variable price negotiations are zero-sum; multi-variable negotiations are usually positive-sum.
