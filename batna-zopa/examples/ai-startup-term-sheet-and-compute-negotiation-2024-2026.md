# Method in Action: An AI Startup's Term Sheet & Compute Contract (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [batna-zopa](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The 2024–2026 AI funding market was bifurcated: a handful of "hot" AI startups fielded competing term sheets at rich valuations, while most founders negotiated from a weaker spot than the headlines suggested. BATNA/ZOPA cuts through the FOMO in both directions. This walks the five steps through a Series A raise, then a shorter compute-contract case — the two negotiations that most often decide an AI startup's fate. Figures are illustrative; the discipline is the point.

### Case 1 — a Series A term sheet

**Step 1 — Compute your BATNA.** A founder feeling flattered by one interested fund has no BATNA — "they might pass" is not an alternative. The real BATNA is concrete: *a second term sheet at $X*, or *18 months of runway at current burn that lets us raise later from a stronger revenue position*, each with a dollar value, timeline, and probability. The single highest-leverage move in the whole raise happens *before* any negotiation: run a **parallel process** so more than one fund is live at once, and extend runway so "no deal now" is survivable. A founder with 3 months of cash and one term sheet has, in effect, no BATNA — and the term sheet's terms will show it.

**Step 2 — Set reservation & aspiration.** Reservation is the worst valuation/terms you'd still sign versus your BATNA — and terms are not just the number: liquidation preference, option-pool shuffle, board control, and pro-rata can swamp headline valuation. Aspiration is a defensible high anchor grounded in comparable 2024–2026 rounds, not a wish. Open above aspiration but justifiable.

**Step 3 — Estimate their BATNA.** The VC's BATNA is *their next-best deal* plus the cost of missing a category winner. In a hot AI sub-sector, fund FOMO is real leverage *for the founder* — a fund that has already missed one breakout is negotiating against its own regret. Read behavior per the skill: a patient investor is signalling a strong BATNA (they have other deals); a fund pushing to sign before you can run your process is signalling a weak one (or is managing *your* BATNA down). Their reservation — the most dilution/price they'll accept — caps what you can get.

**Step 4 — Find the ZOPA.** Overlap of the two reservation prices. If a fund's ceiling is below your floor, negotiating "harder" is wasted motion — make a **structural move** instead: add issues to trade (a smaller round at a better price + more pro-rata; a SAFE bridge to reach a metric that resets comps; a different lead). No ZOPA on valuation often has a ZOPA once you widen past the single number.

**Step 5 — Distribute within the ZOPA.** Open at your favorable, defensible boundary (this composes with [anchoring](../anchoring/SKILL.md)). Concede in small steps; never "split the difference" reflexively. Trade variables — give on board observer seats to hold valuation, or accept a standard 1x non-participating preference to resist a larger option pool. Walk at your reservation, not before and not after. And run the falsifier: *what would tell me my read of their BATNA is wrong?* — e.g., they let your exploding-offer deadline lapse without blinking (their BATNA is stronger than you thought).

> **Founder takeaway (startup):** Your term sheet is won before the negotiation, in the calendar. A live parallel process and an extra six months of runway *are* your BATNA; tactics can't manufacture leverage you didn't build. The founders who got clean 2024–2026 rounds weren't better talkers — they engineered a real alternative and were visibly willing to use it.

### Case 2 — a compute / vendor commitment

The same five steps govern the other AI-era negotiation: committing to a GPU-cloud or inference contract.

- **BATNA:** a second cloud's quote, spot/on-demand capacity, or a smaller commit with burst pricing — priced out, not gestured at.
- **Reservation/aspiration:** the most you'd pay per unit given your BATNA; the discount you'd target given a multi-year commit.
- **Their BATNA:** a provider with tight capacity has a strong BATNA (other buyers want the same H-class GPUs), which is *why* they push long lock-ins; a provider with idle capacity is weaker and will discount.
- **ZOPA / structure:** if the headline rate won't move, trade terms — shorter commit, ramped volume, price step-downs, or portability clauses that lower your switching cost ([switching-costs](../switching-costs/SKILL.md)) — turning a no-ZOPA price fight into a multi-issue deal.

> **Founder takeaway (SMB / capital-light):** Don't sign a long GPU or SaaS commit because a rep implies capacity is scarce and "this price won't last" — that pressure is a read on *your* BATNA. Get one competing quote before you negotiate; a single alternative quote is often worth more than any tactic, because it converts your reservation price from a feeling into a number.
