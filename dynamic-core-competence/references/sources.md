# Sources — dynamic-core-competence

> *Primary sources for the [dynamic-core-competence](../SKILL.md) skill.*

1. **Prahalad, C.K. & Hamel, Gary** (1990). "The Core Competence of the Corporation." *Harvard Business Review*, May-June 1990. https://hbr.org/1990/05/the-core-competence-of-the-corporation. Verbatim: "The real sources of advantage are to be found in management's ability to consolidate corporatewide technologies and production skills into competencies that empower individual businesses to adapt quickly to changing opportunities... Core competencies are the collective learning in the organization, especially how to coordinate diverse production skills and integrate multiple streams of technologies." This article introduced the concept of core competence and established three tests: (1) does it provide potential access to a wide variety of markets; (2) does it make a significant contribution to the perceived customer benefits of the end product; (3) should it be difficult for competitors to imitate?

2. **Teece, David J., Pisano, Gary, & Shuen, Amy** (1997). "Dynamic Capabilities and Strategic Management." *Strategic Management Journal*, 18(7), pp. 509–533. https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-0266(199708)18:7<509::AID-SMJ882>3.0.CO;2-Z. Verbatim: "We define dynamic capabilities as the firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing environments. Dynamic capabilities thus reflect an organization's ability to achieve new and innovative forms of competitive advantage given path dependencies and market positions." This paper extends the static core competence framework by identifying how competitive advantage is regenerated when the environment changes — the "dynamic" dimension that Prahalad & Hamel did not fully address.

3. **Corning Incorporated Annual Reports** (1970–2010). Available at https://www.corning.com/worldwide/en/about-us/investor-relations/annual-reports.html. The fiber optic invention is primary-source documented in U.S. Patent 3,711,262 (Maurer, Keck & Schultz, 1973). Corning's investor materials from 2007–2012 document the Gorilla Glass decision and the application of the existing precision glass competence to the mobile device screen market. These primary documents establish that the Corning case is not reconstructed hindsight — the competence strategy was articulated in investor communications as it was being executed.

**What is NOT cited and why:** The Corning case references Hounshell & Smith (1988) as a structural reference for competence evolution analysis methodology, but the Corning-specific claims are drawn from Corning's own primary documentation. The Hounshell & Smith work covers DuPont, not Corning directly; it is referenced for its analytical framework, not for Corning-specific facts. Readers should note that the Gorilla Glass negotiation with Steve Jobs is described in multiple secondary sources (including biographies), but the primary documentation available is Corning's own investor communications, which are cited above.
