# Method in Action: Corning Incorporated's Competence Evolution, 1851–2007

> *Example for the [dynamic-core-competence](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Corning Incorporated is one of the most thoroughly documented cases of a company correctly identifying and dynamically redeploying its core competence across six technology generations — without ever abandoning the fundamental capability that made each generation possible.

**The core competence correctly identified:** Corning's leadership in the 1870s correctly identified their core competence NOT as "making glass products" but as "materials science and precision manufacturing of glass at technical extremes." This abstraction was precise: it named the mechanism (materials science + precision manufacturing), named the differentiating condition (technical extremes — difficult tolerances, unusual optical properties, extreme environments), and specifically excluded the product application (Edison light bulbs were the current application, not the competence itself).

**Dynamic redeployment across six cycles:**

1. **1880s — Light bulb glass:** Edison partnership. Core competence applied to: precision glass envelopes for incandescent bulbs. Market importance of this application: rapidly declining by 1920s as the market commoditized.

2. **1940s–1960s — CRT (cathode ray tube) glass:** Television industry. Corning's precision glass manufacturing was redeployed to produce TV tube glass. By the 1970s, this was declining as Asian manufacturers achieved cost parity.

3. **1970s — Optical fiber:** Corning researchers Robert Maurer, Donald Keck, and Peter Schultz invented the first optical fiber with loss below 20 dB/km in 1970 — the threshold for practical telecommunications use. This was not a product adjacent to CRT glass; it was the same core competence (materials science, precision manufacturing at technical extremes) applied to a completely new problem.

4. **1990s — LCD glass:** Liquid crystal display substrates. The same precision glass manufacturing capability, now applied to the demanding tolerances of LCD panels.

5. **2007 — Gorilla Glass:** Corning had actually developed chemically strengthened glass in the 1960s but shelved it as no market existed. When Steve Jobs required a scratch-resistant glass for the original iPhone screen, Corning's CEO Wendell Weeks committed to producing it within 6 months — because the core competence was already present. The window was open; the competence was maintained.

**The dynamic dimension:** At each transition, Corning made explicit decisions to reduce investment in the declining application while redeploying the core competence to the rising one. The competence was maintained; the application was dynamic.

**Primary source:** Corning Annual Reports (1970–2010), available at https://www.corning.com/worldwide/en/about-us/investor-relations/annual-reports.html. The fiber optics invention is documented in the patent record: Maurer, R.D., Keck, P.C., & Schultz, P. (1973). U.S. Patent 3,711,262 — "Fused silica optical waveguide." The Gorilla Glass story is documented in Corning's 2012 investor presentation and multiple primary-source interviews with Wendell Weeks.
