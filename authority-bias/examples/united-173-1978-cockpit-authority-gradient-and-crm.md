# Method in Action: United 173 and the Cockpit Authority Gradient (1978 → 1981)

> *Example for the [authority-bias](../SKILL.md) skill.*

On December 28, 1978, United Airlines Flight 173, a DC-8 inbound to Portland, Oregon, experienced a landing gear anomaly on approach — an abnormal indication and an unusual noise when the gear extended. The captain entered a holding pattern to troubleshoot and to prepare the cabin for a possible emergency landing. The airplane held for roughly an hour. During that hour, the fuel ran out. The DC-8 crash-landed in a wooded suburban area about six miles short of the airport. Ten people aboard died, including the flight engineer. Post-crash examination showed the landing gear had been down and functional the whole time.

**S1 — Identify.** The authority: a highly experienced captain, backed by the era's cockpit culture in which the captain's judgment was close to unchallengeable. The question: when to stop troubleshooting and land. The pressure to defer: a steep authority gradient — the first officer and flight engineer were structurally subordinate, and the norm was to advise, never to countermand.

**S2 — Expertise vs. positional.** The captain was a genuine domain expert as an aviator. But the specific question in play — remaining fuel versus remaining time — was squarely instrumented on the flight engineer's panel. The flight engineer had the better read on the binding constraint. Positional authority outweighed the crew member with the most relevant information.

**S3 — Examine reasoning.** The captain's reasoning was locally sound: an unresolved gear indication justifies caution and cabin preparation. But it silently assumed time was abundant. The NTSB found that both junior crew members made advisory remarks about the deteriorating fuel state, yet neither converted those hints into an assertive, unambiguous challenge. The logical gap — "we are preparing carefully" versus "we are about to run out of fuel" — was filled by deference to the four stripes rather than by arithmetic.

**S4 — Check for dysfunction.** Textbook pattern: dissent expressed as mitigated speech (soft advisories, indirect mentions of fuel figures), no escalation when the advisories were not acted on, and a captain absorbed in one problem while the crew's concern never registered as a command-level input. The NTSB's probable-cause finding cited the captain's failure to monitor and respond to the fuel state, and — contributing — the other crew members' failure either to grasp the criticality or to successfully communicate their concern to the captain.

**S5 — Cialdini defense (retrospective).** Q1 — genuine expert on this specific question? On flying the DC-8, yes; on the fuel-time tradeoff at that moment, the flight engineer's data outranked the captain's attention. Q2 — does the reasoning hold under independent scrutiny? No: independent arithmetic on fuel flow contradicted the plan to keep holding. Either N → discount the authority signal and demand the evidence. The evidence was on the panel.

**S6 — Structural counter.** This is why United 173 matters more than most crashes: the NTSB explicitly recommended crew-coordination training addressing exactly this failure mode, and in 1981 United Airlines instituted the industry's first Crew Resource Management program, building on NASA research into flight-deck resource management. CRM operationalizes the skill's structural counters — juniors are trained and authorized to challenge assertively with escalating phraseology, and captains are trained to explicitly solicit input and treat it as data, not insubordination. Awareness alone was never expected to fix the bias; the industry installed structure. CRM subsequently spread to airlines worldwide and into medicine, firefighting, and maritime operations.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify: captain as near-absolute authority; question = keep holding vs. land now; steep gradient suppressing challenge
2. Expertise vs. positional: captain expert in flying, but the binding constraint (fuel) was the flight engineer's instrumented domain
3. Examine reasoning: cautious troubleshooting was sound only under an unstated abundant-time assumption; the gap was filled by rank
4. Dysfunction check: mitigated speech, hints not acted on, no escalation — dissent structurally muted
5. Cialdini defense: Q2 fails — independent fuel arithmetic contradicted the plan; authority signal should have been discounted
6. Structural counter: NTSB recommendation → United's 1981 CRM program → industry-wide assertive-challenge and input-solicitation protocols
