# Method in Action: Jeff Bezos, D.E. Shaw → Amazon (1994)

> *Example for the [regret-minimization](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example. Not a marketing parable — primary-source documented.

In spring 1994, **Jeff Bezos** was 30 years old and a Senior Vice President at the New York quantitative-trading firm **D.E. Shaw**, with a high salary, an upward trajectory, and a clear path to managing director. He came across a statistic — **Web usage was growing at 2,300% per year** — and developed an idea for an online bookstore.

The decision he faced was acutely asymmetric. The downside was concrete and immediate (forgo guaranteed compensation, give up status and progression, start over). The upside was speculative and far away (a then-unproven Internet might or might not produce the company he imagined). **Expected-value math, run honestly, would not have produced a clear answer** — the probability of success was low, the variance was huge, and the time horizon was long.

His boss at D.E. Shaw, David Shaw, made the case for staying. Bezos asked for time. He went on a walk in Central Park, thinking it through. The framework that produced the decision was the one he later named:

> "I knew that when I was 80 I was not going to regret having tried this. I was not going to regret trying to participate in this thing called the Internet that I thought was going to be a really big deal. I knew that if I failed I wouldn't regret that, but I knew the one thing I might regret is not ever having tried. I knew that that would haunt me every day, and so, when I thought about it that way it was an incredibly easy decision."
> — Jeff Bezos, on the Regret Minimization Framework

Walk through the Audit applied to Bezos's case:

- **Decision fork (Step 1):** *leave D.E. Shaw by mid-1994 to start the online bookstore* vs. *stay at D.E. Shaw for at least 2 more years and revisit the idea later if the Internet was still growing.*
- **Age-80 self (Step 2):** A self who valued *having participated* in the formative technological shift of his lifetime; a self for whom the unit was *meaning + contribution*, not security.
- **Paths lived (Step 3):** Path A lived realistically = mostly hard, possibly bankrupt, possibly modestly successful, with the satisfaction of having built something. Path B lived realistically = wealthy, comfortable, watching others build the Internet companies.
- **Regrets (Step 4):**
  - A failed: "I tried and it didn't work — I learned, I tried." → tolerable.
  - A never tried: "I watched the Internet revolution from a trading desk." → **not tolerable.**
  - B (stay) is dominated by "A never tried" — the regret structure of the two options is **the same regret**, just experienced once or repeatedly.
- **Asymmetry (Step 5):** The never-tried regret was the one that would *haunt every day*. The failed-but-tried regret was livable.
- **Unit (Step 6):** Meaning + contribution to a once-in-a-lifetime technological shift — not security, not optimized expected value.
- **Commitment (Step 7):** Bezos resigned in 1994. **Amazon was founded July 5, 1994.** The decision was not revisited.

The result is well-known. The *method* — and what to take from it — is the asymmetry and the commitment, not the outcome. **The framework would have given Bezos the same answer even if Amazon had failed**; that is the test of whether you are using regret minimization correctly. If the framework's recommendation depends on knowing how it turned out, you are not running regret minimization, you are running hindsight.
