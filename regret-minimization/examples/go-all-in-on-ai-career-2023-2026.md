# Method in Action: The "Go All-In on AI" Career / Founder Decision (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [regret-minimization](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A contemporary worked example. Not a prediction and not investment advice — a demonstration of how to *run the audit* on the decision a lot of engineers, PMs, and researchers actually faced between 2023 and 2026: **leave a stable, well-paid big-tech job to go all-in on the AI wave — join a frontier lab, join an AI startup, or found one — versus stay put and wait.**

This is exactly the shape the framework is built for: the money-EV math is genuinely ambiguous (a senior big-tech total-comp package is a large, near-certain payoff; a startup outcome is a low-probability, high-variance one), the horizon is long, and the true unit for most people asking the question is not dollars but *identity, meaning, and "was I in the room for this."*

## Why the timing made this a live decision

The backdrop was real and widely documented, not hype:

- **ChatGPT launched November 30, 2022** and reached an enormous user base faster than almost any consumer product on record, kicking off the generative-AI wave.
- Nvidia, the dominant supplier of AI training chips, saw its data-center business explode; it **first crossed roughly $1 trillion in market capitalization in mid-2023** and, after continued AI-driven growth, **crossed roughly $3 trillion in 2024**, at points ranking among the most valuable companies in the world.
- Capital and talent poured in: **Microsoft's multibillion-dollar investment in OpenAI** (a further large tranche announced in January 2023) and comparable moves by Google and Amazon (its investment in Anthropic) signaled that the biggest incumbents believed the shift was structural.
- The wave kept producing genuine surprises — most sharply the **market reaction in late January 2025 to the Chinese lab DeepSeek's low-cost models**, which triggered a large single-day drop in AI-exposed stocks (Nvidia among them) and reopened the "how durable are the moats" debate.
- It also carried real, decision-relevant *downside* signals: escalating **US export controls on advanced AI chips to China** (successive rounds from 2022 through 2023 and tightened again in 2025), persistent **"is this an AI bubble" commentary**, and heavy **AI capex** by the hyperscalers whose payback was openly questioned.

None of that tells you what to *do*. That is the point of the audit: it converts a noisy, emotionally-charged "everyone's getting rich, am I missing it" feeling into a structured comparison of livable regrets.

## The Regret Audit, step by step

**1. Define the fork specifically.** Not "should I get into AI." The concrete, time-bounded fork for a hypothetical senior engineer at a big-tech company in this window:
> *Leave my big-tech job within the next 6–12 months to go all-in on AI — join a frontier lab or an AI startup, or found one — accepting a pay cut / equity risk,* **vs.** *stay in my current role for at least 2 more years, bank the comp, learn AI on the side, and revisit if the wave is still standing.*

**2. Project to 80.** Describe the age-80 self honestly. For the person who *feels the pull*, the age-80 self is usually someone who cared about *having done the defining technical work of their era* and about *agency* — being a builder, not a spectator. That self does not, at 80, itemize the exact dollar delta between two comp packages from their 30s. If your honest age-80 self is instead someone who values security, stability, and having provided reliably for a family above all — that is a *different* evaluator, and it legitimately points the other way. Build *your* evaluator; do not import the founder-hero version.

**3. Imagine each path lived realistically — including the boring middle.** This is where most AI-wave audits go wrong, because they imagine the founder's IPO against the stayer's stagnation.
- *Go-all-in, lived realistically:* long hours, a real pay cut, a startup or team that **most probably does not become the next OpenAI** (the base rate for startups is failure), possibly a down round or a shutdown, but daily proximity to the frontier and the identity of a builder. The honest modal outcome is "worked intensely on AI for a few years, company didn't win, learned an enormous amount, moved on" — *not* a windfall.
- *Stay, lived realistically:* steady high comp, real projects, competent but incremental AI exposure inside a large org, watching from an adjacent seat as others build the wave. The honest modal outcome is "comfortable, secure, and quietly aware I stayed on the sidelines of the thing I cared about" — *not* career ruin.

**4. Name the regret on each path — split failed-but-tried from never-tried.**
- *Go-all-in, failed:* "I jumped in, my bet didn't pay off, I took a pay cut for a few years." → for the builder-type evaluator, **tolerable** — it reframes as "I was in it, I learned, I tried."
- *Go-all-in, never-tried* — not applicable; this is the trying path.
- *Stay, never tried:* "The single biggest technology shift of my working life happened, and I watched it from a stable seat because the money was good." → for the builder-type evaluator, this is the one that **haunts**.
- *Stay, "failed":* stability is by construction the low-regret-of-action path — its risk is the quiet regret of omission, not a vivid failure.

**5. Test asymmetry — which regret is harder to live with at 80?** This is the load-bearing step and it is *not* automatic. For the builder-type evaluator, the never-tried regret dominates and the audit says *go*. But the framework is honest that this flips by person: someone whose true age-80 unit is security, or who is the sole provider with dependents and thin savings, may find "I gambled our stability on a bet that didn't pay off" the heavier regret — in which case the audit legitimately says *stay, and get AI exposure the lower-risk way.* The skill's rule holds: failed-but-tried is *usually* lighter, **verify for your case.**

**6. Name the unit being optimized.** For the person the pull is real for, the unit is **meaning + identity + agency** ("was I a builder in the defining shift of my era"), explicitly *not* expected dollar value — because on pure money-EV, the near-certain big-tech package often *wins*, and that mismatch is exactly why regret minimization is the right tool here rather than an EV spreadsheet.

**7. Commit and pre-bind.** State the date the decision becomes irreversible and stop relitigating. The failure mode specific to *this* case is re-running the audit every time the news swings — a bubble-warning headline, a DeepSeek-style selloff, a fresh export-control round. Pre-binding exists precisely to carry the decision through that noise. A clean re-open trigger is a *specific observable* ("re-evaluate only if I can't secure a role or funding within X months"), not "the market dropped this week."

## What to take from it

The framework does **not** say "everyone should quit and do AI." Run honestly, it produces *different* answers for different age-80 selves — and that is the feature, not the bug. What it does is stop you from (a) running EV-for-the-option-you-fear and regret-for-the-option-you-crave, (b) comparing a fantasy IPO against a fantasy stagnation, and (c) relitigating the choice on every swing of a genuinely volatile market. The correctness of the *method* does not depend on how any particular AI bet turns out — the same test that tells one person to jump tells another to stay, and both are using it right.
