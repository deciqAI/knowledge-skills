# Method in Action: Gilovich & Medvec and the Evidence for the Age-80 Test (1995)

> *Example for the [regret-minimization](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example from a different domain: not a founder running the framework on a career fork, but the empirical psychology that tested the framework's load-bearing claim. The Regret Audit rests on one asymmetry — that at 80, *never-tried* regret outweighs *failed-but-tried* regret. In 1995 that claim was an open empirical question, and the naive evidence pointed the other way: Kahneman and Tversky's earlier vignette studies had found that people report *more* regret over actions taken than over actions foregone. If that were the whole story, the framework would be built on a false premise.

**The research question, stated as a fork:** over a full life looking back, which regret is heavier — the thing you did that failed, or the thing you never did?

**The method — the age-80 evaluator run as an instrument, not a mantra.** Thomas Gilovich and Victoria Husted Medvec (Cornell) ran a series of retrospective studies asking adults — including older respondents surveying their whole lives — to name their biggest regrets. Each reported regret was coded as a regret of *action* (something done) or *inaction* (something not done). Crucially, respondents reported regrets from lives actually lived, not from hypothetical vignettes — the design structurally excludes the fantasy projection the skill warns against.

**Finding 1 — the asymmetry is real.** When people survey their lives in retrospect, regrets of inaction substantially outnumber regrets of action, and the dominance grows with the length of the look-back. The education not pursued, the relationship not attempted, the risk not taken — these, not the failed attempts, dominate long-run recollection.

**Finding 2 — the temporal pattern reconciles the contradiction.** Actions produce more intense regret *in the short run*; inactions produce more regret *in the long run*. Kahneman and Tversky's vignettes probed the hot, immediate window; Gilovich and Medvec's retrospective surveys probed the age-80 window. Both results are true — which is precisely why the framework instructs you to evaluate at 80 and not at week one. A decision audited in the hot window will systematically over-weigh the pain of acting.

**The mechanism — why never-tried regret compounds.** The paper's analysis of *why* matches the skill's structure. The consequences of an action taken are finite and knowable: you see what happened, you repair, you reframe ("I learned, I tried"). The consequences of an action foregone are counterfactually unbounded: the imagined might-have-been is never falsified, so it keeps growing. Failed-but-tried regret depreciates; never-tried regret appreciates. That is the engine behind Step 4's split and Step 5's asymmetry test.

**The caveat the skill inherits.** The result is a strong population-level tendency, not a law — some action regrets stay heavy for life. The skill's instruction "failed-but-tried regret is *usually* lighter — verify for *your* case" is not hedging; it is fidelity to the data.

The mapped steps:

1. **Define the fork (Step 1):** the research question posed as a concrete binary — regret of action taken vs. regret of action foregone, over a lifetime.
2. **Project to 80 (Step 2):** implemented empirically — retrospective surveys of adults, including elderly respondents, evaluating whole lives looking back.
3. **Paths lived realistically (Step 3):** regrets drawn from lives actually lived; the design admits no heroic version of either path.
4. **Split failed-but-tried from never-tried (Step 4):** the action/inaction coding scheme is exactly this split, applied to real reported regrets.
5. **Test asymmetry (Step 5):** answered with data — inaction regrets dominate the long run by a wide margin; action regrets dominate only the short run.
6. **Name the unit (Step 6):** the dominant reported regrets are in units of meaning, identity, and relationships — foregone selves — not money left on the table.
7. **Commit and pre-bind (Step 7):** the temporal crossover is the justification for the commitment date — pre-binding exists to carry the decision through the short window when the regret of *acting* burns hottest and would otherwise force relitigation.

The Bezos example shows the framework producing a decision; this one shows why the framework's central bet is empirically correct. Use it when a user asks "but is the never-tried-regret thing actually true, or just a founder story?" — the answer is: measured, replicated across samples, with a known mechanism and a known boundary condition.

Primary source: Gilovich, T., & Medvec, V. H. (1995). "The Experience of Regret: What, When, and Why." *Psychological Review*, 102(2), 379–395. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.102.2.379
