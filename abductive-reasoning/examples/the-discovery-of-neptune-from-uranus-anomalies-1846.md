# Method in Action: The Discovery of Neptune from Uranus's Orbital Anomalies (1821–1846)

> *Example for the [abductive-reasoning](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The prediction and discovery of Neptune is celestial mechanics' cleanest demonstration of inference to the best explanation: a planet found not by looking, but by reasoning backward from an anomaly to the cause that would make it "a matter of course."

**S1 — The surprising observation.** Uranus, catalogued as a planet by Herschel in 1781, would not stay on its predicted track. Alexis Bouvard's 1821 tables, built on Newtonian gravitation and the best observations available, soon diverged from where Uranus actually appeared. By the 1840s the discrepancy in longitude had grown past what observational error could excuse. The expected (Newtonian prediction) and the actual (telescope position) had come apart.

**S2 — Generate candidates.** The astronomical community did not converge on one story. Competing explanations were live: (H1) accumulated observational error in the older Uranus sightings; (H2) errors in Bouvard's tables or the adopted mass of Saturn/Jupiter; (H3) a genuine breakdown of Newton's inverse-square law at great distances from the Sun; (H4) a resisting interplanetary medium dragging on Uranus; (H5) an undiscovered massive body beyond Uranus, whose gravity perturbed its orbit; and H_unknown, some cause not yet imagined.

**S3 — Score the candidates.** H1 and H2 lost on coverage — refined data and recomputed tables did not close the gap. H3 was catastrophic on prior and simplicity: discarding the most successful theory in physics to save one orbit was a vast auxiliary cost. H4 predicted secular effects on other planets that were not seen. H5 alone kept Newtonian gravity intact (high prior), added a single new object rather than a new physics (simplicity), covered the full pattern of residuals, and — decisively — carried predictive power: an unknown planet has a *computable* mass and position.

**S4 — Select the best.** Working independently, John Couch Adams (from 1843) and Urbain Le Verrier (1845–46) both chose H5 and did the hard part — they inverted the perturbation problem to compute where the hypothesized planet must be. Le Verrier's solution beat every rival not by being consistent with the anomaly but by turning the anomaly into a coordinate.

**S5 — The discriminating test.** H5 made a falsifiable prediction the rivals could not: point a telescope at a specific patch of sky and a new planet will be there. Le Verrier sent his predicted position to Johann Galle at the Berlin Observatory.

**S6 — Confrontation with evidence.** On the night of 23 September 1846, Galle and Heinrich d'Arrest found Neptune within about one degree of Le Verrier's predicted position, on the first night of searching. The abductive hypothesis was confirmed; the rival explanations were retired.

The mapped steps:
1. S1 — Surprising observation: Uranus deviates from its Newtonian orbit beyond tolerable error
2. S2 — Candidates: bad data, bad tables, failed inverse-square law, resisting medium, an unseen perturbing planet, H_unknown
3. S3 — Score: only the unseen-planet hypothesis wins on coverage, simplicity, prior, and predictive power together
4. S4 — Select: Adams and Le Verrier compute the mass and sky position the hypothesis implies
5. S5 — Discriminating test: the planet must be visible at a specific predicted location
6. S6 — Confront evidence: Galle observes Neptune on 23 September 1846, ~1° from prediction

Primary source: Grosser, M. (1962). *The Discovery of Neptune.* Harvard University Press. See also Le Verrier, U. J. J. (1846), "Recherches sur les mouvements d'Uranus," *Comptes Rendus de l'Académie des Sciences*, vol. 22–23.
