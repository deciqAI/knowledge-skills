# Method in Action: Carl Jung's Bollingen Tower Retreats (1923–1961)

> *Example for the [deep-work](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Carl Jung's Bollingen tower is the case Cal Newport chose to open *Deep Work* (2016), and it remains the cleanest historical demonstration of the **Bimodal philosophy**: a working psychiatrist who could not abandon his shallow obligations, and instead built a physical architecture for alternating between them and depth.

**The diagnosis (Steps 1-2):** In the 1920s Jung's professional life in Zurich and Küsnacht was structurally shallow-saturated: a busy clinical practice with a full patient load, lectures, seminars, and heavy correspondence. This was also the period in which Jung was establishing analytical psychology as an independent school after his break with Freud — an intellectual project competing against the most formidable rival imaginable. The highest-leverage deep work was unambiguous: the sustained theoretical writing that would define archetypes, the collective unconscious, and individuation. That writing could not happen between patient sessions. Jung did not need a tracking week to see the ratio; the clinical calendar made it visible.

**The philosophy choice (Step 3):** Jung could not go monastic — his clinical practice was both his income and his empirical material. He chose the bimodal split: full engagement with practice and public life in Zurich, then regular retreats to a private refuge for concentrated writing.

**The practice (Step 4):** In 1922 Jung bought land at Bollingen on the upper lake of Zurich, and beginning in 1923 built a stone tower there by stages, expanding it over the following decades. The tower was deliberately spartan — no electricity; Jung chopped his own wood, drew his own water, and lit oil lamps. In *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (1962), the chapter "The Tower" describes the place in Jung's own account as a site of repose and renewal where he felt most deeply himself — a purpose-built container for concentration. Newport reconstructs the working rhythm: mornings locked in a private study for undisturbed writing, afternoons given to meditation and walks, early nights. Same location, same setup, hard boundary — the full ritual structure of Step 4, implemented in stone.

**Shallow reduction and defense (Steps 5-6):** Every Bollingen period was clinical income refused and patients turned away — the shallow-work budget enforced at direct financial cost. The retreats could look like withdrawal from a career; they were its engine. The output produced across four decades of this regime — the essays and books that became the *Collected Works* and established analytical psychology as a lasting school — is the long-term evidence Step 6 says to accumulate: output value as the defense of the practice.

The structural lesson mirrors Knuth's but from the opposite pole: Knuth shows the monastic extreme (eliminate the shallow channel permanently); Jung shows that a practitioner who *cannot* eliminate shallow work can still get monastic-grade depth by alternating modes — provided the deep mode has a hard physical and temporal boundary, a ritual, and an accepted cost.

Three operational lessons from the Bollingen case:

**First, bimodal beats waiting.** Jung never had a calendar that "allowed" deep work; he manufactured the allowance by pre-committing whole blocks of days. Anyone whose role genuinely requires shallow engagement — clinicians, managers, founders — should read Jung, not Knuth, as the applicable template: claim retreat units (a day, a week) rather than trying to carve depth out of an interrupt-driven day.

**Second, the environment does defensive work the willpower budget cannot.** A tower with no electricity, reachable only by deliberate travel, made shallow intrusion physically impossible rather than merely discouraged. The modern equivalent is weaker but real: a separate room, a machine with no messaging clients, a location where interruption cannot follow. Build the boundary into the environment so it does not have to be re-decided hourly.

**Third, deep work was not competing against the practice — it fed it.** Jung's clinical work supplied the case material his theoretical writing systematized, and the writing in turn made the practice world-famous. The bimodal split is not a tax paid by the shallow mode; run well, each mode compounds the other. That is the strongest answer to the "my role doesn't allow it" rationalization.

The mapped steps:
1. Instrument: Zurich clinical calendar made the deep/shallow ratio visible — practice, lectures, correspondence consumed the day
2. Identify: the theoretical corpus of analytical psychology, in direct competition with Freud, was the highest-leverage deep work not happening
3. Philosophy: Bimodal — full shallow engagement in Zurich alternating with retreat periods at Bollingen
4. Practice: the stone tower as ritual — fixed location, spartan setup, locked morning study, afternoon walks, early nights
5. Shallow budget: patients refused and engagements declined during retreat periods, at real financial cost
6. Defense: decades of published output — the *Collected Works* — as the standing proof that the protected time paid

Secondary: Newport, C. (2016). *Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World.* Grand Central. Chapter 1 and Rule #1 (Bimodal philosophy).
