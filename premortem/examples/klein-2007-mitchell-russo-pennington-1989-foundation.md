# Method in Action: Klein 2007 and the Mitchell-Russo-Pennington 1989 Foundation

> *Example for the [premortem](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The empirical foundation is the **Mitchell, Russo & Pennington 1989** study in the *Journal of Behavioral Decision Making*. The study tested whether "prospective hindsight" — being asked to imagine an event had occurred and explain it — produced better identification of causal mechanisms than ordinary forecasting. Subjects were given hypothetical scenarios (e.g., a court case verdict, a corporate event) and either asked to predict the outcome (control) or asked to imagine the outcome had occurred and to explain it (treatment). The explanations under the prospective-hindsight condition were more concrete, more numerous, and contained more accurate causal mechanisms.

The 30% improvement number Klein cites is from this study and from his subsequent operational testing:

> "Subjects asked to imagine that an event had already occurred and to explain it generated 30% more distinct causal explanations than subjects asked to predict the same event prospectively. The explanations under prospective hindsight were also rated by independent judges as more specific and more often correct, in cases where the actual outcomes were later determinable."
>
> — Mitchell, Russo, & Pennington (1989), pp. 30-31.

Klein's contribution was to operationalize this finding into a structured project-management technique. His 2007 *HBR* paper described the procedure in essentially the form described above. The paper has become one of the most-cited *HBR* pieces on decision-quality and the foundational reference for the technique.

Klein extended the work in his 2011 book *Streetlights and Shadows*:

> "The premortem isn't just a fancy way of doing risk analysis. It changes the social dynamics of the team. Members who would have been silent about their concerns — the junior engineer worried about the schedule, the operations person who has seen similar plans fail — become the central contributors. The technique licenses dissent by making dissent the assigned task. In our experience running premortems for Fortune 500 firms and government agencies, the most-cited benefit is not the specific risks identified, but the social permission for risk-identification that the structure creates."
>
> — Klein, G. (2011). *Streetlights and Shadows: Searching for the Keys to Adaptive Decision Making.* MIT Press, pp. 232-234.

Subsequent empirical studies have replicated the premortem's effectiveness:

**Veinott, B., Klein, G., & Wiggins, S. (2010).** "Evaluating the effectiveness of the premortem technique on plan confidence." *7th International ISCRAM Conference Proceedings*. Tested the premortem in military emergency-response planning. Teams using the premortem identified 30-45% more failure modes than control teams using standard risk analysis, and produced more concrete mitigation actions.

**Klein, G. (2011).** *Streetlights and Shadows*. MIT Press. The comprehensive operational treatment.

**Russo, J. E., & Schoemaker, P. J. H. (1989).** *Decision Traps: The Ten Barriers to Brilliant Decision-Making.* Doubleday. The broader decision-quality framework in which prospective hindsight sits.

**Kahneman, D. (2011).** *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Chapter 24, "The Engine of Capitalism." Kahneman explicitly recommends the premortem as one of the highest-leverage decision-quality interventions:

> "When a group converges on a decision — especially when the leader endorses it — public doubts about the wisdom of the planned course are gradually suppressed and eventually come to be treated as evidence of weak commitment to the team and its leaders. The suppression of doubt contributes to overconfidence in a group where only supporters of the decision have a voice. The main virtue of the premortem is that it legitimizes doubts. ... I have personally adopted the premortem method and recommend it to all my students."
>
> — Kahneman (2011), p. 264.

**Klein, G., & Snowden, D. (2011).** Crossover article connecting the premortem to Cynefin Complex-domain methodology. The premortem is the canonical Complex-domain "probe" — a safe-to-fail exercise that surfaces information before commitment.

The technique has been adopted as standard practice in:

**Military planning.** U.S. Army and U.K. Defence Forces include premortem-style exercises in operations planning. The Army's "Red Team University" trains officers in structured devil's-advocacy and premortem methodologies. NATO's Bi-Strategic Command Capstone Concept (2010) explicitly includes premortem as a recommended planning tool.

**Intelligence analysis.** The CIA's structured analytic techniques (Heuer & Pherson 2010) include "What If? Analysis" and "Devil's Advocacy" — variants of the premortem applied to intelligence assessments.

**Venture investing.** Many sophisticated VCs (Andreessen Horowitz, Sequoia, Bessemer) have institutionalized premortems for both new investments and follow-on decisions. The premortem is run by partners not on the deal, leveraging the outside-perspective advantage.

**Medical decision-making.** Patient safety initiatives (Institute for Healthcare Improvement; AHRQ) include premortem-style exercises in pre-procedure planning. The technique is used to identify failure modes before high-risk surgeries.

**Software engineering.** Pre-launch checklists at major tech companies (Google, Amazon, Microsoft) include premortem-style exercises. The Amazon "narrative memo" practice (write a press release for the failed launch; what did the press release say went wrong?) is a stylistic variant of the premortem.

**Strategy consulting.** McKinsey, BCG, and Bain include premortems in major engagement playbooks. The technique is particularly used when a client has converged on a strategic direction and the consulting team's role is to stress-test it.

**Policy analysis.** OECD regulatory impact assessment guidelines include premortem-style analysis of proposed regulations. The European Commission's Better Regulation toolkit explicitly references the technique.

Three operational lessons from Klein:

**First, the grammatical mood matters.** "What could go wrong?" produces vague concerns. "It failed — what caused it?" produces specific explanations. The change is small in language but large in cognitive output. The mood must be enforced through the entire exercise; if facilitators allow drift back to "what might go wrong?", the premortem's distinctive value is lost.

**Second, private generation precedes social discussion.** This is non-negotiable. Group discussion before private generation produces conformity to the dominant view. Klein's empirical results depend on the silent-writing step, not just the failure-frame.

**Third, the output is the modified plan, not the failure list.** A premortem that produces a list of risks but does not modify the plan is a wasted exercise. The discipline is to take the high-probability/high-severity items and add mitigations to the plan before commitment — and to monitor the trigger signals over the project's life.
