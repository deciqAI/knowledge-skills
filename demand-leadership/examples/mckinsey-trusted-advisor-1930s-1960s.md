# Method in Action: McKinsey's Construction of the Trusted Advisor Position (1930s–1960s)

> *Example for the [demand-leadership](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Source:** Christopher McKenna, *The World's Newest Profession: Management Consulting and the Creation of Corporate Culture* (2006, Cambridge University Press). ISBN: 978-0-521-81283-2. URL: https://www.cambridge.org/9780521812832

**Context:** In the 1920s, the dominant form of external business advice was accounting — backward-looking, compliance-driven, focused on what had happened. There was no recognized category called "management consulting." McKinsey & Company, under Marvin Bower (who joined in 1933 and led the firm from 1950–1967), did not enter an existing market. It created one. The creation followed the demand leadership sequence precisely.

**Stage 1 — Insight Generation:** Bower identified the latent demand: large corporations had accumulated scale but lacked an objective external perspective on their own strategic problems. Internal staff could not provide this — they were too embedded in the political structure of the organization to give the CEO unfiltered diagnosis. McKinsey's initial value proposition was insight: "We will tell you what your own people cannot."

**Stage 2 — Value Reconstruction:** Bower did not present McKinsey as an "efficiency consultant" or "business analyst" — categories that existed and were associated with cost-cutting and implementation work beneath the C-suite. He explicitly reframed the product as "top management consulting" — advice to the CEO and board about the firm's direction, not its operations. McKinsey's engagement model required direct access to the CEO. This reframe repositioned the customer's problem: from "we need to improve efficiency" to "we need an external strategic perspective that our own people cannot provide." The new category was defined by access level and problem scope, not by analytical methodology.

**Stage 3 — Scene Creation:** As McKinsey embedded in client organizations, its consultants became part of the planning process, not respondents to it. McKenna documents that by the 1960s, major McKinsey clients were sharing strategic decisions in progress with the firm — not finished problems to be solved but problems being formed. The scene: the CEO's strategy session, which McKinsey had made itself the opening input to, not an external validator of.

> "We were not selling analysis. We were selling the confidence that comes from having a trusted outsider who knows your business."
> — Marvin Bower, quoted in McKenna, p. 58

**The demand creation result:** McKinsey did not fulfill an existing demand for management consulting. It constructed the demand by (1) naming the latent need executives had but could not articulate, (2) reframing the solution as a new category distinct from accounting and efficiency work, and (3) embedding itself in the CEO decision process as the default first input. By the 1960s, hiring McKinsey had become a signal of corporate legitimacy — a Stage 3 outcome where the customer's use of the vendor signals something to the market about the customer's own seriousness.
