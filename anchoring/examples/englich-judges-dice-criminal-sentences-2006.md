# Method in Action: Judges Playing Dice with Criminal Sentences (2006)

> *Example for the [anchoring](../SKILL.md) skill.*

If anchoring were merely a laboratory curiosity, it should vanish in the one setting explicitly engineered to exclude irrelevant influences: a criminal courtroom, staffed by professionals whose entire training is the disciplined weighing of evidence. Birte Englich, Thomas Mussweiler, and Fritz Strack tested exactly this in a series of studies published as "Playing Dice with Criminal Sentences" (*Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 2006). Their participants were experienced German legal professionals — judges and prosecutors averaging more than fifteen years of practice — and the dependent variable was the thing that matters most in their profession: the length of a prison sentence.

The core demonstration used a realistic case file describing a woman caught shoplifting. After studying the file, each judge rolled a pair of dice to determine — so they were told — the prosecutor's sentencing demand in months. The dice were loaded: they came up either 3 or 9. Following the classic anchoring paradigm, judges first indicated whether their own sentence would be higher or lower than that number, then stated the exact sentence they would impose.

Run the case through the Anchoring Analysis:

**Identify the anchor.** The anchor was the dice total, presented as a sentencing demand. It did not arrive as a labeled bias hazard; it arrived wearing the costume of a routine procedural input — exactly how anchors arrive in real deals ("the last round was at X," "the other candidate asked for Y").

**Classify informational content.** This is the purest pure anchor in the literature. A genuine prosecutorial demand carries partial information (a professional's read of the case, contaminated by the prosecution's interest). A dice roll carries none — and, critically, the judges *knew* it carried none, because they rolled the dice themselves, at the table, with their own hands. There was no possibility of mistaking the number for evidence.

**The missing anchor-free counterfactual.** No judge wrote down an independent sentence before rolling. The comparative question — "higher or lower than the demand?" — forced every judge to reason *from* the anchor rather than *toward* their own number. The result: judges who rolled a 9 handed down sentences averaging about 8 months; judges who rolled a 3 averaged about 5 months. The same case file, the same evidence, the same law — and a fifty-percent-plus difference in prison time, driven by a number every participant knew to be random. In the paper's other studies, anchors smuggled in through a journalist's question or set by non-experts moved sentences just as reliably, and self-reported confidence did not distinguish the anchored from the unanchored.

**Expertise and awareness did not protect.** Fifteen-plus years on the bench is the strongest form of domain expertise available, and rolling the dice oneself is the strongest possible awareness that the anchor is uninformative. Neither helped. This converges with Northcraft & Neale's real-estate agents (1987): the machinery that produces a numeric judgment uses whatever reference is present, without first auditing its relevance.

**The structural lesson for the respondent.** The judges illustrate exactly what the skill's respondent move exists to prevent. The defensible procedure — name the anchor, generate your own independent number first, consider what would justify the opposite extreme — was structurally unavailable because the paradigm put the anchor in front of them before any independent estimate existed. In practice this maps directly onto negotiation and forecasting: whoever states a demand first has, in effect, rolled the dice for you. If your sentence, valuation, or forecast is produced after the demand and without a written anchor-free counterfactual, expect roughly the judges' outcome — a final number pulled substantially toward a figure that may contain no information at all.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify the anchor: the dice total presented as a prosecutor's sentencing demand — arriving as routine procedure, not as a labeled offer
2. Classify informational content: pure anchor — self-rolled dice, known-random, zero evidential value; bias operated anyway
3. Anchor-free counterfactual: absent by design — no judge produced an independent sentence before seeing the anchor, and the higher/lower question forced anchor-first reasoning
4. Opposite-side anchor: never generated; judges adjusted from the single given number and stopped, yielding ~5 vs ~8 month averages on identical facts
5. Proposer's power: whoever states the demand first sets the range — in real courtrooms, the prosecution anchors the sentence
6. Respondent's defense: name/reset/work-with was unavailable because no independent number existed to reset to — the structural failure the Process is built to prevent
7. Hidden anchors: the demand wore the costume of procedure, not persuasion — audit inputs that arrive as "just process"
8. Stop-rule: expertise and confidence were not diagnostic — anchored judges felt as certain as unanchored ones, so felt-rigor cannot verify de-anchoring
