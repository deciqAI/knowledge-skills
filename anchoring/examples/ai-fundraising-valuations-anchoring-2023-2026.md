# Method in Action: AI Fundraising and Valuation Anchors (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [anchoring](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The 2023–2026 generative-AI boom is a live, high-stakes anchoring laboratory. When capital floods into a category faster than any comparable can be built, prices have to be estimated rather than looked up — and estimation under uncertainty is exactly where anchoring dominates. Two anchors did most of the work in this cycle: a small number of headline rounds (OpenAI, Anthropic, and later foundation-model and infrastructure raises) that reset what a "normal" AI valuation looked like, and Nvidia's soaring market capitalization, which became the reference point everyone reached for when sizing the value of anything AI-adjacent. Neither anchor tells you what the *next* startup is worth — but each silently pulls the next number toward it. Run the case through the **Anchoring Analysis**.

**1. Identify the anchor.** Name the specific reference number. In this cycle the recurring anchors were: (a) the last marquee round's headline valuation — e.g., OpenAI and Anthropic each raising at multi-billion and then, by late 2024 into 2025, reportedly tens-of-billions and (for OpenAI) into the hundreds-of-billions valuations; and (b) Nvidia's market cap, which crossed the trillion-dollar mark in 2023 and, by 2024, briefly ranked among the most valuable companies on earth. Inside a specific negotiation the anchor is even more concrete: it is the founder's ask, or the multiple in the last term sheet, stated first. Like the prosecutor's demand in the courtroom studies, it rarely arrives labeled "anchor" — it arrives as "the market," "the comp," or "where the round is clearing."

**2. Classify informational content.** This is the crux. A headline valuation is **partial information at best**: it reflects a real signal (a specific company's traction, team, and strategic value to that specific set of buyers) heavily contaminated by the proposer's interest, scarcity dynamics, and the fear of missing the category. Nvidia's market cap is genuinely informative about *Nvidia* — but as an anchor for a downstream application startup it is nearly a **pure anchor**: the two businesses share a buzzword, not a cash-flow structure. The classic error of the cycle was treating a category-level number as though it were a company-level comparable. A verifiable audited revenue figure would be data; "the last AI round priced at N× ARR" imported into a different company is mostly anchor.

**3. Run the anchor-free counterfactual.** The disciplined move for an investor is to build the valuation model *before* reading the deck or hearing the ask — project revenue, retention, gross margin, defensibility, and a discount rate, and write down the number that falls out. For a founder setting a raise, the counterfactual is: what would we ask if no comparable round had been announced this quarter? The gap between that independent number and the "natural" number after seeing the anchor is the measure of how far you have been pulled.

**4. Consider an opposite-side anchor.** Deliberately generate the number that would justify the opposite extreme. What would have to be true for this company to be worth a fraction of the headline comp — thin moats, model-provider commoditization, gross margins compressed by compute costs, churn once the novelty fades? Articulating the bear case is not the counter-offer; it breaks the asymmetry created by only ever hearing the bull-case anchor. This directly addresses the recurring 2023–2026 worry that AI-capex and AI-valuation exuberance had detached some prices from underlying unit economics.

**5. If proposer (the founder raising).** Open bold but defensible. In a hot category, a well-supported aggressive ask consistently outperforms a soft one and correlates with higher closes — but the operative word is *defensible*. An ask justified by your own traction and a coherent model survives diligence; an ask justified only by "AI companies are raising at these multiples" is an absurd anchor that loses power the moment a disciplined investor runs their own model, and it damages the relationship.

**6. If respondent (the investor, or the acquirer).** Name it, reset with your independently generated number, or work with the anchor knowingly while discounting your natural agreement point. "That's the headline number from a different company in a different part of the stack; here is the valuation our model produces and why" is the reset. The failure mode is anchoring on the ask, adjusting insufficiently downward, and calling the result independent judgment.

**7. Audit hidden anchors in forecasts.** Beyond the explicit ask, category-level anchors seep into the model itself: revenue-growth assumptions borrowed from the fastest-scaling name in the space, or a terminal multiple pinned to where "AI companies trade." Identify which anchor is doing the work in each line of the model. Nvidia's run and a handful of headline raises are precisely the kind of vivid, widely-cited numbers that become invisible baselines.

**8. Stop-rule.** If your valuation lands right on top of the headline comp or the founder's ask, that convergence is a warning, not a confirmation — re-run the anchor-free counterfactual. Expertise does not exempt you: the courtroom and real-estate studies show seasoned professionals anchoring while sincerely believing they reasoned independently. A room full of experienced investors is not structurally different from a bench of experienced judges.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify the anchor: the last headline AI round's valuation, Nvidia's market cap, or the founder's stated ask — arriving as "the market," not as a labeled offer
2. Classify informational content: headline round = partial (real signal + proposer's interest + scarcity); Nvidia's cap as a comp for a downstream app = near-pure anchor sharing a buzzword, not a cash-flow structure
3. Anchor-free counterfactual: build the valuation model before the deck/ask; for founders, price the round as if no comparable had been announced this quarter
4. Opposite-side anchor: spell out what would justify a fraction of the comp — commoditization, compute-driven margin compression, novelty churn — to break the bull-case asymmetry
5. Proposer's power: founders open bold-but-defensible from their own traction; "AI multiples" alone is an absurd anchor that collapses under diligence
6. Respondent's defense: name the mismatched comp, reset to the model's number, or discount the natural agreement point knowingly
7. Hidden anchors: growth and terminal-multiple assumptions silently borrowed from the fastest-scaling name or "where AI trades"
8. Stop-rule: a valuation that lands on the comp or the ask is a warning; re-run the counterfactual — expert conviction is not evidence of de-anchoring
