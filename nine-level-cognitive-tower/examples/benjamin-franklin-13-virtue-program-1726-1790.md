# Method in Action: Benjamin Franklin's 13-Virtue Program (1726-1790)

> *Example for the [nine-level-cognitive-tower](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Benjamin Franklin designed one of the most documented personal cognitive development programs in history. His *Autobiography* describes a systematic progression through what maps directly onto the tower levels.

At age 20 (circa 1726), Franklin recognized he was operating at approximately Level 1-2: he had opinions, but they were inconsistent, and he was known for argumentativeness rather than insight. He explicitly noted his deficiency in independent judgment and systematic method.

His intervention: he identified 13 virtues (Temperance, Silence, Order, Resolution, Frugality, Industry, Sincerity, Justice, Moderation, Cleanliness, Tranquility, Chastity, Humility) and created a tracking grid — a weekly chart where he marked each failure with a dot. He worked one virtue at a time for 13 weeks, then cycled through the year.

Mapped to the tower:
- **Level 1→2 (simplification):** He reduced his argumentative style to the Socratic method — asking questions rather than asserting — a simplification that made him more persuasive.
- **Level 2→3 (borrow strengths):** He studied and imitated the *Spectator*'s writing by reconstructing articles from notes, comparing his version to the original, then correcting.
- **Level 4→5 (form independent views):** By his 30s, he was forming original positions on electricity, civic governance, and colonial relations that were genuinely his own.
- **Level 7-8 (methodology + creation):** The bifocal lens, the Franklin stove, the lightning rod, the Albany Plan of Union — all represent Level 7-8 outputs: systematic methodology applied to generate new solutions.

Franklin explicitly recorded the failures:

> "I was surpris'd to find myself so much fuller of Faults than I had imagined; but I had the Satisfaction of seeing them diminish."
> — Franklin, B. *The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin* (1791), Part 2. https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/20203

The key lesson: Franklin's advancement was not linear. He stalled repeatedly at Level 3 (imitation with modification) until he forced himself into deliberate comparison and original production. The tower works the same way: most people stall at Level 3-4 because imitation is rewarded by social feedback, and the jump to Level 5 (independent views) requires tolerating the period when your independent view is worse than the consensus.
