# Method in Action: Taleb's 9/11 Example + Business-History Critique

> *Example for the [narrative-fallacy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The narrative-fallacy framework's empirical demonstration is repeated across multiple domains. Three illustrative cases:

**The 9/11 example.** Taleb's 2007 *Black Swan* uses 9/11 as the foundational case. The September 11 attacks were, by definition, not predicted by virtually any institutional intelligence framework. On September 10, 2001, mainstream consensus assigned them essentially zero probability. On September 12, 2001, every major commentator could articulate why the attacks had been "inevitable" — US foreign policy, Saudi religious extremism, intelligence failures, FAA security weaknesses, etc. The same commentators who had failed to predict produced confident retrospective explanations.

Taleb's diagnosis: the explanations were not lies; they were honestly believed. The narrative fallacy operates beneath conscious deliberation. The human brain, faced with a surprising event, automatically constructs a causal story that makes the event seem inevitable. The story feels true. The fact that it was not predicted by anyone in advance is forgotten or rationalized away.

The same pattern repeats in every major historical event: the 2008 financial crisis, the COVID-19 pandemic, the rise of various political figures, the success or failure of specific companies. The pattern is so reliable that it constitutes one of the most-documented cognitive errors in modern social science.

**Business-history mythology.** The standard story of Apple's success (Steve Jobs's vision, design obsession, integrated hardware/software) is compelling and widely-believed. It is also a narrative-fallacy construction. Counter-considerations:

- In the early 2000s, Apple was nearly bankrupt and survived primarily because Microsoft invested $150 million in 1997 to prevent antitrust regulators from breaking up Microsoft. Without that contingent rescue, no later Apple success.
- The iPod's success in 2001-2007 depended on contingent licensing deals with major music labels that came perilously close to falling through.
- The iPhone's success depended on a particular technological window (capacitive multi-touch becoming practical) plus carrier partnerships (AT&T's exclusive deal). Without these, the iPhone might not have shipped.
- Jobs himself attributed much of Apple's success to luck (NeXT being acquired by Apple at exactly the right moment, the iPod's category creation being preceded by 18 months of internal product failures, etc.).

A narrative that strips out the contingencies and the luck — and most narratives of Apple's success do — produces a misleading "vision + obsession" explanation that suggests other founders could replicate the success by adopting Jobs-like vision and obsession. The 1000+ companies whose founders had Jobs-like vision and obsession but failed are absent from the narrative. The lesson is largely fictional in its causal claims.

The pattern repeats for essentially every "great company" story. Phil Rosenzweig's *The Halo Effect* (2007) — the related cognitive treatment — documents how business books and case studies systematically commit narrative fallacy on a near-industrial scale.

**Post-mortems with neat root causes.** Engineering, medical, and management post-mortems regularly produce "the root cause was X" findings that, on closer examination, are narrative-fallacy products. The genuine causal structure of complex-system failures is usually a confluence of many small factors, each individually insufficient. The "root cause" framing strips out the others. Decisions based on "we fixed the root cause" then leave the system vulnerable to the other unfixed contributors.

The medical / aviation / nuclear-safety community has been moving toward "blameless postmortem" and "Swiss-cheese model" framings explicitly to resist the narrative-fallacy temptation. See [`premortem`](../skills/premortem/SKILL.md) and the broader work on systems safety (Reason 1990; Hollnagel 2014).

The intellectual lineage of the framework:

**Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011)** chapter 19 ("The Illusion of Understanding") treats the same phenomenon under the term "illusion of understanding," explicitly linking it to hindsight bias and the illusion of validity. Kahneman attributes the cognitive root to System 1's automatic story-construction (see [`dual-system-thinking`](../skills/dual-system-thinking/SKILL.md)).

**Yuval Harari's *Sapiens* (2014)** ch. 2 makes the broader evolutionary-psychology case that humans are "the storytelling ape" — our cognitive architecture evolved to construct and remember narratives because narratives enable large-scale social coordination. The same evolutionary advantage produces the systematic error of mistaking constructed narratives for causal explanations.

**Mauboussin's *The Success Equation* (2012)** systematically applies the framework to business and investment, providing the methodology for distinguishing skill from luck in observed outcomes (the "luck-skill continuum").

**Rosenzweig's *The Halo Effect* (2007)**, written contemporaneously with Taleb, focuses specifically on business-management research as a domain where narrative fallacy is rampant.

Three operational lessons:

**First, the narrative fallacy is unavoidable but recognizable.** You cannot stop your mind from constructing stories. You can flag when you are receiving or generating one, and treat the story as a compressed retrospective rather than a causal explanation.

**Second, the structural defense is counterfactual reasoning and probabilistic re-expression.** Asking "if X hadn't happened, would Y still have happened?" forces confrontation with contingency. Re-expressing the narrative in probabilistic terms ("Y likely contributed; here's the evidence; here's what's uncertain") preserves the information while removing the false certainty.

**Third, the lessons drawn from narratives are systematically over-confident.** Founders, executives, and investors who study business narratives as causal recipes ("do X like Jobs did") generally produce worse outcomes than those who treat narratives as one input among many (and account for survivorship + contingency in the original case).
