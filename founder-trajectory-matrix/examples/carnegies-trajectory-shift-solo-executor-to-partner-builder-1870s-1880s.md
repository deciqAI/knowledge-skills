# Method in Action: Carnegie's Trajectory Shift from Solo Executor to Partner-Builder (1870s–1880s)

> *Example for the [founder-trajectory-matrix](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Context:** Andrew Carnegie entered the steel industry in the early 1870s operating as a textbook Solo Executor. He had built his early fortune in railroads and bridges through high personal conviction, fast decision-making, and minimal external counsel. His default pattern was to trust his own judgment over advisors who he felt lacked his operational instincts.

**Step 1 (Axis Plotting):** Carnegie's own retrospective account in his *Autobiography* (1920) places himself explicitly at Low mentor-seeking / Low humility in his early steel phase. He describes overriding partners' financial caution on the Edgar Thomson Steel Works investment (1873) against the advice of his associates.

**Step 2 (Trajectory Identification):** Solo Executor. The plateau signal was visible by the mid-1870s: Carnegie's operational span was becoming a bottleneck. He could not simultaneously manage the commercial relationships, the technical metallurgy, the financial engineering, and the labor dynamics of a rapidly scaling steel operation.

**Step 3 (Failure Mode Recognition):** Carnegie documented the information bottleneck explicitly. The decision to bring Henry Phipps Jr. as the finance-focused partner was not framed as a weakness — it was framed as a structural recognition that the Solo Executor model was incompatible with the scale of operation he intended to build. Per *Autobiography* p. 184: "I had to learn that I couldn't do everything myself."

**Step 4 (Upgrade Move):** Carnegie executed the Partner-Builder upgrade move: he brought Phipps for finance, Captain Bill Jones for operations, and Charles Schwab (not the brokerage founder) for commercial management — each chosen for complementary domain strength, not personal loyalty. The Carnegie Steel Company structure that emerged was explicitly a distributed-competency model, not a hub-and-spoke around Carnegie himself.

**Step 5 (Reassessment):** By 1892, Carnegie Steel was the largest steel producer in the world. Carnegie's own assessment in his correspondence (documented in Harold Livesay's *Andrew Carnegie and the Rise of Big Business*, 1975) attributed the scale achievement directly to the partner composition decisions of the 1870s–1880s, not to his individual capability.
