# Method in Action: US Wireless Number Portability — Regulating a Switching Cost Out of Existence (1996–2004)

> *Example for the [switching-costs](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The existing example shows switching costs being built. This case shows the reverse: a regulator running an implicit switching-cost audit on an entire industry and then surgically demolishing the single largest cost component. It is the cleanest documented natural experiment in switching-cost removal.

**The situation.** Through the 1990s and early 2000s, a US mobile subscriber who changed carriers lost their phone number. Congress recognized number retention as a competition problem: the Telecommunications Act of 1996 mandated local number portability for carriers, and the FCC spent the following years (in its Telephone Number Portability proceedings, CC Docket No. 95-116) extending the requirement to wireless. After repeated industry petitions for delay, wireless local number portability took effect on November 24, 2003 in the largest US markets, with nationwide coverage following in May 2004.

**Step 1 — identify the types.** Run the audit on a 2002 wireless subscriber. Financial cost: early-termination fee on the existing contract. Learning cost: minimal — a phone is a phone. Data migration: contacts stored on the handset or SIM. Integration: none in the enterprise sense. But the **relational/network cost was enormous relative to the product's price**: the phone number was the customer's identity, printed on business cards, held by every friend, client, and institution. Losing it meant notifying everyone and losing calls from anyone missed. Risk premium: modest — carriers were functionally similar. One component dominated the entire table.

**Step 2 — asymmetry analysis.** Cost to stay: next month's bill. Cost to switch: termination fee plus the diffuse, hard-to-price relational cost of number loss. For most subscribers the relational component alone exceeded any plausible annual value differential between carriers, so years-to-recoup was effectively infinite. Rational subscribers stayed with carriers they disliked — exactly the ex-post monopoly Klemperer's 1987 model predicts. The industry understood the stakes: carriers litigated and petitioned for years to delay the rule, behavior consistent with number lock-in being a valued asset, not an accident.

**Step 3/4 inverted — the regulator as switching-cost absorber.** Portability is Strategy C (absorb the switching cost) executed by a third party at industry scale: the customer keeps the number, so the dominant cost drops to near zero overnight, for every entrant simultaneously. The empirical logic had support from an earlier episode: when toll-free 800-number portability was introduced in the US in the early 1990s, Viard's study in the *RAND Journal of Economics* (2007) found that prices fell once customers could move numbers between carriers — evidence that the pre-portability price premium had been a switching-cost rent.

**The aftermath — costs are designed, not fixed.** Carriers responded exactly as the skill predicts: with the number no longer holding customers, they rebuilt switching costs from other components — subsidized handsets tied to multi-year contracts with early-termination fees (financial), family plans bundling multiple lines (relational), and free in-network calling (network effects layered on relational cost). The audit table was rebalanced within a few years. Switching costs are a portfolio: remove one line item and incumbents will reinvest in the others.

Points worth internalizing from this case:

**First, run the audit even on cheap consumer products.** A $50/month subscription carried a switching cost large enough to justify a decade of federal rulemaking. Magnitude is set by the customer's life integration, not by the product's price.

**Second, extraction-focused costs are politically and technologically fragile.** Number lock-in delivered retention without delivering value, so customers resented it, regulators targeted it, and no goodwill survived its removal. A value-focused moat (accumulated data, deep integrations) attracts no equivalent demolition campaign.

**Third, watch for Strategy C executed by someone other than a competitor.** Regulators, platform owners, and standards bodies can absorb or abolish a switching cost industry-wide. If one audit component carries your entire retention story, a single rule change can zero it — model this as bypass risk.

**Fourth, incumbent lobbying behavior is a diagnostic.** How hard a firm fights to preserve a friction is a direct measure of how much of its retention is switching cost rather than product preference. Read regulatory dockets the way you read churn dashboards.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify all seven types: one component (relational — the number itself) dominated the audit for an entire industry
2. Asymmetry analysis: relational cost exceeded any carrier value differential, so rational customers never switched; carriers fought to preserve the asymmetry
3. Incumbent design choice: number lock-in was extraction-focused — customers resented it, and it collapsed the moment regulation removed it, unlike value-focused moats
4. New-entrant strategy: portability was Strategy C (absorb the cost) imposed by regulation; post-2003 carriers rebuilt costs from the remaining six types
