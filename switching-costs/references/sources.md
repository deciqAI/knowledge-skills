# Sources — switching-costs

> *Primary sources for the [switching-costs](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Klemperer, P. (1987). "Markets with Consumer Switching Costs." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 102(2), 375-394. The founding economic paper. https://doi.org/10.2307/1885068
- Klemperer, P. (1995). "Competition When Consumers Have Switching Costs." *Review of Economic Studies*, 62(4), 515-539. The follow-up showing rational firm responses in switching-cost markets. https://doi.org/10.2307/2298075
- Shapiro, C., & Varian, H. R. (1999). *Information Rules: A Strategic Guide to the Network Economy.* Harvard Business School Press. Chapter 5 ("Recognizing Lock-In") is the canonical applied treatment. ISBN 978-0875848631.
- Farrell, J., & Klemperer, P. (2007). "Coordination and Lock-In: Competition with Switching Costs and Network Effects." *Handbook of Industrial Organization*, Vol. 3, Ch. 31. The comprehensive theoretical synthesis.
- US Department of Justice (1969-1982). *US v. IBM* antitrust litigation. Documents IBM's switching-cost mechanisms in mainframe markets. Case dropped in 1982 without resolution.
- Telecommunications Act of 1996, Pub. L. No. 104-104, § 251(b)(2); FCC, *Telephone Number Portability* proceedings, CC Docket No. 95-116 (1995-2003). The regulatory record of US number portability — a deliberate, industry-wide removal of a dominant switching cost (wireless implementation effective November 24, 2003).
- Viard, V. B. (2007). "Do switching costs make markets less competitive? The case of 800-number portability." *RAND Journal of Economics*, 38(1), 146-163. Empirical evidence that pre-portability prices contained a switching-cost rent.
- Chen, P.-Y., & Hitt, L. M. (2002). "Measuring Switching Costs and the Determinants of Customer Retention in Internet-Enabled Businesses: A Study of the Online Brokerage Industry." *Information Systems Research*, 13(3), 255-274. Empirical estimation of switching costs in a modern industry.
- Nvidia Corporation — investor relations, quarterly earnings releases (fiscal 2024–2025) and CUDA platform history. Documents the data-center AI-accelerator boom, market-capitalization milestones (crossing $1T in 2023, later multi-trillion), and the ~18-year CUDA software stack that constitutes the switching-cost moat. https://investor.nvidia.com · https://developer.nvidia.com/cuda-zone
- US Department of Commerce, Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) — advanced-computing and AI chip export-control rules (October 2022; October 2023; December 2024 updates). A regulatory bypass vector forcing a non-CUDA parallel ecosystem in restricted markets. https://www.bis.gov · Reuters/Financial Times reporting on the DeepSeek-R1 release and the January 27, 2025 AI-hardware market sell-off (record single-day drop in Nvidia's market value) as a demand-side bypass signal.
