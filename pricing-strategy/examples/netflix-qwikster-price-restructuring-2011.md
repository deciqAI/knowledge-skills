# Method in Action: Netflix's Qwikster Price Restructuring Failure (2011)

> *Example for the [pricing-strategy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example of what happens when every step of the Pricing Audit is skipped at once — documented in Gina Keating's *Netflixed* (2012), Netflix's own Q3 2011 shareholder letter, and the contemporaneous business press. Where the De Beers case shows the framework executed deliberately, Qwikster shows the failure modes firing simultaneously, in a modern subscription business.

**The setup.** In mid-2011 Netflix's most popular plan bundled unlimited streaming with one-DVD-out-at-a-time for **$9.99/month**. That number was the reference point for roughly 25 million subscribers — the anchor against which any future price would be judged.

**The move (July 12, 2011).** Netflix announced by blog post that the bundle would be split: streaming-only at $7.99, DVD-only at $7.99. A customer who wanted what they already had would now pay **$15.98 — an effective ~60% increase against the existing anchor**. The split reflected Netflix's *internal* cost structure (DVD logistics vs. streaming licensing costs) — cost-plus logic — not any articulation of value in the customer's units.

**The compounding move (September 2011).** Facing furious cancellations, CEO Reed Hastings published an apology post — opening "I messed up. I owe everyone an explanation." — and then announced the DVD business would be spun into a separate service, **Qwikster**, with its own website, separate billing, and a separate queue. Customers who kept both would manage two accounts. The apology conceded the framing error while the restructuring deepened it: every hybrid customer now faced pure loss — pay 60% more, or give something up, plus new friction either way.

**The measurement Netflix never ran, the market ran instead.** In Q3 2011 Netflix reported a loss of roughly **800,000 U.S. subscribers** — its first subscriber decline in years. The stock fell from around $300 in July 2011 to under $80 by late November, a decline of roughly 75%. On **October 10, 2011 — three weeks after the announcement — Qwikster was killed** before launch.

Audit the failure against the Process:

- **Value in customer units (Step 1): skipped.** The split was justified by Netflix's costs and strategic desire to separate the declining DVD business — never by what the bundle was worth to the subscriber.
- **WTP per segment (Step 2): skipped.** No survey, no pilot, no conjoint. Hybrid subscribers — the segment hit hardest — were assumed to absorb the increase.
- **Anchor (Step 3): violated.** $9.99 was the entrenched reference point. Loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) predicts a ~60% increase against an established anchor is experienced as a loss ~2× more intensely than the equivalent gain would please — exactly the asymmetry the cancellation wave expressed.
- **Structure (Step 4): destroyed value.** The redesign removed the bundle sweet spot and replaced it with two products that each delivered *less* than the reference offer.
- **Loss-aversion framing (Step 5): inverted.** The change was framed — and received — as taking something away, the single worst framing available.
- **Stress test (Step 6): skipped.** Announced to 25 million subscribers by blog post with no prior exposure to real buyers; Qwikster itself was reversed in three weeks, a stress test run in public at maximum cost.

The mapped steps:
1. Value in customer units: never articulated — pricing driven by internal cost structure
2. Segment WTP: unmeasured; hardest-hit segment (hybrid users) faced a ~60% effective increase
3. Anchor: the existing $9.99 bundle; the new structure was judged against it and read as pure loss
4. Tier structure: bundle sweet spot removed rather than designed
5. Loss-aversion framing: inverted — change framed as taking away, amplified by the Qwikster split
6. Anchor stress test: none before launch; the public reversal came in three weeks
7. Measurement: ~800,000 U.S. subscribers lost in Q3 2011; ~75% stock decline; Qwikster cancelled October 10, 2011

The framework's lesson: raising prices against an entrenched anchor without value articulation, WTP measurement, or loss-aversion framing converts a structural pricing decision into a mass loss event. Netflix survived because its underlying product was strong — the pricing structure, not the product, produced the near-death experience.

Primary source: Keating, Gina. *Netflixed: The Epic Battle for America's Eyeballs*. Portfolio/Penguin, 2012. Corroborated by Netflix's Q3 2011 Letter to Shareholders (October 24, 2011) and the Netflix blog posts of July 12 and September 18, 2011.
