# Method in Action: De Beers and the Engagement Ring (1947 → ongoing)

> *Example for the [pricing-strategy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example of pricing as designed signal, not discovered cost. Primary-source documented in Edward Jay Epstein's 1982 *Atlantic* investigation.

Before **1938**, diamond engagement rings were **not a cultural norm in the United States**. Diamonds were sold; engagement rings were sometimes purchased; but there was no widespread expectation that an engagement *required* a diamond ring, nor any defensible reference point for what one should cost. **De Beers**, the South African mining cartel controlling the global diamond supply, faced collapsing prices in the late 1930s — the Great Depression had cratered diamond demand, and synthetic diamonds threatened to commoditize the category further.

In **1938**, De Beers hired the U.S. advertising agency **N.W. Ayer & Son** to engineer a fundamental change. The objective, stated in Ayer's internal strategy memos (reproduced in Edward Jay Epstein's 1982 investigation in *The Atlantic*, "Have You Ever Tried to Sell a Diamond?"):

> "**To create a situation where almost every person pledging marriage feels compelled to acquire a diamond engagement ring** … The basic strategy is to create the conviction in the mind of the public that a diamond is the only acceptable token of love, and that the size and quality of the diamond should reflect the social and economic standing of the giver."
> — N.W. Ayer & Son, internal strategy memorandum to De Beers, 1938; reproduced in Epstein, *The Atlantic*, February 1982.

The campaign produced the iconic 1947 tagline — **"A Diamond is Forever"** (written by Frances Gerety of N.W. Ayer) — and, critically, **the "two months' salary" reference price anchor**: an explicit advertising suggestion that the appropriate spend on an engagement ring should be approximately **two months of the giver's gross salary** (later revised upward to three months in some U.S. markets).

The pricing strategy decomposes precisely:

- **Anchor** (Step 3): "Two months' salary." This is the largest anchor in modern consumer pricing — it scales the price *to the buyer's income*, capturing approximately the same fraction of WTP across very different income segments. A $50K-income buyer pays ~$8K; a $200K-income buyer pays ~$33K. **One reference point, segment-scaled WTP**.
- **Loss aversion framing** (Step 5): the diamond is positioned as *the symbol of the commitment* — to spend less is framed as a *loss of meaning*, not a savings. Kahneman-Tversky's loss-aversion mechanism applied to identity and social signaling.
- **Resale invisibility**: De Beers's other crucial decision was to **suppress resale markets** — making diamonds non-fungible after purchase, so the buyer is permanently "in" the position (and cannot test the price by reselling). This converted the purchase from an investment-like decision (with reference to market price) into a pure consumption-of-meaning decision (with reference to the social-signal anchor).
- **Tier structure**: De Beers segmented by clarity / color / cut / carat (the "4 Cs"), each providing a tier ladder that buyers could climb without re-evaluating the underlying anchor.
- **Endowment effect** (Thaler 1980): the engagement ring, once given, is sociologically un-returnable — making the WTP-vs-WTA gap effectively infinite. A diamond bought for $10,000 cannot be sold back for anything close to $10,000, but the *meaning* is anchored at the purchase price.

The empirical result, documented in Epstein 1982 and subsequent industry analysis: from a baseline of essentially zero in 1939, by 1990 **over 80% of U.S. brides received a diamond engagement ring**, and the average ring spend tracked U.S. median income with the campaign-engineered anchor. De Beers became one of the longest-running monopoly pricing systems in modern consumer history — collapsing only in the 2010s under the combined pressure of lab-grown diamonds and millennial-generation skepticism about the anchor.

Walk the Pricing Audit on the De Beers 1938 launch:

- **Value to customer (Step 1):** Not "a stone." The signaled value is *social proof of commitment*, *display of provider capacity*, and *cultural participation in a tradition*. Identifying this *correctly* is what made the campaign work.
- **Segment WTP (Step 2):** Income-scaled — every segment has a defensible "two months' salary" point.
- **Anchor (Step 3):** Two months' salary. A scaling anchor, not a fixed dollar.
- **Tier structure (Step 4):** Four Cs as a continuous ladder.
- **Loss-aversion framing (Step 5):** "A Diamond is Forever" — paying less is framed as devaluing the commitment, not as saving money.
- **Stress test (Step 6):** Iterative — by the 1950s, the campaign was tuned to demographic and income data; by the 1980s, similar campaigns were rolled out in Japan and other markets where the "two months' salary" anchor scaled to local incomes.
- **Measurement (Step 7):** Penetration rate of engagement ring purchases (target: > 80%) and ratio of ring spend to median annual income (target: ~15–20% of annual income).

The framework's lesson: **price is not what a thing costs; price is the position you have engineered the customer to feel is appropriate to pay**. De Beers manufactured an anchor that had not existed three decades earlier, attached it to identity rather than utility, and captured pricing power for 70+ years. The same psychology operates in software, services, and consumer goods — usually with smaller magnitudes but the same mechanisms.
