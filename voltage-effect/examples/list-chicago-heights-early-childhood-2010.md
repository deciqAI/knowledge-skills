# Method in Action: The Chicago Heights Early Childhood Center (2010–)

> *Example for the [voltage-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

This is List's own field-experiment program, and it is the case he uses in *The Voltage Effect* to explain why he built the five vital signs in the first place. Beginning around 2010, List and colleagues (including economist Steven Levitt and, later, Dana Suskind) ran a large randomized early-childhood education program in Chicago Heights, Illinois — the Griffin Early Childhood Center and the associated Parent Academy — to test interventions that raise the cognitive and non-cognitive skills of young children from disadvantaged families. The motivation List gives is that the celebrated small-scale early-childhood studies that shaped policy — most famously the Perry Preschool and Abecedarian projects — were tiny (roughly 60–120 children each) and decades old, yet were being cited to justify scaling early-childhood programs nationally. The voltage question: would those results survive scale?

**Step 1 — False-positive test (is the result real?).** List's starting worry was statistical. The foundational early-childhood results came from very small samples (Perry Preschool randomized roughly 120 children; Abecedarian roughly 110). Small samples produce large, headline-grabbing effect sizes precisely because they are noisy, and a policy world eager for good news tends to seize the one striking result. So List deliberately ran a *large* randomized trial — thousands of families across the program's arms — to see whether an effect of the advertised magnitude was even real at scale. **Gate result:** the vital sign #1 discipline is exactly why the program was large and randomized rather than another 100-child study; scaling on an unreplicated small-sample effect is the first way voltage drops.

**Step 2 — Population representativeness (who did it work on?).** The classic studies enrolled highly specific, often intensively selected cohorts served by unusually motivated staff. List's design instead sampled a broad disadvantaged urban population in Chicago Heights — closer to the population any *scaled* national program would actually serve. The vital sign here: an effect measured on a hand-picked, well-supported cohort will not generalize to the average family the scaled program reaches. **Gate result:** by recruiting the representative population up front, the experiment tests the number that scale would actually deliver, not an inflated one.

**Step 3 — Situation representativeness (where/how did it work?).** The famous programs were delivered by a small, exceptional set of teachers in a single site — a "situation" that cannot be cloned across thousands of centers staffed by average educators. A recurring finding List emphasizes is that *parental* investment and the home environment drive much of the effect, which means the intervention's success depends on a context (engaged parents, high-fidelity delivery) that is hard to reproduce at scale. **Gate result:** the Parent Academy arm was designed to test whether the *situation* — parent engagement — could be moved, because if it can't be reproduced, the classroom effect won't replicate.

**Step 4 — Spillover test (what only appears at scale?).** At small scale, a demonstration preschool competes with nothing. At national scale, general-equilibrium effects appear: a limited supply of high-quality early-childhood teachers means scaling bids up wages and pulls the best teachers thin, and a national rollout changes the counterfactual (what children would have gotten anyway). List's broader scaling work names exactly this class of threat — the effect measured when one small program draws on abundant slack resources need not hold when every district competes for the same scarce inputs.

**Step 5 — Supply-side cost trap (do the economics hold at volume?).** This is the vital sign the early-childhood literature most often ignored. A boutique program can hire extraordinary teachers and keep ratios low; a national program cannot reproduce that talent pool at that cost. If the effect depends on scarce, expensive, hard-to-clone inputs (elite teachers, intensive parent coaching), then the per-child cost rises — or the quality falls — as you scale, and the cost-benefit case that justified scaling erodes. **Gate result:** List's insistence on measuring cost per unit of skill gained, at representative scale, is precisely the marginal-thinking lever.

**Step 6 — Scale-decision + levers.** The Chicago Heights program is the model for *designing for scale from day one*: representative population, representative situation, large enough sample to trust the effect, and explicit attention to the scarce inputs and spillovers a national version would hit. The lesson List draws — and the reason the five vital signs exist — is that policy had been scaling early-childhood interventions off small, unrepresentative, low-fidelity-to-scale evidence, and losing voltage in the process.

The mapped diagnosis:
1. False positive: original evidence = small (~110–120 child) trials; List ran a large randomized trial to test if the effect is real at scale
2. Population: boutique cohorts → List sampled a broad disadvantaged urban population
3. Situation: exceptional single-site staff and engaged parents → tested parent engagement directly (Parent Academy)
4. Spillovers: scarce high-quality teachers get bid up; counterfactual shifts at national scale
5. Supply-side cost: elite low-ratio staffing can't be reproduced at national cost → cost per skill-gain rises
6. Verdict: design-for-scale — the reason the five vital signs framework exists

Founder takeaway: your most-cited proof point may be a small, well-staffed, hand-picked pilot. Before you scale it, re-run it at the population, staffing, and cost you will *actually* have at volume — that is what List built an entire research program to do.
