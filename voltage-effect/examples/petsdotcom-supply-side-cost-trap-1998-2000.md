# Method in Action: Pets.com — Demand Was Real, the Cost Curve Was Not (1998–2000)

> *Example for the [voltage-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Pets.com is the canonical dot-com-era case of the supply-side cost trap (vital sign #5): a business where consumer demand was genuine and even growing, but the unit economics got *worse* — not better — as it scaled, so growth accelerated the losses. It is a startup/tech case, and the documented facts come from the company's own SEC filings and its rapid public collapse.

**Step 1 — False-positive test (is the result real?).** The early signal wasn't a noisy A/B test; it was real revenue and a real, memorable brand (the sock-puppet mascot became a pop-culture fixture and the company famously bought a 2000 Super Bowl ad). Demand for buying pet supplies online was authentic. So vital sign #1 mostly *passes* — the trap here isn't a statistical fluke. That is exactly what makes the case instructive: passing sign #1 tells you nothing about signs #4 and #5.

**Step 2 — Population representativeness (who did it work on?).** Early online-pet-supply buyers were internet-forward early adopters — the standard beta-cohort caveat. But this is not where Pets.com died, so we flag it and move on: the fatal flaw was downstream.

**Step 3 — Situation representativeness (where/how did it work?).** The "situation" that made early orders look fine was heavy marketing spend and venture subsidy creating cheap trial. That situation — subsidized customer acquisition and below-cost pricing — does not replicate into a self-sustaining one; it has to be *paid for* on every incremental order, which points straight at the cost curve.

**Step 4 — Spillover test (what only appears at scale?).** Pets.com scaled into a price war with other online pet retailers, and competitive response drove prices down further while acquisition costs rose — a scale-only dynamic. More volume meant more loss-making orders, not more contribution.

**Step 5 — Supply-side cost trap (do the economics hold at volume?).** This is the decisive vital sign. Pet supplies are heavy, low-margin, and expensive to ship; the company reportedly sold many items at or below cost and subsidized shipping, so gross margins were thin or negative on the very products driving volume. Its own filings and the widely reported collapse show a company that had raised roughly $80–110 million in its February 2000 IPO and spent lavishly on marketing, yet **marginal cost did not fall with volume** — shipping heavy bags of pet food and litter has a hard floor. More orders deepened the hole. The company went from IPO in February 2000 to shutting down in November 2000 — roughly nine months — one of the fastest public-to-liquidation arcs of the era.

**Step 6 — Scale-decision + levers.** The lever that was missing is **marginal thinking**: the decision to scale should have rested on the marginal contribution of the next order at volume, which was negative, not on top-line growth or brand love. No amount of high-fidelity implementation fixes a product whose per-unit economics are underwater; designing for scale here would have meant fixing shipping economics (density, fulfillment, product mix) *before* pouring capital into demand generation. Verdict: DO NOT SCALE until vital sign #5 passes.

The mapped diagnosis:
1. False positive: PASS — demand and brand were real
2. Population: early adopters (flagged, not fatal)
3. Situation: subsidized acquisition + below-cost pricing masks the cost curve
4. Spillovers: competitive price war raised acquisition cost and cut prices at scale
5. Supply-side cost: FAIL — heavy, low-margin goods; negative contribution per order; marginal cost won't fall with volume
6. Verdict: DO NOT SCALE — growth multiplied the loss

Founder takeaway: "people obviously want this" (vital sign #1 passing) is the most dangerous kind of green light, because it makes founders skip vital sign #5. Prove the *marginal* unit makes money at scale before you spend to create demand — real demand into a broken cost curve just fails faster.

*Sources: Pets.com, Inc. SEC filings, including its Form S-1 registration statement and prospectus (1999–2000) and subsequent disclosures (U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, EDGAR). Widely reported timeline: IPO February 2000; ceased operations November 2000. IPO proceeds are commonly reported at roughly $80–110 million and are stated here as approximate. The Super Bowl advertisement and sock-puppet mascot are contemporaneously documented in 1999–2000 press coverage.*
