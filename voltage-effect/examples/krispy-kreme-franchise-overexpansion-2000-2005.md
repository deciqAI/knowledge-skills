# Method in Action: Krispy Kreme's Franchise Over-Expansion (2000–2005)

> *Example for the [voltage-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Krispy Kreme is the SMB/franchise case: a beloved regional brand whose early stores produced extraordinary per-store numbers, which then lost voltage as the company expanded aggressively after its April 2000 IPO. The failure modes are vital sign #3 (unrepresentative situation — early-store magic) and vital sign #4 (spillovers — cannibalization and saturation once density rose).

**Step 1 — False-positive test (is the result real?).** The early result was real and large: new-market openings generated hours-long lines, and the brand had genuine cult status. Vital sign #1 passes — this was not a statistical fluke. Again, that is what makes the voltage drop sneaky: the pilot signal was authentic.

**Step 2 — Population representativeness (who did it work on?).** Early demand came in markets where Krispy Kreme was a novelty and where the brand had deep Southern-U.S. loyalty. As expansion pushed into markets with no such loyalty and more competition, the population buying at those volumes was not the same enthusiastic base — expected voltage: dilution.

**Step 3 — Situation representativeness (where/how did it work?).** The eye-popping early numbers were partly a **novelty and grand-opening situation** — a new-market opening produced a spike of first-time-trial buyers and press. That opening surge is not the steady-state run rate; treating launch-week volume as the scalable number is exactly the vital-sign-#3 error. Fresh, hot doughnuts as a destination experience also depended on a specific store format and operator execution that did not clone uniformly.

**Step 4 — Spillover test (what only appears at scale?).** This is the decisive sign. As the company chased growth, it expanded not just company stores but also **off-premise distribution** — selling doughnuts through supermarkets and gas stations — and packed more outlets into each region. Both moves created spillovers that only appear at scale: putting the product on every grocery shelf **cannibalized** the destination-store experience and removed the scarcity and freshness that drove the cult demand, while regional over-density **saturated** the market so new stores stole sales from existing ones. The per-store economics that justified expansion were computed before these general-equilibrium effects kicked in.

**Step 5 — Supply-side cost trap (do the economics hold at volume?).** Rapid build-out and the capital-intensive "factory store" format strained the model, and the company's aggressive expansion coincided with an accounting controversy over how franchise-related transactions were booked — an SEC investigation followed, restatements were required, and the stock collapsed from its post-IPO highs. The growth machine's costs and financial engineering did not hold up as volume and scrutiny rose.

**Step 6 — Scale-decision + levers.** The missing levers were **high-fidelity implementation** (the scaled, supermarket-shelf doughnut is not the hot-off-the-line destination experience — fidelity was sacrificed for reach) and **marginal thinking** (each new outlet's *marginal* sales were increasingly cannibalized, not additive). A voltage diagnosis would have flagged that expansion pace outran the situation and triggered spillovers; verdict: DE-RISK FIRST — slow density, protect the freshness/scarcity that created the effect, and model cannibalization before every new opening.

The mapped diagnosis:
1. False positive: PASS — cult demand and grand-opening lines were real
2. Population: novelty/loyalty markets → later markets lacked the base
3. Situation: FAIL — launch-week novelty surge treated as steady-state; destination format didn't clone
4. Spillovers: FAIL — supermarket distribution cannibalized destination stores; regional over-density saturated demand
5. Supply-side cost: capital-heavy build-out + franchise-accounting problems (SEC scrutiny, restatements)
6. Verdict: DE-RISK FIRST — protect scarcity/fidelity; model cannibalization per opening

Founder takeaway: your flagship's numbers include a novelty spike and your own operating magic — neither clones. Before you franchise or blanket a region, separate opening-week volume from steady-state, and model how each new outlet *cannibalizes* the ones you already have. Reach that dilutes the very thing customers loved is a voltage drop, not growth.
