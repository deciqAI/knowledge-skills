# Sources — voltage-effect

> *Primary sources for the [voltage-effect](../SKILL.md) skill.*

## Primary (John List's framework)

- List, J. A. (2022). *The Voltage Effect: How to Make Good Ideas Great and Great Ideas Scale.* Currency / Penguin Random House. ISBN 978-0593239483. The primary source: defines the voltage effect and the five vital signs (false positives, unrepresentative population, unrepresentative situation, spillovers, supply-side cost trap) plus the scalable levers (high-fidelity implementation, marginal thinking, design-for-scale). Publisher page: https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/652077/the-voltage-effect-by-john-a-list/
- Al-Ubaydli, O., List, J. A., & Suskind, D. (2017). "What Can We Learn from Experiments? Understanding the Threats to the Scalability of Experimental Results." *American Economic Review: Papers & Proceedings*, 107(5), 282–286. https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.p20171115 — the peer-reviewed statement of the scalability threats that *The Voltage Effect* popularizes.
- List, J. A. (2011). "Why Economists Should Conduct Field Experiments and 14 Tips for Pulling One Off." *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 25(3), 3–16. https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.3 — List's method for field experiments, including the representativeness warnings behind vital signs #2–#3.
- Al-Ubaydli, O., List, J. A., & Suskind, D. (2019/2020). "The Science of Using Science: Towards an Understanding of the Threats to Scaling Experiments." (NBER Working Paper / *International Economic Review*.) A fuller formal treatment of the scaling threats and the "science of scaling."

## Case-study field experiments

- List, J. A., Momeni, F., & Zenou, Y. (2020). "The Social Side of Early Human Capital Formation: Using a Field Experiment to Estimate the Causal Impact of Neighborhoods." NBER Working Paper. Part of the Chicago Heights / Griffin Early Childhood Center program used in the Method-in-Action example.
- Fryer, R. G., Levitt, S. D., & List, J. A. (2015). "Parental Incentives and Early Childhood Achievement: A Field Experiment in Chicago Heights." NBER Working Paper 21477. Documents the Chicago Heights Parent Academy arm.
- Perry Preschool Project and the Carolina Abecedarian Project: the small foundational early-childhood randomized studies (roughly 120 and 110 children respectively) that List contrasts against scaled evidence. Sample sizes are the widely reported figures and are treated as approximate.

## Case sources (companies — via primary filings, not press)

- Pets.com, Inc. — U.S. SEC filings (Form S-1 and prospectus, 1999–2000), EDGAR. IPO February 2000; ceased operations November 2000. IPO proceeds commonly reported at roughly $80–110 million (stated as approximate).
- Krispy Kreme Doughnuts, Inc. — U.S. SEC filings (Form 10-K; disclosures around the 2004–2005 SEC investigation and restatements), EDGAR. IPO April 2000.
- Groupon, Inc. — U.S. SEC filings (Form S-1, 2011; Form 10-K), EDGAR. IPO November 2011.
- Dholakia, U. M. (2011). "How Businesses Fare with Daily Deals." Rice University / SSRN — survey research on merchant outcomes from Groupon promotions, supporting the merchant-spillover analysis (figures cited qualitatively).

*Not cited and why:* Popular-press retrospectives on Pets.com, Krispy Kreme, and Groupon are not used as primary evidence; company facts rest on SEC filings and List's framework, with any approximate figure flagged in-text rather than asserted as precise.
