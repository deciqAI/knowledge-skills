# Method in Action: The AI Buildout's True Constraint — Advanced Packaging and Power (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [theory-of-constraints](../SKILL.md) skill.*

During the 2023–2026 AI capital-expenditure boom, the public narrative treated GPU *design* as the scarce resource: whoever had the best chip architecture would win. Nvidia's data-center revenue and market capitalization exploded on that story — the company crossed a $1 trillion market cap in 2023 and, in June 2024, briefly became the most valuable company in the world, above Microsoft and Apple. But a company can *design* a chip in a way that far outruns its ability to *ship* the finished accelerators and to *power* the data centers that run them. The Theory of Constraints asks a sharper question than "who has the best design?": in the multi-step system that turns a chip design into deployed AI compute, where does work actually pile up? Treating the AI buildout as a throughput system exposes two binding constraints that are *not* GPU design: advanced packaging (TSMC's CoWoS and the high-bandwidth memory, HBM, it integrates) and electrical power plus grid interconnect.

The system is a chain: chip design → wafer fabrication → **advanced packaging (CoWoS) + HBM integration** → board/server assembly → data-center construction → **grid power and interconnect** → deployed, running compute. TOC says throughput is set by the slowest step, and improving any other step yields no system-level gain.

**Step 1 — Identify.** Map the steps and find where work-in-process queues. Two steps showed the classic signature of a constraint — demand backed up in front of them while capacity elsewhere sat ready.

- *Advanced packaging (CoWoS).* Nvidia's data-center GPUs (H100, then the Blackwell generation) are not single dies; they combine logic dies with stacks of HBM on a silicon interposer using TSMC's CoWoS (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) packaging. Throughout 2023–2024, CoWoS capacity — not wafer starts on the logic die — was the widely reported bottleneck on how many accelerators could actually be built. TSMC publicly and repeatedly stated it was aggressively expanding CoWoS capacity to meet AI demand and still expected to remain tight. HBM supply (dominated by SK Hynix, with Samsung and Micron) was the paired scarcity: SK Hynix reported that its HBM capacity was effectively sold out well ahead, and Nvidia's own commentary pointed to memory and packaging, not logic wafers, as the gating factor.
- *Electrical power and grid interconnect.* As deployment scaled, the binding constraint migrated downstream to energy. AI data centers draw power at a scale that strains grid interconnection queues and generation. This is why hyperscalers began signing long-horizon power deals — including nuclear: Microsoft's 2024 agreement to restart a unit at Three Mile Island (Crane Clean Energy Center) to supply data-center load, and Amazon's and Google's 2024 announcements of investments in nuclear power (including small modular reactors). When the largest, most capitalized buyers in the world start contracting for reactors, the WIP is queuing in front of *power*, not silicon.

The evidence is the TOC signature: buyers with effectively unlimited budgets could not convert money into deployed compute at the rate they wanted, because output was pinned to packaging and power throughput — not to chip design, where supply was comparatively abundant.

**Step 2 — Exploit.** Get maximum output from the constraint with no new capital. On packaging, this meant redesigning products and allocation to squeeze the most useful compute out of each scarce CoWoS slot and each HBM stack — prioritizing the highest-value configurations and customers, and co-designing chips to the packaging that actually existed rather than the packaging one wished existed. On power, exploitation meant siting new capacity where interconnect and generation were already available, improving power-usage effectiveness, and shifting workloads to the times and places where power was least constrained — extracting more useful compute per available megawatt before building anything new.

**Step 3 — Subordinate.** Pace every other step to the constraint's rate. This is the step the market's "best design wins" narrative got most wrong. Fabricating more logic wafers, or announcing ever-more-powerful GPU designs, produces no additional deployed compute if it just accumulates in front of the packaging and power steps — that is over-production, the factory-floor error transplanted to a $1-trillion supply chain. Subordination showed up as buyers and suppliers reorganizing around the true bottlenecks: allocation of scarce accelerators managed centrally; multi-year, take-or-pay commitments for HBM and packaging to reserve constraint capacity; and hyperscaler expansion plans gated by, and sequenced to, secured power rather than to chip availability. The correct local metric is not "wafers started" or "chips designed" but "compute deployed and powered."

**Step 4 — Elevate.** Only after exploiting and subordinating, add capacity *at the constraint* — because that is the highest-ROI investment in the system. This is exactly where the largest capital went. TSMC invested heavily to multiply CoWoS capacity and expanded advanced packaging (including new packaging facilities); HBM makers (SK Hynix, Micron, Samsung) committed large capital to expand HBM output; and hyperscalers moved to elevate the power constraint directly with the nuclear and generation deals above. Note the discipline: money poured into packaging and power, the constraints — not into designing yet another GPU, the non-constraint. Elevating a non-constraint would have added cost and inventory without adding throughput.

**Step 5 — Repeat.** The constraint moves. As packaging capacity was elevated through 2024–2025, power and interconnect became the more visible binding step, which is why the energy deals clustered in that window. And the whole system faced a demand-side shock that stress-tested the constraint logic: in January 2025, the Chinese lab DeepSeek released models reported to be trained far more cheaply than assumed, triggering a sharp sell-off in AI-exposed stocks (Nvidia fell roughly 17% in a single session on January 27, 2025, one of the largest single-day market-value losses in U.S. history) on fears that far less hardware might be needed. Layered on top were successive U.S. export-control rounds (2022, tightened in 2023 and 2024) restricting advanced-AI-chip sales to China — a policy-imposed constraint on *demand routing* rather than on physical throughput. TOC's Step 5 is precisely this: re-identify, because the binding constraint in 2026 is not guaranteed to be the one that bound in 2023.

The lesson is the one *The Goal* taught on a factory floor, at civilization scale: the system's throughput was never set by its most glamorous step. Design capability was abundant relative to the ability to package it into finished accelerators and to feed it electricity. Whoever mistook the design contest for the real game invested in a non-constraint and got no throughput for it. Whoever identified packaging and power — and exploited, subordinated, then elevated *them* — moved the actual number.

The mapped steps:
1. Identify: WIP queues in front of advanced packaging (CoWoS + HBM) and, downstream, grid power/interconnect — not GPU design or logic-wafer fab
2. Exploit: maximize compute per scarce packaging slot / HBM stack and per available megawatt, with no new capital
3. Subordinate: stop over-producing designs and logic wafers ahead of the bottleneck; measure "compute deployed and powered," not "chips designed"; reserve constraint capacity with long-term commitments
4. Elevate: pour capital into CoWoS/HBM expansion and into new power (including nuclear) — the constraints — not into another non-constraint GPU
5. Repeat: constraint migrates from packaging toward power; demand shocks (DeepSeek, Jan 2025) and export controls re-shape the picture — re-identify
