# Method in Action: Bastiat's Broken Window, 1850

> *Example for the [opportunity-cost](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The 1850 essay is one of the most-cited works in classical economics — not because Bastiat invented opportunity cost (the concept predates him), but because he produced the canonical *operational illustration* of why the concept matters and why people systematically ignore it.

The setup of the broken-window parable is precise. Bastiat wrote:

> "Have you ever witnessed the anger of the good shopkeeper, James Goodfellow, when his careless son happened to break a square of glass? If you have been present at such a scene, you will most assuredly bear witness to the fact, that every one of the spectators, were there even thirty of them, by common consent apparently, offered the unfortunate owner this invariable consolation — 'It is an ill wind that blows nobody good. Everybody must live, and what would become of the glaziers if panes of glass were never broken?'"
>
> — Bastiat (1850), §1.

The spectators' reasoning seems compelling: the glazier earns six francs; he spends those six francs at other shops; the multiplier effect spreads through the economy. By breaking the window, the shopkeeper's son has *stimulated economic activity*.

Bastiat's counter is the entire intellectual move that opportunity-cost reasoning requires:

> "Suppose it cost six francs to repair the damage, and you say that the accident brings six francs to the glazier's trade — that it encourages that trade to the amount of six francs — I grant it; I have not a word to say against it; you reason justly. The glazier comes, performs his task, receives his six francs, rubs his hands, and, in his heart, blesses the careless child. All this is *that which is seen*.
>
> But if, on the other hand, you come to the conclusion, as is too often the case, that it is a good thing to break windows, that it causes money to circulate, and that the encouragement of industry in general will be the result of it, you will oblige me to call out, 'Stop there! your theory is confined to that which is seen; it takes no account of that which is not seen.'
>
> It is not seen that as our shopkeeper has spent six francs upon one thing, he cannot spend them upon another. It is not seen that if he had not had a window to replace, he would, perhaps, have replaced his old shoes, or added another book to his library. In short, he would have used his six francs in some way, which this accident has prevented."
>
> — Bastiat (1850), §1.

The "it is not seen" sentence is the foundational opportunity-cost insight. The broken window doesn't create six francs of economic activity; it *redirects* six francs of activity from one shopkeeper-chosen use to one accident-forced use. Society is poorer by the value of the foregone shoe purchase (or book, or whatever else the shopkeeper would have chosen) and richer only by the glazier's repair work — which would not have been net-new economic activity in any case.

Bastiat extended the same reasoning to multiple targets: military spending (the wages paid to a soldier are "seen"; the productive labor the soldier did not perform is "not seen"), tariffs (the protected industry's gains are "seen"; the consumers' foregone purchasing power is "not seen"), public works (the bridge built is "seen"; the alternatives the tax money could have funded are "not seen"). Each application has the same structure: the visible benefit is real but partial; the invisible foregone alternative is the cost.

The principle was systematized by **Carl Menger** and the Austrian School of economics in the 1870s-1890s, who developed subjective value theory — the recognition that cost is not an objective property of goods but a subjective consequence of decision-makers choosing among alternatives. **Friedrich von Wieser** (1889) coined the term "opportunity cost" (*Opportunitätskosten*) and made it central to the marginalist analysis that became modern microeconomics.

The principle's modern foundational treatment is **James M. Buchanan's 1969** book *Cost and Choice*. Buchanan, a Nobel laureate (1986) for his work on public choice theory, argued that the conventional accounting view of cost — money paid for an action — was confused, and that the economist's view of cost — value of foregone alternative — was the only one that survived rigorous analysis:

> "The choice-making conception of cost is the only one consistent with the logic of human action. The cost of a decision is the most-highly-valued alternative that must be sacrificed when that decision is made. This conception has three properties that distinguish it from the accountant's:
>
> First, the cost is *prospective* — it exists at the moment of choice, not after. Once the choice is made, the foregone alternative is no longer available; the cost has been incurred and is over.
>
> Second, the cost is *subjective* — it is the value to the decision-maker, not to anyone else. Two decision-makers facing the same physical alternatives may face entirely different opportunity costs because they value the alternatives differently.
>
> Third, the cost is *the value of the alternative*, not the price of the chosen option. The accountant's number — what was paid — bears at best a contingent relationship to what was actually given up."
>
> — Buchanan (1969), pp. 38-43.

Buchanan's reformulation has profound operational implications. A founder spending $50,000 on a marketing campaign has not "cost" the company $50,000 in any meaningful decision-theoretic sense. The cost is *what else the company would have done with the $50,000* — perhaps hiring a senior engineer, perhaps extending runway by two weeks, perhaps acquiring a specific tool. The marketing campaign is worth doing only if it produces more value than the next-best alternative use of the $50,000.

The principle has been applied across many operational disciplines:

**Investment management.** Charlie Munger and Warren Buffett have repeatedly cited opportunity cost as the most-important investment concept. Munger's framing: "The whole concept of dividing things up into stocks and bonds and individual investments is wrong. Investment is opportunity cost. Whenever you make an investment, you are deciding *not* to make another investment. If you want to invest properly, you ask: 'What is the best alternative to this investment?' And you compare." (Munger 2003, Berkshire annual meeting transcript.)

**Engineering and product management.** Andy Grove's framing in *High Output Management* (1983): "The output of your time is what matters. If you spend three hours on a meeting that doesn't change a decision, the OC is what you would have shipped, written, designed, decided, or learned in those three hours." Product managers who do not apply OC reasoning to their roadmap systematically over-commit to the visible features and under-invest in the foregone ones.

**Career and life decisions.** The career counselor's framing: "Every job you take is a job you don't take. Every project you commit to is a project you can't do." Founders' job-vs-startup decisions are paradigmatic opportunity-cost cases — see [task pack #4 "Should I quit my W-2"](../task-packs/04-should-i-quit-my-w2.md) for an operational treatment.

**Public policy.** Bastiat's broken-window argument applied to economic stimulus: spending $X on a government program has an opportunity cost (the alternative private uses of $X), and a "stimulus" program is net-beneficial only if its expected value exceeds the foregone alternatives. The argument has been central to debates on fiscal policy from Bastiat through Hayek through modern public-choice economics.

**Strategic resource allocation.** The opportunity-cost framing is central to McKinsey-style strategic portfolio analysis: rank initiatives by expected value, deploy capital and management attention to the top-ranked ones, recognize that resources allocated to lower-ranked initiatives have an opportunity cost equal to the value of the top-ranked alternatives.

Three operational lessons:

**First, the seen-vs-unseen asymmetry is structural.** The visible benefits of a decision are easy to point to; the invisible foregone alternatives require deliberate construction. Decision processes that don't include the construction step systematically over-value the visible and under-value the foregone.

**Second, non-financial OCs are often the largest.** A meeting that costs $0 in cash but consumes 4 hours of founder attention has an enormous opportunity cost in the long-tail of what those 4 hours of focus could have produced. Most startup resource-allocation errors are in attention, time, and team capacity — not in money.

**Third, opportunity-cost analysis is bidirectional.** Bastiat's parable is usually told from the "broken window = foregone alternative" angle. But it also applies to "not breaking windows" — i.e., choosing the status quo also has an opportunity cost (the alternative that could have been pursued). Both action and inaction have OCs; the discipline is to evaluate both honestly.
