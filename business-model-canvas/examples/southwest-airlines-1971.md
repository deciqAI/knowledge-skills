# Method in Action: Southwest Airlines (1971 → ongoing)

> *Example for the [business-model-canvas](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example. Not a CEO-icon parable — primary-source documented, SEC-disclosed, and the textbook case of business-model innovation.

When **Southwest Airlines** launched scheduled service in June 1971 from Dallas, the U.S. domestic airline industry ran on a single dominant business model: **hub-and-spoke routing, multi-aircraft fleets, full-service in-cabin amenities, business and leisure segments served by the same plane, and price discrimination by class.** Every major incumbent — American, United, Delta, TWA, Eastern — varied the model at the margins but did not break it.

Herb Kelleher described Southwest's deliberate, block-level reconstruction of the model in plain terms:

> "The strategy of Southwest is to be the low-cost producer. If you're not the low-cost producer in any business, you're vulnerable to someone who is. We've never lost sight of that."
> — Herb Kelleher, cited in Freiberg & Freiberg, *Nuts!* (1996, p. 51)

**Herb Kelleher**, Rollin King, and the early Southwest team rebuilt nearly every block of the canvas. Map it:

- **Customer Segments:** *price-sensitive intra-state and short-haul travelers* — explicitly the segment **incumbents underserved** because price was set by hub-network economics, not customer willingness-to-pay. Not "all flyers."
- **Value Propositions:** *low fare + frequent departures + on-time arrival* — three things measurable to the customer. Removed: assigned seats, meals, baggage transfer to other carriers, business-class amenities.
- **Channels:** initially **direct phone booking + airport ticket counter**; bypassed travel-agent commissions; later among the first airlines to push fully **direct web booking** in the 1990s.
- **Customer Relationships:** self-service + open-seating; minimal frills; built around the *"low cost = fun, not joyless"* culture (a key non-obvious move; price-sensitive customers were treated as smart, not as second-class).
- **Revenue Streams:** **point-to-point ticket revenue only**, no baggage fees, no frequent-flyer-program revenue, no ancillaries (most of these came later as the industry copied).
- **Key Resources:** **single-aircraft type** — Boeing 737 only, simplifying training, maintenance, parts, and crew scheduling; secondary airports (Love Field, Midway, Hobby — cheaper landing fees, faster turnarounds).
- **Key Activities:** **fast aircraft turnaround at the gate** — Southwest pioneered ~10-minute turnarounds vs. industry's 45+, doubling aircraft utilization. Cross-trained crews. Direct point-to-point routing instead of hub-and-spoke.
- **Key Partners:** Boeing (single airframe vendor); secondary airports (lower-cost city pairs); fuel hedging counterparties (Southwest was famously aggressive on fuel hedging through the 1990s–2000s, a partner choice with massive cost implications).
- **Cost Structure:** **lowest unit cost in the industry**, by structural design — single fleet type, secondary airports, fast turns, no frills, no commissions. Cost-driven business model, where cost discipline IS the value prop.

Walk the Audit on Southwest's 1971 canvas:

- Customer Segments: **VALIDATED** through prior intra-Texas carrier studies and the founding team's own market work.
- Value Propositions: **ASSUMED-then-validated** within 18 months by passenger booking data.
- Channels: **VALIDATED** by direct-booking conversion data.
- Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities: each load-bearing and each tested empirically through the 1970s.
- Key Partners: **ASSUMED** at founding — single-aircraft commitment to Boeing was a major bet (if 737 had a structural problem, Southwest had no alternative). Later validated by 737 reliability.
- Cost Structure: **VALIDATED** quarterly through public SEC filings (Southwest has been continuously profitable from 1973 through 2019, an industry record — only the COVID-19 pandemic interrupted, an exogenous shock that pre-canvas could not have surfaced as a specific failure mode but is exactly the kind of risk inversion would have named as "what could kill us?").

The lesson for the canvas user: **Southwest did not innovate on one block — it innovated on eight of nine simultaneously**, with each block reinforcing the others (single fleet type enables fast turns enables more flights enables higher utilization enables lower cost enables lower fares enables more demand for the price-sensitive segment). This is what the canvas is *for*: surfacing whether your blocks **reinforce each other into a system** or are a list of independently-chosen options that don't compose.
