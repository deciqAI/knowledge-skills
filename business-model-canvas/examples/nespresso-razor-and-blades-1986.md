# Method in Action: Nespresso's Razor-and-Blades Pivot (1986–2000)

> *Example for the [business-model-canvas](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Nespresso is the canonical documented case of a business rescued not by changing the product but by rebuilding the blocks around it. The espresso-capsule system — patented inside Nestlé by engineer Eric Favre in 1976 — was technically sound at launch and commercially failing anyway. The technology never changed. The canvas did.

**The failing 1986 canvas.** Nestlé founded Nespresso SA in 1986 and launched the system into the **office and restaurant segment** in Switzerland, Japan, and Italy, selling machines and capsules through conventional distribution alongside its institutional coffee business. Sales stalled; the venture came close to being shut down. Map the failure block-by-block: the Customer Segment was wrong (offices already had cheap filter coffee and no one owned the purchase decision), Channels were wrong (institutional distribution buried a premium product), and the Revenue Stream had no recurring engine — every block was inherited from Nestlé's existing model rather than designed for this value proposition.

**The 1988 rebuild.** Jean-Paul Gaillard, brought in to run the unit in 1988, kept the product and rewrote the model. Map the new canvas onto the nine blocks:

- **Customer Segments:** affluent **households** — individual consumers who wanted café-quality espresso at home. Not "coffee drinkers"; a named premium segment that offices had never been.
- **Value Propositions:** restaurant-quality espresso, one portioned capsule, one button, zero mess or barista skill — the customer buys *convenience plus status*, in their own units, not coffee-machine features.
- **Channels:** split by product. **Machines** sold through ordinary retail (department stores, appliance shops) to create visibility and lower the entry barrier; **capsules** sold direct-only through the **Nespresso Club** — mail and phone order at first, web later, boutiques from 2000 (first store, Paris). Direct capsule sales meant Nespresso owned pricing, data, and the relationship.
- **Customer Relationships:** the Club itself — every machine buyer enrolled, reorders personal and frictionless, the premium relationship reinforcing the premium positioning.
- **Revenue Streams:** **razor-and-blades.** Machines near cost (margin conceded to partners and retail) to drive installed base; profit concentrated in high-margin **recurring capsule revenue** from a locked-in, patent-protected consumable.
- **Key Resources:** the patent estate around the capsule-machine system (Nespresso built a wall of patents, keeping compatible capsules out for two decades), the Nespresso Club customer database, and the premium brand.
- **Key Activities:** capsule production and quality control, Club operations and direct fulfillment, marketing the brand — *not* machine manufacturing.
- **Key Partners:** machine manufacturers (Turmix, then Krups, De'Longhi and others) building the machines under license — carrying the low-margin hardware so Nespresso could concentrate on the blades. Named partners, with a clear role and real replacement risk deliberately spread across several licensees.
- **Cost Structure:** value-driven, not cost-driven — spend on brand, Club service, and capsule quality; machine costs pushed onto partners.

Run the Audit on the 1988 canvas: the load-bearing **ASSUMED** claims were (1) households would pay a steep per-cup premium for portioned espresso, and (2) they would tolerate a single-source direct channel for refills. Both were testable — and the Club structure *was* the test instrument, since every reorder was observed behavior, not stated intent. Both validated: the business turned profitable in the mid-1990s and became one of Nestlé's fastest-growing billion-franc brands by the 2000s. The remaining ASSUMED claim — patent protection as a Key Resource — expired on schedule: when core patents lapsed around 2011–2012, compatible-capsule competitors entered exactly through that block.

The lesson for the canvas user: 1986 and 1988 Nespresso had the **same product and the same value proposition technology**, yet one model died and one compounded — because Segments, Channels, Relationships, and Revenue Streams are design choices, not consequences of the product. When a good product is failing, audit the other eight blocks before touching the first.

The mapped steps:
1. Customer Segments: pivoted from offices/restaurants to a named premium household segment
2. Value Propositions: restated in customer units — café-quality espresso, one button, no mess
3. Channels: split by economics — machines via retail for awareness/purchase, capsules direct via the Club for delivery/after-sales
4. Customer Relationships: the Nespresso Club as a personal, data-generating relationship
5. Revenue Streams: razor-and-blades — machines near cost, recurring high-margin capsules
6. Key Resources: patent wall, Club database, premium brand
7. Key Activities: capsules, Club operations, brand — machine-making excluded
8. Key Partners: licensed machine manufacturers named, with role (carry hardware margin) and spread replacement risk
9. Cost Structure: value-driven; hardware cost externalized to partners
10. Audit: load-bearing ASSUMED claims (premium willingness-to-pay, single-source refills) tested by observed Club reorder behavior; patent-expiry risk surfaced in Key Resources and later materialized
