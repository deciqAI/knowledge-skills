# Method in Action: Updating Beliefs About AI Capability and Safety From Eval Results (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [bayesian-reasoning](../SKILL.md) skill.*

From 2023 to 2026, decision-makers had to keep updating two linked beliefs as new evidence arrived: *"how capable is this model, really?"* and *"will this capability (or this safety property) hold up once it is deployed at scale and pushed out-of-distribution?"* The same class of evidence — an eval result, a benchmark score, a red-team pass, a safety-evaluation report — kept getting treated as near-proof. And the same Bayesian errors kept recurring: anchoring on a vivid score instead of a base rate, and reading `P(evidence | capability is real)` as if it were `P(capability generalizes | evidence)`.

This example walks the anchor case — updating beliefs about AI capability and safety from 2023–2026 eval results — through the skill's six process steps. It is deliberately distinct from the companion example, which focuses on benchmark *saturation and contamination*; here the object of belief includes **safety/deployment generalization** and the surrounding **capex-and-adoption narrative** that a strong eval is often used to justify.

**Step 1 — Name hypothesis and alternatives.** Pick a concrete, decision-relevant claim. H = "the capability (or safety property) demonstrated by this evaluation will generalize to real, out-of-distribution deployment — including adversarial users, novel inputs, and distribution shift over time." not-H is the exhaustive complement and bundles the concrete failure mechanisms: (a) the eval measured a narrower or more favorable setting than deployment, (b) the model was optimized against the eval (Goodhart — the eval was in the training/evaluation loop and stopped measuring the underlying trait), (c) the safety property was demonstrated in-distribution but degrades under jailbreaks, prompt injection, or unforeseen inputs. E = "the model passed / scored high on this evaluation."

**Step 2 — Anchor the prior *before* evidence.** The prior is P(a capability-or-safety claim that looks strong on an eval survives contact with real deployment). The honest base rate across this period is **not near 1**. The documented record (2023–2025) shows repeated cases where an in-eval result did not fully transfer: safety guardrails demonstrated on curated test sets were subsequently circumvented by jailbreaks and prompt-injection attacks discovered by outside researchers; capabilities that looked robust on a fixed benchmark degraded when the same task was mildly perturbed. Set the prior deliberately, below the confidence the headline number invites — and only then look at the new eval result. Skipping this step is the single most common failure the skill warns about.

**Step 3 — Estimate the likelihood ratio.** LR = P(E | H) / P(E | not-H). P(E | H) is high: a genuinely robust, generalizing capability should pass the eval. The decisive, routinely-skipped term is **P(E | not-H)**: how often does a model *pass this eval* even when the property will *not* generalize? When the eval is public, fixed, narrow, or was itself a target of optimization, P(E | not-H) is also high — a Goodharted, format-overfit, or in-distribution-only model passes too. As P(E | not-H) approaches P(E | H), the likelihood ratio approaches 1 and the eval result becomes **non-diagnostic** for the question that actually matters (deployment generalization / robust safety). "It passed the safety eval" feels dispositive; its evidential weight is P(E|H)/P(E|not-H), and for a static public eval that ratio can be close to 1.

**Step 4 — Compute the posterior.** Posterior odds = prior odds × LR. If the prior on "generalizes to deployment" was moderate and the LR of a fixed public eval is near 1, the posterior barely moves above the prior — the vivid result, correctly weighted, adds little. By contrast, an eval that is **held-out, adversarial, run by an independent party, and refreshed to prevent optimization-to-the-test** has a genuinely large LR: P(E | not-H) is low there, because a model that merely gamed a static eval has nothing to game. That evidence moves the posterior substantially. The rule: an eval's evidential weight is set almost entirely by P(E | not-H), not by how high the number is.

**Step 5 — Check evidence dependence.** Many eval results are not independent updates. Public benchmarks and safety suites can share source data, formats, and even items; a model tuned to do well on one class of academic-exam-style eval tends to do well on correlated ones for the same reason. Stacking five more strong results that share the same contamination or optimization pathway is like five witnesses repeating one rumor — treat it as roughly *one* correlated piece of evidence, not five independent multiplications. Multiplying correlated likelihoods overstates the update, exactly the error the skill flags.

**Step 6 — Commit and act.** State the posterior honestly: a strong result on a fixed, public, possibly-optimized eval leaves the probability of robust deployment generalization *close to the prior*, not near certainty. This has a direct decision consequence for the 2024–2026 environment, where record AI capex, soaring valuations, and rapid enterprise-adoption claims are frequently justified by pointing at eval numbers: the *number* is often low-LR evidence for the *business claim* it is asked to support. The trust threshold for "deploy this in production" or "underwrite this spend" should require high-LR evidence. Name the next evidence that would most move the belief: performance on a private, freshly built held-out set; adversarial and perturbed variants (renamed variables, altered numbers, added distractors); independent red-team results under realistic attack; and post-deployment incident data. If the property survives *those*, P(E | not-H) was genuinely low and the posterior climbs. If a small perturbation or a novel jailbreak collapses it, not-H was the better explanation all along.

The mapped steps:

1. **Name hypothesis and alternatives:** H = capability/safety property generalizes to real deployment; not-H = eval was narrower/favorable, optimized-against (Goodhart), or in-distribution-only. Exhaustive complement.
2. **Anchor the prior before evidence:** base rate that a strong-looking eval survives real deployment is *not* near 1, given the documented 2023–2025 record of jailbreaks, prompt injection, and perturbation-sensitive capabilities.
3. **Estimate the likelihood ratio:** the decisive term is P(E | not-H). On fixed/public/optimized-against evals it is high, driving LR toward 1 — non-diagnostic.
4. **Compute the posterior:** near-1 LR ⇒ posterior ≈ prior; the eval number, properly weighted, adds little to the deployment/business claim. Held-out, adversarial, independent evals have large LR and move it.
5. **Check evidence dependence:** correlated public evals (shared data/format/optimization target) are ~one piece of evidence, not many independent updates.
6. **Commit and act:** trust threshold requires high-LR evidence (held-out, adversarial, independent, post-deployment); name the next test that would most move the belief.

The operational lesson generalizes beyond AI: whenever a signal is *cheap to produce under both hypotheses* — because it can be gamed, memorized, optimized-against, or measured in an easier setting than the one you care about — its likelihood ratio collapses toward 1, and treating it as proof is base-rate neglect wearing the prosecutor's fallacy. Ask not "how impressive is this eval?" but "how often would I see this result even if the capability wouldn't generalize — or the safety property wouldn't hold — in deployment?"
