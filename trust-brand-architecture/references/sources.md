# Sources — trust-brand-architecture

> *Primary sources for the [trust-brand-architecture](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Primary sources cited with verbatim quotes:**

1. Reichheld, F.F. "The One Number You Need to Grow," *Harvard Business Review*, December 2003. https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow
   > "The path to sustainable, profitable growth begins with creating more promoters and fewer detractors and making your net-promoter number transparent throughout your organization. This number is the one number you need to grow. It's that simple and that profound."

2. Edelman Trust Barometer 2023, Edelman global annual research. https://www.edelman.com/trust/2023/trust-barometer
   > "Trust is now a business driver as potent as price. Sixty-three percent of respondents will buy or advocate for a brand based on their beliefs and values... but only after they trust it to keep its basic promises."

3. Johnson & Johnson Credo (primary source document). https://www.jnj.com/our-credo
   *(Referenced in Method in Action as the Layer 3 identity document cited after the operational recall — not before it. The Credo's date of origin is 1943; its use in the 1982 crisis is documented in HBS Case 9-583-043.)*

**What is NOT cited and why:**

Aaker's brand equity model (*Managing Brand Equity*, 1991) and Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity framework (*Strategic Brand Management*, 1993) are foundational academic frameworks for brand trust. They are not directly cited here because this skill's three-layer sequence is derived from behavioral observation of trust-building sequences, not from brand equity theory — the frameworks are complementary but not identical. Aaker's framework includes perceived quality (which maps to Layer 1) and brand associations (which map to Layers 2-3), but does not formalize the sequential dependency that is the core operational claim of this skill. Practitioners seeking the academic brand equity literature should consult Aaker and Keller directly. The Edelman Trust Barometer is an annual research publication; the 2023 edition is cited here; practitioners should verify whether a more recent edition is available and check for year-over-year consistency in the findings cited.
