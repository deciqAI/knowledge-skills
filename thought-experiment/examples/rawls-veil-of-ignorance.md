# Method in Action: Rawls's Veil of Ignorance (1971)

> *Example for the [thought-experiment](../SKILL.md) skill.*

John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) built its central argument on a thought experiment — the "original position" behind a "veil of ignorance." No society can be A/B tested; constitutional principles are the ultimate irreversible, high-stakes decision. Rawls substituted structured reasoning for the impossible experiment, and the result reshaped political philosophy and policy analysis for the next half-century.

**Frame (Step 1):** The question: what principles of justice should govern a society's basic institutions? It cannot be tested empirically — you cannot run rival societies from scratch and compare outcomes, and every real observer's judgment is biased by their own position in the existing order. The useful conclusion type: generate principles, and surface the hidden assumption that corrupts most answers — self-interest disguised as principle.

**Premises (Step 2):** Main premise: fair principles are those that free, rational parties would agree to from a fair choosing position. Auxiliary premises: the parties are rational and self-interested; they know general facts about economics and psychology; crucially, they do *not* know their own place in society — class, wealth, natural talents, race, sex, or even their conception of the good life. Most-controversial premise: that parties choosing under such deep uncertainty would reason conservatively rather than gamble.

**Reason step by step (Step 3):** Behind the veil, a party cannot rig the rules in their own favor because they do not know who they will be. Any principle that advantages one group means the chooser might land in the disadvantaged group. Under total ignorance of your own position, Rawls argued, rational choosers follow a maximin rule — rank options by their worst possible outcome and pick the option whose worst outcome is least bad. Each step is defensible: given the premises, no chooser can safely endorse slavery, caste, or unchecked inequality, because they may be the slave.

**Examine conclusion (Step 4):** The reasoning yields two principles: first, equal basic liberties for all; second, social and economic inequalities are permissible only when attached to positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity and only when they work to the benefit of the least advantaged (the "difference principle"). The surprising implication: inequality is not automatically unjust — it is justified precisely when it improves the floor. The experiment also surfaced its diagnostic finding: most real-world policy arguments smuggle in the arguer's known position; strip that knowledge and the argument changes.

**Reliability check (Step 5):** The skeptic's hole was found quickly and is instructive. Harsanyi (1975) argued that rational parties behind the veil would maximize *expected average* utility rather than apply maximin — the conservatism premise, not the veil itself, drives Rawls's egalitarian conclusion. Nozick (1974) attacked a different auxiliary premise: that distributive patterns, rather than the justice of individual holdings and transfers, are the right object of evaluation. The thought experiment survived as a method even where its conclusions were contested — the controversial premises were now explicit and debatable, which is the point of Step 5.

**Apply (Step 6):** The veil became an operational policy-design tool. Rawls documented the full construction in *A Theory of Justice* so the conclusion is recoverable from the premises. The transferable move for any strategist: when evaluating a rule that affects multiple parties — compensation bands, platform take rates, layoff criteria — ask which rule you would endorse if you did not know which party you would be. Positions defensible only from a known seat fail the test.

The mapped steps:
1. Frame: which principles of justice — untestable empirically, biased by every observer's position
2. Premises: rational self-interested choosers; general knowledge only; no knowledge of own position; maximin under deep uncertainty (most controversial)
3. Reasoning: ignorance of position forbids self-dealing → rank rules by worst-case outcome → reject any rule you couldn't accept from the bottom
4. Conclusion: equal basic liberties + the difference principle; surprising implication that inequality is just only when it raises the floor
5. Reliability: Harsanyi's expected-utility objection and Nozick's holdings objection located the load-bearing premises
6. Application: veil-style reasoning as a reusable fairness test for rules affecting multiple parties
