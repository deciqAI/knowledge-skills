# Sources — resource-time-compression

> *Primary sources for the [resource-time-compression](../SKILL.md) skill.*

**Primary sources with verbatim quotes:**

1. Penrose, E. *The Theory of the Growth of the Firm* (1959). Basil Blackwell. https://archive.org/details/theoryofgrowthof00penr
   > "The resources of a firm consist of a bundle of potential services and can, for the most part, be defined independently of their use, while a service cannot be so defined, the very word 'service' implying a function, an activity... It is the heterogeneity... of the productive services available or potentially available from its resources that gives each firm its unique character."

2. Barney, J. "Firm Resources and Sustained Competitive Advantage." *Journal of Management*, 1991, 17(1), 99-120. https://doi.org/10.1177/014920639101700108
   > "A firm is said to have a competitive advantage when it is implementing a value creating strategy not simultaneously being implemented by any current or potential competitors... Resources are valuable when they enable a firm to conceive of or implement strategies that improve its efficiency and effectiveness."

3. Israel, P. *Edison: A Life of Invention* (Wiley, 1998). Edison Papers at Rutgers: https://edison.rutgers.edu/
   (Historical case source for Method in Action section)

**What is NOT cited and why:**

The specific framing of "resource-time compression" (相同的时间创造更大价值 / "create greater value in the same time") and the three-lever taxonomy (resource discovery, organization, allocation) are drawn from Chinese entrepreneurship and strategy practice literature, not a single attributable academic source. The concept of "跃迁型资源" (quantum-leap resources) is a practitioner construct without a specific academic primary source. The Penrose and Barney citations provide the academic foundation for the underlying resource-configuration logic. Users seeking additional academic grounding for time-based competition should consult: Stalk, G. & Hout, T.M. *Competing Against Time* (Free Press, 1990), which provides the empirical basis for time-compression advantages in manufacturing and service competition contexts.
