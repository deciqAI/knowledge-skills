# Sources — strategic-execution-3d9

> *Primary sources for the [strategic-execution-3d9](../SKILL.md) skill.*

## Sources

1. **Robert Kaplan & David Norton, *The Balanced Scorecard*** (1996). https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=9919 — Foundational framework for strategy execution measurement; the BSC's four-perspective structure (financial, customer, internal process, learning & growth) provides the intellectual foundation for linking strategic goals to operational metrics. The 3D9's Dimension 3 (execution control) builds directly on the BSC measurement architecture. Verbatim: "The Balanced Scorecard translates an organization's mission and strategy into a comprehensive set of performance measures that provides the framework for a strategic measurement and management system."

2. **Larry Bossidy & Ram Charan, *Execution: The Discipline of Getting Things Done*** (2002). https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/293905/execution-by-larry-bossidy-and-ram-charan/ — The most direct primary source articulating execution as a discipline distinct from strategy, requiring specific organizational behaviors embedded in reward systems and operating processes. Bossidy's 40-year career at GE and Allied Signal provides the empirical foundation for the claims. Verbatim: "Execution is a specific set of behaviors and techniques that companies need to master in order to have competitive advantage. It's a discipline of its own... The leader of the organization must be deeply engaged in it."

3. **Welch, J. *Jack: Straight from the Gut*** (Warner Business Books, 2001) — Primary memoir source on GE's execution infrastructure; Welch's first-person account of Work-Out, Session C, and the Operating System provides specific behavioral evidence for the Method in Action section.

4. **Tichy, N. & Sherman, S. *Control Your Destiny or Someone Else Will*** (HarperBusiness, 1993). https://archive.org/details/controlyourdestin00tich — Secondary analysis of GE's transformation under Welch by two researchers with access to GE leadership; provides independent verification of the execution infrastructure claims in the Method in Action section.

**What was NOT cited and why:** McKinsey's "7-S Framework" (Peters & Waterman, 1982) covers overlapping territory (strategy, structure, systems, shared values, skills, style, staff) but does not distinguish execution capability from organizational design — making it less precise for the specific bottleneck-diagnosis use case of the 3D9. Patrick Lencioni's *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* is not cited because it focuses on team dynamics rather than organizational execution capability infrastructure.
