# Method in Action: Cost Forecasts in Public Works Projects (1910–1998)

> *Example for the [dunning-kruger](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The Dunning-Kruger structure — confident self-assessment uncorrected by absent feedback — operates at institutional scale in infrastructure planning. **Flyvbjerg, Holm, and Buhl (2002)** assembled the first statistically significant sample of large transportation projects and compared forecast costs against actual costs. The result is the effect written in concrete and steel.

**The claim (Step 1):** Every major public works project begins with a cost forecast presented as accurate. Promoters and forecasting engineers implicitly self-assess as competent estimators — the budget is the confidence claim. Stakes: parliamentary approval and billions in public funds ride on the number.

**The metacognitive prerequisite fails (Step 2):** Infrastructure forecasters had almost no feedback loop. Projects take a decade to complete; forecasters move on before outcomes land; agencies rarely audited final costs against original estimates. The skill needed to *produce* a good forecast — realistic modeling of risk, scope change, and geology — is the same skill needed to *evaluate* one. Lacking systematic outcome data, the profession could not see its own miscalibration.

**The external data (Step 3):** Flyvbjerg and colleagues gathered 258 rail, bridge, tunnel, and road projects across 20 nations, spanning roughly seventy years of construction. Costs were compared in constant prices: actual outturn versus the estimate at the decision to build.

**The diagnosis (Step 4):** Costs were underestimated in roughly nine out of ten projects. Average real cost escalation was on the order of 28 percent — rail projects averaged around 45 percent over forecast, bridges and tunnels around 34 percent, roads around 20 percent. Most damning for the self-assessment question: the authors found no improvement in forecast accuracy over the seventy-year period. Seven decades of "experience" produced zero calibration — exactly what Kruger-Dunning predicts when feedback is absent. Years without feedback yield confident incompetence, not expertise. Flyvbjerg et al. concluded the persistent one-sided error could not be explained as honest technical error alone; the pattern fit systematic optimism and strategic misrepresentation — overconfidence with incentives pushing the same direction.

**The intervention (Step 5):** The corrective was external measurement, not internal vigilance — the skill's core prescription. Flyvbjerg operationalized Kahneman and Tversky's "outside view" as **reference class forecasting**: ignore the forecaster's inside-view self-assessment, place the project in a class of comparable completed projects, and read the expected overrun from the actual outcome distribution of that class. The UK Treasury adopted mandatory optimism-bias uplifts along these lines in its Green Book guidance from 2003, and the method was subsequently applied to major UK and Danish transport schemes (Flyvbjerg 2006).

**Re-measurement (Step 6):** Under reference class forecasting, budgets are set at a chosen percentile of the reference class distribution, and outturn costs are audited against forecasts — restoring the feedback loop whose absence created the miscalibration in the first place.

The mapped steps:
1. State the claim: project budget = forecaster's confidence claim; stakes = billions in public funds
2. Test metacognitive prerequisite: no outcome audits, decade-long lag — feedback loop absent
3. Get external data: 258 projects, 20 nations, actual vs. forecast costs in constant prices
4. Diagnose pattern: ~9 in 10 underestimated, ~28% average escalation, no improvement over 70 years — novice-pattern overconfidence at institutional scale
5. Design intervention: reference class forecasting — substitute the outcome distribution of comparable projects for inside-view self-assessment
6. Re-measure: percentile-based budget uplifts plus mandatory forecast-versus-outturn audits

The operational lesson mirrors the skill exactly: a profession cannot self-evaluate a skill it lacks the feedback to see. Calibration arrived only when an external party measured outcomes and imposed the outside view.

Primary source: Flyvbjerg, B., Holm, M. S., & Buhl, S. (2002). "Underestimating Costs in Public Works Projects: Error or Lie?" *Journal of the American Planning Association*, 68(3), 279-295.
