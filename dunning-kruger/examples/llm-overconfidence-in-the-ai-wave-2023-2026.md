# Method in Action: "I Built a Demo, So I Understand Production AI" (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [dunning-kruger](../SKILL.md) skill.*

When ChatGPT reached a mass audience in late 2022 and generative AI became the dominant business story through 2023–2025 and into 2026, it created an unusually pure Dunning-Kruger environment: a domain where light exposure feels like deep understanding, and where the feedback that would correct the miscalibration arrives only much later, in production. A working weekend demo builds real confidence; shipping a reliable AI product exposes hallucination, evaluation, latency, cost, safety, and data-quality problems the demo never touched. The gap between the two is precisely the metacognitive blind spot the effect predicts.

**The claim (Step 1):** Domain — building and deploying LLM-based products. Verbatim claim — *"I built a demo over the weekend, so I understand production AI"* (and its executive cousin, *"our team can ship this in a quarter"*). Self-assessed percentile — high; a convincing prototype reads as near-mastery. Basis — a small number of successful prompt-and-API sessions plus heavy public discourse. Stakes — hiring plans, roadmap commitments, and in aggregate the very large capital expenditures announced across the industry through 2024–2025.

**The metacognitive prerequisite fails (Step 2):** The skills needed to *evaluate* an AI system — designing evaluations, catching hallucinations, reasoning about distribution shift, estimating inference cost at scale, and handling safety and security failure modes — are the same skills needed to *build* one well. A newcomer who has only seen the demo path cannot articulate what production failure looks like, has received no feedback from a system under real load, and cannot yet judge another engineer's AI work. The demo's success is a curated best case; the tooling is deliberately easy to start with, which compresses the *apparent* distance to competence while leaving the real distance intact.

**The external data (Step 3):** The corrective signal is the gap between prototype and deployed reliability. Industry surveys and reporting through 2024–2025 repeatedly described a majority of enterprise generative-AI pilots stalling before production value, with only a minority reaching meaningful ROI — the outcome data that a weekend demo cannot supply. The foundational research literature already documented the failure modes newcomers underweight: large language models are prone to fluent-but-false output ("hallucination"), a survey literature that predates the hype wave (e.g., Ji et al., 2023). Formal evaluation and safety frameworks — public model cards, red-teaming practices, and third-party benchmarks — exist precisely because self-assessment of an AI system is unreliable.

**The diagnosis (Step 4):** Self-assessed competence high; external competence (measured by ability to ship a reliable, evaluated, cost-controlled system) low → **bottom-quartile-high novice overconfidence**, at both individual and organizational scale. The signature marker is present: confidence rests on curated exposure, not on feedback from a system that has failed in production. This is the institutional cousin of the public-works case — optimism uncorrected by an audit loop — now moving at the speed of a hype cycle, with reinforcing incentives (fundraising narratives, board pressure, competitive FOMO) all pushing the estimate the same direction.

**The intervention (Step 5):** External measurement, not internal conviction. (1) A calibration test: before building, predict specific production metrics — hallucination rate on a held-out set, p95 latency, cost per thousand requests, task success on a real workload — then measure against them. (2) A blind evaluation harness scoring outputs against a rubric on representative, not cherry-picked, inputs. (3) Expert review by engineers who have operated an LLM system under load. (4) A real-stakes pilot on a bounded slice of live traffic. Each substitutes an outside view for the demo's inside view, mirroring reference-class forecasting.

**Re-measurement (Step 6):** Baseline the calibration-test predictions against first production numbers, then re-measure on a 30/90/180-day schedule as data and traffic grow. Genuine improvement shows as *shrinking* prediction error — forecasts converging on measured reliability and cost — not as a more polished demo.

The mapped steps:
1. State the claim: "I built a demo, so I understand production AI" / "we can ship this in a quarter"; stakes = roadmap, hiring, and large-scale AI capex
2. Test metacognitive prerequisite: only the curated demo path seen; no production-failure feedback; cannot yet evaluate others' AI work
3. Get external data: prototype-to-production reliability gap; stalled-pilot reporting; documented hallucination and evaluation literature
4. Diagnose pattern: high self-assessment + low shipped-reliability competence = novice overconfidence, amplified by aligned incentives
5. Design intervention: pre-commit predictions on hallucination/latency/cost/success, then a blind eval harness, expert review, and a bounded live pilot
6. Re-measure: 30/90/180-day cadence; improvement = shrinking gap between predicted and measured production performance

The operational lesson is the skill's core: a convincing demo is exposure, not calibrated skill. Only an external feedback loop — evaluation on real inputs, under real load — converts confidence about AI into competence with it.
