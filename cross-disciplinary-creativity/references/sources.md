# Sources — cross-disciplinary-creativity

> *Primary sources for the [cross-disciplinary-creativity](../SKILL.md) skill.*

1. **Koestler, Arthur. *The Act of Creation.* Hutchinson, 1964.** https://archive.org/details/actofcreation0000koes
   > "The creative act... consists in the discovery of hidden similarities... I have coined the term 'bisociation' to make a distinction between the routine skills of thinking on a single 'plane'... and the creative act, which always operates on more than one plane."
   The foundational theoretical treatment of cross-domain creative combination (bisociation) — the source of the mechanism-vs-metaphor distinction that is this skill's core contribution.

2. **Csikszentmihalyi, Mihaly. *Creativity: Flow and the Psychology of Discovery and Invention.* HarperCollins, 1996.** https://www.harpercollins.com/products/creativity-mihaly-csikszentmihalyi
   > "Creative individuals are remarkable for their ability to adapt to almost any situation and to make do with whatever is at hand to achieve their goals... Most creative people... are able to move easily between different ways of being in the world."
   Empirical documentation of cross-disciplinary mobility as a characteristic of productive creativity, derived from a study of 91 exceptional creators across domains.

3. **Eisenstein, Elizabeth L. *The Printing Press as an Agent of Change.* Cambridge University Press, 1979.** https://archive.org/details/printingpressasa0000eise
   Primary-source historical analysis of Gutenberg's cross-disciplinary combination and its civilizational effects — the definitive treatment of the mechanism-in-action case.

**What is NOT cited and why:** Edward de Bono's "lateral thinking" (1967) is omitted — it describes a process for generating domain-crossing ideas but does not provide the mechanism-vs-metaphor distinction or the bisociation theoretical framework that makes cross-disciplinary creativity rigorously applicable. Tom Kelley's IDEO creativity methods are omitted — they are practitioner process frameworks, not theoretical explanations of why cross-disciplinary combinations produce structural novelty.
