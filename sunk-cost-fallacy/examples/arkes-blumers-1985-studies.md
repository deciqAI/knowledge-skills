# Method in Action: Arkes & Blumer's 1985 Studies

> *Example for the [sunk-cost-fallacy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Hal Arkes (Ohio University) and Catherine Blumer's 1985 paper in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes* established sunk-cost fallacy as a robust, replicable phenomenon. The paper ran ten experiments; we cover the three most diagnostic.

**Study 1: Ohio Theater Tickets.** (Detailed above.) Naturalistic experiment with real money, real tickets, real attendance behavior. The discount manipulation was random, eliminating self-selection. Full-price subjects attended 25% more plays. The mechanism is clean: identical product, identical preferences, different sunk costs → different behavior.

**Study 4: The $10-Million Plane.** (Detailed above.) Hypothetical scenario. Subjects asked whether to spend the last $1 million completing a project that was 90% done, given a competing product that was faster and cheaper. 85% said yes when the $10M was sunk; 15% said yes when there was no prior investment. The presence of the sunk cost flipped 70 percentage points of subjects.

**Study 10: Resort Vacation Trade-Off.** Arkes & Blumer presented subjects with this scenario:

> "You have just purchased a $50 ticket to a weekend ski trip in Wisconsin. Several weeks later, you purchase a $100 ticket to a weekend ski trip in Michigan. You think you will enjoy the $100 Michigan trip more than the $50 Wisconsin trip. After purchasing both tickets, you discover both trips are on the same weekend; the tickets are non-refundable and non-transferable. Which trip will you go on?"

Subjects had to choose. By stipulation, the Michigan trip was the *better* trip. Standard decision theory says: take the Michigan trip; the $150 total is sunk regardless. But:

> "Of the 61 subjects, 33 (54%) chose the Wisconsin trip. The most cited reason: 'I didn't want to lose the more expensive ticket.' The decision was a clean preference reversal: by the subjects' own enjoyment forecast, the Michigan trip dominated, but the $100 ticket weighed more than the $50 ticket in their reasoning, even though both were equally sunk and both equally unrefundable."
>
> — Arkes & Blumer (1985), p. 138.

The Wisconsin choice is irrational by every standard decision-theoretic measure: lower expected enjoyment, and both costs are equally unrecoverable. But the *larger* sunk cost created a stronger pull. The fallacy operates not just on the absolute level of sunk cost, but on the *relative* magnitude across competing options.

The Arkes-Blumer paper triggered three decades of follow-up research:

**Staw, B. M. (1976).** "Knee-deep in the big muddy: A study of escalating commitment to a chosen course of action." *Organizational Behavior and Human Performance*, 16(1), 27-44. Predates Arkes-Blumer; established that organizational decision-makers escalate commitment to failing courses of action, especially when personally responsible for the original decision. Foundational work on the management implications.

**Thaler, R. H. (1980).** "Toward a positive theory of consumer choice." *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization*, 1(1), 39-60. Thaler's paper introducing behavioral economics included sunk-cost reasoning as one of the major systematic violations of standard rationality.

**Garland, H. (1990).** "Throwing good money after bad: The effect of sunk costs on the decision to escalate commitment to an ongoing project." *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 75(6), 728-731. Showed in a controlled experiment that as sunk costs increased, willingness to spend additional resources increased monotonically — even when the additional spending could not change project outcome.

**McAfee, R. P., Mialon, H. M., & Mialon, S. H. (2010).** "Do sunk costs matter?" *Economic Inquiry*, 48(2), 323-336. The methodological critique: argued that many alleged sunk-cost effects are actually rational reputational signaling or option-value reasoning. Some apparent sunk-cost behavior is defensible. But Arkes-Blumer's core findings (decisions reversed by *equivalent* sunk costs) cannot be defended this way.

**Roth, S., Robbert, T., & Straus, L. (2015).** "On the sunk-cost effect in economic decision-making: A meta-analytic review." *Business Research*, 8(1), 99-138. Meta-analysis of 64 studies across 30 years. Confirmed the effect is robust (mean effect size d ≈ 0.6, large), present across cultures, present across age groups, and weakly correlated with general intelligence (smart people show the bias only slightly less).

The framework has shaped operational practice across industries:

**Software project management.** Stage-gate funding models (Cooper 1990) require each tranche of project funding to be justified on its own forward-looking merits, with the previous tranche treated as sunk. The structure exists because individual project managers reliably escalate commitment to their own projects.

**Pharmaceutical R&D.** Major pharma firms use kill-criteria committees independent of project teams. The team that is 90% done with a Phase 2 trial is precisely the team that cannot objectively decide to discontinue. The committee, with no prior involvement, applies pre-committed decision rules.

**Venture capital.** Sophisticated VCs separate "follow-on" investment decisions from initial-investment decisions; sometimes the lead partner on the initial deal is excluded from the follow-on vote. This is a structural sunk-cost defense.

**Military planning.** Vietnam-era "escalation" scholarship (Halberstam 1972; Sheehan 1988) documented sunk-cost escalation at the strategic level: each year of war was justified in part by the years already spent and the lives already lost. Modern military decision frameworks (e.g., Effects-Based Operations) explicitly try to bound this with measurable objective criteria.

**Investing.** Behavioral finance has documented "loss aversion riding" — investors holding losing positions far past the rational quit point because selling realizes the loss. The structural countermeasure: pre-committed stop-losses, with the rule itself enforced by an external party.

**Personal life.** The classic Kahneman example: relationships, careers, and educational programs that people continue past their value-maximizing exit point because of accumulated investment. The fresh-start test ("if I weren't already in this, would I choose to start?") is the operational antidote.

Three operational lessons:

**First, the fallacy is robust to explicit warning.** Telling subjects "ignore sunk costs" only modestly reduces the effect. Warning is necessary but not sufficient; structural countermeasures (pre-committed criteria, stage gates, decision-rights separation) are what actually reduce the bias.

**Second, the fallacy is amplified by personal responsibility.** Subjects who chose the original investment escalate commitment more than subjects who inherited it. The corollary: do not let the person who initiated a project be the sole decider on whether to continue it. This single intervention is the highest-leverage organizational fix.

**Third, the fallacy interacts with completion proximity.** Projects "almost done" trigger the fallacy more powerfully than projects in mid-execution. This is the rationalization: "We're so close." But "so close" matters only if completion provides value. Without value, "so close to nothing" is still nothing.
