# Method in Action: The Concorde Program (1962–1976)

> *Example for the [sunk-cost-fallacy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The Anglo-French Concorde program is the case that gave the fallacy its other name. Dawkins and Carlisle, writing in *Nature* in 1976, coined the term "Concorde fallacy" for exactly this pattern: justifying continued investment by past investment rather than future return. The program is the fallacy operating at nation-state scale, in public, for over a decade.

**Step 1 — Sunk cost and current decision.** In November 1962, Britain and France signed a treaty to jointly develop a supersonic airliner, with development costs initially estimated at roughly £150–170 million. Costs escalated relentlessly; by the early 1970s the bill had grown to well over £1 billion. The recurring decision facing both governments, year after year: continue funding development, or cancel. The treaty contained no withdrawal clause — cancellation by either party risked penalties and diplomatic rupture — so every annual budget round re-posed the continue-or-quit question.

**Step 2 — Fresh-start test.** Would either government, arriving fresh, have started Concorde given what was known by the mid-1960s? The incoming Wilson government reviewed cancellation in 1964 and concluded the project's economics were poor — yet continued. By the early 1970s the answer was unambiguous: rising fuel prices, sonic-boom overflight bans, and per-seat operating costs several times those of the new wide-body jets meant no rational actor would have launched the project from scratch. The fresh-start test failed repeatedly; the program continued anyway.

**Step 3 — Marginal future NPV.** The forward-looking case collapsed years before completion. Sixteen airlines had once held options on over seventy aircraft; after the 1973 oil crisis every option evaporated. Only fourteen production aircraft ever entered airline service, all with the two state-owned flag carriers, British Airways and Air France, on heavily subsidized terms. The marginal cost of completing development exceeded any plausible sales revenue — and this was knowable, and known, well before the final funds were committed. Development costs were never recovered.

**Step 4 — Sunk-cost weight.** Set the prior investment to zero and the decision is trivial: cancel. The load-bearing arguments for continuing were precisely the illegitimate ones — the hundreds of millions already spent, the jobs already created around the program, and the treaty already signed. The money spent argument appeared explicitly in parliamentary and ministerial debate throughout the 1960s and 1970s. When "we have already spent £X" is doing the work, the sunk cost has captured the decision.

**Step 5 — Mechanism.** Three mechanisms compounded. *Social signaling:* Concorde was national prestige made of titanium — cancelling meant admitting failure before the world and before a treaty partner. *Self-justification:* the same ministries that launched the program evaluated whether to continue it. *Structural lock-in:* the no-exit treaty converted normal escalation pressure into a formal obligation, making the sunk-cost argument feel like a legal one.

**Step 6 — Structural countermeasure (the one that was missing).** The 1962 treaty is an anti-pattern: a multi-decade commitment with no break clause, no stage gates, and no pre-committed kill criteria. Peter Hall's *Great Planning Disasters* (1980) analyzes Concorde as a canonical planning failure of exactly this shape. The fixes the skill prescribes are the ones Concorde lacked: tranche funding with independent re-authorization, exit clauses priced in at signing, and decision rights separated from the program's political sponsors.

The mapped steps:
1. Sunk cost and decision: £1 billion+ in escalating development spend; annual continue-or-cancel decisions from 1962 to first deliveries in 1976
2. Fresh-start test: failed as early as 1964 and decisively by 1973 — neither government would have started fresh
3. Marginal future NPV: negative — completion costs exceeded any plausible revenue after airline options collapsed to fourteen state-carrier aircraft
4. Sunk-cost weight: with prior spend at £0, cancellation is obvious; prior spend was the explicit public argument for continuing
5. Mechanism: social signaling (national prestige), self-justification (sponsors as deciders), structural lock-in (no-exit treaty)
6. Countermeasure: the absent break clause and absent stage gates are the negative template — pre-commit exit terms before the first pound is spent

Primary source: Dawkins, R., & Carlisle, T. R. (1976). "Parental investment, mate desertion and a fallacy." *Nature*, 262, 131-133. https://doi.org/10.1038/262131a0 — the paper that named the Concorde fallacy. See also Hall, P. (1980). *Great Planning Disasters.* Weidenfeld & Nicolson.
