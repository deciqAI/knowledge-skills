# Method in Action: AI Build-Out Sunk Costs (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [sunk-cost-fallacy](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The 2023–2026 generative-AI wave produced a textbook environment for the sunk-cost fallacy: large, fast, irrecoverable commitments made under uncertainty, followed by pressure to keep funding them *because* of what was already spent. Two archetypes recur. First, the **enterprise** that stood up an in-house model or a legacy on-prem stack, sank real money and headcount into it, and then hesitated to move to a cheaper, better external option because "we already built our own." Second, the **founder** whose product is a thin wrapper over a foundation-model API — a category that commoditized rapidly as model providers shipped native features — who keeps going largely because of the runway and reputation already invested. Neither pattern requires any specific proprietary figure to diagnose; the reasoning error is visible on its face. This example walks the generic case through the skill's process. Treat every number below as illustrative unless a public source is cited.

**Step 1 — Specify sunk cost and current decision.** Take the enterprise archetype. *Prior investment:* a year-plus of engineering time, GPU procurement or reserved cloud capacity, a fine-tuning and data-labeling pipeline, and internal political capital spent selling "we'll own our model" to leadership. *Current decision, at renewal or re-budget:* keep investing in the in-house/on-prem stack, or migrate inference to a hosted frontier model (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, or an open-weights model served cheaply) and redeploy the team. *Argument for continuing:* "we've already sunk a year and millions into this; switching wastes it." *Argument against:* external model quality and price-performance improved dramatically over 2023–2025 — API prices per token fell sharply across providers while capability rose — so the in-house stack may now be worse *and* more expensive on a forward basis.

**Step 2 — Fresh-start test.** If this team arrived today with no prior code, no reserved GPUs, and no leadership promises, would it choose to build and maintain a from-scratch model or on-prem stack for this use case? For the majority of application-layer use cases in this period, the honest answer became "no" — buy inference, don't build the base model. When the fresh-start answer is "no" but the team continues, the sunk cost is the tell.

**Step 3 — Marginal future NPV.** Only forward flows count. Continue: ongoing GPU/opex + maintenance headcount + opportunity cost of that team, against the value the in-house stack delivers over a hosted alternative (which is often near zero or negative once external price-performance is accounted for). Quit + reallocate: one-time migration cost + retraining, against lower ongoing inference cost and the team freed for differentiated product work. In many application-layer cases the marginal NPV of *continuing* the bespoke stack turned negative once hosted models caught up — and the sunk build cost appears on neither side of that comparison.

**Step 4 — Sunk-cost weight.** Set the prior investment to $0. Would anyone choose to *start* maintaining this in-house stack today rather than call an API that is cheaper and better? If setting prior spend to zero flips the recommendation from "continue" to "migrate," the sunk cost was doing illegitimate work. The same substitution exposes the AI-wrapper founder: with prior runway and prior investor commitments set to zero, would you start *this* commoditized wrapper today, knowing the model provider now ships the feature natively? If not, "we've already raised and built on this" is the load-bearing — and illegitimate — reason.

**Step 5 — Diagnose mechanism.** Several compound in this era. *Self-justification:* the leaders who championed "build our own AI" or pitched the wrapper thesis to investors are the ones evaluating whether to continue. *Social signaling:* "we have a proprietary model" and "we're an AI company" are prestige claims that are costly to retract publicly. *Cognitive accounting:* the build is booked as an asset in people's mental ledgers, so abandoning it registers as writing off a loss rather than as freeing capital. *Completion compulsion:* on-prem migrations and in-house model projects are often "almost production-ready," and proximity-to-done feels like a reason to finish.

**Step 6 — Structural countermeasure.** The fixes are the ones the skill prescribes. *Pre-committed kill criteria:* "if a hosted model matches our quality at ≤X of our per-inference cost, we migrate" — decided before the next re-budget, not during it. *Stage-gate funding:* fund the in-house effort in tranches, each re-authorized against a build-vs-buy benchmark run at the gate. *Fresh-eyes review:* a build-vs-buy assessment by people who did not initiate the project and are not measured on its survival. *Decision-rights separation:* the continue/kill call sits with someone other than the original champion. For the wrapper founder, the analogue is a pre-committed pivot trigger ("if the model provider ships this natively, we move up the stack or shut down") set at founding, plus a board empowered to enforce it.

The mapped steps:
1. Sunk cost and decision: a year-plus and millions in an in-house model / on-prem stack (or a founder's runway in a commoditized wrapper); decision at each re-budget is continue vs. migrate/pivot
2. Fresh-start test: for most application-layer cases in 2023–2026, a fresh team would buy inference, not build — the test fails
3. Marginal future NPV: continuing the bespoke stack often turned negative as hosted price-performance improved; the sunk build cost belongs on neither side
4. Sunk-cost weight: set prior spend to $0 and the recommendation flips to migrate/pivot — the sunk cost was load-bearing
5. Mechanism: self-justification, social signaling ("we're an AI company"), cognitive accounting, completion compulsion
6. Countermeasure: pre-committed build-vs-buy kill criteria, stage-gate tranches, fresh-eyes review, decision-rights separation from the original champion

The durable facts underneath this example: generative-AI adoption and capital expenditure rose sharply from 2023 onward; foundation-model API prices per token fell substantially across major providers over 2023–2025 while capability improved; and commentators through late 2025 and into 2026 openly debated whether AI infrastructure spending and AI-company valuations had outrun near-term returns. Those conditions are exactly the ones under which sunk-cost reasoning does the most damage — but the fallacy diagnosis does not depend on how that debate ultimately resolves.
