# Method in Action: Carnegie Steel's Dimension Progression (1875–1901)

> *Example for the [point-line-plane-solid](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Andrew Carnegie's ascent from single mill to the world's largest private company is the clearest documented application of the point–line–plane–solid progression in industrial history.

**Point (1875):** The Edgar Thomson Steel Works opened in Braddock, Pennsylvania — a single Bessemer steel mill. A point: Carnegie could produce steel. So could a dozen competitors.

**Line (1878–1889):** Carnegie systematically integrated vertically. He acquired iron ore leases in the Mesabi Range (Minnesota), purchased or built dedicated railroad connections (the Pittsburgh, Bessemer and Lake Erie Railroad), and controlled coke production through the H.C. Frick Coke Company partnership. By 1889, Carnegie could move iron ore from Minnesota mine to finished steel rail without leaving his own supply chain — a complete, proprietary, integrated pipeline. Competitors who relied on market prices for any input stage were structurally disadvantaged.

**Plane (1890–1898):** Carnegie built a distribution and customer ecosystem — long-term contracts with major railroads (J. Edgar Thomson of Pennsylvania Railroad was an early backer, giving Carnegie guaranteed rail contracts), construction companies, and government infrastructure projects. The "network" of buyers and suppliers who organized their own supply chains around Carnegie's reliability created ecosystem density.

**Solid (1899–1901):** By 1900, all layers reinforced each other: technology (Bessemer process mastery + open-hearth adoption), infrastructure (owned mines + railroads + mills), market dominance (37% of U.S. steel), managerial systems (Carnegie's cost accounting — the most sophisticated in the industry), and capital advantages (retained earnings funding expansion). When J.P. Morgan organized U.S. Steel in 1901 and purchased Carnegie's share, the price was $480 million (approximately $17 billion in 2024 dollars). Morgan could not have replicated the solid — he had to buy it.
