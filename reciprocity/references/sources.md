# Sources — reciprocity

> *Primary sources for the [reciprocity](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Regan, D. T. (1971). "Effects of a Favor and Liking on Compliance." *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, 7(6), 627–639. The Coke experiment: the founding empirical demonstration that reciprocation produces asymmetric repayment and that it operates independently of liking. https://doi.org/10.1016/0022-1031(71)90025-4
- Cialdini, R. B. (1984; 2007 rev. ed.). *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*. Harper Business. The canonical applied treatment of reciprocation as one of six weapons of influence. Chapter 2 is the operational reference for this skill. ISBN 978-0061241895.
- Cialdini, R. B., Vincent, J. E., Lewis, S. K., Catalan, J., Wheeler, D., & Darby, B. L. (1975). "Reciprocal Concessions Procedure for Inducing Compliance: The Door-in-the-Face Technique." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 31(2), 206–215. The primary experimental documentation of rejection-then-retreat / door-in-the-face as a reciprocity-based technique. https://doi.org/10.1037/h0076284
- Mauss, M. (1925/1990). *The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies*. Translated by W. D. Halls. W. W. Norton. The cross-cultural anthropological foundation: gift-and-obligation as a structural feature of nearly every documented human society.
- Gouldner, A. W. (1960). "The Norm of Reciprocity: A Preliminary Statement." *American Sociological Review*, 25(2), 161–178. The classic sociological argument for reciprocity as a universal moral norm. https://doi.org/10.2307/2092623
- Wazana, A. (2000). "Physicians and the Pharmaceutical Industry: Is a Gift Ever Just a Gift?" *JAMA*, 283(3), 373–380. Systematic review of the empirical evidence that even small gifts from pharmaceutical companies measurably influence physician prescribing behavior. https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.283.3.373
- Dana, J., & Loewenstein, G. (2003). "A Social Science Perspective on Gifts to Physicians from Industry." *JAMA*, 290(2), 252–255. The behavioral-economics argument that small gifts produce disproportionate effects and that the bright-line institutional defense is required where per-event analysis is too forgiving. https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.290.2.252
- Kunz, P. R., & Woolcott, M. (1976). "Season's Greetings: From My Status to Yours." *Social Science Research*, 5(3), 269–278. The Christmas-card field experiment — Kunz sent 578 cards to randomly selected strangers and received roughly 200 cards in return, illustrating that reciprocation operates even without prior relationship.
