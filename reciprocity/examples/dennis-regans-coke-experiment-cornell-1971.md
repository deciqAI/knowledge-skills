# Method in Action: Dennis Regan's Coke Experiment, Cornell, 1971

> *Example for the [reciprocity](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The clearest piece of primary-source evidence on reciprocity comes from a single experiment run by social psychologist **Dennis T. Regan** at Cornell University, published as "Effects of a Favor and Liking on Compliance" in the *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, Vol. 7, No. 6, November 1971, pp. 627–639. Half a century later, no other experimental result has produced as clean a separation of reciprocation from liking, and no other study has been cited more often by influence researchers (including Robert Cialdini) as the foundational empirical demonstration of the rule.

Regan's design was direct. Subjects were recruited under the cover story that they would be participating in "art appreciation research." Each subject was brought into a laboratory with one other "subject" — actually a confederate Regan called **Joe**, whose behavior was scripted. The pair was given a stack of paintings to rate. Halfway through, the experimenter announced a short break.

In the **favor condition**, Joe left the room briefly during the break and returned carrying two open bottles of Coca-Cola. He gave one to the subject, saying *"I asked him* [the experimenter] *if I could get myself a Coke, and he said it was okay, so I bought one for you, too."* In the **no-favor condition**, Joe left during the break and returned empty-handed. There was a third **experimenter-favor condition** where the experimenter provided the Coke — important because it isolated whether the personal source of the favor mattered.

At the end of the rating session, Joe asked the subject for a favor in return: he was selling raffle tickets at 25¢ each for a new-car prize his company was sponsoring; would the subject buy any? Joe added: "Any would help, the more the better." The number of tickets each subject bought was the primary dependent measure.

Two findings carried the paper. **First**, subjects who had received the Coke from Joe bought significantly more raffle tickets than subjects who had not — about twice as many on average. The mean number of tickets purchased in the favor condition was approximately 1.93; in the no-favor condition, approximately 1.00. Given that subjects were undergraduates with limited cash and tickets cost 25¢, this difference represented a meaningful net dollar transfer.

The economics matter. The Coke had cost Joe about 10¢ (in 1971 prices). The average subject in the favor condition paid Joe back approximately 48¢ in raffle ticket purchases. **The repayment was nearly five times the cost of the favor**. This is the *asymmetric exchange* property of the reciprocation rule, captured with experimental precision.

The **second** finding was the one that placed the paper in the canon. Regan had independently measured how much each subject liked Joe — via a post-experiment questionnaire administered under the cover of a separate "person perception" research line. In the **no-favor condition**, the subject's liking for Joe correlated strongly with how many raffle tickets they bought (r = 0.41). This was expected: when no favor was in play, people who liked Joe more were more inclined to help him. **In the favor condition, the correlation between liking and tickets purchased dropped to essentially zero (r = -0.01).** Regan summarized the result:

> "The fact that the favor was effective in producing compliance regardless of liking suggests that the favor was effective for reasons other than the increased liking which it generated. The favor seems to have evoked a feeling of obligation independent of its effects on liking."

— Regan, D. T. (1971), p. 635. https://doi.org/10.1016/0022-1031(71)90025-4

This is the crucial result. In ordinary social life, we expect that *whether we'll help someone* depends on *whether we like them*. Regan's experiment showed that the reciprocation rule operates on a different channel: **once the favor has been received, liking ceases to predict compliance**. The obligation does the work directly. You can extract compliance from a stranger you do not particularly enjoy being around — and in the experimental record, you can do so quite reliably.

A third result, often under-cited but operationally important, distinguished the **personal-favor condition** (Joe brought the Coke) from the **experimenter-favor condition** (the experimenter brought the Coke). The reciprocation effect was much stronger when *Joe personally* delivered the favor. The mere fact of being given a Coke during the experiment did not trigger reciprocation; the favor had to be **attributable to a person** in order to install an obligation toward that person. This is the *targeting* property of the rule: it routes the obligation to whoever delivered the gift, not to the environment.

The Coke experiment is small, simple, and almost unsettling in how decisively it works. A 10¢ unsolicited Coke produces, on average, a return of nearly 50¢ in compliance — even from subjects who do not particularly like the person who gave it. The experiment has been replicated, varied, and stress-tested for fifty years, and the result holds.

Several things about this episode are worth internalizing.

**First**, the rule is **not weak**. In a controlled laboratory setting, with educated undergraduate subjects who would, if asked beforehand, almost certainly have predicted that "of course liking matters more than a small drink," the actual behavior showed the opposite. The reciprocation rule overrides what people, including themselves, would have predicted about their own behavior. This pattern recurs across the reciprocity literature: subjects *underpredict* how strongly they will respond to small favors.

**Second**, the rule is **automatic**. Regan's subjects were not consciously trading raffle tickets for Coke. The favor created a feeling — "I owe Joe one" — that influenced behavior without explicit deliberation. This is part of what Cialdini later called the "click-whirr" property of fixed-action responses. The rule fires, the behavior follows; deliberation is largely retrospective rationalization.

**Third**, the rule is **commercially valuable**, which is why entire industries are built around its deployment. Free samples in supermarkets and farmers' markets are not generosity; they are scaled installations of small obligations. The 90-minute "free" timeshare presentation works on the same logic. Pharmaceutical detailing — small gifts from drug company reps to physicians — has been studied extensively and shown to influence prescribing behavior even at low dollar amounts (Wazana 2000, *JAMA* 283(3); Dana & Loewenstein 2003). Charitable mailings that include a small "free gift" (address labels, calendars) raise more money than mailings that don't. *Krishnamurthy, Goldberg & Larsen 1987* and a long lineage since have shown the lift is large and consistent.

**Fourth**, the rule has **a documented countermeasure**, and the countermeasure is the most useful operational takeaway from this skill. Cialdini's recommendation, derived from Regan's findings and extended in *Influence* (1984, ch. 2): once you notice that the favor was instrumental — not a genuine social act but an install — the obligation is **substantially reduced**. The trick is to mentally **redefine the favor as a manipulation**, which it then is. The Coke from a salesperson at a timeshare presentation is not a gift; it is a marketing expense. Recognized as such, it stops triggering the rule. This is not foolproof — even informed subjects show some residual effect — but the magnitude drops significantly. **Recognition is the defense.**

**Fifth**, in the experiment, the reciprocation rule **survived the absence of liking** — but it does not, as far as the experimental record shows, survive the explicit detection of manipulation. There is an important asymmetry here: the rule is invisible enough to override liking under normal conditions, but it is fragile to the named recognition that *the favor was deployed to install obligation rather than expressing real care*. The Coke experiment was designed precisely so that subjects would not detect the install; in real-world settings where you can ask "why is this person being so generous to me?", you have a tool the experimental subjects lacked. This is the operational core of the defense: **before accepting an unexpected favor, ask the question.**

The Regan 1971 paper is barely 13 pages long. It contains one experimental design, one set of statistics, and a small number of findings. Yet the modern operational understanding of reciprocity — including all the strategic and ethical applications in this skill — rests substantially on it. Cialdini's 1984 *Influence* synthesized this finding with cross-cultural evidence from Mauss and Gouldner into the framework most practitioners now use. But the empirical claim that the rule overrides liking, that it produces asymmetric repayment, and that it operates automatically — that claim came, originally, from the Cornell laboratory, a confederate named Joe, and a 10¢ bottle of Coca-Cola.
