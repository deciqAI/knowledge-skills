# Method in Action: Free AI Credits, Freemium Tiers, and Open-Weight Releases (2023–2026)

> *Example for the [reciprocity](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The 2023–2026 AI boom ran on generosity — or what looked like it. Foundation-model providers handed out free API credits to new developers. AI coding tools shipped genuinely useful free tiers. Labs released open-weight models the world could download and run for nothing. To a founder or an SMB owner deciding what to build on, all of this feels like a gift. Run the **Reciprocity Analysis** and it resolves into something more precise: a large-scale, deliberately-staged reciprocity install — powerful, often legitimate, and easy to mis-price if you don't name the mechanism. This example works the case from **both sides**: the founder *deploying* reciprocity as growth, and the founder/SMB owner *on the receiving end* deciding what to adopt.

This is a *pattern* worked example, not a claim about any single company's undisclosed numbers. Where figures would be needed, treat them as illustrative and run your own.

### Side A — the founder deploying reciprocity (growth)

**1. Name the favor.** Not "we have a free tier." Specifically: *X dollars of free inference credits, no card required, usable for 30 days*; or *unlimited free autocomplete for individual developers*; or *a downloadable open-weight model under a permissive-ish license*. Vague generosity can't be analyzed or defended in a board meeting.

**2. Estimate cost to giver.** Free credits cost real GPU-hours; a free tier carries real serving cost; open-weighting a model forgoes some licensing revenue and hands capability to competitors. The *pull* on the recipient is proportional to this perceived cost — a lavish free allotment installs more obligation than a stingy one. This is why "generous" free tiers convert better than token ones: the asymmetric-exchange property (documented since Regan 1971) means recipients tend to repay *more* than the favor cost.

**3. Estimate value to the recipient.** Often far above the giver's cost — a developer gets a working prototype in an afternoon. The gap between low cost-to-giver and high value-to-recipient is exactly where reciprocity is most efficient as a growth mechanism.

**4. Identify the eventual ask.** Be honest in writing: the ask is *convert to a paid plan*, *stay when the free credits expire*, *build your product on our SDK so switching later is painful*, or *adopt our open model so our ecosystem/standard wins*. Product-led growth is reciprocity plus [switching-costs](../switching-costs/SKILL.md): the free gift installs goodwill now; lock-in collects later.

**5–8 collapse into a design rule.** The install is *benign and durable* only if the recipient would rationally stay **on the merits** once the gift is withdrawn (step 5's counterfactual, applied to your own funnel). If retention depends on the obligation rather than the product, you've built a leaky funnel that churns the moment credits run out — and the reciprocity literature is explicit that recognized manipulation stops triggering the rule.

> **Founder takeaway (startup):** Give value first — genuinely useful free tiers and credits are among the highest-ROI growth moves precisely because reciprocity produces asymmetric repayment. But instrument the *post-gift* counterfactual: measure retention **after** free credits expire, not activation during them. A free tier that only works while it's free isn't a growth engine; it's a discount with a countdown.

### Side B — the founder / SMB owner on the receiving end (adoption)

Now flip it. You run a 12-person startup or a small business, and a vendor offers $50k in free AI credits, a free pilot, and hands-on "solutions engineering" for six weeks. Run the analysis as the *recipient*:

**1. Name the favor.** $50k credits + ~6 weeks of a named engineer's time + priority support. Specific.

**2. Cost to giver.** Real and high — engineer-weeks are expensive. High perceived cost = strong pull. Notice the feeling: *"they've invested so much in us."*

**3. Value to you.** A working integration. Real — but separable from the obligation.

**4. Eventual ask.** An annual contract, an expansion commitment, or a public case-study/logo. Now or at pilot's end.

**5. Run the counterfactual.** *Absent the free credits and the engineer's attention, would we still sign this vendor over the alternative on the merits — price, reliability, exit cost?* If yes, the install is benign; proceed. **If no, you're converting a "no" into a "yes."** Decline the *install* (or re-scope the pilot to a paid, no-strings trial), not necessarily the product.

**6. Check for door-in-the-face.** Watch for the large ask ("3-year enterprise commit") that "concedes" to the moderate one ("just a 1-year") right after all that free help — the concession is anchoring, and the prior generosity amplifies the pull to match. This composes with [anchoring](../anchoring/SKILL.md) and [door-in-the-face](../door-in-the-face/SKILL.md).

**7. Decide.** Accept and evaluate the eventual contract on its own written merits; or accept with an explicit frame reset ("the pilot doesn't obligate us to renew"); or decline the install up front.

**8. Audit cumulative install.** The free credits, the dinners, the conference passes, the "we prioritized your feature request" — analyze the *whole* relationship, not the latest favor. This is where procurement discipline (write your comparison criteria *before* the generosity arrives, and don't update them because of it) does the work per-event analysis can't.

> **Founder takeaway (SMB):** The most expensive vendor decisions get made feeling grateful. Write your selection criteria and your walk-away exit-cost number **before** the free pilot starts, and re-read them at renewal. Recognition is the defense: naming "this is a marketing expense, not a gift" measurably reduces the pull — Cialdini's operational countermeasure, straight from the Regan tradition.

### Why this is the same rule as a 10¢ Coke

Nothing here is new mechanically. A free API credit is a scaled, instrumented version of the confederate's Coca-Cola: a small-cost favor that installs a disproportionate, largely automatic obligation, routed to the giver, that overrides cool evaluation of the merits — *until* the recipient explicitly recognizes the install, at which point the pull drops. The AI era didn't change reciprocity; it just gave it a metered, venture-funded distribution channel and A/B-tested the free-tier size.
