# Method in Action: Sailing Ships vs. Steamships — Technology Substitution (1819–1920s)

> *Example for the [s-curve-technology-adoption](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The century-long displacement of sail by steam in ocean shipping is the canonical case of one S-curve succeeding another — and of an incumbent industry perfecting the first curve while the second curve killed it. Richard Foster, a McKinsey director, used it as a central case in *Innovation: The Attacker's Advantage* (1986) to show why incumbents who dominate a maturing technology systematically fail to jump to its successor.

**Plot adoption over time (Process step 1).** Steam power entered ocean shipping slowly. The SS Savannah made the first steam-assisted Atlantic crossing in 1819 — mostly under sail, with the engine as a novelty. For the following three decades, steamships were a marginal share of ocean tonnage: expensive, coal-hungry, and confined to routes where speed and schedule reliability justified the cost (mail, passengers, coastal runs). Sail carried the overwhelming bulk of world cargo into the 1860s. Plotted as steam's share of ocean tonnage over the nineteenth century, the data trace the classic sigmoid: a long flat tail, a steep middle in the 1870s–1890s, and saturation by the early twentieth century, with sail surviving only in low-value bulk niches (grain, nitrates) before disappearing after World War I.

**Locate the position and confirm with category signals (steps 2–3).** The early buyers of steam were the innovators and early adopters of their day: navies, subsidized mail lines, and passenger operators who bought scheduled departure times — something sail structurally could not offer — and tolerated rough edges like enormous coal consumption and unreliable engines. The pragmatic majority of shipowners, moving bulk cargo on thin margins, judged steam by cost per ton-mile and correctly kept buying sail for decades. Different buyers, different decision criteria, exactly as the adopter-category model predicts.

**Estimate the ceiling and project the trajectory (steps 4–5).** Steam's saturation ceiling was effectively the entire ocean-freight market — but only once its economics beat sail's on ordinary cargo routes. Each engineering advance moved the crossover: compound engines in the 1850s–60s and triple-expansion engines in the 1880s cut coal consumption dramatically, extending steam's profitable range from short runs to transoceanic bulk routes. The opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 — a route sailing ships could not use effectively — shifted the Asia trade to steam almost at a stroke. The steep middle of the substitution curve followed mechanically once steam's cost per ton-mile undercut sail's on mainstream routes.

**What the next category required (step 6).** Winning the pragmatic majority was not a faster version of the early-adopter sale. It required the whole product: a worldwide network of coaling stations, engines reliable enough that insurers and charterers would accept them on long routes, and freight rates that beat sail's on ordinary cargo. Until that apparatus existed, steam's early-adopter virtues — speed, prestige, punctuality — were irrelevant to a bulk shipowner. The decades between steam's debut and its mainstream victory were spent building exactly what the next category demanded.

**The incumbents' response — improving the dying curve (steps 7 and 9).** This is the part Foster made famous. Sailing-ship builders did not stand still: threatened by steam, they produced the fastest and most refined sailing vessels ever built — the clippers of the 1840s–50s and later the great steel windjammers, which cut crew sizes and carried unprecedented cargo. Sail technology improved more in its final half-century than in the previous two. It did not matter. The incumbents were spending their engineering effort pushing a saturating S-curve toward its ceiling while the attackers rode a new curve with a far higher ceiling. Almost none of the leading sailing-ship builders became leading steamship builders. Foster's generalization: when performance gains on the current technology's curve flatten, more effort yields less return, and the rational move is to start the second curve — yet incumbents, whose skills, assets, and customers are all attached to the first curve, almost never do it in time.

**The stop-rule in period costume (step 10).** A shipowner in 1865 extrapolating sail's recent improvements linearly — faster clippers every year — would have concluded sail was safe. Reading the same data as two overlapping sigmoids, one flattening and one steepening, gives the correct forecast: sail's curve was near its ceiling, steam's was entering its steep middle. The forecast was knowable decades before the outcome; the incumbents' failure was one of framing, not of information.

Historians of innovation later gave the incumbents' pattern a name — the "sailing ship effect": a mature technology improving fastest precisely when its successor threatens it. The improvement is real; the strategic conclusion drawn from it is the error. A flattening curve that suddenly steepens under competitive pressure is still a flattening curve.

The mapped steps:
1. Adoption data: steam's share of ocean tonnage, 1819 → 1920s — a textbook sigmoid over roughly a century
2. Position and signals: innovators/early adopters (navies, mail lines, passenger operators) bought scheduled reliability; the pragmatic majority bought cost per ton-mile and stayed with sail until steam's economics crossed over
3. Ceiling and trajectory: successive engine improvements plus the Suez Canal moved the crossover route by route; the steep middle followed in the 1870s–1890s
4. Strategy audit: sailing-ship incumbents optimized the old curve (clippers, steel windjammers) instead of jumping curves — record products, terminal trajectory
5. Second curve: the defining lesson — the time to start the successor curve is while the first still looks healthy; almost no sail incumbent made the jump
6. Stop-rule: linear extrapolation of sail's late improvements predicted safety; reading the data as two sigmoids predicted displacement — the sigmoid was right

Foster's case has been re-run against every substitution since: piston to jet aircraft engines, vacuum tubes to transistors, film to digital photography, combustion to electric vehicles. The names change; the paired sigmoids and the incumbent trap do not.

Primary source: Foster, R. N. (1986). *Innovation: The Attacker's Advantage*. New York: Summit Books.
