# Method in Action: Ryan & Gross's Iowa Hybrid Corn Study (1943) and Rogers's Synthesis (1962)

> *Example for the [s-curve-technology-adoption](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The empirical foundation for the S-curve model of innovation diffusion was laid by two rural sociologists at Iowa State College in a paper published in 1943, and synthesized into the dominant modern framework by Everett Rogers nineteen years later. Both works together constitute the canonical primary-source case for this skill.

In the late 1930s, **Bryce Ryan** (then a new PhD at Iowa State) and **Neal C. Gross** (his graduate student) set out to study a specific puzzle: hybrid seed corn, introduced commercially in Iowa around 1928, was demonstrably superior to traditional open-pollinated varieties — producing yields roughly 20% higher per acre, more uniform in size, and more resistant to drought and lodging. By any economic logic, every Iowa farmer should have switched within a year or two. They did not. Adoption took more than a decade.

Ryan and Gross's study, *"The Diffusion of Hybrid Seed Corn in Two Iowa Communities,"* published in *Rural Sociology*, Vol. 8, No. 1, March 1943, pp. 15–24, addressed two questions: how did adoption actually unfold over time, and what distinguished the early adopters from the later ones? They selected two farming communities — Jefferson and Grand Junction townships in Greene County, Iowa — and conducted face-to-face interviews with **all 259 farmers operating land in those communities**. They asked each farmer: in what year did you first try hybrid seed corn? In what year did you first plant your entire farm in hybrid? They cross-checked against agricultural-extension records. They tracked who had told whom about hybrid corn, who had influenced whom, and how each farmer described the decision.

The results, which Ryan and Gross presented as a cumulative-adoption-over-time curve, produced the canonical S-shape:

- **1928–1932 (years 1–5 since hybrid was available):** only a handful of farmers tried it — about 10% of the eventual adopters during the first five years
- **1933–1936 (years 6–9):** dramatic acceleration; cumulative adoption rose from ~10% to ~60%
- **1937–1941 (years 10–13):** the rate slowed as it approached universal use; cumulative adoption reached ~98%

The duration was significant. Even with a clearly superior, economically obvious technology, **adoption required thirteen years to reach near-saturation**. The first few adopters spent five years in apparent isolation; the bulk of the curve happened in the middle three years; the final stragglers held out for another four years. Plotted on paper, this was the first quantitative S-curve in the diffusion-of-innovations literature, and the shape has reproduced for nearly every innovation studied since.

Ryan and Gross's deeper finding, however, was not the shape — it was the **behavioral differences between early and late adopters**. They wrote:

> "The first acceptors were unusually successful and progressive farmers, more often subscribers to farm magazines, with larger farms and higher incomes, more travelled, and with more contacts with agricultural extension agents. The later adopters were generally older, with smaller farms, less education, and less involvement with formal information sources; they reported having decided to adopt mainly because of conversations with neighbors who had already tried hybrid corn successfully."

— Ryan, B., & Gross, N. C. (1943). "The Diffusion of Hybrid Seed Corn in Two Iowa Communities." *Rural Sociology*, 8(1), p. 18 (paraphrased; the paper is short, descriptive, and the precise quotation is for synthesis rather than reproduction).

This was the original empirical observation that **adopters are not homogeneous** — that the people who adopt early are categorically different from the people who adopt late, in their information sources, decision criteria, and social position. Ryan and Gross did not name the categories. They reported the differences and let the data speak.

The structural reason for the S-curve, as Ryan and Gross suggested and as subsequent research confirmed, is the interaction of two feedback loops. **A reinforcing loop**: each adopter influences a few neighbors to consider adopting (word of mouth, observation of successful use, social signaling). This loop accelerates adoption as the adopter base grows — more adopters means more influencers. **A balancing loop**: as adoption approaches saturation, the pool of remaining non-adopters shrinks, and the rate of new adoption slows. The integration of these two loops produces the S-shape mechanically. (For the underlying dynamics, see [`feedback-loops`](../feedback-loops/SKILL.md).)

Nineteen years later, in 1962, **Everett Rogers** — also a rural sociologist, also Iowa-trained — published *Diffusion of Innovations*, a synthesis of Ryan-Gross and dozens of other diffusion studies that had accumulated in the intervening years (rural sociology, public health, education, agriculture, medical sociology). Rogers's contribution was systematization. He took the empirical pattern Ryan and Gross had documented and gave it categorical structure that has dominated thinking ever since.

Rogers proposed dividing the population into **five adopter categories** based on standard-deviation cuts of an assumed normal distribution of "innovativeness":

> "Adopter categories are the classifications of members of a social system on the basis of their innovativeness. Five adopter categories are used in this book: (1) innovators (the first 2.5 percent), (2) early adopters (the next 13.5 percent), (3) early majority (the next 34 percent), (4) late majority (the next 34 percent), and (5) laggards (the final 16 percent)."

— Rogers, E. M. (1962/2003). *Diffusion of Innovations* (5th edition, Free Press, 2003), p. 282.

The percentage breakpoints were chosen for statistical convenience (one and two standard deviations from the mean of a normal distribution); the *real* contribution was Rogers's documentation that each category had distinct behavioral characteristics drawn from the cumulative empirical literature:

> "Innovators are venturesome, eager to try new ideas... Early adopters have the highest degree of opinion leadership in most social systems... The early majority adopt new ideas just before the average member of a social system; they follow with deliberate willingness, but seldom lead... The late majority adopt new ideas just after the average member; they approach innovations with a skeptical and cautious air... Laggards are the last in a social system to adopt an innovation; many are near isolates in the social networks of their system."

— Rogers (1962/2003), pp. 282–285.

Rogers's framework became the dominant paradigm for thinking about technology diffusion in business, marketing, public health, and public policy for the next sixty years. The 1962 book has sold over a million copies through five editions; it is one of the most-cited books in the social sciences.

Geoffrey Moore's *Crossing the Chasm* (1991) extended Rogers's framework specifically for high-technology B2B products, identifying the **chasm** between early adopters and early majority as the specific high-mortality zone where most technology companies fail. Moore's contribution was not to overturn Rogers but to operationalize one transition — the most commercially consequential one — and to derive the specific strategic moves required to cross it.

The Ryan-Gross-Rogers tradition teaches several points that running an S-curve analysis correctly requires you to internalize.

**First, the S-curve is generated by feedback dynamics, not by aggregating individual preferences.** The shape is structural. It is the integrated behavior of a reinforcing word-of-mouth loop interacting with a balancing saturation loop. If you understand this, you understand why every innovation that spreads through a population — economic, technical, cultural, biological — produces an S. The shape is not an empirical regularity to be explained; it is a mathematical consequence of the underlying loop structure. (See [`feedback-loops`](../feedback-loops/SKILL.md).)

**Second, adopters at different points on the curve are categorically different.** This is the most operationally important insight. Ryan and Gross showed in 1943 that the early adopters of hybrid corn were not "the same as later adopters, but earlier" — they were different people, with different incomes, different information sources, different decision criteria, different relationships to the agricultural-extension service. Rogers systematized the categorization. The strategic implication: the marketing, sales, product, and pricing that work for one category will, in general, not work for the next. **You cannot win the early majority with the strategy that won the early adopters.** Companies that fail to recognize this often die exactly when they should have been crossing the chasm.

**Third, the curve is predictable in shape but not in timing.** Once you have several data points on the curve and an estimate of the saturation ceiling, the rest of the curve's shape is fairly well-constrained — the math of logistic growth is tight. But the *speed* at which the curve unfolds depends on the strength of the word-of-mouth loop, the magnitude of the perceived benefit, the complexity of adoption, the availability of supporting infrastructure, and a half-dozen other factors. Hybrid corn took 13 years in 1928–1941. The fax machine took 20 years (1970s–1990s). Smartphones took about 10 years (2007–2017). AI tools like ChatGPT crossed similar thresholds in months. Same curve, very different timescales. **Don't predict the curve's timing by analogy alone — measure the slope of the early data.**

**Fourth, the chasm is the structural consequence of category mismatch.** Early adopters buy on the basis of "transformative vision" — they want the new thing because it is new. Early majority buy on the basis of "proven ROI in companies like mine" — they want a complete solution backed by references in their specific industry. These are different sales. A company that has won early adopters has, by selection effect, not yet developed the complete-solution, industry-specific-reference, low-risk-onboarding apparatus that the early majority requires. The chasm is the time spent building it. Companies that don't recognize this and try to push more early-adopter sales harder are pushing on the wrong loop; the structural fix is to build the apparatus the next category needs.

**Fifth, the S-curve forecasts saturation — which is also the strategic deadline for the second curve.** The math of the S says that growth slows as you approach the saturation ceiling. Once you are at 80%+ of TAM, new-customer growth is essentially zero. **The strategic implication is that you must have started growing a second S-curve before the first one saturates** — typically by the time the first curve is 50–70% of the way up. Companies that fail to launch the second curve in time are profitable for several more years (the first curve still pays) and then die when it saturates. The succession of S-curves is what distinguishes durable companies from one-product wonders. The relevance to strategic planning is direct: **what is the second curve, and when does it need to start growing measurably?**

The hybrid-corn study was a small, descriptive sociology paper conducted in two Iowa counties by two researchers using face-to-face interviews. It is also one of the most consequential empirical findings in the social sciences of the 20th century — because the shape and dynamics it documented have been validated across hundreds of subsequent diffusion studies, and the framework Rogers built on it has shaped how business, public health, and policy think about the spread of new things for sixty years. Every modern application of S-curve analysis in technology strategy ultimately rests on Ryan and Gross's 1943 finding and Rogers's 1962 synthesis.
