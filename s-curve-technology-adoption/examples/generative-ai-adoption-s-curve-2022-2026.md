# Method in Action: Generative AI on the Adoption S-Curve (2022–2026)

> *Example for the [s-curve-technology-adoption](../SKILL.md) skill.*

When ChatGPT launched publicly on 30 November 2022, generative AI went from a research curiosity to the fastest-adopted consumer application in recorded history. By early 2026 the framing debate — "are we at the inflection or near saturation?" — had become a multi-hundred-billion-dollar capital-allocation question. The S-curve is the right lens, but only if you first separate two curves that are constantly conflated: the **adoption** S-curve (how many people/organizations use the technology) and the **capability/scaling** S-curve (how good the models get per unit of compute and data). They are moving through their phases at different speeds, and mixing them up is the single most common error in the "AI bubble" argument.

**Plot adoption over time (Process step 1).** The consumer adoption data is unusually clean. A UBS analyst note (reported by Reuters on 1 February 2023, drawing on Similarweb estimates) put ChatGPT at approximately 100 million monthly active users about two months after launch and called it the fastest-growing consumer application to that point; the figure was an outside estimate, not an official OpenAI disclosure. OpenAI later reported roughly 100 million weekly active users by November 2023, and by early 2025 was publicly citing figures in the hundreds of millions of weekly active users (around 300 million in late 2024, 400 million by February 2025). Enterprise is a separate, slower series: OpenAI reported surpassing 1 million business customers during 2025, and Microsoft, Google, and others reported rapid but off-a-low-base seat growth for Copilot/Gemini products. Plotted, the consumer curve shows an almost vertical early ramp; the enterprise curve shows a classic slow-then-accelerating early ramp — two different S-curves for the same underlying technology.

**Locate the current position (step 2).** Position depends entirely on how you define the population and which curve you mean.
- **Consumer trial/usage:** somewhere past the innovators and deep into early adopters, plausibly touching the early majority. Reaching hundreds of millions of users in under three years is early-majority *reach* by any historical benchmark. But reach is not the same as habituated daily use, so the honest read is "late early-adopter, crossing into early majority."
- **Enterprise production deployment:** still early — innovators and early adopters. Surveys through 2024–2025 (McKinsey's State of AI, among others) reported that a majority of organizations were *experimenting* with genAI but that the share reporting material, measurable bottom-line impact at scale was far smaller. That gap between "piloting" and "in production driving ROI" is the chasm signature.

So the correct answer to "what phase is genAI in?" is: **consumer adoption is near or entering its steep middle; enterprise adoption is still at the chasm.** One technology, two curves, two phases.

**Confirm with category signals (step 3).** The customer framing confirms the split. Early enterprise buyers in 2023 spoke in innovator/early-adopter language — "we're experimenting," "we want to be at the frontier," tolerating hallucinations and rough integration. By 2025 the dominant enterprise question had shifted to pragmatist framing — "show me the ROI," "what's the accuracy/governance/data-security story," "which of our competitors is actually in production" — the exact language of the early majority demanding a complete, de-risked solution. That linguistic shift *is* the approach to the chasm.

**Estimate the saturation ceiling (step 4).** The ceiling for "occasionally uses a genAI tool" is plausibly most of the online knowledge-working population — very large. The ceiling for "genAI embedded in mission-critical enterprise workflows delivering audited ROI" is bounded by real constraints: accuracy/reliability requirements, regulation (the EU AI Act entered into force in 2024 with phased obligations), data governance, inference cost, and the availability of workflows where probabilistic output is acceptable. These are two very different ceilings, which is why a single "% adopted" number for "AI" is nearly meaningless.

**Fit a curve / project the trajectory (step 5).** Consumer: past ~10–20% of the addressable population with strong word-of-mouth and network effects — the structural signature that says the steep middle is *ahead*, not behind. Enterprise: low single-digit-to-teens percent of workflows in genuine production — the flat early ramp, with the steep middle gated on crossing the chasm (reliability, integration, governance). The stop-rule (step 10) bites hardest here: extrapolating the consumer ramp linearly onto enterprise produces the "AI will transform everything by next year" error, while extrapolating current enterprise ROI-disappointment linearly produces the symmetric "AI is a bust" error. Both linear extrapolations are wrong for the same reason — the curve is a sigmoid, and the two curves are on different clocks.

**Identify the next category and what it requires (step 6).** The next category for enterprise genAI is the early majority — pragmatists. They require what the technology mostly did not have in 2023: proven, referenceable ROI in their own industry; reliability and hallucination controls; security and compliance (SOC 2, data residency, the EU AI Act's high-risk-system obligations); integration into existing systems of record; and predictable cost. This is a "whole product" problem, not a "better model" problem — which is why 2024–2025 saw the frontier labs and their partners pour effort into enterprise features (fine-tuning, retrieval, evals, guardrails, agents) rather than only raw capability.

**Audit strategy against next-category requirements (step 7).** A vendor still selling on "look how capable the frontier model is" is running the innovator/early-adopter playbook into an early-majority buyer. The audit: positioning must move from "most powerful model" to "proven outcome in your workflow"; channels from AI conferences and developer Substacks to industry references and system-integrator partnerships; pricing from experimental credits to predictable per-seat/per-outcome enterprise contracts; onboarding from "here's an API" to a complete deployment with governance. Mismatches on any of these are the enterprise chasm made concrete.

**Predict the chasm (step 8).** Enterprise genAI is *at* the chasm now — the highest-mortality zone. The market reaction of late January 2025 — when DeepSeek's R1 model (released 20 January 2025) drove a sharp selloff in AI-exposed equities on 27 January 2025, with Nvidia's single-day market-value drop of roughly $600 billion widely reported as the largest one-day loss for any company on record at the time — was in part the market re-pricing chasm risk: the possibility that frontier *capability* would commoditize faster than *adoption ROI* would materialize. See [`pmf-crossing-the-chasm`](../pmf-crossing-the-chasm/SKILL.md).

**Plan the second curve — and separate it from the capability curve (step 9).** Here is where the two-curve discipline pays off. The **capability/scaling S-curve** — model performance per unit of compute — is its own sigmoid, and the live 2024–2026 debate is whether pretraining-scaling is flattening (the "scaling wall" argument) even as a *new* capability curve, test-time / inference-time reasoning (OpenAI's o1/o3-style reasoning models, from late 2024), begins its own steep climb. That is a classic paired-sigmoid / second-curve pattern *inside the capability dimension*, independent of adoption. Meanwhile the industry's enormous capital expenditure — the hyperscalers guiding to hundreds of billions of dollars of combined AI-related capex through 2025–2026 — is a bet that the **adoption** curve's steep middle is imminent enough to justify building the compute ahead of demand. The bear case and bull case are really a disagreement about the *phase* of two different curves: bears see a flattening capability curve plus a stuck-at-the-chasm adoption curve; bulls see a fresh reasoning-capability curve plus an adoption curve about to enter its steep middle.

**The stop-rule (step 10).** Any 2026 forecast that draws a straight line — up ("exponential forever") or sideways ("it's plateaued, it's a bubble") — has abandoned the S-curve. The disciplined read holds both curves as sigmoids on different clocks and refuses to collapse them into one number.

The mapped steps:
1. Adoption data: ChatGPT ~100M monthly users in ~2 months per UBS/Reuters estimates (fastest consumer app to that point); hundreds of millions of weekly users and 1M+ business customers reported by 2025 — a near-vertical consumer ramp, a slow-then-accelerating enterprise ramp
2. Position: consumer near/entering the steep middle (early adopters → early majority); enterprise still at the chasm (innovators/early adopters piloting, few at audited-ROI scale)
3. Signals: enterprise buyer language shifted from "we're experimenting at the frontier" (2023) to "show me the ROI, governance, and peer references" (2025) — the pragmatist/early-majority signature
4. Ceilings: "occasionally uses a genAI tool" is huge; "embedded in audited mission-critical workflows" is bounded by reliability, regulation (EU AI Act), cost — two different ceilings
5. Trajectory: consumer steep middle ahead; enterprise steep middle gated on crossing the chasm
6. Next category (enterprise early majority) requires proven ROI, reliability/hallucination controls, compliance, integration, predictable cost — a whole-product problem, not a bigger-model problem
7. Strategy audit: sell proven outcomes, not raw capability; industry references over developer buzz; enterprise contracts over experimental credits
8. Chasm: enterprise genAI is *at* it now; the Jan-2025 DeepSeek selloff re-priced chasm-and-commoditization risk
9. Two-curve discipline: adoption curve vs capability/scaling curve; pretraining-scaling possibly flattening while a new inference-time-reasoning curve climbs — paired sigmoids inside capability, independent of adoption; hyperscaler capex is a bet on the adoption steep middle arriving
10. Stop-rule: reject both "exponential forever" and "it's plateaued" — both are linear extrapolations of a sigmoid; hold the two curves separately

The enduring lesson: "Is AI at inflection or saturation?" is unanswerable until you say *which* curve and *which* population. The capability curve and the adoption curve are different sigmoids on different clocks — and most of the 2024–2026 bull-vs-bear argument is really a disagreement about the phase of two curves that the disputants have accidentally merged into one.
