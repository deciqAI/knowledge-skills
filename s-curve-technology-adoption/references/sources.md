# Sources — s-curve-technology-adoption

> *Primary sources for the [s-curve-technology-adoption](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Ryan, B., & Gross, N. C. (1943). "The Diffusion of Hybrid Seed Corn in Two Iowa Communities." *Rural Sociology*, 8(1), 15–24. The founding empirical study; the first quantitative S-curve in the diffusion-of-innovations literature; the first systematic documentation that early adopters differ behaviorally from late adopters. The full text is accessible through major library databases.
- Rogers, E. M. (1962). *Diffusion of Innovations*. Free Press of Glencoe. The synthesis text that defined the modern five-category adopter framework (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards) and the canonical percentages (2.5%, 13.5%, 34%, 34%, 16%). Updated through five editions; current edition: Rogers (2003), 5th ed., Free Press. ISBN 978-0743222099.
- Moore, G. A. (1991). *Crossing the Chasm: Marketing and Selling High-Tech Products to Mainstream Customers*. HarperBusiness. The extension of Rogers's framework to high-technology B2B contexts; documents the early-adopter to early-majority transition as the principal mortality zone for technology companies. Updated through three editions; current: Moore (2014), 3rd ed. ISBN 978-0062292988.
- Bass, F. M. (1969). "A New Product Growth Model for Consumer Durables." *Management Science*, 15(5), 215–227. The formal mathematical model of diffusion as the interaction of innovation (external influence) and imitation (internal influence) parameters; the canonical quantitative model for fitting and forecasting S-curves. https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.15.5.215
- Geroski, P. A. (2000). "Models of Technology Diffusion." *Research Policy*, 29(4–5), 603–625. The standard modern review of competing diffusion models (logistic, Bass, Gompertz, probit) and the empirical conditions under which each fits. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0048-7333(99)00092-X
- Christensen, C. M. (1997). *The Innovator's Dilemma*. Harvard Business Review Press. The complementary framework for understanding the *succession* of S-curves and why incumbent firms often fail to launch the second curve before the first saturates. ISBN 978-1422196021.
