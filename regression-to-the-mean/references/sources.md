# Sources — regression-to-the-mean

> *Primary sources for the [regression-to-the-mean](../SKILL.md) skill.*

- Galton, F. (1886). "Regression towards Mediocrity in Hereditary Stature." *Journal of the Anthropological Institute*, 15, 246–263. The original paper.
- Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux. ISBN 978-0374533557. Chapter 17, "Regression to the Mean."
- Stigler, S. M. (1986). *The History of Statistics: The Measurement of Uncertainty before 1900.* Belknap. ISBN 978-0674403413. Historical context for Galton's work.
- Senn, S. J. (2011). "Francis Galton and regression to the mean." *Significance*, 8(3), 124–126. Modern statistical interpretation.
- Mlodinow, L. (2008). *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives.* Pantheon. ISBN 978-0307275172. Popular treatment.
- Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 185, 1124–1131. The cognitive-bias treatment.
- Campbell, D. T., & Kenny, D. A. (1999). *A Primer on Regression Artifacts.* Guilford. ISBN 978-1572304826.
- LMSYS Org — Chatbot Arena Leaderboard (2023–). https://lmarena.ai/ — public, frequently-updated ranking of frontier LLMs; illustrates the rapid turnover of benchmark leaders that makes any single-release "#1" spike a poor durable-superiority signal.
- Sainz, O., et al. (2023). "NLP Evaluation in Trouble: On the Need to Measure LLM Data Contamination for each Benchmark." *Findings of EMNLP 2023.* https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-emnlp.722/ — documents benchmark data-contamination/overfitting, one reason a single-eval spike overstates true capability and should be expected to regress on broader testing.
