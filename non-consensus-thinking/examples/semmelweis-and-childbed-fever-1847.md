# Method in Action: Semmelweis and Childbed Fever (1847)

> *Example for the [non-consensus-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

In 1847 Ignaz Semmelweis was a physician at the Vienna General Hospital's First Obstetrical Clinic. The consensus among European physicians was that childbed fever (puerperal fever) was caused by miasma — bad air — or constitutional factors. This consensus was held by the leading medical authorities of the era and was reflected in hospital practice worldwide.

**Step 1 — Consensus:** "Childbed fever is caused by miasmatic or constitutional factors; hand hygiene has no bearing on mortality." Evidenced by: standard hospital protocols, published mortality statistics accepted without source analysis, leading textbooks of the era.

**Step 2 — Why it held:** The miasma theory was the dominant scientific framework for infectious disease. Physicians had strong reputational incentives to accept it; questioning it implied that doctors themselves were killing patients. The herding mechanism and the ego-protection mechanism both reinforced consensus.

**Step 3 — Falsifying condition:** "The consensus would be wrong if mortality rates differed between clinic populations with different exposure to decomposing organic material." Semmelweis observed that the First Clinic (staffed by medical students who came directly from cadaver dissections) had mortality rates 3–5x higher than the Second Clinic (staffed by midwives). This was specific, testable, and damning.

**Step 4 — Edge:** Semmelweis's edge was (a) direct observational data from within the institution that was not being analyzed by others, and (b) an analytical framework borrowed from the death of his colleague Jakob Kolletschka, who died of a similar infection after a scalpel wound during an autopsy. This gave Semmelweis the causal mechanism others lacked.

**Step 5 — Asymmetry:** If he was right and implemented chlorinated lime handwashing, mortality would fall. The downside of the intervention was negligible. The downside of ignoring it was continued preventable deaths. The asymmetry was extreme.

**Outcome:** Semmelweis implemented mandatory handwashing. Mortality in his clinic fell from approximately 10% to below 2%. The consensus rejected his findings for years — he was not vindicated until Pasteur and Lister developed germ theory decades later. The case is the canonical example of a correct non-consensus position held against powerful herding and incentive mechanisms.
