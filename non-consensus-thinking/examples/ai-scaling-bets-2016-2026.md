# Method in Action: Non-Consensus AI Bets That Paid Off (2016–2026)

> *Example for the [non-consensus-thinking](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Between roughly 2016 and 2026, a handful of investors, researchers, and operators made large, unpopular bets on deep learning at scale: that transformer-based foundation models would keep improving predictably as compute and data grew, and that the hardware to train them would become one of the most valuable assets in the economy. At the time, much of the mainstream AI and investment community was skeptical — deep learning was seen by many as narrow, and the idea that simply scaling models would keep working was not the default view. This walks the case through the skill's six process steps. Note the survivorship caveat in Step 5: the point is the *reasoning structure* that separated an edge from a lucky guess, not that "contrarian equals right."

**Step 1 — State the consensus precisely.** Around 2016–2019 the prevailing view among many practitioners was: "Deep learning is powerful but narrow; larger models bring diminishing returns; general-purpose 'foundation models' and dedicating enormous compute to them is not the clear path to broad capability." Evidenced by: the relative scarcity of large-scale training efforts before 2020, and the broad research emphasis on task-specific architectures rather than scale. (The "foundation model" framing itself was only named formally by Stanford's CRFM in 2021, underscoring how non-obvious the category was earlier.)

**Step 2 — Audit why the consensus held.** Several forces sustained it. *Data limitation:* the empirical scaling relationships were not yet published or widely accepted — OpenAI's "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" appeared in January 2020, and the GPT-3 paper ("Language Models are Few-Shot Learners") in mid-2020. *Path dependence:* research incentives and publication norms rewarded novel architectures over "just make it bigger." *Herding:* skepticism toward "brute-force scale" was intellectually respectable and widely shared. The mechanism was primarily **data limitation compounded by incentive misalignment**, not stupidity.

**Step 3 — Identify where it could be wrong.** The consensus would be wrong if capability scaled smoothly and predictably with compute, parameters, and data — i.e., if there were a *law*, not a plateau. Observable evidence pointing that way accumulated: the 2017 transformer paper ("Attention Is All You Need"), then GPT-2 (2019) and GPT-3 (2020) each showing large jumps in general capability from scale, and the 2020 scaling-laws results giving the relationship a quantitative form. This was specific and testable: plot loss against compute and see whether the curve bends.

**Step 4 — Audit the edge.** The people who bet correctly generally held a real, nameable edge, not a hunch:
- **Analytical framework:** treating capability as a predictable function of compute — a view held inside a few labs before it was published or accepted.
- **Information / proximity:** researchers and their close backers saw internal training curves before the field did.
- **Structural / capital:** Nvidia had spent since 2006 building CUDA, turning GPUs into general compute — a decade-long structural position that let it own AI training hardware when demand exploded; Microsoft's 2019 investment (initially reported at roughly $1 billion) in OpenAI locked in privileged access and cloud economics.
This is the gate the stop-rule enforces: each winner could name *why they were better positioned*, not merely that they disagreed.

**Step 5 — Size the asymmetry.** Four-cell table, from the vantage of an early scaling believer:
- *Follow consensus (stay skeptical) + right:* modest — you avoided one expensive research direction.
- *Follow consensus + wrong:* large regret — you miss the defining platform shift of the decade.
- *Non-consensus (bet on scale) + right:* enormous, roughly power-law upside — Nvidia's market capitalization crossed roughly $1 trillion in 2023 and later reached multi-trillion-dollar territory as AI-chip demand surged; foundation-model labs became among the most valuable private companies.
- *Non-consensus + wrong:* survivable *if sized correctly* — a bounded research or capital allocation. Fatal if you bet the whole firm. **Survivorship caveat:** many scale bets failed or were premature; the winners are visible precisely because most attempts are not. The framework's value is that it demands a nameable edge (Step 4) and a survivable downside here — the discipline that separates the vindicated from the merely lucky.

**Step 6 — Decide and record, with an update trigger.** A disciplined believer would pre-commit an update trigger: *"Abandon the scaling thesis if published loss-vs-compute curves flatten materially, or if a dramatically cheaper approach erases the compute moat."* This is not hypothetical. In January 2025, the release of China's DeepSeek models — reported to achieve strong performance at far lower stated training cost — triggered a sharp one-day sell-off in AI-exposed stocks, with Nvidia's shares falling roughly 17% on 27 January 2025, one of the largest single-day market-value drops for a U.S. company. That is exactly the kind of pre-committed trigger this step demands: a concrete signal that the compute-moat premise might be weakening, forcing a re-run of the edge audit rather than entrenchment.

**Outcome:** The scaling thesis was broadly vindicated through 2024–2025 — foundation models became the dominant AI paradigm, and AI-chip demand drove Nvidia to among the highest market capitalizations of any public company. But the DeepSeek reaction, the U.S. export-control rounds on advanced AI chips (2022 and tightened in 2023 and 2024), and open debate about an "AI capex bubble" show the thesis remains contestable. The lesson is the skill's core: being *right* required being non-consensus, but being non-consensus was worth acting on only where a specific edge and a survivable downside were present — and where an update trigger was set before conviction hardened.
