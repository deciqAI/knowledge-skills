# Method in Action: The AI Foundation-Model Industry (2024–2026)

> *Example for the [porters-five-forces](../SKILL.md) skill.*

By the mid-2020s the market for frontier "foundation models" — large general-purpose AI systems sold via API and enterprise contracts — had become one of the most heavily capitalized industries in tech. (This snapshot is written from public information available through early 2026; the 2024–2026 window is the analysis frame, and the later part of it should be treated as forward-looking rather than settled.) A handful of frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, and others) competed to train ever-larger models, while raising and spending sums that only a few years earlier would have been unthinkable for a software business. The framework asks the structural question underneath the hype: *even if these firms execute brilliantly, does the industry's structure let them keep the profit they create — or does it flow through to suppliers and buyers?* Note the SKILL's own caution: a very new market has no stable structure yet, so this is a **snapshot to be re-run**, not a durable verdict.

**Step 1 — Boundary.** The industry is the training and sale of frontier general-purpose foundation models offered to developers and enterprises (via API, cloud marketplaces, and direct contracts). From the typical buyer's substitution view, the competitive set is other frontier model providers a buyer would actually consider swapping between — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and comparable labs — plus, increasingly, open-weight models a buyer could self-host. It is *not* the chipmakers upstream (those are suppliers) nor the downstream application companies that wrap these models (those are buyers/channels). Keeping that line clean is what prevents the analysis from hiding where the profit actually pools.

**Step 2 — Score each force (1 = weak/favorable, 5 = strong/hostile).**

- *New entrants — moderate (3).* Entry barriers are genuinely high: training a frontier model requires multi-billion-dollar compute budgets, scarce specialized talent, and access to constrained cutting-edge accelerators. Yet capital has been unusually available — the leading labs raised very large rounds and struck multi-billion-dollar partnerships with cloud providers in the years through early 2026 — and open-weight releases lowered the bar to a *capable* (if not frontier) model. So barriers are high in absolute dollars but repeatedly cleared by well-funded entrants.
- *Supplier power — strong (4–5).* This is the striking feature of the industry. The critical input — advanced AI accelerators — was dominated by Nvidia, whose data-center GPUs were the default for frontier training, with manufacturing concentrated at TSMC's leading-edge nodes. Compute supply was widely reported as constrained, and Nvidia's data-center business grew enormously over this period. A concentrated, capacity-limited supplier of an indispensable input captures a large slice of the value the labs create.
- *Buyer power — moderate to strong (3–4).* Buyers split into two groups with different leverage. Hyperscale cloud providers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google) are simultaneously distribution channels, investors, and — via their own in-house models — potential competitors, giving them substantial leverage. Enterprise and developer buyers individually have less power, but face low switching costs at the API layer (many apps are written to be model-agnostic and can re-route to whichever model wins on price-performance), which keeps pricing pressure high.
- *Substitutes — strong (4).* Open-weight models (e.g., Meta's Llama family and other openly released models) are the key substitute: for many tasks a "good enough," self-hostable model displaces a paid frontier API, capping what the frontier can charge for commoditized use cases. Rapid price-per-token declines across the industry, widely reported through early 2026, are the visible signature of this pressure.
- *Rivalry — intense (5).* A small number of extremely well-funded competitors race on a fast-moving capability frontier, with high fixed costs (each training run is enormous and largely sunk), little durable differentiation at the model layer as leaders leapfrog each other on successive releases, and aggressive price competition. Every capability lead is temporary; rivals match it within months.

**Step 3 — Binding force.** The single most binding force is **supplier power over compute**. The labs compete fiercely (rivalry) and face substitution from open weights, but the structural feature that most limits their profitability is that an indispensable, concentrated input — advanced accelerators from Nvidia, fabricated by TSMC — sits upstream and captures much of the value created. Intense rivalry and low buyer switching costs then ensure that whatever margin survives the compute bill is competed away rather than retained. The residual left for the model labs is thin relative to the value moving through them.

**Step 4 — Strategic response.** With supplier power binding, the classic move is to attack the supply chokepoint: **backward integration and multi-sourcing of compute.** Consistent with the framework's prescription, the major players pursued custom in-house silicon and diversified accelerator supply (hyperscalers building their own AI chips; labs partnering across multiple compute providers) precisely to reduce dependence on a single dominant supplier. The secondary move — against rivalry and substitutes — is to build switching costs and differentiation *above* the raw model: enterprise trust/safety, tooling, integrations, and agentic products that a model-agnostic buyer cannot cheaply re-route away from. Positioning shifts value from the commoditizing model layer toward stickier surrounding layers.

**Step 5 — Validation.** The framework predicts that in a structure with strong supplier power, intense rivalry, and low buyer switching costs, *the input supplier — not the model labs — captures the outsized returns.* That is exactly the pattern widely reported through early 2026: the dominant accelerator supplier posted extraordinary revenue and profit growth and became one of the most valuable companies in the world, while the model labs, despite very large revenues, were widely reported to be spending heavily and operating at a loss as they raced. Predicted profit pool (upstream compute) and observed profit pool agree. Because the industry is young and fast-moving, the SKILL's caution applies: re-run this when the technology or supply picture shifts.

The mapped steps:
1. Boundary: training and sale of frontier general-purpose foundation models, defined by buyer substitution; chips are suppliers, apps are buyers
2. Force scores: entrants moderate (3), suppliers strong (4–5), buyers moderate–strong (3–4), substitutes strong (4), rivalry intense (5)
3. Binding force: supplier power over compute (concentrated, capacity-limited accelerator supply)
4. Strategic response: backward-integrate / multi-source compute; build switching costs and differentiation above the model layer
5. Validation: profit pooled upstream at the dominant compute supplier while labs spent heavily — matching the structural prediction; re-run as a snapshot
