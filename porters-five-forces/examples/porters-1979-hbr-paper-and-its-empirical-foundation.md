# Method in Action: Porter's 1979 HBR Paper and Its Empirical Foundation

> *Example for the [porters-five-forces](../SKILL.md) skill.*

The framework's primary source is Porter's own 1979 paper. The paper's opening statement remains the canonical articulation:

> "The essence of strategy formulation is coping with competition. Yet it is easy to view competition too narrowly and too pessimistically. While one sometimes hears executives complaining to the contrary, intense competition in an industry is neither coincidence nor bad luck. Moreover, in the fight for market share, competition is not manifested only in the other players. Rather, competition in an industry is rooted in its underlying economics, and competitive forces exist that go well beyond the established combatants in a particular industry."

— Porter, M. E. (1979). "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, 57(2), p. 137.

Porter then enumerated the five forces:

> "Customers, suppliers, potential entrants, and substitute products are all 'competitors' to firms in the industry and may be more or less prominent depending on particular circumstances. Competition in this broader sense might be termed *extended rivalry*. The five forces are: (1) threat of new entrants, (2) bargaining power of suppliers, (3) bargaining power of buyers, (4) threat of substitute products or services, (5) rivalry among existing competitors."

— Porter (1979), p. 137.

The empirical foundation of Porter's framework was the industrial-organization (IO) economics literature he had studied at Harvard — work by Joe Bain (Harvard Business School, 1956, *Barriers to New Competition*), Edward Mason, Richard Caves, and others. The IO school had documented for decades that industry structure correlated strongly with industry profitability. Porter's contribution was synthesizing this academic work into an actionable framework for business strategists.

Empirical validation has been substantial. Anita McGahan and Michael Porter's 1997 paper "How Much Does Industry Matter, Really?" in *Strategic Management Journal* analyzed variance decomposition across 12,000 US public companies and concluded that **industry effects explain ~19% of profit variance across firms**, second only to firm-specific effects (~32%). Year and corporate-parent effects explain smaller portions. The 19% industry-effect is structurally meaningful — it says that *which industry you are in* is a substantial determinant of long-run profitability, controlling for everything else.

The framework has been refined and extended over four decades but the core has held. Critics' alternative frameworks (resource-based view, dynamic capabilities, blue ocean) tend to supplement rather than replace Porter's. The reason for the durability: the framework asks the right question — *what is the industry's structural attractiveness?* — even if specific scoring methodologies have evolved.

Three implementation lessons from extensive use:

**First, the most common failure is defining the industry too broadly or too narrowly.** "Software" or "consulting" lose precision; "vector databases for sub-100-employee AI startups" excludes substitutable alternatives. The right test: define the industry such that competitors are mutually substitutable from buyers' perspectives.

**Second, the binding force is usually one or two forces, not the average.** Mediocre 5-forces analyses sum or average the scores and report "moderately attractive industry." The honest analysis identifies *which* force is binding and concentrates strategic response there. The other forces are background.

**Third, dynamic shifts can move forces over years.** Cloud computing changed software's economics (lower entry barriers; new supplier power from hyperscalers). Generative AI is restructuring multiple industries simultaneously. A 5-forces snapshot is useful; treating it as static for >3-5 years is dangerous.
