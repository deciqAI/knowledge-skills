# Method in Action: Porter's US Passenger Airline Analysis (1992–2008)

> *Example for the [porters-five-forces](../SKILL.md) skill.*

Porter's 2008 Harvard Business Review update of the framework uses the US passenger airline industry as its lead exhibit — the canonical case of an industry where every one of the five forces is hostile at once. His exhibit of average return on invested capital across US industries, 1992–2006, places airlines at the bottom of the league table, far below the cross-industry average, despite decades of intelligent management, heavy investment, and repeated waves of innovation. The framework explains why: the industry's structure, not its executives, sets the ceiling.

**Step 1 — Boundary.** The industry is scheduled passenger air transportation within the US. From the typical traveler's perspective, the substitutable set is other scheduled carriers on the same city pairs — not aircraft manufacturers, not freight carriers. Porter is explicit that industry definition must follow buyer substitution, and the airline case shows why: mixing in adjacent businesses (aircraft leasing, airports) would hide the fact that those adjacencies are the *suppliers* extracting the profit.

**Step 2 — Score each force.** Walking the airline industry through the five forces, per Porter's analysis:

- *New entrants — strong.* Despite chronic losses, new carriers kept entering for decades. Aircraft can be leased rather than bought, gates and routes can be obtained, and there is little proprietary technology. Low entry barriers cap prices even when incumbents consolidate.
- *Supplier power — strong.* Aircraft come from the Boeing–Airbus duopoly; labor is unionized and can ground an airline; airports and reservation systems are concentrated chokepoints. Each supplier group captures a slice of the value the carriers create.
- *Buyer power — strong.* Travelers face near-zero switching costs, treat seats on a given route as interchangeable, and — increasingly through online fare comparison — buy almost purely on price.
- *Substitutes — moderate to strong.* Cars, trains, and buses compete on shorter routes; for business travel, videoconferencing substitutes for the trip itself.
- *Rivalry — intense.* Many competitors, a perishable commodity product (an empty seat at takeoff is worth nothing), high fixed costs that push carriers to fill planes at any fare, and exit barriers (specialized assets, labor agreements, bankruptcy protection) that keep failing capacity in the market.

**Step 3 — Binding force.** No single force dominates in isolation; the diagnostic finding is that rivalry and buyer power bind jointly, and low entry barriers prevent the escape route that consolidation would otherwise offer. Any fare increase is competed away or shopped away.

**Step 4 — Strategic response.** The structure explains the strategies that have worked at the margin: carriers that positioned where the forces are weakest — point-to-point routes with less head-to-head rivalry, differentiated service, loyalty programs that create switching costs — earned returns above the industry's dismal baseline. It equally explains why "execute better" strategies failed: efficiency gains in a structure this hostile pass through to buyers and suppliers, not to shareholders.

**Step 5 — Validation.** Predicted ROIC from the force scores: bottom-decile. Actual: Porter's 1992–2006 data confirm airlines as the lowest-return US industry in the sample. Prediction and data agree — the analysis holds without invoking bad luck or bad management.

The mapped steps:
1. Boundary: US scheduled passenger air transport, defined by traveler substitution
2. Force scores: all five forces score high — entrants, suppliers, buyers, substitutes, rivalry
3. Binding force: rivalry + buyer power jointly, with low entry barriers blocking consolidation relief
4. Strategic response: position where forces are weakest (niche routes, switching costs), not execution alone
5. Validation: 1992–2006 ROIC data place airlines last among US industries, matching the structural prediction

Primary source: Porter, M. E. (2008). "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, 86(1), 25-40. https://hbr.org/2008/01/the-five-competitive-forces-that-shape-strategy
