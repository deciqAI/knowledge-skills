# Method in Action: SpaceX and the Cost of a Rocket (2002–2008)

> *Example for the [first-principles](../SKILL.md) skill.*

A worked example of the First-Principles Teardown applied to a market price — the canonical case of a citable number that is bedrock for a *buyer* but not for a *builder*.

**The claim (step 1):** "Putting a payload into orbit costs tens of millions of dollars, because that is what rockets cost." In 2002 Elon Musk went shopping for launch capacity and found nothing cheaper than refurbished Russian ICBMs; Ashlee Vance documents the Moscow trips where the sellers' quotes struck Musk as arbitrary and rose between meetings. The market price was real and citable — and everyone in aerospace treated it as a floor.

**Surface and tag (steps 2–3):** On the flight home, Musk decomposed the claim instead of accepting it. The assumptions underneath it:

- "A rocket is made of aerospace-grade aluminum alloys, titanium, copper, and carbon fiber, plus propellant" — **BEDROCK**: a bill of materials, priced off commodity markets.
- "Rockets cost what incumbents charge" — **INHERITED**: a market price, not a production cost.
- "Only governments and defense primes can build launch vehicles" — **INHERITED**: an observation about who *had* built them, not about what building them requires.

**Interrogate (step 4):** Is the incumbent price generated by physics or by structure? Traced down, it decomposed into cost-plus contracting, thin competition, and long subcontractor chains — industry structure, not material necessity. **DEMOLISHED** as a measure of what a rocket must cost. Musk later summarized the arithmetic publicly: the raw materials of a rocket ran on the order of a couple of percent of its typical selling price (Anderson, *Wired*, 2012).

**Stop correctly (step 5):** The regress halted at commodity spot prices. For a company *building* rockets from raw stock, metal prices are citable bedrock; reducing further ("but why does aluminum cost that?") would have been performance, not rigor.

**Reconstruct (step 6):** Rebuild launch cost from surviving bedrock only: materials plus propellant plus the engineering to turn one into the other. The enormous gap between material cost and market price was manufacturing, integration, and margin — engineering problems, not laws of nature. SpaceX attacked exactly that gap: Vance documents the company building its engines, airframes, and most components in-house rather than buying from the aerospace supply chain, and advertising Falcon 1 launches at a small fraction of prevailing prices.

**Diff against convention (step 7):** Convention was wrong about the floor — orbital launch did not have to cost tens of millions — but right that integration is brutally hard; SpaceX's first three Falcon 1 flights failed before the fourth reached orbit in 2008. The teardown demolished the inherited price, not the inherited physics.

The mapped steps:

1. Claim stated: "orbital launch must cost tens of millions, because that is what rockets cost."
2. Assumptions surfaced: bill of materials; incumbent pricing; who is capable of building.
3. Tagged: commodity material costs `BEDROCK`; "rockets cost what the market charges" `INHERITED`.
4. Interrogated: the market price traced to cost-plus contracting and thin competition, not physics — `DEMOLISHED` as a cost floor.
5. Regress stopped at commodity spot prices — citable bedrock for a builder.
6. Reconstructed from bedrock: in-house manufacture from raw materials, priced at a fraction of incumbents.
7. Diff: convention's price floor was structure, not necessity; convention's warning about difficulty survived intact.

Primary source: Vance, Ashlee (2015). *Elon Musk: Tesla, SpaceX, and the Quest for a Fantastic Future*. New York: Ecco/HarperCollins. See also Anderson, Chris (2012). "Elon Musk's Mission to Mars." *Wired*, October 2012.
